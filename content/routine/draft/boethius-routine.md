**Boethius \- Lessons from a Dying Philosopher**

1. # Fortune Doesn’t Change 

## Quote 

You have a sharp bump, and you think that Fortune’s attitude toward you has changed. But you’re wrong. She hasn’t changed a bit. She was always whimsical, and remains constant to her inconstancy. You were wrong to take her smiles seriously and to rely on them as the basis for your happiness.” (pg. 28\)

Sentence\_ID: xxx

## Theory

There is a special kind of anger that comes from feeling betrayed. When how we are treated doesn’t match what we think we deserve. A friend who goes behind our back, a broken promise, an illness or injury that comes at too young of an age.

Boethius knew this feeling well. Born in 480 AD, Boethius was an early christian and a lover of philosophy. He planned in his lifetime to translate all of Plato and Aristotle into Latin, making their work more accessible to others.

At the peak of his career, he held the position of chief administrative officer of the Ostrogothic Kingdom of Italy \- the kingdom that controlled Italy after the fall of the Western Roman Emperor. He was essentially second in command to the Ostrogothic king.

So, while not quite a Marcus Aurelius in terms of political office, he was close.

In his early 40s, Boethius had a political fall from grace. He was accused of treason, imprisoned without a fair trial, and then executed.

What came from this episode in history was one of the greatest pieces of philosophical exploration ever written. Boethius wrote in prison *The Consolation of Philosophy*. In this book, he imagines himself visited by lady philosophy, a personification of all the  of philosophyical lessons he had studied. Across its chapters, he argues with Lady Philosophy about how best to understand his fate and what has happened to him. 

Like Marcus Aurelius’ *Meditations* this work is a personal exercise. Boethius is using his writing to practice philosophy and better understand his own personal tragedy.  
In the next 7 lessons, you will learn Boethius’ approach to handling tragedy, and making sense of a world that can seem unfair. You will learn and practice the lessons of Lady Philosophy, so that you can live calmly and not begrudge what happens to you.  
   
While in prison, Boethius wrestled with these emotions of anger and betrayal. Except, instead of ruminating on the betrayal of evil men, he used philosophy to address his anger at the cruelty of the world.

He confronts Lady Philosophy with his anger: How could this change in fortune be fair or deserved? Why has the world suddenly turned on him? He was a kind and honestan honest man, and now he is sentenced to death because of political scheming. How is that change in circumstances fair?

Lady Philosophy’s response is blunt: Fortune has not changed \- it was always fickle but sometimes we stop paying attention.

She says to him:

“You have a sharp bump, and you think that Fortune’s attitude toward you has changed. But you’re wrong. She hasn’t changed a bit. She was always whimsical, and remains constant in her inconstancy. You were wrong to take her smiles seriously and to rely on them as the basis for your happiness.” 

Boethius’ first lesson is this: Whenever we are angry or feel unfairly treated by circumstances, it means we thought we were owed something by nature. That chance had some sort of obligation she didn’t follow through on. That nature made a promise with us that she broke. 

But those kinds of stories are false. Nature doesn’t owe us anything and constantly changes..

Reflect on this today. What are the stories you tell yourself about how your life is ‘supposed to go’? And how do those stories leave you bitter or angry when they don’t come to pass?

2. # Prepare for Change 

## Quote 

You knew the mutability of fortune and you should have prepared yourself against the constant threats of betrayal…If you submit your neck to her yoke, you cannot then complain about what happens to you… (Pg 29).

## Theory

Boethius is in prison. His execution is scheduled. The crime he committed was standing up for a friend. 

Cyprianus, a Roman official, accused Boethius' friend of treason against the king. When Boethius stood to defend his friend against the charge, Cyprianus made the same accusation against Boethius. Scholars agree these charges were false \- in this game of cunning court politics, Boethius had fallen on the wrong side.. 

A Roman official accused Boethius' friend of treason. When Boethius defended him, the same charge was turned on Boethius. The accusation was false. He had landed on the wrong side of court politics.

Treason was a capital offence.

By all accounts Boethius had tried to lived a fair and honest life, butand now he was set to die for treason, a capital offence. 

Boethius turned to philosophy for consolation. What other lessons could he learn from reason? 

He engages in a conversation with Lady Philosophy, like the protagonist of his own socratic dialogue. 

Lady Philosophy makes another argument against grief. She tells Boethius:

“You knew the mutability of fortune and you should have prepared yourself against the constant threats of betrayal…If you submit your neck to her yoke, you cannot then complain about what happens to you…”

Lady philosophy’s argument is that it makes no sense to assume the rules of life don’t apply to us. We know, from our own lives and watching others, that fortune changes. Sometimes luck is good and sometimes it is bad.

People are one day healthy, and then the next day sick. CaesarCeasar the champion of Rome, only to be be killed by those who he thought supported him. Likewise, sometimes our circumstances improve just as quickly. We get a unique job offer from a social connection, or we meet a new friend by change.

Fortune is fickle and changing, both good and bad. 

Boethius’ luck was good when he rose to political prominence. But that same good luck put him in the line of political intrigue and betrayal. This kind of intrigue is unavoidable at court. You can’t base your life on politics, and then be surprised by the bad parts of it.

To paraphrase Marcus Aurelius, we can’t be surprised when a fig tree produces figs.

The Stoics argued that externals are outside of our control. Anything that is not our character, can change. This includes our job, our reputation, and even our health. 

Expect change, so that when it inevitably arrives, you will be prepared.

3. # Fortune’s Loan

## Quote 

When nature brought you out of your mother’s womb, you were naked and poor, and helpless. And I accepted you and was kind to you. I pampered you and gave you more than you needed. Indeed, one could say that I spoiled you and that is why you are now so angry with me (pg. 31\)

Sentence\_ID: xxx

## Theory

Listen to how we talk about loss. We say something was taken from us. Stolen. Ripped away. The words assume the thing was ours to begin with.

Accused of treason, Boethius’ treatment was proportional to his alleged crime. He spent his last days in a dark cell in Pavia, the capital of the Ostrogothic kingdom. Isolated from his friends and family \- he was reportedly tortured before his eventual execution.

He had, in the traditional sense, lost everything. When we speak of losing something, we often fall into the language of theft. We might say that something was taken or stolen from us \- as if it belonged to us.

Lady Philosophy challenges Boethius here to rethink things. As she says:

“When nature brought you out of your mother’s womb, you were naked and poor, and helpless. And I accepted you and was kind to you. I pampered you and gave you more than you needed.”

Her point is that nothing fortune gives us is truly ours. 

Good fortune might give us many things, but these are extras. We are pampered. The right way to approach this situation is to remember that all these things are on loan.

400 years before Boethius sat in a cell, the Stoic epictetus wrote:

“Do not say of anything “I have lost it,” but rather, “I have given it back.” Has your wife died? You have given her back. Has your child died? You have given him back. Have you lost your home? You have given it back. “But,” you may retort, “a bad person took it.” It is not your concern by what means something returns to the Source from which it came. For as long as the Source entrusts something to your hands, treat it as something borrowed, like a traveller at an inn.”

Epictetus knew this fact, and Lady Philosophy taught it to Boethius. Fortune can give you things, but it can also take them back. That’s the deal.

4. # Be Grateful

## Quote 

“Balance out the good things and the bad that have happened in your life and you will have to acknowledge that you are still way ahead.” (Pg 36\)

## Theory

Boethius is in a cell, waiting to die. He turns to philosophy for techniques to ease himself and his situation.

And he has discovered many.

Up until now we’ve discussed different strategies Boethius takes for handling the apparent cruelty of his situation: First, Give up any stories that make us angry at fate; then remember that we should have been prepared for change, and finally, don’t feel entitled to anything fortune has loaned us. 

The strategies are defense. They are about reducing pain. Lady Philosophy uses a medical analogy \- first philosophy must numb the pain before it can provide a deeper treatment. 

Once Boethius has moved past his most extreme anger and grief, Lady Philosophy adopts a different approach: Be grateful. 

By all accounts Boethius had an incredibly successful and happy life, punctuated by a terrible ending. He achieved incredible professional success, rising to the highest administrative position in the entire kingdom. 

Intellectually he was well educated, well-read. He had healthy children, and his two sons held the position of consul of Rome. He has, at the time of his imprisonment, a healthy father, mother and wife. Fortune has been kind to the people he cares about. 

So, Lady philosophy asks him to consider all these things, alone in his cell. She says: 

“Balance out the good things and the bad that have happened in your life and you will have to acknowledge that you are still way ahead.” 

There are a few philosophical strategies here, but they are all about expanding our perspective.

The Stoics taught us to expand our perspective to get a better understanding of the world as it really is. For example, taking the view from above allows us to imagine our lives as one small part of a larger world. Memento Mori encourages us to think of ourselves as already dead. 

Stoics play with perspective because it reveals to us truths we can miss when we focus on ourselves in the present moment. 

Boethius does two things here. First, when counting up his blessings and pains \- he applies the math over his entire life. He changes his perspective of time.

Second, he also shifts his perspective to more people than just himself \- something we don’t actually see that often in the Stoics.

Maybe Boethius is suffering, but his wife is healthy and his sons are successful. It’s a good point \- when calculating our luck, why stop with us? We should also consider the luck of those we love and want to be happy. 

There is a concept of reversion to the mean \- The more things you measure, the more likely you are for the average of your measurements to represent the true average. One trip to the casino might make you a millionaire, but measure a 1000 trips to have a better sense of your success.

Over a large enough time scale, and over enough people, lives tend to be average, or less bad or good than any particular situation indicates. Keep this in mind like Boethius did. 

If things seem unfairly bad \- you are probably focusing on too specific of a moment.

5. #  You Can’t Lose What’s Most Important

## Quote 

Is there anything more precious to you than yourself? No certainly not. But if you are in possession of yourself, then you have that which you don't want to lose and which fortune cannot take away from you. 

Sentence\_ID: xxx

## Theory

For the Stoics and other ancient philosophers, negative emotions are like disorders. Disorders have treatments.

But nNot all treatments have the same function. Sometimes we treat to kill the pain and other times we treat to cure the illness. It'sIts the difference between attacking the cause and the symptoms.

Boethius’ symptom is his grief and anger. You can treat that by reframing his situation. But then lady Philosophy goes after the cause of these symptoms. The deep beliefs that Boethius has. Specifically he gives chance the power to determine his life, because he overvaluesover values what it can give him. He is grieving because he thinks the things outside of his control, external to him, are what make him happy.

But this belief is false. 

Lady Philosophy asks:

“Is there anything more precious to you than yourself? No certainly not. But if you are in possession of yourself, then you have that which you don't want to lose and which fortune cannot take away from you.”

This is a Stoic argument. Fortunate can only harm externals, but not you. And, when we interrogate what really matters to us, we care about ourselves more than fame or political success. 

In his Discourses, the Stoic Epictetus writes about a hypothetical argument with a tyrant. The Tyrant says:

“Tell me the secret which you possess.”  The Tyrant says.

I will not, for this is in my power. The Stoic responds.

 “But I will put you in chains.” Man, what are you talking about? Me in chains? You may fetter my leg, but my will not even Zeus himself can overpower my choice. “I will throw you into prison.” My poor body, you mean.

Epictetus’ story is about how the tyrant can only threaten what he can control \- your body and your reputation. He can’t really harm your character.

And we all understand in some sense that we value ourselves more than anything external. What it means for our life to go well is for us to do well.

We understand that distinction, but we lend out our happiness to others.

We always have the thing most important to us \- our own choice. Not even fortune can take that unless we give it to them.

6. # The Burden of Wealth

## Quote 

My view is that nothing is good that is harmful to him who owns it…But riches have very often harmed those who possess them…So there you are, with your stuff, and you find yourself fearful about being waylaid or even murdered for your possessions. 

Sentence\_ID: xxx

## Theory

When Plato wrote about tyrants in his famous book the Republic, he did not just criticize them \- he pitied them. Often when we talk about powerful or corrupt people today it is to condemn them.

We wonder how the warlord, or corrupt politician, or selfish business leader could do the things they do. But Plato pitied them, because he saw them as a soul corrupted. Their soul had been weakened by its desire for money and possessions. Being a tyrant was not a good way to live. It is not a good life. It is not one we would choose, if given the opportunity.

So why then, are we so sad when we lose our own fame and fortune?

Lady Philosophy says to Boethius:

“My view is that nothing is good that is harmful to him who owns it…But riches have very often harmed those who possess them…So there you are, with your stuff, and you find yourself fearful about being waylaid or even murdered for your possessions.”

Lady Philosophy’s lesson is this. We seek money and possessions because we think it will make us secure and happy. But often it has the opposite effect. The more we have, the more we are afraid of losing it. This was as true 1500 years ago in Boethius’ time as it is today. The wealthy are still worried about losing their money. The famous influencer will still spiral if a new post gets less likes than the last one. 

So Lady philosophy is targeting another core belief \- that our happiness comes from our wealth. She points out that even though we grieve the loss of all of our external things, they are not responsible for our happiness \- often they make our lives worse.

She raises the same point around our fancy job or title. She says, quote:

“If those offices and their powers had anything inherently good in them, they would never be possessed by wicked men, for in nature opposites do not attract one another. And since there can be no doubt that offices are often held by evil men, then it follows that they are not in themselves good.”

In other words, your job, title and office do not make yourself or your life good, because there are unhappy or evil people that have the same title as you. 

It is a very Stoic argument. Your wealth and titles are external to you. They don’t make you happy \- often they contribute to your stress, anxiety, or unhappiness. We become rich only to become anxious about losing it all. We become powerful but have to give up our honor and honesty along the way. 

We pity the tyrant because they are controlled by their desire for wealth and status, but we desire these things ourselves. In life stay focused on what is up to you \- yourself.

7. # Know thyself

## Quote 

Indeed, the condition of human nature is just this; man towers above the rest of creation so long as he realizes his own nature, and when he forgets it, he sinks lower than the beasts. For other living things to be ignorant of themselves, is natural; but for man it is a defect.”

Sentence\_ID: xxx

## Theory

Boethius doesn’t get a happy ending. At the young age of 44 he is executed in Paiva Italy. He does not get to reunite with his wife. He does not ever see again the sons he is so proud of. He is not saved at the last minute. No one rushes in to disprove the charges or punish the unjust men who accused him

By all traditional accounts, like Socrates, Seneca and so many philosophers before him, he comes to an unfortunate end. How we view Boethius’ life is itself a lesson, a kind of rorschach test for how convincing his arguments have been.

Do you see Boethius as a tragic figure, who spent his last days trying to intellectualize away the sad nature of his impending death? Or do you see Boethius as the last of the Roman philosophers, unharmed by external circumstances because he kept his character and reason intact? 

Regardless of where you land, one key lesson is this: As philosophers, we must examine our own lives and the stories we tell about them. We can’t take for granted what we are told.

As Boethius puts it:

“Indeed, the condition of human nature is just this; man towers above the rest of creation so long as he realizes his own nature, and when he forgets it, he sinks lower than the beasts. For other living things to be ignorant of themselves, is natural; but for man it is a defect.”

In his words, we only  reach our true potential when we reflect on our own lives. This means to engage in the philosophical project of self-examination.

And we can bring to this project some of Boethius’ tools: Recognize that fortune is always fickle and change, be grateful for what you what and only ever look at it as on loan to you, no one can take your character or choices from you, and finally the external money and power we chase after often makes us more unhappy than happy. 

These lessons are all ways of shifting our perspective, to get us closer to the truth. And that is the point of philosophy \- to see things as they really are.

So let’s end with a reflection on Boethius \- was he happy or cursed? Was his story a tragic one or inspiring?

Your answer will say a lot about your philosophy.
