# Principles from the Boethius Review-and-Revision Cycle

The review of the Boethius routine has now been partly absorbed into a revised
Drive draft. Reading the review next to the revision tells us two things:

1. Which review observations had purchase in the actual rewrite (the
   *grounded* upshots — what the revision proves about how routines should be
   written).
2. Which review comments drove change and which ones got skipped — useful for
   improving how we write reviews next time.

This doc captures both.

## What the revision actually changed

For grounding, here is what moved between the local draft and the new Drive
version. Each item maps to a review comment that landed.

**Lesson openers reset to a hook, not the same biographical scene.**

- L2 now opens "A friend gets sick. A company lays off half its staff. A
  marriage ends," then turns to Boethius. The local draft opened with
  "Boethius is in prison."
- L4 now opens "There is a saying: 'You're only as good as your worst day'."
  The local draft opened with "Boethius is in a cell, waiting to die."
- L3 already had the strong "Listen to how we talk about loss" hook and was
  kept.

**Mechanical fixes were swept.** "to be be killed" → "to be killed";
"Fortunate" → "Fortune"; "epictetus" → "Epictetus"; "hold stead" → "hold
steady"; "You must grateful" → "You must be grateful"; the duplicated "his one
personal tragedy" sentence removed; "constant to her inconstancy" reconciled
to "constant in her inconstancy" across both occurrences.

**The reframe is named and walked, not just stated.** L3 gained: "When you
take Epictetus' advice, and reframe things as returned instead of lost or
stolen, then your outlook changes. You hold things more lightly. When they
go, you feel less pulled apart." That is the explicit-benefit language
CLAUDE.md calls for.

**L7 closes with a numbered tools recap.** The local draft had a single
run-on sentence collecting the four moves. The Drive version breaks them out:
"First, recognize that fortune is always fickle... Next, Lady Philosophy
taught us... Then, realize... And finally..." The arc is now legible.

**L4 made "widen the view" the structural spine, not just the closer.** The
strong line the review flagged to keep ("Count the years. Count the people.")
is no longer only the last beat — the body now builds toward it with "When
fortune turns, our view narrows... Lady Philosophy asks Boethius to widen the
frame. Look at the years, not the day."

## What the revision did *not* change

Tracking the misses is at least as useful as tracking the hits.

- All `Meditation`, `Commitment`, and `Action` sections are still `xxx`/`xx`.
- All quotes still say `Quote_ID: TBC`. No `Source` or `Location` added.
- Em dashes were not swept anywhere in the doc.
- L1 still opens on "Life isn't fair" (review flagged as platitude).
- L7 still has "we only reach our true potential" (review flagged as
  off-voice).
- L7 does not call back to the opening quote.
- L2 still has "Luck runs both ways" duplicated within a few lines.
- L2 still says "fruit tree produces fruit" (the local "fig tree produces
  figs" was the better version and review asked for the revert).
- L5's Epictetus tyrant dialogue is still garbled ("but my will not even Zeus
  himself...").
- L3's long Epictetus block quote was not trimmed.
- L3's "Endings, of a job, a relationship, stop feeling like attacks, they
  become returns" comma splice remains.
- L6 still carries two long Lady Philosophy quotes back-to-back.

## Content upshots (grounded in what changed)

These are principles for writing routines that the revision *acted on* —
worth promoting into AGENTS.md or PARADIGMS.md once confirmed.

1. **Open on the listener's life, not the historical figure.** When three
   adjacent lessons opened by re-introducing Boethius's cell, two of them got
   rewritten to lead with the reader's experience first (a friend gets sick;
   you're only as good as your worst day). Use the historical figure as
   illustration of a feeling the listener already has, not as the cold-open.

2. **Vary openers deliberately across a series.** Repetition across lessons
   is invisible while drafting one lesson at a time and obvious when read in
   sequence. Treat opener variety as a dedicated pass.

3. **Walk the reframe; do not just state it.** "Treat losses as returns" on
   its own is a slogan. The revision earned the move by adding the
   step-by-step ("you hold things more lightly. When they go, you feel less
   pulled apart"). Spell out what changes for the listener when they apply
   the move.

4. **A multi-lesson arc needs an explicit numbered recap at the end.** The
   final lesson's tools list was the single biggest structural improvement.
   Without it, the lessons read as standalone; with it, the series lands as a
   sequence.

5. **Amplify strong lines structurally, not just preserve them.** L4's
   strong closer ("Count the years. Count the people") was promoted into the
   organizing frame of the lesson. The cleanest line in a draft is usually a
   hint about what the lesson is actually about — build around it.

## Review-craft principles (what made our comments land or miss)

These are principles for writing reviews, learned from which comments got
actioned and which got skipped.

1. **Always include the replacement, not just the diagnosis.** Comments that
   shipped a verbatim alternative — "Try: 'In his words, we are most
   ourselves when we examine our own lives'" — were the type of comment most
   likely to be acted on, *except* the writer didn't take that specific one.
   Net pattern: where a concrete fix was obvious and one-step, it landed;
   where the fix needed a fresh sentence written by the reviewer, write it.
   The "true potential" miss is the cautionary case: we named the problem
   and offered a replacement, but the replacement was buried two clauses
   into the comment. Lead with the replacement.

2. **Promote bulk-edit chores to a top-line checklist.** Em dashes were
   flagged in "Voice & style" and never swept. Anything requiring a
   doc-wide pass — em dashes, quote ID lookup, Quote_ID/Source/Location
   blocks — deserves a checkbox at the top of the review, not a paragraph
   buried mid-document.

3. **One anchor, one action.** The L3 Epictetus comment bundled three asks
   (capitalize the name, trim the long quote, restructure the surrounding
   passage). Only the capitalization landed. Split compound asks into
   separate anchors so the writer can resolve them independently.

4. **State directives, not options.** "Reconsider whether the swap was an
   upgrade" and "consider numbering them" both read as optional and were
   deferred. The numbered-tools recap *did* land because the same point was
   restated as a top-priority structural fix. Hedged framing makes comments
   skippable; pointed framing makes them actionable. If you believe a swap
   was a regression, say "Revert," not "Reconsider."

5. **Pattern observations beat per-instance flags.** Naming the repeated
   "Boethius is in prison" opener as a cross-lesson pattern produced a
   sweeping rewrite across two lessons. Three separate "this opener is
   repetitive" comments would not have. When you notice the same problem
   twice, group it and name the pattern.

6. **Citing a paradigm rule is half a comment — finish it.** "Close by
   calling back to the opening quote" was an authoritative cite from
   PARADIGMS.md and still did not produce a callback in the Drive revision.
   Citing the rule sets the expectation; drafting the actual sentence shows
   the writer what satisfies it. Pair every rule citation with a sample
   line.

7. **Affirmations are leverage, not filler.** "Keep" comments on the
   strongest lines did not merely preserve those lines — the writer leaned
   into them structurally. Be deliberate about marking the load-bearing
   sentences. They are signals about what to build around.

8. **Separate writer-blocked items from review items.** The
   Meditation/Commitment/Action stubs are not review feedback; they are
   drafting prompts. This review already separated them into "Once you draft
   X." Keep that separation — mixing them into the per-lesson critique
   dilutes the action list.

9. **The first read of a revision should map review hits and misses.**
   Reviewing the revision against the prior review (not the prior draft) is
   the highest-leverage moment in the loop. The misses are the next review's
   top of the agenda, and the patterns in the misses (every bulk-sweep
   skipped, every soft-pedaled comment skipped) are how we improve our
   reviewing.
