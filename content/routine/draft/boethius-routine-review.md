# Review — Boethius Routine

Review is against the **Drive version** linked above (more recent than `boethius-routine.md` in this repo). Comments are anchored to short quoted snippets so each one can be pasted in as a Drive comment.

## Top priorities

1. **Every Meditation, Commitment, and Action is a stub (`xxx`/`xx`) across all 7 lessons.** Per `AGENTS.md`, the meditation should enact the theory, and the commitment/action should mirror it. Until those are drafted, the routine can't be reviewed as a routine — only as theory essays. Recommend treating each lesson as four interlocking pieces in one drafting pass rather than finishing all theory first.
2. **No quote has an `Id`, `Source`, or `Location`.** All 7 say `Quote_ID: TBC`. Per `AGENTS.md`, pull from `resources/quotes/quotes.md` and use the `Quote ID` verbatim; if not in the library, mark it clearly so an editor can replace it. Source should be `The Consolation of Philosophy` with book/section (the local draft has page numbers — those are translation-specific and should be replaced with book/section refs).
3. **Several quotes are over 5 sentences.** `AGENTS.md` flags this as overflow risk on the in-app quote card. Trim each to its load-bearing sentence(s); the rest belongs in the Theory body where you're already quoting again. Specifically: Lessons 1, 2, 3, 5, 7.
4. **Arc is loose.** Lessons 3–6 could be reordered without breaking the through-line. `PARADIGMS.md` (`dichotomy-of-control-routines-doc.md` exemplar) names the progression of each entry. Consider naming the arc explicitly — Lesson 1 already promises "the next 7 lessons" but doesn't say what each is for. Suggested frame: 1 Diagnosis → 2 Preparation → 3 Reframing loss → 4 Widening the lens → 5 Locating the self → 6 Disarming external goods → 7 Self-examination.
5. **No closing callback to the opening quote.** `PARADIGMS.md`: "Close by calling back to the opening quote." Lesson 7 closes on "the point of philosophy — to see yourself and the world as they really are" but doesn't return to Lady Philosophy's "She hasn't changed a bit" or any earlier Boethius line. A direct callback would land the series.

## Voice & style

- **Em dashes.** `AGENTS.md` rule: no em dashes; use a period, comma, colon, or "and." The export uses hyphen-surrounded-by-spaces as a stand-in throughout (e.g. "hold stead - to be the exception", "She hasn't changed a bit - it was always fickle"). Sweep all of them.
- **Repetitive lesson opener.** Lessons 2, 3, and 4 all open by re-establishing "Boethius is in prison / in a cell, waiting to die." Each beat should pick up where the last left off, not reset to scene. Caleb's CLAUDE.md flags this kind of resetting language as something to catch.
- **Tone drift toward inspirational.** A few spots slide out of Stoa's measured house voice:
  - Lesson 1: "Life isn't fair" as opener reads as platitude. `PARADIGMS.md` wants a hook that earns attention with a sharp image, question, or claim. `justice-routines.md`'s trio of "If you promise..." / "If someone asks..." is the corpus gold standard.
  - Lesson 7: "we only reach our true potential when we reflect on our own lives" — "true potential" is the register CLAUDE.md flags as not-Stoa.
- **Long biographical setup before the hook lands.** Lesson 1 spends ~200 words on Boethius's career arc before the lesson's claim ("Nature doesn't owe us anything") appears. `AGENTS.md` suggested improvement #3: "A one-line opener followed by context reads better aloud."
- **Quote opener variety.** Of the 7 quotes, openings are: "You have...", "You knew...", "When nature...", "Balance...", "Is there...", "My view...", "Indeed...". That's varied — good. No repeats of the corpus-heavy "It is...", "We...", "When..." patterns.

## Lesson 1 — Fortune Doesn't Change

- Anchor: `Life isn't fair. We all kn...e abstract sense`
  Hook is a platitude. Try opening on the betrayal-anger image you have lower down ("There is a special kind of anger that comes from feeling betrayed" — actually stronger; this is the local-draft opener and was removed in the Drive version). Reconsider whether the swap was an upgrade.

- Anchor: `Boethius is using h...his one personal tragedy. In this what he is...`
  **Duplicate sentence and typo.** The sentence "Boethius is using his writing to practice philosophy and better understand his one personal tragedy" appears twice in the same paragraph, and "his one personal tragedy" should be "his own personal tragedy." Likely an editing artifact.

- Anchor: `In the next 7 lessons, you will...`
  This series-frame paragraph is good for Lesson 1 only — confirm it's not duplicated downstream. Consider naming the lessons explicitly here so the arc is signposted (see top priority #4).

- Anchor: `She was always whimsical, and remains constant to her inconstancy.`
  Top-of-lesson quote has the line as "constant to her inconstancy"; the in-theory restatement has "constant in her inconstancy." Pick one (the second reads more naturally).

- Anchor: `Reflect on this today. What are the stories...`
  Theory ends with a reflective prompt. `AGENTS.md` rule 3 wants Theory to "end with delivering value" — a prompt is fine, but this is also the work the Meditation should do. Risk of doubling up. Decide whether the contemplative move (interrogating one's "supposed to go" story) belongs in Theory or in Meditation; ideally only the latter.

## Lesson 2 — Prepare for Change

- Anchor: `we expect our own lives to hold stead - to be the exception`
  Em dash. Also "hold stead" reads odd; likely meant "hold steady."

- Anchor: `Caesar was the champion of Rome, only to be be killed`
  Typo: "to be be."

- Anchor: `Luck runs both ways. ... Luck runs both ways.`
  The line "Luck runs both ways" appears twice in close succession — editing artifact.

- Anchor: `Anything that is not our character, can change.`
  Comma splice / awkward break. Either "Anything that is not our character can change" (no comma) or restructure.

- Anchor: `we can't be surprised when a fruit tree produces fruit`
  Local draft used "fig tree produces figs" (closer to the Aurelian phrasing). Drive version generalized it. The specificity ("fig") was the better version — concrete vignettes beat abstract ones per `PARADIGMS.md`.

## Lesson 3 — Fortune's Loan

- Anchor: `Listen to how we talk about loss. We say something was taken from us. Stolen. Ripped away.`
  Strongest hook in the routine. Keep.

- Anchor: `Boethius felt that everything was taken from him.`
  Good — turns the hook back on Boethius's situation in one sentence rather than restating his bio.

- Anchor: `400 years before Boethius sat in a cell, the Stoic epictetus wrote:`
  Capitalize "Epictetus." Also: the Epictetus passage that follows runs 5 sentences and is the longest block-quote in the routine. It's inside Theory (not the lesson quote), so overflow isn't a concern, but it does dominate the section. Consider trimming to "Do not say of anything 'I have lost it,' but rather, 'I have given it back.' ... treat it as something borrowed, like a traveller at an inn." and dropping the middle examples.

- Anchor: `Endings, of a job, a relationship, stop feeling like attacks, they become returns.`
  Comma splice and rhythm issue. Try: "An ending — a job, a relationship — stops feeling like an attack. It becomes a return." (Then sweep the em dashes per house style.) Better: "When a job ends, or a relationship, it stops feeling like an attack. It becomes a return."

## Lesson 4 — Be Grateful

- Anchor: `Boethius is in a cell, waiting to die.`
  Same opening framing as Lessons 3 and (effectively) 2. Vary.

- Anchor: `Lady Philosophy adopts a different approach: You must grateful.`
  Typo: "You must be grateful."

- Anchor: `There is insight in this structure. You can't instill gratitude in someone grieving`
  This is a sharp meta-observation about the sequence of philosophical care — keep. It also justifies why this is lesson 4 and not earlier, which is the kind of arc-naming the routine needs more of (top priority #4).

- Anchor: `If your situation feels unbearable today, the work is to widen the view. Count the years. Count the people. See what is still good.`
  Cleanest closer in the routine. Keep.

## Lesson 5 — You Can't Lose What's Most Important

- Anchor: `For the Stoics and other ancient philosophers, negative emotions are like disorders.`
  Strong opener. Keep.

- Anchor: `This is a Stoic argument. Fortunate can only harm externals, but not you.`
  Typo: "Fortune," not "Fortunate."

- Anchor: `In his Discourses, the Stoic Epictetus writes about a hypothetical argument with a tyrant.`
  Cite the location (`Discourses` 1.1 or wherever the passage actually sits). The Epictetus quote is paraphrased — the punctuation in the dialogue ("'Tell me the secret which you possess.' The Tyrant says.") is muddled; mark stage directions clearly or render as straight dialogue.

- Anchor: `you may fetter my leg, but my will not even Zeus himself can overpower my choice`
  Garbled — likely "but my will — not even Zeus himself can overpower my choice." Repair.

- Anchor: `We understand that idea in the abstract, but we lend out our happiness to others.`
  Strongest line in the lesson. Keep — and consider letting the Meditation enact exactly this ("notice where you've lent your happiness out, and recall it").

## Lesson 6 — The Burden of Wealth

- Anchor: `When Plato wrote about tyrants in his famous book the Republic, he did not just criticize them - he pitied them.`
  Good lede. Em dash to fix.

- Anchor: `So why then, are we so sad when we lose our own fame and fortune?`
  Strong pivot.

- Anchor: `She raises the same point around our fancy job or title. She says, quote:`
  Second Lady Philosophy quote in one Theory section. The first one already carries the argument. Either cut this one or make the second one shorter (one sentence). Right now Theory has three long block quotes (Lady Philosophy x2 + the Epictetus parallel implied) and reads dense.

- Anchor: `We pity the tyrant because they are controlled by their desire for wealth and status, but we desire these things ourselves.`
  Good closer. Keep.

## Lesson 7 — Know thyself

- Anchor: `Boethius doesn't get a happy ending.`
  Strong, direct. Keep.

- Anchor: `In his words, we only reach our true potential when we reflect on our own lives.`
  "Reach our true potential" reads as motivational, off-voice. Try: "In his words, we are most ourselves when we examine our own lives."

- Anchor: `And we can bring to this project some of Boethius' tools:`
  The 4-tool recap is the right move for Lesson 7 (it earns the series). Consider numbering them and matching the language of each prior lesson's central claim so the callback is exact.

- Anchor: `And that is the point of philosophy - to see yourself and the world as they really are.`
  Closer doesn't return to the opening quote (`PARADIGMS.md`: "Close by calling back to the opening quote"). Try closing on a callback to Lady Philosophy's "She hasn't changed a bit" from Lesson 1 — the routine opened on Fortune's constancy, and ending there would close the loop.

  Note: the local repo draft of this lesson ends with a rorschach-test reflection ("Was he happy or cursed? Was his story a tragic one or inspiring?"). That was removed in the Drive version. The Drive version is cleaner, but the rorschach question was doing real work — it forced the listener to take a position. Consider whether a version of it belongs in the Commitment or Action ("Today, commit to noticing where you treat fortune's loans as your own.").

## Once you draft Meditation/Commitment/Action

A few cross-lesson notes to use when filling them in, since the four pieces should be one tightly linked set per `AGENTS.md` suggested improvement #4:

- **Lesson 1's meditation should rehearse interrogating a "supposed to go" story** — pull from your own week, name it, then notice the expectation underneath. Don't run a generic body scan.
- **Lesson 2's meditation should premeditate loss** (the Stoic *praemeditatio malorum*). The pattern from `final/managing-negative-emotions.md` ("view from above" but for change) would suit.
- **Lesson 3's meditation is the natural fit for the Epictetus "given back" reframe** — list three things and rehearse the move from "lost" to "returned."
- **Lesson 4's meditation should literally do the math** — count years, count people, in silence. The theory closer ("Count the years. Count the people.") already names the meditation.
- **Lesson 5's meditation should rehearse the dialogue with the tyrant** — listener as the Stoic, an imagined external as the tyrant.
- **Lesson 6's meditation should sit with a possession or title and rehearse losing it** — close to praemeditatio but specifically of "goods."
- **Lesson 7's meditation should be a *self*-examination** — the day in review, the practice Marcus Aurelius and Seneca both used. This is the natural meta-callback to the series.

Commitments should follow the `Today, commit to <specific behavior>.` pattern from `AGENTS.md`. Actions should mirror in past tense. Keep both tight — they render in-app and overflow.
