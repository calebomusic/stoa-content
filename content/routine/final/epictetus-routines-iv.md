# On Being Content  

## Quote

For what then have they made you responsible? For that which alone is in your power, the proper use of appearances. Why then do you draw on yourself the things for which you are not responsible?   
Sentence\_id: 11266-11268

## Theory

In this series, we will continue to dive deeper into Epictetus’ Discourses, chapter by chapter. Good work continuing on.  

Book 1 chapter 12 is about contentment. The Stoics believe there is a simple trick to being happy \- control your desire for change. We are content when we appreciate the way things are, but discontent when we focus on how things could be different.

Put differently, this is about loving Nature \- or the universe as it is, not as we want it to be. We have been given this incredible ability of intelligence and self-awareness. But it can be used poorly. 

We can use our intelligence to ruminate \- to think on how different we want the world to be. 

In the first season of the television show *True Detective*, the cynical Rustin Cohle, played by Matthew Mcconaughey, argues that self-awareness is a step too far. He says: “I think human consciousness is a tragic misstep in evolution. We became too self-aware, nature created an aspect of nature separate from itself…”

This is the cynical way to approach intelligence, as a curse, as the cause of our suffering. And Epictetus raises this point. We use our intelligence to begrudge the universe. He says:

“But you are unwilling to endure and are discontented: and if you are alone, you call it solitude; and if you are with men, you call them knaves and robbers; and you find fault with your own parents and children, and brothers and neighbours.”

Epictetus’ point is about using our intelligence to label appearances poorly. We take a fact about the world, being alone, and we label it solitude. We take a person and label them a thief. 

Instead we should use our power of intelligence to see the good:

“But you ought when you are alone to call this condition by the name of tranquillity and freedom, and to think yourself like to the gods; and when you are with many, you ought not to call it crowd, nor trouble, nor uneasiness, but festival and assembly, and so accept all contentedly.”

This is what nature wants from us, Epictetus says. Call being alone freedom, not solitude. Call a group of people a festival, not a crowd.

At the very end of True Detective, the nihilist Rust makes a shift in perspective. His partner Marty mentions how the sky is mostly dark in the morning.

Rust responds:

“You are looking at it wrong, that sky thing. Once there was only dark. If you ask me, the light’s winning.”

Our perspective is our most important gift. Use it to find the light.

# Don’t Complain 

## Quote

Do you see in what direction you are looking, that it is towards the earth, towards the pit, that it is towards these wretched laws of dead men? but towards the laws of the gods you are not looking.  
Sentence\_id: 11278-11279

## Theory

Sometimes, we just get whiny. 

We complain about things that are not really that bad. We complain about things that we have the tools to handle.

Epictetus hated complaining.

He also ran a school for wealthy Romans. He was training the future political elite, those with large estates and many slaves. 

In Discourses Book 1, Chapter 13, Epictetus turns his attention to how these rich Romans would complain about fake problems, even about the service of their slaves. 

“But when you have asked for warm water and the slave has not heard, or if he did hear has brought only tepid water, or he is not even found to be in the house… How then shall a man endure such persons as this slave? Slave yourself, will you not bear with your own brother…”

It is easy for us to mock the rich Roman angry with his slave for bringing tepid water. But, how often do we do the same thing? How often are we rude to a service worker just trying to do their job? A waiter who brings us the wrong order or is slow on a busy shift? Our family when they annoy us in ways we should be able to anticipate and expect? 

Epictetus often uses slave as an insult \- it’s not something his elite Roman students were used to being called. But he is not judging slaves, he was one himself. His point is that if you think slaves are to be looked down upon, not worthy of respect, then you are acting like the real slaves in that moment.

We forget, in these moments of ingratitude, our connection to each other. We get angry over nothing, when we should be grateful to even exist. 

When we do this, we are looking the wrong direction. Epictetus says:

“Do you see in what direction you are looking, that it is towards the earth, towards the pit, that it is towards these wretched laws of dead men? But towards the laws of the gods you are not looking.”

Don’t look towards the pit. Look up. Train yourself to make good use of impressions. Train yourself to be grateful. 

We are not spoiled Romans with slaves, but we complain just like they did. Don’t complain about things you have the tools to handle and don’t be the kind of person who is angry at your fellow human because you lost perspective.

# Everything is Connected  

## Quote

And what shall you swear? Never to be disobedient, never to make any charges, never to find fault with any thing that he has given, and never unwillingly to do or to suffer any thing that is necessary.  
Sentence\_id: 11303-11304

## Theory

Discourses Book 1, Chapter 14 is about providence and the connection of all things.

It starts with Epictetus convincing a student that there must be a god \- some sort of higher power \- because of how ordered the universe is:

“Well, do you not think that earthly things have a natural agreement and union with heavenly things? I do. And how else so regularly as if by God's command, when He bids the plants to flower, do they flower? When He bids them to send forth shoots, do they shoot? When He bids them to produce fruit, how else do they produce fruit?”

For the Stoic, god is providential reason or the logos \- the active principle that structures the universe. This logos is a part of all things, and it gives them order. So in this way everything is connected to god, and it is connected to each other.

Regardless of your view on god \- Epictetus here is stressing the connection of things. When we feel alone \- we are not alone. We are part of a larger world, we are connected to that world. 

And we should swear an oath to this god, to nature, the same way people swore oaths to Caesar. Epictetus says to his student:

“And what shall you swear? Never to be disobedient, never to make any charges, never to find fault with any thing that he has given, and never unwillingly to do or to suffer any thing that is necessary.”

That last part of the quote is Stoicism in a nut-shell: ‘Never unwillingly do or suffer any thing that is necessary.’

So many parts of life are necessary. The passing of time, growing old, changing jobs, experiencing bad bosses or heartbreak, disagreeing with people, losing friends and making new ones.

Where or not you believe in god, whether or not you believe these things were planned for us, part of our greater purpose, they are still necessary. Not only should we not suffer because of them \- we should go a step further and actually do them willingly. 

Embrace what is necessary. Feel connected to the world as it is, not as you’d like it to be. Don’t endure life, but lean into it.

This is the Stoic goal \- this is why we need to be connected to nature.

# Philosophy’s Promise 

## Quote

Philosophy does not propose to secure for a man any external thing. If it did (or, if it were not, as I say), philosophy would be allowing something which is not within its province. For as the carpenter's material is wood, and that of the statuary is copper, so the matter of the art of living is each man's life.  
Sentence\_id: 11307-11309

## Theory

Philosophy is about becoming a better person. This should be a clear idea by now. It is the craft of living well. 

Often we think mastering one craft means you have skill in another. We look to celebrities for political advice. We think athletes are good people. We think successful entrepreneurs will are wise about subjects outside business. 

But this is a mistake. A craft does not make you better at anything outside of itself. Discourses 1, Chapter 15 reminds us that this is true \- even for philosophy. 

In this chapter, a student asks Epictetus how he can calm the anger of his brother. 

Epictetus replies: “Philosophy does not propose to secure for a man any external thing. If it did…philosophy would be allowing something which is not within its province. For as the carpenter's material is wood, and that of the statuary is copper, so the matter of the art of living is each man's life.”

That is all philosophy promises, and all it can provide \- the art of living your own life better. Philosophy is a skill, but the skill you are learning is how to manage your own character. Stoicism teaches you how to respond to your brother's anger, but it can’t teach you anything about changing your brother.

If you come to philosophy to make your brother forgive you, or make your parents see your side, or make someone love you \- then that is like coming to a carpenter for heart surgery. This is not what philosophy does.

If you want to help your brother \- he will need to seek out philosophy himself. Send him to Epictetus and hope for the best.

So, philosophy is about the internal, but like other crafts, it takes years to master. It doesn’t promise quick progress:

“Nothing great, said Epictetus, is produced suddenly, since not even the grape or the fig is. If you say to me now that you want a fig, I will answer to you that it requires time.”

Like a body builder who transforms their body \- or a painter who takes years to master the techniques or their craft \- philosophy takes time. So the key lesson here is to remember what philosophy is: A craft focused on your character that takes years to master. 

Keep this in mind and you will be more focused in your training.

# Find Meaning 

## Quote

If then I was a nightingale, I would do the part of a nightingale. If I were a swan, I would do like a swan. But now I am a rational creature, and I ought to praise God: this is my work   
Sentence\_id: (11352-11354)

## Theory

Stoicism can seem on the surface cold and fatalistic. Like it is telling you just to accept things as they are, because there are things you cannot control that will happen regardless of what you think or feel, and there is nothing you can do about them. Accept the impersonal facts of the matter, the fatalist says, no matter how brutal or unpleasant they are.

But that is not exactly right. Because for the Stoics \- nature has a plan for us. Facts are not without meaning. They reveal something about how we should be \- not just what we are or what is necessary. 

This is providence. Fate is when the outcomes of our lives are predetermined or necessary. But providence is when that outcome was planned by something greater than us for a specific purpose.  

In Discourses Book 1, Chapter 16 Epictetus returns to the theme of providence. He has two points to make \- first because the world is planned, we should notice the small choices nature has made. We should pay attention carefully to the facts of the matter. Then we should pause and find the meaning in those facts. We must ask why the world is this way. How does it fit into the universes’ plan? 

We have not been dropped into a world that gives us no indication of how to live, but one full of meaning. Our job then, is not to create meaning or purpose, but to notice it. As Epictetus says:

“If then I was a nightingale, I would do the part of a nightingale. If I were a swan, I would do like a swan. But now I am a rational creature, and I ought to praise God: this is my work.”

Epictetus’ life is meaningful because he understands what he is. He is a rational creature, able to understand the world. So his job is to play the part of a rational creature \- to strive towards knowledge and understanding of the world, and to appreciate god, or the universe. 

So what does Epictetus’ lesson mean for us? It means we don’t have to look outside ourselves for meaning. Play your role as a rational creature, focused on understanding the world and living with virtue. Then find meaning in your other roles, as a child, a parent, a friend, a partner. All of these roles, like a nightingale, or a swan, come with meaning and purpose. Our job is to find it.

# On Logic  

## Quote

For we have no need of Chrysippus for his own sake, but in order that we may understand nature.  
Sentence\_id: 11395

## Theory

Logic was one of the core parts of Stoic theory, along with Ethics and Physics. I taught logic in university. Some students love it, but some really hate it. It can seem academic or detached from the project of living well. 

But in Discourses Book 1, Chapter 17 Epictetus makes an excellent metaphor on the importance of logic. The Romans used a wooden container called a modius to measure corn. It produces no corn by itself.  

However, the modius is still useful, Epictetus points out, because it is a thing that measures corn. Likewise \- logic produces no fruit or corn. But it is the tool we use to measure how to live.

In other words, Epictetus is candid that we don’t study logic for its own sake. We study logic because it helps us distinguish and measure the things that do matter. Logic doesn’t produce fruit \- but we still need it.

What is essential to living well is understanding the world as it is, applying our value judgments accurately to impressions, and living by a true philosophy of life. Logic is a necessary tool for getting there. 

But Epictetus takes the comparison a step further. Just as logic is a tool, so is the writing of other philosophers. Epictetus points to the 3rd head of the Stoic school, Chrysippus, and says:

“…We have no need of Chrysippus for his own sake, but in order that we may understand nature.”

We are after the truth, Epictetus says. Studying Chrysippus, admiring him, learning from him, is only helpful if it gets us closer to the truth.

Epictetus goes on to say the same thing about other philosophers and about the diviners and priests who make predictions about fate \- all of them are tools to be used, not revered for their own sake.

Epictetus’ lesson in this chapter is about finding a balance between living well and the tools we use to get there. There is value to be found in logic, books, and the theories of the great thinkers who have come before. But they are not the goal of philosophy either. 

Being good at logic doesn’t make you a good person. But likewise, you can’t be a good person if you are an undisciplined thinker \- you’d be too easy to trick or decieve.

So again, we need logic as a tool to evaluate arguments, just like a farmer needs tools to measure their crops. Just don’t forget your true goal is to change how you act and live.

# Forgive the foolish  

## Quote

What do you mean by thieves and robbers? They are mistaken about good and evil. Ought we then to be angry with them, or to pity them? But show them their error, and you will see how they desist from their errors.  
Sentence\_id: 11417-11421

## Theory

It is a core Stoic principle that no one does wrong willingly. This idea comes originally from Socrates. The argument is that everyone does what they think is right in the moment. People can be better or worse than others, because your understanding of what is good can be true or false, but no one chooses to do what they think is, all things considered, the bad choice. 

But if that is true, then how can we ever be angry at other people?

Epictetus asks his students this. When they are demanding punishing for criminals he says:

“What do you mean by thieves and robbers? They are mistaken about good and evil. Ought we then to be angry with them, or to pity them? But show them their error, and you will see how they desist from their errors.”

Epictetus continues. To be angry at the thief is like being angry at the blind man for not being able to see. To want to punish someone who harmed you is like trying to hurt a deaf man for not being able to hear.

Bad people are already punished. They are deprived of an ability they would rather have \- the ability to tell right from wrong. Instead of seeing this fact, we add insult to injury and we punish them more. 

To really understand the Stoic idea that no one does wrong willingly is to learn to forgive.

While this is easy in the abstract, it is often hard to do to the person who has harmed us in particular. Maybe we want kindness for the thieves and robbers in other places, but the person who robbed us should be punished \- even if they didn’t know any better. 

But Epictetus is suspicious of this kind of thinking. He says:

“Why then are we angry? Is it because we value so much the things of which these men rob us? Do not admire your clothes, and then you will not be angry with the thief.”

Epictetus quickly follows up saying:

“Learn that a thief and an adulterer have no place in the things which are yours, but in those which belong to others and which are not in your power. If you dismiss these things and consider them as nothing, with whom are you still angry? But so long as you value these things, be angry with yourself rather than with the thief and the adulterer.”

Epictetus’ point is that we are angry at people because we feel harmed. And we feel harmed because we valued something outside of ourselves.

In these moments, focus less on punishment and more on self-improvement.
