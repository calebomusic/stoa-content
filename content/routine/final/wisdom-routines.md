1. # Introduction

## Quote

“Wisdom comes haphazard to no man. Money will come of its own accord; titles will be given to you; influence and authority will perhaps be thrust upon you; but virtue will not fall upon you by chance.”  
Id: 1386

## Theory

Consider a moment of extreme change in your life. A mid-life crisis, where one has to choose between different paths in life. A health concern where one has to re-evaluate their priorities. A lay-off, where you face both extreme risk but major possibilities. A bad relationship, where you need to decide to end it or make serious changes to correct its course. 

These are complicated situations where we desperately want to know the correct course of action. What should we do? How should we act?

Now, consider the person in your own life you go to for advice in these situations. The one who helps clarify things for you, helps you make sense of things.

This is the person who shows the Stoic virtue of wisdom. 

This can be you. With wisdom you can give counsel to yourself and others. 

Being wise is just having that ability to make sense of things for yourself and others. 

The Stoics defined wisdom as the knowledge of what was good, bad or indifferent. But, put another way, it is really about understanding what matters most in a situation. What is important and worthy of concern and consideration, what does not deserve our attention, and how we should act given this information.

When you think of a Stoic role model for Wisdom, think of Marcus Aurelius. Remember how he gave importance to ruling well, gave little importance to fame and wealth, and thought carefully about how his decisions impact others.

To live without wisdom is to live thoughtlessly. To put no consideration into your goals, or how your life builds towards them. It is to act at random, and be surprised by the results.  
It is to be disappointed with life, without realizing the opportunities you had to intervene.

In this course we will learn about and develop Stoic wisdom. We will practice becoming the kind of people that make sense of things, and act on what is most important.

Wisdom is not reserved for a special few, but it is something we have to practice. As Seneca points out: 

“Wisdom comes haphazard to no man. Money will come of its own accord; titles will be given to you; influence and authority will perhaps be thrust upon you; but virtue will not fall upon you by chance.”

2. # Right Aim

## Quote

“When a man does not know what harbour he is making for, no wind is the right wind.”  
Id: 488  
Source: Letters  
Location: 71.1-4

## Theory

Here is an everyday example that requires wisdom. You are a manager at work. Two members of your team, Jaime and Alex are having consistent disagreements that are affecting morale. Jaime, the more senior team member, feels that Alex is undercutting his authority and not agreeing with his decisions. Alex feels that Jaime is dismissive of her good ideas, just because she is more junior.

They come to you to intervene. What do you do? Who’s right, who’s wrong? What is the wise way to act here?

In situations with no obvious answer, you need wisdom to guide you. 

If you are the kind of person who acts foolishly in moments like these, your entire life will be disturbed. You will harm those around you, fail to live up to your roles, and promote more disagreement. 

But if you can act wisely in these moments, you can have a smooth and tranquil life. You can’t control other people, and regardless of how you act some people will still be angry with you. But that anger and frustration is much easier to handle when you are confident you acted thoughtfully and well.

To better put wisdom into practice, the Stoics broke it down into subcategories. The first one of those is called ‘Right Aim’. That’s what we’ll cover today.

Right aim is having the correct target, or goal, for what you do.

In our example, right aim is what you want out of the discussion. Do you want to solve things fairly? Do you want any excuse to fire Alex or Jaime? Do you want to maximize profit, regardless or what happens to either of them? Are you aiming at being an excellent boss? Or some kind of practical result, like settling the dispute by any means necessary?

Without an aim or target, you can’t evaluate the success of your decisions. That is why Seneca says:

“When a man does not know what harbour he is making for, no wind is the right wind.”

But we don’t want to just aim for any harbour. Our target also has to be the right one.

Stoicism provides the ultimate aim: virtue, or a great character. Mastery of the things that are up to you. This aim brings with it happiness, less stress and better relationships. It alone is the ultimate target.

The Stoic boss aims at fulfilling their role well. In acting like a great boss would act. That alone is up to them.

But besides work, you will face other conflicts that you will need to skillfully navigate. As a partner, a parent, a sibling or a child. 

To do any of these our roles well requires a clear answer of what great looks like. Virtue should be your guide here, an unwavering focus on what is up to you in each of these cases.

Let’s meditate on our most important aims today.

3. # Situational Awareness

## Quote

“His searching questions at meetings. A kind of single-mindedness, almost, never content with first impressions, or breaking off the discussion prematurely.”   
Id: 1922  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 1.6

## Theory

There is an exercise in design thinking, the field dedicated towards creative solutions to novel problems, called the 5 Whys. 

To do the 5 Whys, when encountering any problem we always ask why it is happening at least five times, almost like a young child does. 

A common example involves a local government frustrated that a symbolic statue is constantly dirtied with bird droppings. They want to know how to stop this problem. 

So you ask why is it covered in droppings? \- Because there are many birds around it.

Why are there so many birds? \- Because they swarm the statue at night.

Why do they do that? \- Because there are many bugs at night they like to eat.

Why are there so many bugs at night? \- Because we shine lights on the statues at night that attract the bugs.

Why do we shine the light? \- Because we want to make the statue visible.

By doing this exercise, a new context is revealed. The real question is not how do we remove the birds, but do we have a better way of making the statue visible at night or is it worth the bird droppings to keep things how they are?

This is an example of a wisdom exercise. One the Stoics would connect to the sub-component of virtue called situational awareness.

Situational awareness is about understanding the context of things.

Let’s return the example of a boss with coworkers fighting, Alex the more senior and Jaime the more junior. A wise choice here makes all the difference in a happy workplace. 

Right aim is the target. The Stoic would aim at being a good boss. Achieving the right outcome to fit that role.

But you can’t hit your target or aim without understanding the situation.

Situational awareness is the next part of living with wisdom.

Is there a history here you were missing? Are people telling the truth? Is there something in the way the work is set up that makes their jobs conflict with each other?

These are the kind of questions we must ask and answer in order to understand the circumstances we face.

Marcus was Emperor of Rome. He encountered situations much more complex than Jaime and Alex’s fight. Marcus praised this quality of situational awareness in his adoptive father, saying that he admired:

“His searching questions at meetings. A kind of single-mindedness, almost, never content with first impressions, or breaking off the discussion prematurely.”   
Id: ???  
Source: Meditations

Consider how to be more like Marcus’ father in your own life. Never satisfied with first impressions, when presented with a conflict you would seek to understand what is really going on. You would gather information from both sides, compare conflicting stories, and question your own assumptions about what needs to be done. 

Be like Marcus’ father. Don’t be content with first impressions. Always be searching. Understand what is happening and why.

4. # Good Judgment

## Quote

Remember that in life you ought to behave as at a banquet. Suppose that something is carried round and is opposite to you. Stretch out your hand and take a portion with decency. Suppose that it passes by you. Do not detain it. Suppose that it is not yet come to you. Do not send your desire forward to it, but wait till it is opposite to you. Do so with respect to children, so with respect to a wife, so with respect to magisterial offices, so with respect to wealth, and you will be some time a worthy partner of the banquets of the gods.  
Id: 251

## Theory

Everyday we are bombarded with choices.

So many choices, we often stop noticing all of them.

We have the obvious choices, like what to watch on TV, what to have for dinner. But we have larger choices. We can choose to go to work our job or quit, to take care of our family or leave, to change our name or leave town. We have so many options available to us.

Good judgment, a Stoic sub-component of virtue, is about picking the right option. It is about understanding which option is best, and acting on it. 

Life is always asking us to judge, constantly throwing questions at us. Should I be angry? Should I be anxious? Do I buy this advertised product? Do I take this piece of news seriously?

Good judgment is about answering these questions well. It is aligning our choices with reality, with what is best for us. 

To imagine good judgment in action, Epictetus gave the example of a banquet: 

“Remember that in life you ought to behave as at a banquet. Suppose that something is carried round and is opposite to you. Stretch out your hand and take a portion with decency. Suppose that it passes by you. Do not detain it. Suppose that it is not yet come to you. Do not send your desire forward to it, but wait till it is opposite to you. Do so with respect to children, so with respect to a wife, so with respect to magisterial offices, so with respect to wealth, and you will be some time a worthy partner of the banquets of the gods.”  
Id: 251  
Source: Discourses  
Location: ??

We show good judgment when we do things in a practical and convenient way. Just as we don’t gorge ourselves at a banquet, we shouldn’t ruin our long-term stability for a short-term adventure.

Just as we don’t desire the plate of food that has not arrived at the table yet, we shouldn’t choose to pursue something we can’t have yet at our point in life.

And just as we are fine when the plates are removed from the table, we should also let go of things in our life that are naturally changing or leaving us.

Developing the habit of good judgment in life is like being a good dinner guest. It is noticing the questions being asked of us, and answering them practically and reasonably. 

Imagine your life like this. Never jumping to conclusions, never pursuing the wrong option, or over committing to a bad idea. Instead, observing your options, acting confidently, doing what needs to be done, and not overextending yourself.

This would be a life worthy of being called wise. And it is achievable with practice. Thankfully, we all have many opportunities to practice. We are already at a banquet. Life puts things before us and takes things away. We won’t always select properly, or be ready to let things go. 

But you are already at the banquet. You can start practicing good judgment today.

5. # Acuity

## Quote

“When Florus was deliberating whether he should appear at Nero's shows, taking part in the performance himself, Agrippinus replied, 'Appear by all means.' And when Florus inquired, 'But why do not you appear?' he answered, 'Because I do not even consider the question.'”.  
Id: 285  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.2

## Theory

Epictetus tells a story to his students about Roman elites navigating the court of the mad emperor Nero. Epictetus says: 

“When Florus was deliberating whether he should appear at Nero's shows, taking part in the performance himself, Agrippinus replied, 'Appear by all means.' And when Florus inquired, 'But why do not you appear?' he answered, 'Because I do not even consider the question.'”.  
Id: 285  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.2

There are many lessons to be gained from Agrippinus, but one is about acuity, another Stoic sub-component of virtue. Acuity is the ability to find the right action quickly. To understand, almost intuit, what is the most important or central point in the matter.

Agrippinus did not even consider what he took to be the wrong choice of action: appeasing Nero, feigning loyalty to an emperor he did not respect, selling his integrity for political success.

If Right aim is about having the right goal for your actions, situational awareness is understanding the full picture, and good judgment is coming to the right answer, then acuity is being able to do all of this this quickly. 

Consider two bosses. One is cautious, slow, and deliberate. They come to the right answer, but they have to think about it first. The other acts with acuity. They are confident, direct, and come to the right answer immediately. 

Both may be good leaders, but the acute boss inspires more confidence and wastes less time. 

Acuity is key so that we don’t waste our time down the wrong path. Florus does not think of himself as someone without integrity. He might try to rationalize how appearing at Nero’s shows is better for everyone long-term, how it might be better to appease the emperor now, so that Florus can make a stand later. 

But we can always attempt to rationalize or justify any action we take after we make it. This doesn’t make the action correct. Acuity cuts this off before it starts. Agrippinus did not decide, on a weight of evidence, that it is better not to support Nero. He did not even consider the question.

Acuity is a skill, it comes with experience. To act quickly and confidently when you don’t know better is just to be fool-hardy, ignorant of your own short-comings.

We become acute by first being slow and deliberate. Slow, careful deliberation will lead to confident, quick action.

But the ultimate goal is to be acute . It is better to be Agrippinus than Florus. It is best to not even consider poor options.

6. # Resourcefulness

## Quote

Our actions may be impeded ... but there can be no impeding our intentions or our dispositions. Because we can accommodate and adapt. The mind adapts and converts to its own purposes the obstacle to our acting. The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way.  
Id: 941  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 

## Theory

Sometimes, no matter how wise you are, you will face situations where it seems like there is no good option. In these moments, you need to be resourceful.

Resourcefulness for the Stoics is the ability to push through challenging situations by skillfully finding a good response. Another way to think of resourcefulness, is the confidence to find creative solutions that make the best of a difficult scenario.

Let’s return to our example of applying Stoic wisdom in a normal context: If Alex and Jaime have a disagreement,  A normal boss may have to side with either one of them, and alienate the other team member. Or worse, offend both and leave the entire team frustrated.

A resourceful boss would figure out a way for both to leave happy, and feeling understood. 

A Stoic’s capacity to find external success has limits. There are some things outside of our control. We can’t always find the solution that makes everyone happy.

But we can find the best possible solution. 

The way the Stoics do that is by being adaptable. Marcus Aurelius has a famous line about resourcefulness. It goes:

Our actions may be impeded ... but there can be no impeding our intentions or our dispositions. Because we can accommodate and adapt. The mind adapts and converts to its own purposes the obstacle to our acting. The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way.  
Id: 941  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 

As Marcus says, the Obstacle is the way. This is the Stoic insight to resourcefulness. If we are stuck on a problem, we can find a solution by looking at the opportunity in the obstacle itself. 

Normally, this quote is applied internally to our character. If something is challenging and scary, like a work pitch, a performance, or a big change, then we can use it to improve ourselves, for example to build our courage.

But this insight applies externally too. If two coworkers are fighting because they are both prideful, find the opportunity in the problem. Adapt to the problem instead of fighting against it. What if both of them had full responsibility over a separate piece of work? Something they could both be proud of? 

A Stoic approach to resourcefulness has a willingness to accept things as they are. Stoics find opportunities by accommodating and adapting to the world, not pretending it is different than it is. We don’t try to change our coworkers, we adapt to their personalities. We accept our own strengths, limitations, talents, and deficiencies, and incorporate those into our plans.

Resourcefulness will not always lead to a perfect solution. Sometimes there isn’t one. But we can always improve our situation through resourcefulness. We can always adapt to any situation well, and find in what seems like an obstacle the opportunity to improve our  wisdom, courage or discipline.

Let’s build this skill by meditating on obstacles we can turn into opportunities in our own life.

## Theory

Epictetus divided the practice of philosophy into 3 disciplines:

The discipline of desire, concerning what we consider good and bad

The discipline of action, concerning how we treat others

And the discipline of judgment, concerning what we take to be true or false.

Wisdom is such a crucial virtue because it cuts across all three equally. Every aspect of how we live can be improved with wisdom.

Let’s return to our practical example one last time, and see how a Stoic would put wisdom into action.

At work, two employees are fighting. Jaime, the more senior, and Alex the more junior.

Both feel disrespected. Jaime that his experience is not being listened to, and Alex that her good advice is being dismissed. How would you intervene, if you were a wise, Stoic boss?

First you would have the right aim. This means you would know your target and it would be a good one. Something like, you would want to resolve the conflict fairly, or act as a good boss would act. 

Next, you would practice situational awareness. You wouldn’t jump to conclusions, but carefully gather evidence, and impartially hear from both sides.

With the insight you have gained through experience, you would quickly identify the best practical option. Something like giving Jaime, as the senior team member, final decision making authority, but Alex an opportunity to run a smaller project as the decision-maker. 

With good judgment you would pick this option as the best one, above tempting but unhelpful options like dismissing the issue, or siding with whoever you like more.

With resourcefulness, you would find solutions others could not, by looking for the opportunity in the challenge. The Stoic turns obstacles into opportunities by accepting them as they are, and then adapting around them, or leaning into them.

The workplace is one application of wisdom, but these attributes can extend into all aspects of our lives. Our character is just the quality of decisions we make from moment to moment. How we decide to act in light of the evidence. To be wise is to stack the cards in your favor. It is to be able to reflect well, and then act confidently.

Throughout this course we’ve brought to mind examples of wise people. You can be one of those people. 

Continuing practice to become the kind of person that can find good options in complicated situations. The kind of person that people go to for advice. That is Stoic wisdom.
