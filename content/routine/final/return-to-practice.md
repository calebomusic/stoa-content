# **1\. You're Already Back**

## **Quote**

"When force of circumstance upsets your equanimity, lose no time in recovering your self-control, and do not remain out of tune longer than you can help. Habitual recurrence to the harmony will increase your mastery of it." 

Id: 159

## **Theory**

You stopped practicing. That's fine. Even Marcus Aurelius fell off track. 

The Roman Emperor and Stoic philosopher wrote a journal to himself called *Meditations*. In it, he constantly reminds himself to get back on track. Not just once. Over and over. The book is full of these reminders because Marcus knew that falling off is part of the process.

For example, he wrote:

"When force of circumstance upsets your equanimity, lose no time in recovering your self-control, and do not remain out of tune longer than you can help. Habitual recurrence to the harmony will increase your mastery of it."

id: 159

Notice what Marcus does not say. He does not say never lose your equanimity or your sense of calm. He says when you lose it, recover quickly. He assumes you will fall out of tune. When this happens the goal is simply to return. That’s what’s up to you.

Think of meditation. When you meditate, your mind wanders. That’s natural. The practice is noticing that your mind wandered and bringing it back. The return is the exercise.

The same is true for your practice as a whole. You stepped away. 

Sometimes you do this just for a minute – other times for months, still others years.

Whatever it is – now you are here. 

That act of returning is itself a Stoic skill. You are already back.

The Stoics did not believe we could snap our fingers and become perfectly disciplined. Change takes time and repetition. We will fall back into old patterns. We will forget what’s important to us and miss the mark.

True failure is not falling off once. True failure is giving up on trying. You have not done that.

So do not waste time feeling guilty about the gap. What is useful is what you do right now.

Let's practice the skill of returning.

# **2\. Remember Why**

## **Quote**

"At dawn, when you have trouble getting out of bed, tell yourself: I have to go to work \- as a human being. What do I have to complain of, if I'm going to do what I was born for \- the things I was brought into the world to do? Or is this what I was created for? To huddle under the blankets and stay warm?" 

Id: 1734

## **Theory**

Why did you start practicing Stoicism in the first place?

Maybe you were dealing with anxiety or stress. Maybe you wanted more discipline. Maybe someone recommended Stoa, or you read something that clicked. Whatever it was, something brought you here.

That reason still matters.

When habits fade, it’s often because we lost sight of why we started.

Marcus Aurelius understood this. Even as the most powerful man in the Roman Empire, he struggled to get out of bed. He wrote this reminder to himself:

"At dawn, when you have trouble getting out of bed, tell yourself: I have to go to work \- as a human being. What do I have to complain of, if I'm going to do what I was born for \- the things I was brought into the world to do? Or is this what I was created for? To huddle under the blankets and stay warm?" 

Id: 1734

His strategy was simple. Focus on your purpose. Bring it to mind when your commitment wavers.

Purpose can come from many places. For some, it is a specific goal. For others, it comes from their roles in life: parent, partner, professional, friend. We all have roles that demand the best version of ourselves.

If you are having difficulty motivating yourself to practice, you either don't have a clear purpose or you have lost sight of it. Today, reconnect with it.

When you remember why you practice, the habit becomes easier to maintain. Purpose is what turns a routine into something meaningful.

# **3\. Start Small**

## **Quote**

"When faced with anything painful or pleasurable, anything bringing glory or disrepute, realize that the crisis is now, that the Olympics have started, and waiting is no longer an option; that the chance for progress, to keep or lose, turns on the events of a single day." 

Id: 845

## **Theory**

You do not need to overhaul your life to get back on track.

In his article *The Mundanity of Excellence*, Daniel Chambliss tells the story of Mary Meagher. When Mary was 13 years old and had qualified for the National Championships, she decided to try and break the world record in the 200-Meter Butterfly race. She made two changes to her routine: she began coming on time to all practices, and she began doing all of her turns correctly during those practices. Most swimmers don't do this.

They may physically show up to practice, but not be there mentally. They may practice well but not actually do every technique correctly.

Those are the two changes Mary Meagher remembers making. Later she broke the world record. Twice.

Self-control is about getting the basics right.

The Stoics saw virtue as a craft. Craft is built by doing many small things well over time. Each and every action counts.

Epictetus captures this well:

"When faced with anything painful or pleasurable, anything bringing glory or disrepute, realize that the crisis is now, that the Olympics have started, and waiting is no longer an option; that the chance for progress, to keep or lose, turns on the events of a single day." 

Id: 845

The crisis is now. But that does not mean you need to do something dramatic. It means the small things matter today. Show up. Do one thing well. That is how you rebuild.

Seen the right way this is at once easy – all you need to do is show up – and exceptionally difficult. Do everything as well as you can. Whatever you are practicing, master the basic technique.

In the end, excellence is common sense.

Epictetus also said:

"If you would be a reader, read; if a writer, write."

id: 288

Don't try to reconstruct an entire practice overnight. Pick one small discipline and follow through on it. That alone is progress.

# **4\. Stay Motivated**

## **Quote**

"We must make the best use that we can of the things which are in our power, and use the rest according to their nature."

sentence\_id: 10702

## **Theory**

You've been showing up. How do you keep showing up?

Motivation is tricky. It comes and goes. You've probably experienced this before. You start a new habit, feel great for a while, and then one day it fades.

The Stoics noticed something important about why this happens. Motivation dies when we refuse to accept the things we cannot control.

Think about what drains your energy. It is usually not the hard work itself. It is the frustration that things aren't going the way you want. Your career isn't moving fast enough. Your relationship isn't improving. The results aren't showing up. We exhaust ourselves fighting what is not up to us.

Epictetus taught:

"We must make the best use that we can of the things which are in our power, and use the rest according to their nature."

Notice the second half of that sentence. "Use the rest according to their nature." That means accepting things as they are, not as you wish them to be. It means embracing reality instead of fighting against it.

In the book *Good to Great*, author Jim Collins describes a conversation he had with Admiral James Stockdale. Stockdale was a prisoner of war in Vietnam and a student of Stoic philosophy.

Collins asked him which prisoners didn't survive. Stockdale said it was the optimists. They kept saying they'd be out by Christmas. Then Easter. Then Thanksgiving. When each deadline passed, they lost hope.

The optimists were not accepting their situation. They were wishing for things that were completely out of their control to be different. And when the world refused to cooperate, they broke.

The same patterns show up in ordinary life.

A parent burns out trying to fix a child's struggles, refusing to accept that their child's path is not entirely in their hands. A person grieving a loss exhausts themselves wishing things had gone differently. Someone managing a chronic illness loses hope because treatment isn't producing the results they expected.

In each case, the person is refusing to accept something that is not up to them. They are fighting reality, and that fight is what exhausts their motivation.

Stockdale survived because he accepted where he was. He did not pretend things were fine. He did not set false expectations. He embraced the difficulty of his situation and focused on what he could do within it. Whatever you are facing, you can do the same.

When you stop demanding that reality cooperate with your expectations, something changes. The parent who accepts what is not up to them can focus on being present and supportive. The grieving person who stops resisting reality can begin to move forward. The person managing illness who recognizes the pace and uncertainty of recovery can commit to what is in their power each day.

Acceptance frees up the energy spent on frustration. You stop fighting reality and start working with it. You build momentum by taking what is up to you and improving it, little by little.

Make the best use of what is in your power. Accept the rest according to its nature. That is how you stay motivated.

# **5\. Keep Going**

## **Quote**

"Would you have a great empire? Rule over yourself." 

Id: 1664

## **Theory**

Excellent work – you've made it through this course. 

Over the last four days, you've practiced four skills. You practiced returning to the path. You reconnected with your purpose. You started small. And you learned that acceptance frees up energy to focus on what is up to you.

These are not just lessons for this week. They are tools for the rest of your practice.

The Stoics treated philosophy as a daily exercise. It was not something you learn once and move on from. It was something you practiced every day, like physical training.

Seneca once wrote:

"I shall keep watching myself continually, and, a most useful habit, shall review each day. For this is what makes us wicked: that no one of us looks back over his own life. Our thoughts are devoted only to what we are about to do. And yet our plans for the future always depend on the past." 

Id: 1419

The daily review. The daily practice. This is how the Stoics built character – through sustained, ordinary effort.

You already know what to do. You've done it before. The only thing left is to keep doing it.

Set a consistent time for your practice. Make it part of your day. When you miss a day, remember the return is the practice. When your motivation wavers, remember to reconnect with your purpose. When it feels like too much, remember to just do one thing well. And when results are slow, remember to focus on what is up to you.

The famous Roman writer Publius Syrus once said:

"Would you have a great empire? Rule over yourself." 

Id: 1664

You don't need to conquer the world. You just need to show up for yourself, consistently. That is how great things are built.
