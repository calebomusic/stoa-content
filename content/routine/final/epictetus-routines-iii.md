# 1.5 

## Quote

“If a man, said Epictetus, opposes evident truths, it is not easy to find arguments by which we shall make him change his opinion.”  
Id: ?  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.5.1

## Theory

Philosophy is about living well with others and pursuing the truth. But how do we treat people that don’t want to listen? How do we improve people that don’t want to genuinely engage?

In Epictetus’ time, this is how he felt about the skeptics. These were people who refused to admit that it was day when the sun was up, or that they were awake and not dreaming. The skeptics avoided at all costs believing something that might not be true, but the Stoics thought this approach held them back from many obvious and important kinds of knowledge.

Epictetus calls these kinds of people those that “refuse to assent to what is apparent”. 

In today’s language, we might say this applies to anyone who is not engaging in conversation or debate genuinely; anyone who is not open to their mind being changed.

These people come in different forms.

There are the stubborn people, who refuse to listen. There are internet trolls who just want to upset people and don’t care about the truth. There are manipulators, who want something out of the conversation but won’t ever change.

What is Epictetus’ Stoic advice for changing the opinions of these kinds of people? Stop trying to change them. In the words of Epictetus, someone who doesn’t respond to genuine arguments is ‘worse than a dead person’. 

In other words, you can lead a horse to water but you cannot make them drink. You cannot have a meaningful, opinion changing discussion with someone who is not willing to participate and do their part.

Both participants have to be ready to listen, ready to accept when they misunderstood something, or change their position when a good argument is made.

If they are not, then you are not having a discussion, and the wisdom here is to reset your expectations, or to walk away.

Sometimes real Stoic wisdom is knowing that just like your own beliefs are up to you, changing someone else’s beliefs is up to them.

## Theory

It is always tempting to feel bad for ourselves.

We want to point fingers and place the blame elsewhere. Why did this happen to me? Why was I so unlucky? Why are things so unfair? One Epictetus’ students embodies this mentality when he complains about a runny nose. 

Epictetus snaps back: “For what purpose then, slave, have you hands? Is it not that you may wipe your nose?”

This may seem harsh, Epictetus was a strict teacher. But it is not cruel. There is a lesson here.

In any situation there are two perspectives you can take. One about how bad and difficult things are for you. Another about the tools you have to fix the situation, and the benefits that come from it.

A Stoic trains themselves to take this second view. They snap themselves out of complaining, into action.

Epictetus goes on to ask his student:

“What do you think that Hercules would have been if there had not been such a lion, and hydra, and stag, and boar, and certain unjust and bestial men, whom Hercules used to drive away and clear out? And what would he have been doing if there had been nothing of the kind? Is it not plain that he would have wrapped himself up and have slept?”

The purpose of life is to live well. Feeling bad for yourself when challenges arise does not help you do that, but thinking about the tools you have to improve your situation does.   
Hercules would not have been Hercules if he just slept in bed. And you will only be the best version of yourself if instead of focusing on what’s wrong with your life, you focus on how to improve it.

## Theory

Epictetus’ Discourses book 1 chapter 7 is a passionate defense of the study of logic, arguments, and reasoning.

If this seems boring to you, it also seemed this way to Epictetus.

When Epictetus was a student, he could not understand why his teacher Musonius Rufus cared about the study of logic so much. 

When Musonius criticized him for a mistake in his homework, Epictetus shot back: “It is not as if I have burned down the Capital”.

“Slave” \- Musonius shot back \-  “Are these the only crimes? To burn down the Capitol or kill your father?” 

What Musonius means is that it is harmful to make logical mistakes. Something does not have to be murder or arson to be a bad thing. Thinking carelessly can be bad too.

Epictetus’ students begrudged the study of logic, just as Epictetus did, but the fact remains that it is essential for living well.

According to Epictetus, the goal of studying logic is:

“To establish true propositions, to remove the false, to withhold assent from those which are not plain.”  
Id: ?  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.7

In other words, good reasoning is about proving what is true, disproving what is false, and admitting when you don’t know or need more evidence.

But, as Epictetus rightly points out in this chapter, knowing that the goal of logic is to prove what is true is like a merchant knowing he should reject counterfeit gold, and only accept real gold as payment.

How is that helpful to the merchant, unless he also has a system for identifying real coins and rejecting false ones?

This is the study of logic in Stoicism. It is not some abstract academic discipline, but your system for establishing what is true and false in practice.

Logic is about understanding what sources of information you should believe and which are biased.

It is your standard of proof for having your beliefs changed in light of new evidence.

It is your ability to not be persuaded by appeals to emotions, or shocking images, but to stay diligently focused on the facts of the matter.

Logic is your ability to tell the real gold from the fake. 

And when you think of logic in this way, Epictetus is right that there is almost nothing more important for living well.

## Theory

Even though studying logic is important, mastering arguments is not enough to be a great person.

Immediately following Epictetus’ discussion of Logic in Discourses book 1 chapter 7, he turns his attention to the art of persuasion, or the ability to construct arguments to convince *others* of what we want them to believe. Think of this skill like that of a high school debater, or a skilled politician.

This is a distraction, Epictetus warns us. Philosophy is about transforming who we are on the inside, not about mastering a tool for influencing others.

In the words of Epictetus:

“But if you ask me what is the good for humans, I cannot say to you anything else than that it is how you make choices with respect to impressions.”

In other words, making proper use of impressions, making the right choices is the calling of a Stoic. Not using logic to become a great speaker or writer. 

But, Epictetus’ students quip, what about someone like Plato? He was a persuasive arguer and a great philosopher.

Epictetus fires back:

“And if Plato was handsome and strong, ought I also to set to work and endeavor to become handsome or strong, as if this was necessary for philosophy, because a certain philosopher was at the same time handsome and a philosopher? Will you not choose to see and to distinguish in respect to what men become philosophers, and what things belong to them in other respects?”  
Id: ?  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.8

He even jokes later, asking his students if they should aspire to have an injured leg and a limp like he does, if they want to become a philosopher?

There are two key ideas here:

First, focus on what matters and is essential: Namely improving yourself. Some people are called to be great writers. It is necessary for some. But what is necessary for everyone is practicing philosophy. Don’t let rhetoric, or other intellectual pursuits, become more important than living well.

Second, when copying your role models, focus on what is essential to the part of them you aspire to be like. What is essential to a philosopher is the quality of their character, the choices they make. Whether they are handsome, have a limp, or are an excellent debater, is all secondary to this. Don’t confuse one for the other. Don’t try to be like Epictetus by walking with a limp, or Plato by speaking well. Be like Epictetus in how you show virtue.

In life, always focus your attention on what is essential. Living like a Stoic is about having a virtuous and resilient character, and showing it in your actions. If you put anything above that, such as how well you write or speak, you are missing the point.

## Theory

Stoicism, and Epictetus in particular, tells us that the only good thing is a good character, and the only evil thing is a bad one.

If you really pause and think about this, it has some serious consequences. Death is not a bad thing, nor exile from your community, nor experiencing slavery or the risk of starvation.

In Discourses Book 1 chapter 9, Epictetus explores what Stoicism has to say about these extreme situations: Death, exile, slavery.

Some of these ideas may be foreign to you. You may not have had to face starvation or war. But we all face difficulty and tragedy in our lives. We all have situations that push us to our limits. And we will all die.

A Stoic, or at least the sage, approaches these scenarios with calm. If they happen, then you will make the best of them. If they don’t happen, then you will have been better off for not being fearful today.

As Epictetus says to his students:

“When you have been well filled to-day, you sit down and lament about the morrow, how you shall get something to eat. Wretch, if you have it, you will have it; if you have it not, you will depart from life. The door is open.”  
Id: ?  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.9

The door is open, says Epictetus. This is a favorite phrase of his. It is a call to memento mori, or remember death. Death is a possibility for all of us, and we should keep this in mind so we are prepared to act well when it happens to us or the people we care about.

The Stoics demand we think about death. We do not want to be caught off-guard by tragedy and hardship when it impacts our lives. Much better to prepare for it now, while we have the time and energy. 

If we can accept the possibility of the worst case scenario with a stoic calm, then our anxiety will disappear. 

Consider this line again: “If you have it, you will have it; if you have it not, you will depart from life”. Imagine the freedom that comes from accepting this idea fully. 

In other words, don’t live in anxiety. If fortune blesses you, make the most of it. When misfortune occurs, face it well.

## Theory

Enter any workplace, and you will see people treating their affairs like it is the most important thing in the world.

They are stressed about the next promotion, about politics, about completing a certain project.

Consider this in your life. We have all treated something trivial like it mattered. We have all taken on a little world that keeps ourselves busy and working.

At the very least, think back to what you thought mattered in high school and how irrelevant that seems to you now. 

In this chapter, Epictetus pokes fun at this busy-ness. He points to the old men, who even close to death work tirelessly to navigate the politics of Rome, or secure themselves a slightly higher position. What is it for, he asks?

But Epictetus is not encouraging laziness:

“Well then do I say that man is an animal made for doing nothing?” \- He asks his students. Certainly not.”

Epictetus wants us to be active and hard-working, but why don’t we spend that energy on the right things?

Why don’t we take that busyness, that sense that this world of work or politics is the most important thing, and apply it to the study of philosophy?

What if we cared about Stoicism or living well, the same way we cared about importing goods, buying land, or making a profit?

Become the kind of person that takes living well as seriously as someone else takes office politics, and you will unlock for yourself a kind of superpower. Direct your intelligence towards helping others. Put your work ethic into cultivating virtue and showing it in your actions.

This lesson then is really one about values. Humans have an incredible ability to motivate ourselves to do what we think is important. Ancient Stoics believed philosophy was more important than their personal title in Rome. You’ve got to think it is more important than external success too.

Id: ?  
Source: Discourses

## Theory

Passionate love, like any intense emotion, is not always good for us or the people we care about. 

One day Epictetus is visited by a senior politician who is suffering. 

I am miserable, the magistrate says, because of how much I love my wife and children.  
I love my daughter so much that when she is sick I had to leave, because I could not bear to see her ill. 

Epictetus pauses at this. The magistrate calls this love, but is it something healthy? Or is it more like a sickness?

“Come then”, Epictetus says, “If you were sick would you wish your relations to be so affectionate, and all the rest, children and wife, as to leave you along and deserted?”

“By no means”, replies the magistrate. 

Even the father realizes this is not how he wants to be loved.

He is harming his children, not because he loves them, but because he loves them in the wrong way.

He loves them in a way that does not accept, or take into account, their mortal nature. He is not prepared for the fact that his children might get sick, or may even die before him, and so in these moments of illness he is not able to be a good parent. 

Just because something feels natural, does not mean it is good.

The kind of blind optimistic love this father has for his daughter feels natural, it comes to him easily, but it is not a good kind of love. 

Epictetus compares it to a tumor. It originates without us doing anything, but we need to intervene to remove it. 

Other kinds of love are the same way. Obsessive or jealous love arise naturally, but they are not good for us. 

Good Stoics love other people in an intentional and healthy way, a way that lets them fulfill their role as a parent, partner, or child. They have relationships that matter to them, but understand that positive feelings can turn dangerous if left unchecked or unexamined. 

This is not to say a Stoic love is cold, just the opposite. The Stoic parent can love their child fully, while also being prepared for the chance they will get sick. And so, when their child is sick, they are able to parent them without fear.

This is better love than that of a parent who runs away.
