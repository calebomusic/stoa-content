1. # The Obstacle is the Way

## Quote

## Theory

Sometimes it’s difficult to wake up in the morning. So we don’t, then we regret it. Other times we know what we should do and perhaps even want to do it, but the motivation is not there.

It can feel like the lack of motivation holds us back from the life we deserve. 

Even the Roman emperor talked about the difficulty of waking up in the morning. The productive Seneca talks about the seductiveness of procrastination. 

This course is all about building motivation. Yes, Stoics like Marcus Aurelius and Seneca struggled with motivation too. But look at what they accomplished. 

This can bring about solace in two ways. First, there’s the solidarity of the struggle. To be unmotivated is human. Second, there’s hope. Even though they experienced a lack of motivation they found their drive and did great things nonetheless. We can join their ranks.

The first idea of this course is related to this. It’s the classic Stoic message that Marcus Aurelius captured:

"The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way."  
quote\_id: 56

Perhaps you began this course because you're struggling with developing motivation. 

The idea of Marcus Aurelius' quote is that lacking motivation itself is an obstacle we can overcome. 

We can conquer procrastination the same way we can practice Stoicism to defeat other challenges.

Throughout this course, we’ll cover the Stoic techniques and philosophy that enabled men and women to live like forces of nature. Today, we’ll focus on the theme that the obstacle is the way. Whether that obstacle is internal or external. You can move forward.

Develop a Stoic attitude. Become like an all consuming fire that blazes brighter no matter what you throw into it. 

Marcus Aurelius says this too:

"Persevere then until you shall have made these things your own, as the stomach which is strengthened makes all things its own, as the blazing fire makes flame and brightness out of everything that is thrown into it."  
sentence\_id: 1631

The Stoic emperor is talking about a general skill to adapt to whatever life throws at us. That includes external and internal events. Feelings of laziness, the overwhelming sense that there’s so much to do, the temptation to push things off one more day – each of these are things we can push through.  

You can develop the skills to keep going, get energy from events that would defeat other people, and rest when you must.

This doesn’t mean you're energetic and motivated every single second. It means that you adapt to where your mind, body, and circumstances are at as well as you can. 

If you’re feeling a lack of motivation, Stoicism can help you muster the drive to move forward.

# Purpose

## Theory

Seneca said:

“If a man knows not to which port he sails, no wind is favorable.”  
quote\_id: 356

If you want to be more motivated, the essential question is for what.

Busyness for its own sake is a waste. It’s a simple and obvious observation, but wasting time is wasting life. Our actions must have a purpose. 

Marcus Aurelius says that we should have our aim in mind every moment:

"Since it is possible that you may depart from life this very moment, regulate every act and thought accordingly."  
sentence\_id: 397

If we do this, then motivation is much easier to find.

It’s aimlessness that often causes a lack of energy. Or the belief that what we’re supposed to do isn’t worthwhile.

But if you clarify your purpose and it is worthwhile you can use that as a motivating force.

This, of course, brings out the issue of how one determines your purpose. 

This is a massive topic. But the Stoics offer us wisdom we can use and internalize in even a few moments. 

First, they remind us that our day to day purpose is to find. This comes from our  everyday  roles. Look at the circumstances you find yourself in. Look at your roles. All you need to do is be excellent in them. Who are you? A student? A teacher? A friend? Spouse? Citizen? Founder? Athlete? Each day we find ourselves playing many roles. Do your best to fulfill them. Use common sense to find meaning in your day.

Second, the overarching purpose of a life is happiness for Stoics. That doesn’t mean feeling good. It means living in line with our human nature. Being an excellent human. In a way, this is the universal role we all share. It is our cosmic role.

So, you can start by noticing the ordinary, everyday purposes you already find yourself in. But keep in mind our larger cosmic purpose too.

Marcus Aurelius captures this in his meditations:

"Every moment, think steadily as a Roman and a man to do what you have in hand with perfect and simple dignity, and feeling of affection, and freedom, and justice; and to give yourself relief from all other thoughts. And you will give yourself relief if you do every act of your life as if it were the last."  
quote\_id: 384

Notice the combination of both kinds of purposes. The everyday ones he already has, with this reminder of a cosmic purpose. You can use the same technique. 

Bring to mind the ordinary purposes you have. Flesh out what your cosmic purpose is.

When you have your purposes in mind, use them to motivate you. Contrast the world where you do your best to fulfill your roles and the world where you don’t. Use that contrast as fuel.

# Memento Mori

## Quote

## Theory

In the previous lesson we introduced the importance of clarifying your purpose.

As Seneca said:

“If a man knows not to which port he sails, no wind is favorable.”  
quote\_id: 356

There are two kinds of purposes: cosmic and everyday. Both of them are available to you.

For Stoics, our cosmic purpose is to live well as a human. That means finding calm and happiness through the use of reason: thinking well and living well with others.

Our everyday purposes come from  roles we find ourselves in due to our situation and personality. Here Stoicism joins other philosophies and religions who advise being excellent wherever and whoever we are: child, parent, boss, employee, neighbor, citizen, craftsman, athlete – whatever it is. 

Today we’ll focus on a classic Stoic technique for building motivation to achieve your purposes: remembering that life is short.

This is one of the Roman emperors Marcus Aurelius’s, constant themes.

"Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good."  
quote\_id: 915

Keep these lines close to you. Rehearse them in your own words. As you go throughout your day remember that life is short. That is not just a cliche phrase. It is a reality. 

We do not know how much time we have to become excellent in our everyday roles. Be an excellent friend, artist, athlete, spouse, parent – whatever it is – while you can. 

Nor have you reached the height of your cosmic purpose. 

The reality of death is out of our control. Of course, we should be prudent. A longer life, healthy life is preferable and helps us achieve our purposes. But eventually our time will come.

As such, the Stoics thought we should embrace it. That means accepting the reality of mortality and living in a way that respects it. 

Why push off the project of happiness? Seize it while you can.

As the poet Horace said: carpe diem.

"Life ebbs as I speak – so seize each day, and grant the next no credit."  
quote\_id: 1877

# Putting it together

## Quote

## Theory

We all have two kinds of purposes: the cosmic and the everyday.

The Stoic sees these purposes clearly and, with clear vision, is motivated to achieve them.

We lose motivation when our fatigue, doubt, or distractions cloud our vision. We forget our purpose and sometimes it is only too late when we realize our mistake.

The Stoic doesn’t do this. They get energy from the fact that their nature calls to be lived out. We can do the same.

As we covered in the last lesson, we all have a cosmic purpose: to be an excellent human. That means pursuing knowledge and living prosocially. But we also have our own unique everyday purposes. This isn’t an exhaustive list but it’s a useful frame for checking in and getting energy to propel yourself.

Throughout your day,  ask: am I living as a human being? Marcus Aurelius uses this very question to motivate himself to get up in the morning:

"1. In the morning when you rise unwillingly, let this thought be present- I am rising to the work of a human being."  
quote\_id: 658

This is the cosmic question. 

Alternatively, you can focus on the everyday roles that are right in front of you. Ask: am I an excellent student, employee, boss, parent, spouse – whatever it is. And then be it. That’s what you’re meant to be.

The skilled Stoic is able to combine these two kinds of goals into a single life. In each moment they achieve their cosmic purpose by living out their day to day roles. 

When you’re performing well in your roles, you’ll be happy. This happiness grounds energy and motivation.

This can be true in circumstances pleasant and difficult. 

Marcus Aurelius faced catastrophes during his reign as Roman emperor. Plague and war. In those moments, it was his job to rule well. That’s it. 

Yet he also made time to write letters to his tutors – tutors he clearly saw as friends. 

In this way, perhaps he wasn’t that much different from the rest of us. We all have competing roles. And like Marcus, if we devote ourselves to fulfilling them well, we’ll live energetic lives.

# Building Motivation

## Quote

## Theory

Genuine confidence is knowledge. 

That’s an underrated Stoic idea. If you’re confident when everyone around you is full of doubt, that’s because you know something that they don’t. You know that you will be successful.

Confidence has been defined as the expectation of success.

That’s why people are drawn to the confident – because they believe that it’s a sign that success will come. They want to be around that.

But it’s important that the confidence is not fake. Fake confidence is ignorance. Just because you expect success doesn’t mean that it will come. 

Confidence is best grounded in knowledge. The expectation of success because you know you have what it takes. How do you build that knowledge? Through action. Start small and focus on what’s up to you, not outside results. Show yourself that you will do your best, whatever happens.

What does confidence have to do with motivation? Building it is an excellent way to be motivated. Success feeds on each other. What holds people back so often is the expectation of failure. If you can be confident that you’ll take the next step, you’ve already made it. Just do it. You don't need more motivation. 

The best way to start was suggested by Epictetus. Begin with the small things. Do it day after day. If you would be a writer, write. If a runner, run. It’s that simple. Prove to yourself that you’re moving forward each day.

Let’s end with a reminder from Epictetus:

"Every habit and faculty is maintained and increased by the corresponding actions: the habit of walking by walking, the habit of running by running."

## Quote

## Theory

When you get stuck, use the breath as an anchor.

It’s a source of energy and a reminder of the present.

As Marcus Aurelius said:

“Confine yourself to the present.”  
sentence\_id: 1059

We remember the past and let memories of past failures ruin our confidence. We speculate about the future and become overwhelmed at the task before us.

Both of these are illusions all you ever have is each moment. 

The Stoic practiced limiting their attention to the present alone.

Simply acting moment by moment. Shaping themselves and the world around them into something better. 

If you’re caught off course you can use the breath as a reminder to find the rhythm. It’s useful because it changes every moment. # Stoic Motivation System

## Quote

## Theory

You get motivation from your opinions. You are not your thoughts, but you are how you think.

That’s the central Stoic insight. Most things are not bad in and of themselves, but it is our opinion that makes them so.

So, if you’re struggling with motivation you need to change the decisions you're making. Essentially, you must choose to have your purposes in front of mind and then act on them.

By doing this over and over again you will build confidence. It gets easier over time.

We talked about two kinds of purposes in this course: cosmic and everyday. Our cosmic purpose is to be an excellent human. That means using our ability to reason. That’s what makes us special according to the Stoics. Our everyday purposes are the roles we already find ourselves in. We can find meaning and motivation in being an excellent student, friend, parent, builder, craftsman, artist, whatever it is. Life is short – why not be excellent in all that you do? According to the Stoics, this is the way to happiness.

And it makes sense. Think of the energetic and motivated people you know. The happiest ones are good at what they do. They find the drive in themselves to keep going when things get tough because they care about being someone who is excellent regardless of what happens. They may not realize it, but that is Stoicism.

The central way for a Stoic to stay motivated is to remember their purposes, why they matter, and act accordingly. This requires staying in the present. Sometimes the past and future can overwhelm our efforts to get things done. But let the past be and the future come as it will. The present is all that we have now. Break your problems into manageable chunks. Something you can do now.

Don’t let any moment pass you by because you will only get each one once. And your whole life, who can say how much time you have?

So, the Stoic motivation system involves keeping your values and purposes close. Proving that they matter with your actions. And reminding yourself to stay present – with philosophy and meditation – and practicing memento mori. 

By practicing this you will find energy and, more importantly, happiness.
