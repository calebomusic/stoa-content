## **Resilience**

## Intro

#### Theory

Imagine a resilient individual. Someone you admire for their strength and emotional fortitude. What makes them resilient? How are they different from everyone else?

Maybe they are the kind of person who never becomes needlessly upset. Resilience can look like the ability to stay calm in extreme situations, like a mountain climber who never panics, no matter what goes wrong. It can take the form of never letting harmful emotions get started. As Seneca says: “If reason prevails, the emotions will not even get started” (Seneca 1711). 

Or maybe your resilient role-model is someone who bounces back from difficulty very quickly. Like a professional athlete who is upset to lose the game, but bounces back with full enthusiasm for the next one. As Marcus Aurelius says, “When you have been compelled by circumstances to be disturbed in a manner, quickly return to yourself and do not continue out of tune longer than the compulsion lasts.” (Marcus, 947\) Resilience can look like being able to return to yourself and calm yourself down, whenever you do get upset.

What each of these forms of resilience have in common is the ability to respond to stressful situations well. Resilience is the capacity to not be significantly harmed by the stress, pressure, tragedy, and chaos of our lives.

All people should want to cultivate emotional fortitude. Not because we want life to be easier, but because many things worth doing are difficult.

To become more resilient, the Stoics tell us to develop character traits, called virtues, that allow us to maintain even emotion in extreme situations. When you have courage, it is easier to do scarier things, when we have temperance, it is easier to go without the things we want. Virtues are obtained, in large part, by developing healthy patterns of thinking, self-reflection, and judgment.

Meditation is a path to virtue, by promoting healthy patterns of thinking.

## **DoC**

#### **Theory**

In the previous lesson, we discussed how each individual has the ability to become resilient. For the Stoics, this is because resilience is a mental state, not a physical state. It is a way of approaching situations so that they do not take control of your emotions. But how do we achieve that state? 

The Dichotomy of Control is a key Stoic tool for achieving resilience. At its simplest form, the Dichotomy of Control says that some things are up to us and other things are not. We lose our resilience, and experience emotional turmoil, when we try to control things that are not up to us. 

Epictetus gives the example of a musician. He says “Take a lyre player: he’s relaxed when he performs alone, but put him in front of an audience, and it’s a different story, no matter how beautiful his voice or how well he plays the instrument. Why? Because he not only wants to perform well, he wants to be well received – and the latter lies outside his control.” (Epictetus, ID 672\)

The musician can play perfectly by himself, but he loses emotional composure in front of an audience. Why is that? It is because, Epictetus says, the musician cares about the opinion of the audience, but their opinion is outside his control. Yes he can influence it by playing well or poorly, but if the arena is hot, or the audience is in a foul mood, or they are fans of a different genre, the audience will dislike the music no matter what the lyre player plays. 

So naturally, the musician is stressed. He wants to impress the audience, but he cannot know for certain that he will. So he gives himself up to the audience, and makes the quality of his day dependent on them, not on himself.

The dichotomy of control applies not just to wanting to be liked, but also to harm or insults. Epictetus says:

“Remember, it is not enough to be hit or insulted to be harmed, you must believe that you are being harmed. If someone succeeds in provoking you, realize that your mind is complicit in the provocation. … Take a moment before reacting, and you will find it is easier to maintain control.” (Epictetus, ID 808\)

Epictetus’ point is that being hit or insulted is outside our control, it is the behavior of another person. But actually being provoked, getting upset, that is up to us. That is something we determine. When we forget that fact, we give up our capacity to be resilient in the face of harm. 

This is why we should always keep in mind this key distinction by the Stoics, between what is up to us and what is not. To end with a final quote by Epictetus, “Make a practice at once of saying to every strong impression: ‘An impression is all you are.’ Then test and assess it with your principles. Ask, ‘Is this something that is, or is not, in my control?’” (Epictetus, 1670\)

## **Model**

#### **Theory**

The Stoics loved the use of role models. A good role model can teach, inspire, and motivate all in one action.

For the Stoic Epictetus, Socrates was his role model. Socrates would walk around ancient Athens, 500 years before Epictetus lived, and engage people in philosophical discussion. He would question them, interrogate what they thought they knew, and try to find to truth of the matter.

Besides being a great philosopher, Socrates was accused of a crime he claims he did not commit, corrupting the youth of Athens. Rather than plead guilty for a lesser charge, Socrates fought against the crime, refused to admit wrong, and was sentenced to death.

Rather than show a fear of death and try to break out of jail, Socrates accepted his execution without fear or sadness, confident in his knowledge that he had acted correctly and lived as a philosopher. 

Epictetus returns to the example of Socrates often. On the idea of dying, Epictetus says “Death is nothing terrible, else it would have appeared so to Socrates.” Socrates models how to act for Epictetus, and shows him how it is possible to be brave and true to your values in the face of death.

When developing resilience, we can look to role models from our own lives. Perhaps a family member who you admire for their calm demeanor, no matter the situation. Or, like Socrates, they can be people from history we have never met. Or then can even be fictional characters from books or movies. Maybe there is a character from a TV show you like, you continues to do what’s right, no matter how difficult things become for them. Regardless of who you choose, they should just be an example you want to emulate. Let these examples guide you, like a north star, towards the person you want to be.

As you go throughout your day and try to cultivate resilience, imagine your own role model. Picture how they would think and act. And then think and act well.

## **Invincibility**

#### **Theory** 

The highest Stoic goal for resilience was invincibility, but not physical invincibility, like a Hercules. That is something which no human can achieve. Instead, their goal was an emotional invincibility against unhealthy passions and freedom from being controlled by extreme harmful emotions. 

This kind of invincibility is achieved by accepting the world as it is, and not expecting the world to be different, beyond what you can do to make it better. When you know the nature of yourself, and the world around you, and accept that nature, then what you see as natural will not upset or emotionally harm you.

Here is a famous quote from Epictetus on training this invincibility: 

“If you love an earthen vessel, say it is an earthen vessel which you love; for when it has been broken you will not be disturbed. If you are kissing your child or wife, say that it is a human being whom you are kissing, for when the wife or child dies you will not be disturbed.” (ID 238\)

This is an extreme example, and one of the hardest Stoic lessons, but the idea is simple. If you love a human being, recognize that you love a mortal thing. Understand that mortal things die, and so, the person you love will die too one day. You cannot turn a human being into something immortal. So to love that person as they are in their nature, is to also love and accept their mortality. If you can achieve that, their death will not come as a surprise nor will it disturb you.

Whether you want to be disturbed by the death of loved ones is a personal choice, and we do not have to agree with Epictetus here, but the point remains with less extreme examples. By the nature of the world, you cannot make everyone like you. You cannot make difficult tasks easy. You cannot make your own life significantly longer than 100 years. We should accept these truths. If we accept the nature of the world, that nature will not disturb us.

Let’s meditate on accepting the nature of the world, and gaining invincibility from that.

## **Non-Judgement**

#### **Theory**

Everyday we are bombarded by people and situations that compete for our opinion. People expect us to have an opinion on whatever they care about, no matter how trivial. Many people even expect us to not just have an opinion, but also conviction.

To suspend judgment about a situation you are ignorant about is not a sign of apathy, but a sign of intellectual maturity. It is easier to consider the evidence, and form a justified opinion, when you haven’t chosen a side from the beginning. That’s part of the reason Stoics made suspending opinion, and judging situations carefully, one of their core tools.

Suspending judgment also has emotional benefits. Marcus Aurelius famously said:

“It is in our power to have no opinion about a thing and not to be disturbed in our soul; for things themselves have no natural power to form our judgments.” (ID: 962\)

We are disturbed by our opinions, not situations. We are disturbed when we judge something to be bad, and elated when we judge something to be good.

This is why considerate judgment creates resilience. Someone who judges carefully, will not be pulled around by their impressions or the emotions of others. They will be calm until the situation warrants not being calm. If they are upset they will not indulge in their emotions, but recover from them when it is obvious that it is no longer appropriate to be upset. 

The Stoic should practice withholding a definitive judgment until they have considered the facts, and only believe something with the conviction the evidence warrants. Doing this saves them from unnecessary disturbances. They are no longer pulled around by every piece of news, or pulled into a rage by a half-truth.

Let’s meditate on practicing considerate judgment, and gaining emotional resiliency through being careful with our opinions.

## **Thought-patterns**

#### **Theory**

Much of our resilience depends on the mindset with which we approach situations. Epictetus once said:

“The true man is revealed in difficult times. So when trouble comes, think of yourself as a wrestler whom God, like a trainer, has paired with a tough young buck. For what purpose? To turn you into Olympic-class material. But this is going to take some sweat to accomplish.” (ID: 618\)

If you can think of difficult times as a kind of test, like an athletic opponent sent by a trainer to make you make you stronger, then you immediately have a greater sense of purpose confronting your challenges. And with a greater sense of purpose, comes greater ability.

Furthermore, there really is truth to this statement. Like anything else in life, every situation has its benefits and its downsides. If you train your thoughts to focus on the benefits, then the situation becomes more bearable. 

This is just one example of how certain thought patterns can help prepare you for life’s challenges. So it is crucial to be intentional with how you think, and both accept the negative aspects  and recognize the positive. 

You cannot ignore the negatives of life, for they are also a  part of nature, and a mind that ignores them is not resilient but just lying to itself. However, you should also embrace the positives of each situation, even if the only positive is that this experience will make you more resilient in the future.

In this way we should adopt the thinking of a wrestler, or athlete, who acknowledges the skill of their opponent and the challenge of overcoming them, but also looks forward to this challenge and the benefits it might bring.

Let’s meditate on focusing on thought-patterns on the positive side of difficult situations.

## **VFA**

#### **Theory**

Sometimes, when we face hardship, we lose perspective. It is easy when things are hard to feel like our situation is uniquely poor, or that we are the only one who has had to endure such indignities.

But this is a mistake. No matter what you have encountered, someone else has gone through something similar, and they have navigated the situation well. As Seneca said:

“Fortune has not chosen you as the only man in the world to receive so severe a blow: there is no house in all the earth, and never has been one, that has not something to mourn for.” (ID: 1185\)

It is not possible to be a human and not face setbacks or hardships. Our job is not to avoid these situations altogether, but to do what is in our control to the best of our ability. Namely, our job is to act as virtuously as possible. And if someone before you has navigated the situation virtuously, then so can you.

One Stoic tool used to gain this perspective is called the view from above. When we take this view, we consider ourselves as just one person amongst a vast world of human experiences, or even as one living thing in the grand universe. From this perspective, our problems can seem more manageable, or at the very least, we can recognize them as a normal part of human experience. This helps us to avoid distractions, and to focus on what is essential: acting as well as possible given the situation circumstances have put you in.

The view from above  gives us a broader perspective. In doing so, it pulls us out of the unhelpful narratives or stories we tell ourselves. When we view a situation objectively, we can see that all that remains for us to do is act well, rather than focus on what is inessential. As Marcus put its:

“Just that you do the right thing. The rest doesn't matter. Cold or warm. Tired or well-rested. Despised or honored. Dying...or busy with other assignments.” (ID: 30\)
