# 2.1

## Theory

Naive optimism has a dark side. 

It can lead us to unreasonably expect behavior from others that we shouldn’t. When they don’t live up to our standards, we’re frustrated.

Marcus Aurelius opens book two of the Meditations with:

“Begin the morning by saying to yourself I shall meet with the busy-body, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial.”

He reminds himself – every morning – of the fact that he will face obstacles from others.

If you don’t expect that, you may be seeing reality with false optimism. Many people are angry at traffic. But that anger is often the result of a weird kind of optimism: expecting driving to always be comfortable and convenient. When stated that way, it’s obvious that is not a reasonable expectation. Then why is one frustrated? Prepare yourself to face traffic – and far worse obstacles throughout your life.

Preparing for adversity diminishes its power over us.

The goal isn't merely to lower our expectations. Instead, it’s to take an objective look at them and align them with reality.

We can’t expect perfection from our circumstances or other people. See them and accept them as they are. Likewise, we cannot expect perfection from ourselves. Life is an act of perpetual improvement.

As Marcus Aurelius reminded himself:

“Do not go expecting Plato’s Republic”

Plato’s Republic was a political utopia. As emperor of Rome, Marcus Aurelius couldn’t pretend that he was dealing with other philosopher kings. He had to be ready to meet other people – and himself, where they were at. This means keeping the real possibility that others will act in ungrateful, arrogant, and unsocial ways in mind.

At the same time, Stoicism is an ambitious philosophy. We should expect better of ourselves – because we can be better. Tolerate and accept others. Improve ourselves.

Happiness is possible. We can transform who we are, day after day. There’s an optimism and beauty in that Stoic message.

## Theory

In the last routine, we heard Marcus Aurelius’s reflections from the beginning of Meditations 2:

“Begin the morning by saying to yourself I shall meet with the busy-body, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial.”

Marcus Aurelius continues:

“All these things happen to them by reason of their ignorance of what is good and evil.”

This is of central importance. 

Marcus Aurelius isn’t criticizing his fellows. Instead, he is recognizing that we’re all ignorant. Rather than attributing people's wrongdoings to malice, he sees them as stemming from a fundamental lack of knowledge. This opens up the possibility to meet others with acceptance and compassion.

If people truly knew the nature of the world and happiness, they would act out of justice, courage, and kindness. But they don’t.

So, people do wrong because of confusion. They choose what they think is best. But they have mistaken views about reality. We’re each corrupted by our selfish interests, society, and, to some degree, our biological nature. Evolution programmed our genes to survive. That’s a recipe for creating morally complicated animals. While capable of noble sacrifices, we are also prone to rationalizing our own harmful actions. Society itself tells us to overvalue non-essential things. It seems like some level of wealth, pleasure, and status is necessary for a happy life. But this is an illusion. 

As you encounter others, remember that they face these pressures. 

This doesn’t mean that we should passively accept their wrongdoing. Understand why others make mistakes. See, to the extent you can, the world from their perspective. Then determine how to respond with virtue and excellence. 

We’re all imperfect beings aiming to do what we think is best. As you face success and failure, ease and adversity, luck and misfortune, remember that. Accept others as they are. Do not see them as enemies, but partners doing the best they know how.

After all, that’s the route to a resilient and meaningful life.

## Theory

It may not be easy, but we can handle whatever life throws our way.

For the Stoics, a happy life is one in which we make excellent judgments and decisions. All that is required to live well is to do our best. Think and act well.

We don’t need to hold our well-being hostage to events in the external world. Factors like our health, reputation, and wealth are, ultimately, beyond our control. We’re like the poker player who can make all the right decisions but still lose. Or the archer who aims as well as they can only to find their shot disturbed by an unpredictable gust of wind. 

Our job is to play the game of life well. Let the cards fall how they may.

In the last two routines, we meditated on Marcus Aurelius’s opening of Meditations 2\. Let’s continue that today:

“I who have seen the nature of the good that it is beautiful, and of the bad that it is ugly, and the nature of him who does wrong that is akin to me… I can neither be injured by any of them, for no one can fix on me what is ugly, nor can I be angry with my kinsman, nor hate him, For we are made for cooperation.”  
Let’s summarize the meaning of this statement.

First Marcus Aurelius notes that others cannot truly harm us; they cannot obstruct our pursuit of happiness. Yes, they can do wrong to us. Others can mistreat us. When that happens, we need to respond with justice and compassion. But can they make us unhappy? No.

As Marcus Aurelius says “No one can fix on me what is ugly.” Whether or not we’re unhappy is up to us.

Instead of seeing others as obstacles or enemies, Marcus Aurelius reminds himself to see them as kin and family. 

We’re all humans – animals that share a prosocial and rational nature. We each contain within ourselves the power of reason – reason that is capable of understanding the order of the world, creating beautiful works of art, and living with nature in better and better ways. Moreover, our nature is inherently prosocial. When we hear about people sacrificing themselves for strangers, it’s this prosocial instinct that motivates them.

Ultimately, we’re made for cooperation. You can see our life as a choir. Our job is to sing our part well with others. The other members of the choir cannot harm us – only we are capable of determining our performance. But our job is still to sing well with them. If someone is off-pitch, we can adjust, just as we hope others adjust for us. This is all for the sake of a greater story and harmony. 

Let’s end with Marcus Aurelius’s final thoughts from this section:  
“To act against one another then is contrary to nature; and it is acting against one another to become upset and turn away”

# 2.2

## Theory

What are you?

Marcus Aurelius states:

“Whatever this is that I am, it is a little flesh and breath, and the ruling part.”

What does he mean?

The Stoics were materialists. They believe that everything is physical. This includes our ruling part – our reasoning mind.

Unlike other philosophers, they don’t believe we’re separate from the world. This is why we have such deep kinship with other people and Nature.

We’re fundamentally rational beings, but we live in the world, not separate from it.

So, Marcus is reminding himself to care for himself. Look after the reasoning mind. Think well. Because that is ultimately what is up to him.

He continues:

“As if you waste now dying, despise the flesh; it is blood and bones and a network, a weaving of nerves, veins, and arteries.”

Even though the Stoics were materialists they cared more about the material of the mind than the body. Do not overvalue your appearance, health, or the external world. People gather up material goods that they will only lose when the story ends. They sculpt their bodies, even when they must eventually age and decay. Both of these things – wealth and health – are indifferent. What matters is how we use them. If our mind is excellent, we’ll use them well. 

Like Marcus Aurelius, we can do our best to live well now. Willingly accept whatever is not up to us. Show virtue in whatever is. Invest all of yourself into becoming excellent.

As Marcus Aurelius writes to himself:

“You are an old man; no longer let this be a slave; no longer be pulled by the strings like a puppet to antisocial movements, no longer either be dissatisfied with your present lot, or shrink from the future.”

# 2.3

## Theory

Marcus Aurelius once wrote:

“Now the universe is preserved, as by the changes of the elements, so also by the changes of things made up of the elements.

Let these principles be enough for you, let them always be fixed opinions.”

He reminds himself to always keep Stoic principles in mind. If we’re able to do that, we’ll live resilient and meaningful lives.

Before analyzing these principles, it’s important to understand how keeping these ideas close can transform your life.

The mind works by receiving impressions and sensations. We have the power to agree or disagree with the impressions we receive. If someone bumps into me on the subway, I might initially feel wronged.. I can react and angrily turn around. However – I also have the power to suspend judgment. Whether or not someone wronged me depends on the situation. If it’s a child who bumped into me by accident, then it doesn’t make sense to say that they wronged me. The act was unintentional.

Our mind has the power to interpret every circumstance differently. Stoic principles are what we use to respond well, instead of reacting. For example, I can remember to suspend judgment when I do not have knowledge and avoid reacting with anger. 

These principles are of central importance because they help us make good decisions. In order to take advantage of them, we need to wrestle with the ideas, constantly remind ourselves of them, and internalize them into our behavior.

Here in *Meditations* book two, section 3, Marcus Aurelius reflects on key Stoic principles.

The first is that the world is providentially ordered. As he says:

“That which is from fortune is not separated from nature or without an interweaving and interplay with the things which are ordered by providence.”

He means that the world is set up with a purpose behind it. There’s a reason for everything. Perhaps you don’t believe that. Nonetheless, you can be alert to the reasons that are initially hidden to us. If someone bumps into me on the subway, I don’t have to respond immediately. I can keep the possibility that there’s a good reason for what happened in mind. Oftentimes there is.

Marcus Aurelius reminds himself of a related principle: things come and go. The worries of today will be gone in time. Yet, our individual nature and purpose remains the same:

“Now the universe is preserved, as by the changes of the elements, so also by the changes of things made up of the elements.”

As the circumstances we find ourselves in change, we can remain solid. Our purpose is to be the best we can be and live well with others. That’s it.

Always act with the whole in mind. We are a part of a much larger story than our own narrative. We’re bound to other people. What’s good for the hive is what is good for the bee.

Marcus Aurelius reminds himself of the constancy of change in order to live well.

The universe is a transformation. Everything is temporary. Let that inspire a sense of gratitude – we’re granted wonderful things in the short time we have – and let it also motivate urgency – there is no time to waste. 

Keep your principles close. Apply them. And live well.

# 2.4

## Theory

Life is short.

This is a cliché, yet it is also true.

No one knows how much time they have left.

If we see this fact in the right way, it may bring about gratitude and urgency.

We can be grateful for the life we have. The positive things that come our way. The friends and family we have or have had throughout our life. Rather than dwelling in the past or fretting over the future, let us anchor ourselves in the present

Realizing the shortness of life motivates us to use our time well. Remove nonsense from your life. Make a conscious effort to acknowledge life's positives. Ensure that you pause from time to time and take in what’s good in life

You can use time well in many ways. Today, just focus on increasing what’s positive about life and removing what isn’t.

Bring to mind one beautiful thing, a good relationship, or a pleasurable activity. Be grateful for it. Be present when it arrives. That’s the positive approach.

For the negative approach, think of ways that you waste your time. If there are specific activities like pointless arguments, see them as they are: times when you let other people or things take your life. If you can, remove them. 

With this insight, I hope you’re inspired to experience joy and cultivate excellence while you can.

Remove nonsense from your life. Take action now. If you don’t stop wasting time now, when?

The Stoics advised that we apply our pursuit of virtue to using time well.

As Marcus Aurelius wrote:

“Remember how long you have been putting off these things, and how often you have received an opportunity from the gods, and yet do not use it.

You must now at last perceive of what universe you are a part...and that a limit of time is fixed for you which if you do not use for clearing away the clouds from your mind, it will go and you will go, and it will never return.”

## Theory

Life is nothing more than a series of moments.

Each moment is a question. How we respond is our answer.

Too often we’re asleep at the wheel. Life happens and we miss it. People push our buttons and we don’t respond freely, but react. Our old thinking patterns stop us from living the life we want to lead.

But things do not have to be that way. Look at what is in front of you. Be present. This is within your power. With practice, it becomes easier.

The past is gone, and the future will arrive in its own time. Do what is right, with determination and calm. If you carry excellence with you in every moment you will be free.

As Marcus Aurelius reminded himself:

“Every moment, think steadily as a Roman and a man to do what you have in hand with perfect and simple dignity, and feeling of affection, and freedom, and justice; and to give yourself relief from all other thoughts.

And you will give yourself relief if you do every act of your life as if it were the last.”

Do not be overwhelmed by the whole of life. Think and act as well as you can in each moment. You do not need anything else for a good life.

Be like the runner. Just put one foot in front of the other. You may need to gauge the remaining distance in the race – after all you don’t want to sprint when you have miles and miles to go – but you don’t need to dwell on it. Embrace the present, even as you prepare for the future and learn from the past.

Practice this attitude in your activities today. We’ll practice it in meditation. If you do this, as Marcus says, you will give yourself relief.
