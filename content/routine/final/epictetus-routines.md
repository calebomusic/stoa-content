# Intro

## Theory

Stoicism is for everyone.

Unlike other ancient philosophical schools, the Stoics allowed women into their classes. Unlike many other philosophies, the philosophy is named, not after the founder, like Epicureanism or Platonism, but after a location: the Stoa. The Stoa was the place where everyone met to discuss Stoicism.

Fittingly, emperors and slaves practiced it.

This course is on the slave-turned-philosopher Epictetus. We’ll cover his life and key ideas. Each day will include a lesson and philosophical meditation.

Epictetus is an inspiring and resilient figure. By understanding him and his philosophy, you learn how to persist no matter what life throws at you and how to fulfill your roles with excellence.

Let’s introduce Epictetus today.

Epictetus was born a slave in present-day Turkey around 50 AD. He was born shortly before the Stoic philosopher Seneca died and died himself shortly after the Stoic emperor Marcus Aurelius was born.

The name Epictetus literally translates into acquired.

Fortunately, one of Epictetus’ owners allowed him to study with the Stoic philosopher Musonius Rufus. 

The ancient Greeks and Romans had a complicated relationship with slavery. Individuals identified as promising were often trained to take on great responsibilities. Once, one of the richest men in Athens was a slave. If someone was fortunate enough to be purchased by a wealthy owner, they may be allowed to live a more prosperous life than many poorer Romans or Greeks –  and perhaps he would eventually be freed. 

However, most slaves were not that fortunate. Even Epictetus, who had at least one good owner, was reportedly lame, due to an injury to his leg that he may have sustained while enslaved.

Epictetus was eventually freed. With his education, he became a philosopher.

When Epictetus was an adult, in 89 AD Emperor Domitian banished philosophers from Rome. 

So Epictetus left for Nicopolis in Greece and founded a school of Stoic philosophy. This school trained young men in philosophy, war, and politics.

His teachings were transcribed by one of his students, Arrian. Arrian himself lived an accomplished life, playing the roles of historian, military commander, and philosopher. He popularized and recorded Epictetus’s teachings in a form of the handbook or Enchiridion and the Discourses. 

Despite enslavement, exile, and the contingencies of history, Epictetus’s thought survived into the modern age.

Epictetus was above all a teacher. Someone who exhorted his students to be as good as possible.

He is direct. For this reason, he is both inspiring and harsh. For instance, here he is accosting his students:

“How long are you going to wait before you demand the best for yourself and in no instance bypass the discriminations of reason? You have been given the principles that you ought to endorse, and you have endorsed them. What kind of teacher, then, are you still waiting for in order to refer your self-improvement to him?”

His work combines the importance of theory and practice.

In his *Discourses,* he satirically criticizes those who spend all their time on logic puzzles:

“Come and listen to me read my commentaries... I will explain Chrysippus to you like no one else can, and I'll provide a complete analysis of his entire text ... If necessary, I can even add the views of Antipater and Archemdemos... So it's for this, is it, that young men are to leave their fatherlands and their own parents; to come and listen to you explain words? Trifling little words?”  
id: 1752

Yet Epictetus himself is well versed in theory. He knew that both were important and included difficult theoretical texts in his curriculum. 

Remember that as you go throughout this course. Understand the ideas deeply, explore and challenge them, and put the best ones into practice.

Let’s end with a line from Epictetus:

"Don't explain your philosophy. Embody it."  
id: 254

# DoC

## Theory

So much of our life is spent trying to control things that are not up to us and failing to take responsibility for what is.

The sobriety organization Alcoholics Anonymous is well known for using the serenity prayer in their meetings:

“God grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and wisdom to know the difference.”  
id: 1689

This prayer resembles the beginning of Epictetus’ handbook:

Some things are within our power, while others are not. Within our power are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever is of our own doing; not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not of our own doing.  
id: 806

Epictetus saw the world as a force that constantly asks us questions. Every scenario or situation we’re placed in is asking us something. How we judge and decide is our answer.

The situations and questions we face are not up to us. Our choices are.

It’s important to say that Epictetus does not strictly speaking use the word “control” to describe this idea. Instead, he talks about what is up to us. He talks about what is “our own” or “within our power”.

To better understand this, think of a simple diagram of an input, decider, and output. Input comes into the decider and then the decider then sends out output.

Imagine the circumstances the world throws at us as input. Picture us as the receiver. The world appears to us as input. The results of our decision and judgments are the output. 

To make this more concrete, in 1983 Stanislov Petrov, an officer of the Soviet Air Defense Forces, found himself staring at an early warning radar that reported a US missile launch. The missles were headed towards the Soviet Union. At that moment, Stanislov had to decide whether the input, the message of missiles from his rader, was reliable or not. If he reported it to his superiors it would almost surely escalate into a nuclear retaliation. 

He didn’t escalate. Later, it was determined that his machine had malfunctioned. This single decision was one of the most momentous ever made. 

In this situation, he received input that he needed to act on. The results of his decisions are the output. 

Epictetus says that we should focus our attention on the decider in the center. Like Stanislov Petrov we should carefully asses what is true and act on that. We shouldn’t spend as much time trying to control everything else.

In this world, even our body isn’t up to us. Ultimately, we cannot prevent it from malfunctioning. History is full of people who lived healthy lives only to descend into illness due to biological accident or genetic inheritance.

The external world is something we can influence through our decisions, but it is not ultimately our own. Anything can thwart our external plans or actions. Our body will not always obey our decisions, this is especially true when it begins to age.

What is our own is our judgments and decisions. We have the power to shape ourselves into the people we want to be. There’s a message of hope, optimism, and responsibility there.

Consider this as you go throughout your day. You may not be thrown into situations as intense as Stanislov Petrov, but, like him, you can do the best you can with what you’re given.

Let’s end with a line from the slave philosopher:

"Don’t let the force of the impression when first it hits you knock you off your feet; just say to it, ‘Hold on a moment; let me see who you are and what you represent. Let me put you to the test.’"  
id: 686

# Poker

## Theory

Life is like a game of chance. 

Take poker for example. Poker is interesting because there are optimal strategies – but even when someone plays as best as possible, there’s some chance of losing. Players aren’t responsible for the cards they dealt, only how they play them. 

Likewise in life. We are responsible for what we do with what we’re given. But we cannot choose what is external to us. We’re shaped by biological and social forces that are out of our hands.

A poker player aims to act as someone who does the right thing. They don’t feel bad when they realize that folding a bad hand could have paid off. If they never fold bad hands then they’ll sometimes get lucky and win big. But most of the time, the bad hand will not pay off. So why play it? The best players focus on the quality of their decisions, not short-term outcomes.

Stoicism ultimately focuses on dispositions, that is what we are disposed to do because of our character.

We want to be the kind of person that does the right thing, just like the poker player aims to form the disposition to make the strategically optimal play. This is something we can do no matter our circumstances.

One can see how Stoicism would have been appealing to a slave. It offers the promise of a good life as long as acts with good character. This is something that is attainable for everyone. 

This doesn't mean that one should passively accept one’s lot. Obviously, fighting for one’s rights is important. Often the best thing to do is to stand up for what’s right and refuse to be exploited by others, even in the face of serious consequences. 

That’s our responsibility. How the world responds is out of our hands.

Let’s end with a line from Epictetus on this theme:

“How then shall a man preserve firmness and tranquillity, and at the same time be careful and neither rash nor negligent? If he imitates those who play at dice. The counters are indifferent; the dice are indifferent. How do I know what the cast will be? But to use carefully and dexterously the cast of the dice, this is my business.[2](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0236%3Atext%3Ddisc%3Abook%3D2%3Achapter%3D5#note2) Thus then in life also the chief business is this: distinguish and separate things, and say, Externals are not in my power: will is in my power. Where shall I seek the good and the bad? Within, in the things which are my own.”  
id: 1751

# Training Program: Desire

## Theory

Epictetus talks about how Stoicism can be divided into three training programs: the discipline of desire, action, and judgement. The French philosopher Pierre Hadot describes them as follows:

“The discipline of desire essentially consists in placing oneself within the context of the cosmic All, and in becoming aware of human existence as being a part, one that must conform to the will of the Whole… The discipline of action consists essentially in placing oneself within the context of human society; this entails acting in conformity with the Reason which all human beings have in common.. Finally, the discipline of judgement consists in allowing oneself to be guided by the logical necessity which is imposed upon us by Reason”

These are deep disciplines. We will explore them all in this series and throughout the Stoa app.

With Pierre Hadot’s description in mind, let’s consider the three disciplines again. First, the discipline of desire is limiting our will to what is up to us. The discipline of action is about treating others and ourselves well. The discipline of judgment is about perfecting our ability to examine our impressions. All of these come together into a project to perfect our *hegemonikon* or our ruling faculty. It’s our nature as rational beings that grounds all three disciplines in what the Stoics call *logos* or Reason.

As rational and social beings, our purpose is to pursue knowledge and act in a prosocial manner. This means making excellent decisions and judgments. Our nature is fully realized when we see reality for what it is, align our desires with what is possible, and act with virtue.

To approach this goal, let’s focus on the first part of Epictetus’s training program: the discipline of desire.

This aspect of Stoicism is all about aligning our desires with what is under our control.  We should seek what is good and cultivate aversion to vice.

Epictetus writes:

\>\[A philosopher\] must adapt his own will to events in such a way that, among all events which occur, there may be none which occur when he did not want them to occur, and that, of all events which do not occur, there may be none which does not occur when he wanted it to happen. 

id: 1750

So much frustration in our lives is caused by failing to align our desires with the world or within ourselves. 

Often we want things that are not up to us. When we unrealistically dream of success, health, or simply feeling good, we are bound to be frustrated. In each case, we fail to adapt our will to the world. These desires are grounded in false beliefs. Often they’re based on an unrealistic optimism. When we lose control after being mistreated, we are expecting to live in a world where we are always treated well. But such a world is fantasy.

Yet such things will happen regardless of what we do or how we feel about them.

Much suffering also derives from our conflicted will. This may be most obvious in the case of an addict. They want to use or do whatever they are addicted to. Yet they also desire to stop. An addict is a paradigmatic and extreme case, but we all have conflicting desires within ourselves.

An example illustrating this is offered by Epictetus is a story of a chamber pot.

A student asks what he should do if someone instructs him to hold the chamber pot. This was seen as a demeaning activity.

Epictetus instructed that he do the following: rebel or simply hold the chamber pot. Whatever he does he must not choose one route and wish that he had chosen the other. 

If he rebels, he must face the consequences of that decision. If he doesn’t, he must do the very same. 

Behind the question of the student is the desire that the external world is otherwise. But he cannot have the best of both worlds. To be upset at that fact is silly.

Practicing the discipline of desire concerns aligning our will with reality. All we need to do is assess the situations that life throws our way and then decide what to do. If any new information arises, then we can decide to change. We do not need to waste our thoughts on unnecessary value judgments or the wish that things be other than what they are.

Let’s end with a line from the English novelist and poet, Rudyard Kipling:

“If you don't get what you want, it's a sign either that you did not seriously want it, or that you tried to bargain over the price.”

# Training Program: Action

## Theory

Previously we introduced the first step of Epictetus’s training program: the discipline of desire. It’s all about limiting our desires to what is up to us.

For the Stoics, we have control only of our decisions and judgments. Everything else is external.

So, we ought to align our wants with the world and within ourselves. The ultimate way to do this is to focus on what we can control: our decisions and judgments.

The discipline of action is all about choosing the best actions.

Throughout Epictetus’s writings, he encourages us, like a demanding coach, to always act well and demand the best of us.

But how do you decide how to act?

Epictetus reframes this question. Too much self-help and philosophy is focused on what one should do, and not enough is focused on who one should become. It is more important to ask who you would be, then what you would do in a single situation.

To answer the question, of who should I be, Epictetus taught the philosophy of role ethics. 

The idea of role ethics is that we each have particular roles, both natural and acquired. These roles provide guides for who we should be.

First, we have our natural role as human beings. We’re fundamentally rational and social creatures. As such, our primary role is to pursue knowledge and choose to live well with others. This is fundamentally what we ought to do. Any act which is done out of ignorance or which cruelly harms others should not be considered. Because these acts contradict our fundamental role.

Then, we have various social roles. We are children, parents, employees, neighbors, students, citizens, and on and on. The correct action is often given by the role we’re occupying at the moment. Simply ask, what would the model neighbor, child, or citizen do in this situation? Often the answer is simple. 

As Epictetus said:  
“First, say to yourself what you would be; and then do what you have to do. For in almost everything we see this to be the practice.”  
id: 1749

What do you do when these demands contradict? 

Many of us run up against apparent conflicts between career and family.

One should not use the potential conflict between two goals as an excuse. People who take their careers seriously may mistreat their family. They may not be completely present or may snap at them. But such things are not forced on them just because they prioritize their career. One can treat family well in each moment while placing a large focus on work. One should be ambitious.

Of course, this isn’t to say that there aren’t any tradeoffs to make. Hard conversations about prioritization need to happen.

The crucial question is, if you had to make the two roles work – maybe because someone was holding a gun to your head – could you? 

Often, the answer is yes.

Epictetus encouraged us to do our best:

"When faced with anything painful or pleasurable, anything bringing glory or disrepute, realize that the crisis is now, that the Olympics have started, and waiting is no longer an option; that the chance for progress, to keep or lose, turns on the events of a single day."  
id: 845

# Training Program: judgment

## Theory

We’ve covered the first two pillars of Epictetus’s training program: desire and action.

The final one is the discipline of judgment.

The discipline of judgment is about seeing the world as it is, without adding unnecessary value judgments.

To understand this, one must internalize the Stoic theory of psychology.

In a nutshell, it is captured by the line:

"Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by the opinions about the things;."  
id: 241

The Stoics believed that we were presented with an initial sensation. We then form judgments, including strong value judgments, in response to that representation.

For instance, you may experience the beginning of anger if someone clumsily pushes your back. Yet that feeling will not transform into anger if you realize it was a child who simply slipped into you, through no fault of their own. In this situation, you first experience sensations like the physical touch, the rush of feeling to the face, and initial feelings of anger in the chest. But once you see the child, you do not commit to making the value judgment that were unjustly wronged. So the feelings never transform into anger.  
   
We practice the discipline of judgment by refraining from agreeing to unnecessary value judgments.

Epictetus offers an example:

He was sent to jail. What happened? He was sent to jail.  But "He is unhappy" is added by oneself.  
id: 1743

Referring to our earlier example, we could say that we were pushed by the child. We should not make the additional judgment that we were harmed.

Sometimes false stories make us feel better. If we believe we’re wronged, we can lash out at someone. We no longer need to restrain ourselves. There’s a perverse way in which giving in to negative emotions can be pleasurable in the short term. 

In the long term, it’s obvious that remaining ignorant makes our life worse. If we construct a fantasy world, we will make poor choices in the real one.

Instead, if someone could fully internalize the discipline of judgment, they would be resilient and tranquil.

They’d be able to quickly see situations for what they were and make the right decisions. They wouldn’t get distracted by false narratives or stories we tell ourselves.

Epictetus encourages us to become this person:

"How long are you going to wait before you demand the best for yourself and in no instance bypass the discriminations of reason? You have been given the principles that you ought to endorse, and you have endorsed them. What kind of teacher, then, are you still waiting for in order to refer your self-improvement to him?"  
id: 1698

# Freedom

## Theory

Epictetus had a skill for stripping down Stoicism to its essentials.

What matters for philosophy is perfecting our decisions and beliefs. We should pursue knowledge and fulfill our roles, both natural and acquired, to the best of our ability.

Happiness depends on not being dependent on anything external. Truthfully, the Stoic cannot be constrained:

"‘But the tyrant will chain –’ What will he chain? Your leg. ‘He will chop off –’ What? Your head. What he will never chain or chop off is your integrity."  
id: 785

Many find unhappiness because they make themselves dependent on things outside of themselves. Some do this to avoid responsibility. Others simply do it out of ignorance. 

We become who we should be by molding our desires, habits, and thinking patterns.

This requires constant training and vigilance.

Over time, through practice, one becomes a free and tranquil person.

Epictetus tells the story of the model Stoic, Agrippinus:

"When Florus was deliberating whether he should appear at Nero's shows, taking part in the performance himself, Agrippinus replied, 'Appear by all means.' And when Florus inquired, 'But why do not you appear?' he answered, 'Because I do not even consider the question.' For the man who has once stooped to consider such questions, and to reckon up the value of external things, is not far from forgetting what manner of man he is."

Aggripnus became someone who doesn’t need to deliberate about doing the right thing.

To follow in the footsteps of people like that we must train.

As Epictetus says:

\>“They who have merely received bare maxims are presently inclined to throw them up, as a sick stomach does its food. Digest it, and then you will not throw it up; otherwise it will be crude and impure, and unfit for nourishment. But show us, from what you have digested, some change in your ruling faculty; as wrestlers do in their shoulders, from their exercise and their diet; as artificers, in their skill, from what they have learnt.” 

You know the virtuous by their works.

Summed up succinctly:

Don't explain your philosophy. Embody it.
