# Letter 15

## Quote

“Without philosophy the mind is sickly, and the body, too, though it may be very powerful, is strong only as that of a madman or a lunatic is strong.”

## Theory

Today, many obsess over their physical health. Doing this promotes a longer and more comfortable life. But it doesn’t necessarily make for a happier one. And shouldn’t that be the priority?

Seneca said that the health of the body is second to that of the mind. In his *Moral Letter* 15, he wrote:

“Without philosophy the mind is sickly, and the body, too, though it may be very powerful, is strong only as that of a madman or a lunatic is strong.”

Without a clear head, our lives are messy and stressful. It doesn’t matter how much external success someone has, if their internal world is torture then their lives are a hell. This is why Seneca says that the health that matters is the state of our minds.

A ready mind demands training and practice. It’s something you cultivate with careful attention and thoughtful decisions. You must monitor what you think and what you consume. Your environment shapes your mind. Choose well.

Seneca reminds his readers that philosophy requires practice. He says:

“The mind must be exercised both day and night, for it is nourished by moderate labour; and this form of exercise need not be hampered by cold or hot weather, or even by old age. Cultivate that good which improves with the years.”

Just as you must exercise you body, you must train the mind. This is the project of a lifetime. It doesn’t matter who you are or what stage of life you are in.

But, how do you improve your mind? Through meditation, reading, conversation, listening, writing. Such work must be deliberate and intentional.

That’s what we aim to provide with Stoa – a tool to help you practice philosophy. By actively listening to and meditating on Stoic philosophy you can transform yourself. Stoa is one piece of a larger system. As Seneca said: the mind must be exercised day and night.

Train your soul throughout your day. Return to philosophical quotes, think about your life and world deeply, and discourse with others. Such things put you on the same path as Stoics hundreds of years ago.

In Moral Letters 15 Seneca provides an example of a philosophical quote we can meditate on:

“The fool’s life is empty of gratitude and full of fears; its course lies wholly toward the future.”

Even if someone has external success, if they are ungrateful and constantly anxious about the future, we would not want to be them. They are not living well. To live well, do the opposite of the fool. Focus on the internal, not the internal. Cultivate joy and equanimity. 

Don’t stop training the mind. Meditate on philosophy and sit with the words of the Stoics. 

Good job doing that today.

# Letter 16

## Quote

“You must persevere, must develop new strength by continuous study, until that which is only a good inclination becomes a good settled purpose.”

## Theory

Philosophy is about creating habits of mind. By changing our mind we change our life.

In *Moral Letters* 16 Seneca writes to Lucilius:

“You must persevere, must develop new strength by continuous study, until that which is only a good inclination becomes a good settled purpose.”

In other words, once you understand the precepts of philosophy you must transform them into habits. Philosophy must become the air you breathe. 

We’ve seen a familiar pattern in Stoic practice. Many come to philosophy to solve a problem. They practice it in order to fix the struggles in their life or use it to overcome obstacles. But once they make progress on those, if they continue training, they practice philosophy because it is who they are. Philosophy has become a part of them. They were brought to it by external factors, but continue in their ways due to their own internal motivation.

Someone who has reached that level lives naturally, with skill no matter the situation.

On the other hand, people who live without a firm philosophy are slaves to their own desires or the whims of others. That is not a recipe for a good life.

Seneca approvingly quotes the philosopher Epicurus:

“If you live according to nature, you will never be poor; if you live according to opinion, you will never be rich.”

Living according to nature means simply thinking and acting well. It means acting with virtue: courage, discipline, self-control, and wisdom. Fulfilling our everyday roles with skill.

Another modern Stoic put it this way: you must live in accordance with the facts. A happy life is not found in illusion and fantasy.

Without grounding your life in reality it becomes unrooted and aimless. Your life will be governed by desires for material and social goods implanted by other people. Letting the opinions of others run your life is slavery. 

How do you know whether you are aligned to nature or opinion?

Ask yourself whether you are happy and you may find the answer. 

Seneca suggests a simple rule we can use to distinguish the two. If you thought a new career, relationship, or success would bring you happiness, but now that you have those things find yourself wanting more – that’s evidence that your life is led by opinion. Because other people’s desires, judgements, and actions, whether they intend to or not, often lead us to becoming unsatisfied and unhappy. 

Living and thinking well is enough. It doesn’t need anything else.

As Seneca says:

“Natural desires are limited; but those which spring from false opinion can have no stopping-point. The false has no limits. When you are travelling on a road, there must be an end; but when astray, your wanderings are limitless. Recall your steps, therefore, from idle things, and when you would know whether that which you seek is based upon a natural or upon a misleading desire, consider whether it can stop at any definite point. If you find, after having travelled far, that there is a more distant goal always in view, you may be sure that this condition is contrary to nature.”

Returning to the original theme, this is a habit of mind. Real satisfaction, calm, and tranquility must come from within. 

Reflect on this today. Remove limitless desires from your life. Travel on the path that takes you to a life well lived.

# Letter 17

## Quote

“If you wish to have leisure for your mind, either be a poor man, or resemble a poor man.”

## Theory

Today, let’s talk about the dangers of wealth.

Seneca said:

“If you wish to have leisure for your mind, either be a poor man, or resemble a poor man.”

The pursuit of prosperity comes with serious risks. The path towards financial success is full of snares.

There’s something rich about this coming from Seneca. He was one of the wealthiest men in the Roman empire. 

But don’t forget that he offered all of his riches to Nero in an attempt to extract himself from politics. He didn’t think it was all important. People don’t talk about that fact of Seneca as much.

Many seek freedom through wealth. They want “F U Money” – money that gives them the power to work how they want, with who they want, doing what they want.

But do you need it? If you require F U Money to be free – there’s the concern that material objects have already come to own you. Why can’t you be free now? Is more money in your bank account really holding you back from being who you want to be? If you dedicate yourself to the venture of becoming rich –  are you putting of the project of becoming excellent?

That must come first. It’s that which Seneca says is all important. 

Seneca was exiled several times in his life. He used these periods to write plays and philosophy. Social alienation gave him the ability to pursue leisure. 

He didn’t always like it. And he fought to return to the action of Rome. But despite that, he used his time well. 

Poverty gives us the time to pursue leisure.

Today, when we think about leisure we often associate it with entertainment. During our downtime, we consume TV, algorithmic slop, and digital junk. But classical leisure was about spending time with great works and great people. That’s what Seneca is calling us to do.

A career driven by the craving of power and wealth will crowd this out. 

This is why many find it better to take a pay cut in order to work on their calling, carve out more time for family, and add more leisure to their life. Excellence before riches.

Of course, there’s a balance. Some people’s calling restricts leisure – think of the Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius for example. He was meant to be a king, not someone whose life leaves more room for leisure, like an academic philosopher or family man. But most of us don’t have that clear cut of a role in life. Our roles also change.

Some seasons give us little free time to develop ourselves. Some deadlines are worth meeting. Perhaps early parenting won’t give you the time to read as much as you used to. That’s OK.

Free time itself has its own risks. Perhaps it’s better to be working and doing something prosocial than to waste time on the couch. 

But if you use your time well – that’s invaluable. 

Don’t let the siren call of success distract you from that. Becoming happy matters more than becoming rich, powerful, or successful.

# Letter 18

## Quote Set aside a certain number of days, during which you shall be content with the scantiest and cheapest fare, with coarse and rough dress, saying to yourself the while: “Is this the condition that I feared?

## Theory

In Seneca’s *Moral Letter* 18 he wrote simple advice: go without for a few days. Give yourself the challenge of voluntary discomfort.

Did he know this simple line, by crystalizing Stoic practice, would influence the lives millions of people? Today, voluntary discomfort is one of the most well-known and practiced Stoic techniques.

The Stoic philosopher wrote:

“Set aside a certain number of days, during which you shall be content with the scantiest and cheapest fare, with coarse and rough dress, saying to yourself the while: “Is this the condition that I feared?””

Seneca urges his Roman friend to go with worse clothing and food than he is used to. Today, modern Stoics fast, go with cool showers, and put themselves in uncomfortable social situations.

Let’s understand why Seneca says you should do this.

First, by choosing hardship you cultivate the ability to take pleasure in the small things. He says:

“Let the pallet be a real one, and the coarse cloak; let the bread be hard and grimy. Endure all this for three or four days at a time, sometimes for more, so that it may be a test of yourself instead of a mere hobby. Then, I assure you, you will leap for joy when filled with a pennyworth of food, and you will understand that a man’s peace of mind does not depend upon Fortune; for, even when angry she grants enough for our needs.”

Second, more importantly, through this practice you recognize that your happiness does not depend on fortune. Whether or not you have a good day does not depend on your food, showers, or any other material thing. What matters is how you respond. It’s your decision making and state of mind that provide happiness, not external things.

It’s essential to keep this insight in mind when practicing voluntary discomfort. To know it and feel it in your bones. If you do this you will become intimate with adversity and life will never catch you off your guard. 

Before you let this practice get to your head Seneca also warns:

“There is no reason, however, why you should think that you are doing anything great; for you will merely be doing what many thousands of slaves and many thousands of poor men are doing every day.”

Use this as motivation. Whatever discomfort you choose to undergo is something many have faced before. There is nothing intrinsically special about it. Voluntary hardship is not for showing off. It won’t matter unless you convert discomfort into transformation. That’s what the best people have done. You can follow in their footsteps.

## Quote

## Theory

Excellent work continuing your Stoic practice. In this series, we’ve been covering letters from Seneca. Today’s letter, letter 19, Seneca discusses the issue of retirement with a successful Roman. This man has won honors, office, and wealth. He may be reluctant to leave it behind. Today, you can think of the academic who is struggling to face the reality of their mental decline, or the elderly businessman who may not want to let go of their business. More generally, you can think of times where you don’t feel ready to let go – even though the world is pushing you that way.

How do you do it?

For every success, there is a cost. Seneca reminds his friend:

“There’s thunder even on the loftiest peaks.”

Do not forget this when you’ve attained great heights nor when you are looking up at them. Whether you’re in a place of success now or craving to get there, there will be trouble, discontentment, and adversity of every kind in your future. What matters is who you are when you arrive. Not where you find yourself.

Seneca reminds his rich friend of this and also the simple point – the best things in life can be found in any station. He approvingly quotes the philosopher Epicurus:

“You must reflect carefully beforehand with whom you are to eat and drink, rather than what you are to eat and drink. For a dinner of meats without the company of a friend is like the life of a lion or a wolf.”

Friends make life worth living. It doesn’t matter whether you’re working or retired. It’s the bonds we make with others that bring joy. Seneca’s letters are a great expression of this.

Great relationships require attention and skill. We must find people worth being friends with but also be people worth befriending. 

Today, remember this message. Find joy in what you have. Whether it’s your relationships or the ordinary blessings of life. And be excellent – be worthy of befriending.

After all, everything is temporary. Our success will fade into obscurity. As Marcus Aurelius quipped:

"All things of the body stream away like a river, all things of the mind are dreams and delusion; life is warfare, and a visit to a strange land; the only lasting fame is oblivion."

# Letter 20

## Quote

What is wisdom? Always desiring the same things, and always refusing the same things.

## Theory

A goal for the Stoic is consistency. 

Seneca writes in his 20th *Moral Letter*:

“This, I say, is the highest duty and the highest proof of wisdom,—that deed and word should be in accord, that a man should be equal to himself under all conditions, and always the same.”

This is a high standard. Let your thoughts, words, and actions align. Do not lose yourself in the company of others or when the going gets tough. 

If you could live life like this you would be satisfied. Without the remorse that acting inauthentically accuses. Avoiding the feeling that bent when you shouldn’t have bent. Or the regret that happens when you act hypocritically.

In fact, living consistently brings tranquility and calm. If you’re able to achieve it, you know who you are and know what you want. Your behavior doesn’t depend on the people who surround you or the events that are happening. 

So how do you do it?

Seneca advises starting with what’s in front of you.

Do your clothes communicate who you are? How about how you spend money? Does your behavior change when you're with one friend but not another?

As someone else once said, the small things are the big things. 

Don’t just talk about Stoicism. Put into practice in all areas of your life. Act with consistency when it comes to the ordinary and exceptional things of life: fashion, diet, art, career, relationships.

Many people act inconsistently simply because they are buffeted about by the winds of fortune. They didn't have strong desires to begin with so they let other people tell them what to want. They let other people run their life – in big and small ways because they don’t possess an enduring target.

You need to desire enduring happiness. You must want to be excellent and have a clear picture of what that means for you. Cultivate resolve. If you do, you will act accordingly.

Notice whenever you stray from the path and get back on it. Take pride after you stick with the course for longer and longer periods of time.

This way of life places happiness in your hands. After all, whether or not you possess a coherent character is up to you. There’s an optimism in that. You can take steps forward. Nothing can stop you, except yourself. Remain true to yourself. Show a unity of thought and action. Live with integrity.

Let’s end with Seneca’s wisdom:

# Letter 21

## Quote

If you wish to make Pythocles rich, do not add to his store of money, but subtract from his desires.

## Theory

Seneca writes to Lucilius:

“Your greatest difficulty is with yourself; for you are your own stumbling-block. You do not know what you want. You are better at approving the right course than at following it out. You see where the true happiness lies, but you have not the courage to attain it.”

Those are surely words anyone can relate to. 

We get in our own way. Our haphazard and purposeless actions keep us off a better philosophical path. We can see that there’s a better way of life, but we don’t follow it.

The Stoic philosopher, Epictetus, would say that our beliefs are weak. In some sense, we know it would be better if we lived differently, but we haven’t fully internalized that. We forget it. We make excuses that chip away at our resolve. We say things like “I will quit tomorrow, when it’s easier” or “I’ll just do wrong just this one – after all it’s no big deal” or “I know I set a rule not to do this, but this time is different.”

How do we strengthen our beliefs? Both Seneca and Epictetus advise targeting desires that take us off the path. Perhaps it is these that prevent us from obtaining knowledge.

This is why Seneca approvingly quotes the maxim: 

“If you wish to make Pythocles rich, do not add to his store of money, but subtract from his desires.”

He also notes that this applies to more than just wealth.

“If you wish to make Pythocles honourable, do not add to his honours, but subtract from his desires”

 “If you wish Pythocles to have pleasure for ever, do not add to his pleasures, but subtract from his desires” 

In each case we can target cravings for for status, pleasure, wealth, comfort – whatever it is. Let them cease. Think and act as if they do not matter to you. Do not let them rule your life. 

After all, it’s wants that make a slave out of people. They literally govern their lives, preventing them from living authentically and freely.

Defeat desires that take you away from a happy life today. Use that progress to summon courage to be happy.
