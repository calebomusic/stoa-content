1. # Simplify Stoicism

## Theory

Welcome to Stoa, in this course. We'll cover Musonius Rufus, the Stoic philosopher. He was the teacher of Epictetus. 

We're fortunate to have a set of his lectures. What this course will do is go through and pick out the most important lessons which I hope you can use to live a more resilient and virtuous life. Musonius Rufus was called the Roman Socrates. He was immensely influential to the Roman Stoics and as such helped his students and peers live better.

Lecture one: one does not need many arguments. 

One does not need many arguments to prove a point.

As Musonius Rufus once said:

“​​It is not the doctor who brings many drugs to sick people who deserves praise, but rather the one who helps them in a noteworthy manner with the few drugs which he prescribes.”

Musonius Rufus is highlighting this fact to his Roman philosophy students. This kind of person is prone to debating and disputing argument after argument. But as the Stoic Aristo of Chios said:

“Dialectic reasonings are like cobwebs—artificially constructed, but otherwise useless.”

So there’s one lesson – don’t waste time on many words when only a few are needed. 

There are deeper lessons here too.

First, there’s a psychological fact. The search for an argument is not always motivated by truth.

Often looking for more evidence may simply postpone a decision. Sometimes when we’re hunting for more information we’re simply procrastinating. At other times, we may be rationalizing – trying to convince ourselves or others, when, in some sense, we already know what is true.

Second, there’s an epistemic fact. Most arguments are not very good. If you’re thinking of making a big decision you may write up a pro con list – perhaps with tens of items. Only a handful of those points are actually important. Our job is to locate which ones those arguments are. Everything else is noise.

So, what’s an example of a good argument? Musonius Rufus gives one:

“if we take the well-known premise that every good is desirable and if we add to this the second well-known premise that some pleasures are not desirable, we show that pleasure is not a good-we show the unfamiliar through the familiar.”

Taking pleasure in our own failure or the suffering of others is not admirable. So, pleasure cannot be intrinsically good. 

That kind of clear and simple reasoning ought to be what we’re after. And then we must act.   
After all as Musonius says:  
“Only by exhibiting actions in harmony with the sound words which he has received will anyone be helped by philosophy.”

2. # You’re Good

   ## Theory

The Stoics believed that we have a natural capacity for goodness. All of us come into the world with an innate conception of the good. 

In Musonius Rufus’s words “the seed of virtue exists in each one of us.” We all have the ability to act virtuously. Everyone is naturally disposed to do what they believe is right.

There is a message of self-reliance here. We do not need to appeal to authorities to determine what is right. Though others help us learn the nature of the good, our capacity for acting well is not dependent on other people. There is also a message of equality. Everyone has an idea of the good. All of us have the capacity for virtue.

Of course, this does not mean everyone acts virtuously. Each of us has a capacity for music and medicine, but this doesn’t make all of us musicians or medics. Right now, I could try my best to perform a, but I would fail. I am ignorant of art.

If I wanted to become a surgeon, that would require training in medical theory and practice.

Living well requires the same training, and that’s Musonius’s key point:

“A person who has not studied letters, music, or sports does not say that he knows them. Nor does he pretend to possess these skills if he is unable to name also the teacher to whom he went. So why, by the gods, do we all declare that we have virtue? A human being has no claim by nature to any of those other skills, and no one comes into life with a natural ability for them.”

Through philosophy we determine what is good and through practice we train in applying it. This is the pursuit of wisdom. This is the project of the philosopher.

And focus on a single area where you can train more. No one comes into the world as a sage. We each need to train to act well. Choose one area in your life to focus on and improve.

3. # Everyone Should Study Philosophy

## Theory

Should everyone study philosophy?

That brings up the question, what is philosophy – simply the study of how to live well. We’re not concerned with merely theoretical or academic studies, but serious inquiry into the good life. As Musonius Rufus said: 

“Just as there is no use in medical study unless it leads to the health of the human body, so there is no use to a philosophical doctrine unless it leads to the virtue of the human soul.”

All humans naturally possess reason. In the words of Musonius Rufus we each have “received from the gods the same reasoning power.”

Everyone has an interest in philosophy, because everyone has an interest in living well. Each person is capable of philosophizing because of their command of reason. Because each person has an interest in and is capable of philosophy, philosophy is for everyone.

This is a simple argument for equality. But it’s one that has evaded people throughout history. Too many have ignored the reality of others or conjured fantasies to justify special treatment. All of us feel the drive to be special – unfortunately that sometimes means we shut others out.

Even the ancient Stoics, who were early to understand equality in the abstract didn’t fully comprehend the import of their ideas. They let slaves and women partake in philosophy, but left many social roles of ancient Roman society unquestioned.

This highlights the question – what simple ideas are we not fully realizing the import of today? In our personal lives, have we adequately internalized the meaning of equality?

Let’s end with a line from Marcus Aurelius:

"When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: the people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous and surly. They are like this because they can't tell good from evil. But I have seen the beauty of good, and the ugliness of evil, and have recognized that the wrongdoer has a nature related to my own \- not of the same blood and birth, but the same mind, and possessing a share of the divine. And so none of them can hurt me."

Let’s now inquire into the idea of equality. It’s a complex one. For the Stoics, humans were equal in virtue of our capability to reason. How would you define the idea? Contemplate this question now. 

For some people, realizing equality requires taking other’s needs more seriously. For others, it requires taking their own needs more seriously. We each possess the divine spark of reason. Consider how you can realize the idea of equality in your life now.

4. # Educate Yourself

## Theory

What does the philosophically educated person look like?

Perhaps a brilliant professor comes to mind. Someone who is exceptionally well read and thoughtful.

Or perhaps an excellent psychotherapist arises instead. Someone who knows ancient and modern wisdom and teaches it to others.

Musonius Rufus described the educated person more simply. He said that philosophy is:

“Nothing but the practice of noble behavior.”

The brilliant professor and psychotherapist may be philosophically educated. But so can someone who has never heard of ancient philosophy. 

Sure, they may have never heard of Musonius Rufus or be able to read ancient Greek. Nor do they know techniques from cognitive behavioral therapy. 

But they can still be who they were meant to be. They may be educated enough to live well. What does such a person look like?

“The person educated correctly, whoever it be, whether male or female, must become accustomed to endure toil, to not fear death, and to not become dejected in the face of any misfortune.”

We all face struggle, mortality, and tragedy in our lives. It is in those times that we show what lessons we’ve learned. Whether they’re learned from the Stoics, religion, our family, or on our own, doesn’t matter. 

Remember Stoicism’s egalitarian nature – the good is available to all. Use this as motivation today.

5. # Practice vs Theory

## Theory

What is more important: practice or theory?

Suppose you are about to have an important surgery performed. You could go with a fast and smooth talking surgeon who talks eloquently and in detail about his art. However, it turns out it would be his first surgery. Or you could choose another doctor who doesn’t say much, but has a record of a hundred successful surgeries.

You’d choose the person with the track record of being correct.

Thomas Carlyle developed a detailed philosophy of clothing in *Sartor Resartus*, but that didn’t stop him from being described as one of the “worst-dressed men in Victorian England.”

Just because you talk about a craft doesn’t mean you can practice it.

Hence the Roman Stoic Musonius Rufus asked rhetorically:

“Isn't being self-controlled and prudent about all one's actions much better than being able to say what is involved in prudence or self-mastery?"

And his student Epictetus would put it even more pithily:

“Don’t talk about your philosophy, embody it.”

So what’s the point of theory? Even though Stoics emphasize practice, theory is not ignored.

Theory teaches us how to act. It’s not possible to hit the target unless you know its location and the required method. Stoic theory provides a picture of the good life and techniques for how to get there. That’s its role. 

Great performers may spend most of their time in practice. But they need spend time reflecting on their past performance and current techniques in order to perfect their craft. In order to live better, we must do the same.

So, theory is required for living well. Nonetheless, never forget practice.

As Musonius Rufus says:

“But as a matter of fact, practice is more important than theory because it more effectively leads humans to actions than theory does.”

Taking time to train Stoic techniques in meditation or writing makes it easier to exhibit virtue throughout the day. Success breeds success.

6. # Philosophy is a skill

## Theory

Philosophy is the skill of living well. Musonius Rufus said that philosophy was: 

“Nothing but the practice of noble behavior.”

Like any other skill, we can train in our ability to act well. 

Everyone is capable of doing this. No magical abilities are required to attain a good life.

That’s the good. The bad news is that there’s nothing harder. Stoicism should challenge us to our core.

For the Stoics, a happy life is living according to Nature. We’re rational and social beings. Living well means pursuing knowledge. It does not mean pursuing pleasure or wealth while avoiding pain and poverty.

Society and biology pushes us to chase pleasure. As Musonius Rufus writes:

“Nevertheless, even though we have heard these ideas, because of the corruption which has been ingrained in us all the way from childhood and because of the wicked behavior caused by this corruption, we think it a bad thing when pain comes on us, and we think it a good thing when pleasure comes.”

Each of us is corrupted by the world, others, and ourselves. Philosophy requires nothing more than an act of self-transformation.

This is a tall order.

So, you can take pride in any progress you have made so far. Change is possible. And with each step forward, it becomes easier to take several more.

Today, remind yourself what you fundamentally value. 

Ignore pleasure and pain – they will come of their own accord. Focus on what truly matters. We’ll do a praemeditatio malorum exercise today to practice this. We’ll imagine facing adversity and remaining steadfast.

Good luck.

# 7\. Don’t Be Put To Shame

## Theory

Addicts will go to great lengths to satisfy their desires. 

Any fiend will suddenly gain 25 IQ points as soon as they need to find their next fix.

There’s a real sense in which they give the project of finding the next hit their all – no matter how painful it is.

Can you truly say you’re working that hard to be virtuous?

The Stoic teacher Musonius Rufus puts the challenge to us. 

“Is it not amazing that they would put up with all this on account of dishonorable things, but that we to gain a noble character, to escape the wickedness that ruins our lives, and to acquire virtue which provides for all good things-would not readily withstand every pain?”

He also notes how animals endure pain:

“One might with reason consider some animals' ability to endure pain, and this ought to encourage us to do the same.”

The addict is willing to suffer to get high, but you won’t suffer to live the good life? A mere beast will risk its life to get what it wants, but you won’t take risks to live meaningfully?

Don’t let drug addicts and animals put you to shame.

# 8\. The Path To Success

## Theory

Should the successful study philosophy? Not necessarily.

Musonius Rufus asked a similar question – should a king study philosophy? A king – one ancient definition of success. A role that men died for.

Yet there is no guarantee that a king is happy. 

Kings are rich and powerful. But happy – maybe not. I’m reminded of the sword of Damocles. In this myth, a peasant gleefully swaps places with the king. Chuffed with his new role, the peasant nestles into the king’s throne – only to see that there’s a sword dangling above him. This sword hangs above all of us. Perhaps it is more clearly seen by the king.

Stripped bare of extraneous theory, philosophy is simply the study of how to be a good person. Musonius Rufus argues that a king would benefit from such study, just as much as a peasant, professor, or politician. 

A king must be virtuous. Any character building regimen worth its salt will include philosophy. As he says:

“Being fearless, undaunted, and bold-these are the products of courage. And how else could these become someone's qualities more effectively than if he would become firmly convinced that death and pain are not evils?”

By changing how we think, we can transform what we value. With new values, we become who we were meant to be. This is what philosophy enables.

So, should the successful study philosophy?

Behind this question is the very idea of success. Wealth, health, and friendship are nice, but they do not make one a good person. 

If your idea of the good is perverted, you must study philosophy to live well.

Yet the truly successful have no need for the study of philosophy – they already embody it’s lessons. A good person has become who they were meant to be. 

That is not most of us. With work, however, more of us will become successful. We can put away philosophical theory as we embody it in practice.

At first, we do what is good out of self-interest. And then we do what is good out of duty. Finally, we do the good because that is who we are. 

Most of us are at the first two stages. With work, we can approach the last stage – the stage where we embody the true, beautiful, and good.

# 9\. Thriving in Exile

## Theory

One of the worst fears for ancients was exile.

“The exile,” says Xenophon, “loses home, liberty, country, wife, and children.” When he dies, he does not have the right to be buried in the tomb of his family.

The Stoics meditated on exile because it was perceived as one of the worst things that can happen to an ancient Greek or Roman. It robbed someone of their family, community, and honor.

In such circumstances, the philosophers distinguished themselves by arguing that exile was not an evil.

Today, instead of exile, we face unemployment, estrangement, financial ruin, or discrimination.

The Stoics tell us that situations do not truly harm us either. 

Musonius Rufus gives several reasons that we can rely on in his lecture on whether exile is an evil.

One reason we can endure exile is that we can enlarge our identity. The early Greeks and Romans identified with their cities. To be banished would then provoke a crisis of identity. The exile would be forced to redefine who they were. But they did not need to tie themselves so closely to their city. As the Stoics said, everyone possesses the divine spark of reason. It doesn’t matter where they live.

The modern lesson here is that we need not tie our sense of identity to our job, community, or any other such trait. We can flourish in many lines of work. Wherever we are, we can meet other people like us.

Another reason. Instead of focusing on the negative aspects of such an experience, we can search for the good in them. The Stoic teacher noted that many exiles actually had health conditions cured because of their experience. Banishment to a mediterranean island isn’t always so bad. Even in more challenging circumstances, remember that many of our heroes faced exile. The Stoics brought to mind the mythic Odysseus who persevered through a  decade of torments and trials. Many of our heroes today have faced unemployment, shame, and stigma honorably. We can do the same.

Most importantly, exile does not prevent us from being good. As Musonius Rufus wrote of the exile:

“it does not deprive him of things that are truly good. The person in exile is not prevented from having courage, justice, self-control, wisdom, or any other virtue, just because he is in exile.”

Wherever you are, you can act with excellence. 

So keep those in mind when you face the prospect of stigma. Enlarge your identity, reframe your experience, and, while you can, be good.

# 10\. Live Without Revenge

## Theory 

Does the philosopher seek revenge?

Musonius Rufus said that the philosopher should never file a lawsuit against another.

“When they are whipped in public and revel in being whipped, Spartan boys make it clear that such things are neither shameful nor injurious. If a philosopher cannot scorn blows or jeering, he is useless, inasmuch as a philosopher must make it clear that he scorns even death.”

The Stoic Cato the Younger, through a mix of social intrigue, had his bride essentially taken from him. It’s a complicated story, but needless to say it was a painful and humiliating experience for him, to say nothing of the bride. He considered bringing a lawsuit against the agitator, but decided against it. The philosopher does not seek revenge.

The fundamental reason for this is that the philosopher is not harmed by others. Moreover, they realize that many do not have ill intent. They act out of ignorance. They do not know what they do.

For Roman aristocrats who strove to maintain their dignity at all costs, this may have been an unwelcome message. But, as Musonius points out, the sage does not need to defend their dignity – for no one can threaten them.

So the philosopher never seeks to defend their social standing. Does this mean that the philosopher always turns the other cheek. Not necessarily.

There’s an amusing story about Zeno, the founder of Stoicism:

“We are told that he was once chastising a slave for stealing, and when the latter pleaded that it was his fate to steal, ‘Yes, and to be beaten too,’ said Zeno.”

Social roles may require that misdeeds are punished. Stoics are not pushovers. The social order must be maintained. But the key is to focus not on revenge. The starting point is always questions like – what does character demand of me? What is my role here? It is never a focus on protecting reputation. That’s the concern of gossipers.

As Musonius said:

“A philosopher is, after all, inconsistent if he says that a good man could never be wronged by a bad man, but then, while claiming to be a good man, charges that he is wronged by bad men.”

## Meditation

Take a moment to prepare to meditate. 

Quickly remind yourself why you're doing this and commit to following through.

Let’s start by calmly rehearsing events and situations that may arise today. Do this in an objective way, like a scientist calmly assessing possibilities. 

Continue to consider events that may arise in an objective, dispassionate way.

Now bring to mind one way in which others may frustrate or slight you. Whether it’s an innocent annoyance, personality quirk, or personality flaw, bring it to mind. 

Now that you’ve thought of a scenario, rehearse it happening. Do this in detail. Maintain the calm and objective approach. 

Bring to mind what’s important in this situation. What do you need to do that matters?

And consider the idea that others cannot harm you. According to the Stoics, you are a choice making being. Others, in a deep sense, are incapable of hurting you. Only you can damage your inner citadel. With this in mind, picture yourself facing the situation with calm.

And bring your attention to the rest of the day. Who will you be? 

Finally, ask yourself whatever happens today, what ultimately matters?

Good work.

Take a few moments to wind down.

# 16\. Pay Attention

## Theory

When one of Musonius Rufus’s students asked, should children always obey their parents? He answered, no. Let’s see what we can learn from understanding why.

The Sage would always do the right thing. There is no guarantee that parents know what the right thing is. 

We would not always follow their medical advice, especially if they are not doctors, so why should we follow parents all things? He said this in a context where parental power, though decreasing, was exceptionally high. Remember that according to Roman paterfamilias a father owned everyone else in the household. He could have them killed at will\!

What it is to be a good son or daughter does not include always obeying one’s parents. 

But, how often should one’s parents be obeyed? 

Stoicism is a virtue ethic. Stoic ethics holds that one should do what the best person would. It adds to this picture the idea of roles – we can approach the question, who should we be, by considering our roles both natural and acquired. Some of our roles are natural: they are unchosen; while others are acquired: we voluntarily choose them. The role of child is a natural one.

So, what is a good child? How often do they follow their parents' wishes? Such a question is incredibly general. As soon as we begin to answer it, we learn the details matter.

Consider the example of your father giving medical advice. If your father is a doctor, perhaps it is better to follow his medical advice. But perhaps, though he is a doctor, your issue isn’t covered by his specialty. Maybe – looking at his track record – he’s not a very good one. 

Whatever you should do is determined by what the virtuous person would do and that includes carefully weighing all factors. Moreover, we haven’t determined who you are. In the case of the text, Musonius Rufus is considering what young Romans should do. We cannot answer the question about what a good child is in your case without knowing your age, culture, and relationship with your parents.

Only one general rule emerges from this: the factors of each specific situation matter. To know them, you must focus on knowledge and pay attention. Heuristics are useful. We must use rules of thumb. Yet, these will always be broken.

# 20\. Furnishing The House

## Theory

Musonius Rufus argued that his students overrated furnishing. He stated that he would rather be sick than live in luxury:

“Being sick harms the body only; living in luxury harms both soul and body, by making the body weak and powerless and the soul undisciplined and cowardly.”

​​There’s something to this. Though it’s not clear that the poor tend to be more virtuous than the rich. Poverty too includes many factors that can corrupt who we are.

Nonetheless, Musonius Rufus is clearly onto something.

He is presenting this message to Roman aristocrats. They’re the elite of his day. Rich and cultured, with all of their basic needs met.

Many of you may not be so fortunate.

At the same time, the material comfort we live in vastly exceeds the comfort of any Roman. The political scientist Vaclav Smil argues that the typical American has as much energy at their calling as a Roman commanding 200 slaves. Simple things like modern plumbing and electricity would have been magical luxuries for an ancient Roman.

If the typical man and woman of Rome was corrupted by the comfort of their lives, think about how much worse we have it. We may face much larger burdens of convenience than even them\!

How can you know when you’re leaning too much into comfort? Those who live in luxury no longer make sacrifices.

They do not give to their family, friends, or city. Musonius Rufus says:

“The person who lives luxuriously would also be entirely unjust, inasmuch as he would shrink from performing tasks he ought to undertake on behalf of his own city, if performing them meant abandoning his luxurious lifestyle.”

The poison of luxury is that it gives us something to hold onto – but that something is not ultimately good. It distracts from what is ultimately important.

We must sacrifice and be prepared to sacrifice for what really matters – courage, discipline, justice, and wisdom.

# 21\. Cutting Hair

## Theory

Roman philosophers occasionally argued over whether philosophers should have beards. It was a broader issue in Roman culture. Not having a beard was interpreted as effeminate. There wasn’t that much worse for a Roman male than being effeminate.

Musonius argued that men should not shave their beards:  
“Zeno, he said, was right: just as our hair grows in accordance with nature, so we should cut it in accordance with nature-when, for example, it gets too heavy or hinders our movements.”

What’s the lesson here? It can only be that it is too easy to add our own cultural preferences when it comes to interpreting nature.

Here is Musonius Rufus again:

“Clearly, such men have been broken by luxurious living and have become completely emasculated: they don't mind looking androgynous and womanlike, something real men would never tolerate.”

Another lesson here is a skeptical one. When Musonius Rufus makes claims we’re reminded that he hardly has a perfect track record.

That should cause each of us to go back and think through what we think is right. This isn’t an invitation to treat his Stoic teachings as a buffet – where we pick what we like and leave the rest. It’s a challenge to think through what is actually true. For we may hold beliefs that are tainted by our cultural milieu in much the same way. 

Perhaps it is us, not Musonius Rufus who have been misled.

Musonius Rufus said that philosophy is nothing more than noble behavior. This includes noble thought. Such thought doesn’t come for free.
