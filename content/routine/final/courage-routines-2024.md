# Courage

## Quote

"Bravery is not thoughtless rashness, or love of danger, or the courting of fear-inspiring objects; it is the knowledge which enables us to distinguish between that which is evil and that which is not."  
id: 1424

## Theory

Welcome to these routines on courage with Stoa.

Who comes to mind when you think of someone courageous?

Perhaps someone who is able to face their fears. Someone able to push through mental and physical resistance.

Another person, cool and confident. Entering into the room with ease and clear command.

Courage is shown in life’s battles, tiny and large. In history and myth, We think of courageous acts in great moments like leading the charge in battle, becoming the first person to protest an injustice, or keeping one’s cool at the highest levels of statecraft.

But it’s present in our ordinary lives too. When we stand up for ourselves and others, when we do what needs to be done even when it’s difficult, and when we decide what risks to subject ourselves to – each of these acts requires courage.

As it has been said, a Stoic is someone who does the ordinary in an extraordinary way.

Becoming courageous has the side benefit of reducing negative emotions. The courageous person experiences negative emotions, but not extreme fear or anxiety. By transforming yourself into someone braver, you too will be less fearful, anxious or anxious rash.

But what is courage?

Aristotle introduced the idea of the virtuous mean. He defined every virtue as a mean between excess and deficiency. Think of it like the golden mean: not too much, not too little.

According to Aristotle, courage falls in between recklessness and cowardice. The courageous person is prudent, not thoughtless. 

The reckless person foolishly charges into situations and makes silly mistakes. Their fearlessness is grounded in blindness, not virtue. 

On the opposite side, the coward shrinks from the world to avoid making any mistake at all. They ruminate over ways things could go wrong and end up paralyzed. 

In between these two vices, lies the brave. They throw themselves into the fray. They make the leap, even when it’s scary. At the same time, they experience fear. They listen to it when it’s useful and overcome it when it’s not.

The courageous person experiences and listens to fear, but is never driven by it. In contrast, the fearful person is gripped by terror – it fills their entire vision. The brave don’t let this happen.

It’s useful to remember that feelings of fear serve a useful purpose. They communicate information about what situations are safe. Many people make mistakes because they do not feel enough fear or listen to it when it arises. We don’t talk about these people enough. Perhaps because anxious people end up in therapy and social media, but reckless people end up in jail or the morgue. 

That’s why the Stoics defined courage as knowing what to avoid. Think back to examples of courageous action.

The image of the protestor standing up for injustice knows they don’t need to avoid others' scorn or threats. Justice is more important.

Someone asserting themselves when another is trying to rush them into a decision knows that social pressure doesn’t harm them. Some decisions are worth thinking through carefully and slowly. 

Another person standing up to a bully knows that they’re not threatened by the bully’s presence, but by the fact that justice may not be done. 

The virtuous person is cautious. They treat what matters – their health, relationships, and responsibilities with the care they deserve. Even when there’s social pressure to do otherwise. They don’t play with dice when it comes to what matters. In other words, they don’t put their character at risk. They transform fear into excellent decisions, listening to it when it’s useful and overcoming it when it’s not.

In this series, we’ll take a two-pronged attack to defeat cowardice and recklessness: changing how we think and making more courageous choices.

By changing how we think, we transform who we are. We become courageous people. 

Thought alone isn’t enough – we need to show the fruits of our practice in action.

Excellent action and thought are reinforcing. By thinking and acting like a brave person you make anxiety irrelevant and courage a part of who you are.

With each meditation in this series, we’ll practice changing how we think by internalizing Stoic principles and preparing ourselves to make courageous choices. Then, with commitment and action, we’ll take the Stoic principles with us into our everyday life.

Do not let your fears stand in the way of a good life. Live now.

# The Source

## Quote

"Today I escaped from anxiety. Or no, I discarded it, because it was within me, in my own perceptions—not outside."  
id: 225

## Theory

How does one become more courageous?

The Stoic insight is that by changing how we think, we can change who we are.

A key teaching of Stoicism is that our minds are more powerful than many think.

Within all of us is a deep well of strength. All we need to do is pull from it.

We have the power to respond to the world as we choose. We each possess the agency to shape ourselves into who we want to be. 

This is why Marcus Aurelius wrote:

"Today I escaped from anxiety. Or no, I discarded it, because it was within me, in my own perceptions—not outside."  
id: 225

Anxiety and fear are often driven by our own narratives – or narratives of others – that downplay our agency.

Yes, we’re shaped by past and present pressures. But we can’t let that prevent us from changing.

Instead, we can take solace in the fact that how we respond to the world is up to us. No one else – nothing else – can force us to make decisions we don’t want to. It’s this ability which grounds our freedom and courage.

What should we fear? Misusing this power. Making mistakes. Failing to heed reality or submitting to illusions.

Instead, as Seneca urges us, we should never give in to adversity. We’ve been given the gift to use our minds in extraordinary ways. It’s up to us whether we use it.

How do we do that?

We need to break through the stories and narratives we tell ourselves about anxiety. Yes, life calls us to do difficult, even terrifying things. Yes, so much of what we fear is surmountable. Other people have conquered their fears. We can do the same.

We have the power to push through. Whenever you face your fears, you remind yourself that you can act with courage. 

If only you would look.

Each situation we face is a question – how we respond is our answer. Focus on these responses.

Every thought and decision takes us closer or further away from the courageous life. Make the choices and decisions that take you closer. 

You don’t have to put yourself in terrifying situations. Just push yourself towards bravery, step by step. Action by action. It's not the size of the step that matters, but the direction you're moving in. With consistent effort, you can become a more skillful, brave, and prudent person.

Each judgment and choice will reinforce each other. Pause and push through.

# Cato’s Courage

## Quote

"Choose therefore a Cato. Choose a master whose life, conversation, and soul-expressing face have satisfied you; picture him always to yourself as your protector or your pattern. For we must indeed have someone according to whom we may regulate our characters; you can never straighten that which is crooked unless you use a ruler."  
id: 1869

## Theory

Seneca says:

"Choose therefore a Cato.. Choose a master whose life, conversation, and soul-expressing face have satisfied you; picture him always to yourself as your protector or your pattern. For we must indeed have someone according to whom we may regulate our characters; you can never straighten that which is crooked unless you use a ruler."  
id: ?

The goal is to pick a model. Someone whose behavior we can emulate.

But who was Cato?

Cato the Younger was a Roman statesman and philosopher. A mortal enemy of Julius Caesar. 

The biographers Jimmy Sonni and Rob Goodman describe him well, saying:

“He is in politics yet somehow not of politics. He is a man of action and a spellbinding orator, but he is also deeper; in quieter moments, he gives the impression of barely restraining the urge to retire and become a philosopher. He is the man brought into being by the crisis, and he is its solution, the mirror image of the greed and self-seeking that surround him.”

Cato is someone who showed courage in the big moments.

He fearlessly negotiated with armed mobs, refused generals, and stuck to his principles. His unwavering commitment to his ethics made him a legendary figure in history.

For those reasons alone, every Stoic can take him as a role model.

The Stoic account of courage is knowing what to fear. Cato didn’t fear the jeering of the crowd or threats of physical violence. Instead, he feared the destruction of the Roman Republic. Because, if the Republic fell, that would show he played his role poorly. His job was to use his social position to be an excellent officer and senator. A laser focus on that enabled him to disregard social and physical threats that would paralyze others.

For example, early in his career he took the office of Quaestor. The position essentially made him supervisor of the Roman treasury. Typically, this was a stepping stone position that didn't involve much work. But Cato saw corruption and, instead of playing the usual game of playing people off for political favors, he cleaned the books and fired corrupt bureaucrats.

He could have paused and asked how that would have impacted his career. It made a lot of enemies. It may not have been the courageous act that he’s most known for, but it must have been scary nonetheless. With the conviction that the real thing to fear is letting a corrupt office corrupt your character – not the condemnation of your peers – Cato acted with courage.

As you go throughout your day, think about his example or examples from other people you admire. Note how what they fear is often not conventional. Yes, they show prudence in the face of risking wealth, reputation, and comfort – but ultimately our models are more concerned about deeper values. 

Embody that lesson in your actions today.

# Human Endurance

## Quote

“Persist and resist.”  
id: 1870

## Theory

In this series, we’re taking a two-pronged attack to defeat cowardice and recklessness: changing how we think and making more courageous decisions.

The first part involves internalizing Stoic principles. By making them second nature we can better handle circumstances as they arise. 

The second involves making progress in action. This involves techniques like exposure – doing what you fear, rationally, to create excellent habits and prove that you can be more courageous.

These two tactics are related. If you think like a Stoic, then you’ll make courageous decisions. You’ll embody your philosophy. If you act like a Stoic, you’ll make it easier to take Stoic principles seriously.

This is why it’s so important to get the little things right. With a virtue like courage, it’s easy to focus too much on the big moments – that one movie moment where you show courage in a high-stakes situation. That moment is the product of many smaller thoughts and decisions. Without paying attention to the small acts of bravery, you won’t be prepared to face the high-stakes moments either.

With this thought in mind, we can get motivation from a simple fact today: you’ve faced fear before.

Negative emotions are endurable. We know this because humans throughout history have faced all kinds of pains – and survived. Your ancestors have endured severe setbacks.  They had to defeat fear, in ordinary and extraordinary ways for you to end up where you are today. 

You possess the same power of mind they did. You can do it too.

In fact, you’ve shown bravery throughout your life already. You can do it again.

You’ve made progress throughout this course. All you need to do is focus on that. And move in the same direction. Action by action.

Epictetus had a famous saying: persist and resist.

It’s a reminder that we can persist through pain and anxiety and resist the temptation to succumb. 

As you go throughout your day, bring that mantra to mind. And act with courage.

# Confidence

## Quote

"The whole future lies in uncertainty: live immediately."  
id: 550

## Theory

Confidence is the expectation of success. 

It’s built by proving to ourselves that we can succeed through practice and managing our expectations.

Both overconfidence and underconfidence occur when our expectations become divorced from our abilities. The overconfident strides into a situation and makes a mess of it. It’s a vice related to recklessness. The underconfident makes the opposite mistake and sits on the sidelines.

This is why Aristotle saw courage as the mean between cowardice and recklessness.

To become more confident, we must change how we act and how we think. Like with any skill, when you’re first beginning, it’s unclear what to expect from yourself. But with more practice, you can improve how you think, set more realistic expectations, and see your skills improve in real-time.

What does this have to do with courage?

The Stoics defined courage as knowing what to avoid. The key thing to avoid is corrupting your character – who you are. If you avoid those actions, the rest of the possibility is an open space.

The courageous Stoic focuses on what is up to them – and perfects that. That’s what allows them to act with bravery in matters big and small.

The overconfident put too much faith in their own power. They believe they can control things they can’t. The underconfident put too much stock in what is outside of their own power. They’re afraid of failure and often succumb to external or internal pressure because they haven’t fully internalized what is up to them.

The source of courage is the knowledge that we all can make excellent decisions and judgments. Everything else is not up to us. 

There’s no time to waste. As Seneca said:

"The whole future lies in uncertainty: live immediately."

# Fun

## Quote

“For life to be really fun, what you fear should line up with what you desire.”  
id: 1705

## Theory

The philosopher and trader Nassim Taleb has an aphorism that reads:

“For life to be really fun, what you fear should line up with what you desire.”  
id: 1705

I think this hints at another way to build courage.

Of course, we should immediately dispel some incorrect interpretations. Sometimes caution is good. The things that we fear are often reasonable. This is not a call to act recklessly. 

But what this aphorism gets at is that people who are skilled love the parts of their craft skill that novices don’t – they get energy from pushing themselves. 

In The Mundanity of Excellence, the sociologist Daniel Chambliss studied high-performing athletes, especially swimmers. What was their secret? 

“What these athletes do was rather interesting, but the people themselves were only fast swimmers, who did the particular things one does to swim fast. It is all very mundane.”

And – they enjoyed doing it. The best swimmers not only enjoyed their craft but reveled in waking up early and putting the reps in. There’s a joy in pushing yourself. Energy can be found in discomfort.

You can get in a similar state. By facing your fears, you can learn to enjoy them.

Public speakers transform their fear before a speech into a thrill. Athletes tossing and turning before the big game use their state as energy – and put it to use in competition. People lean into the joy of the dare.

Is this strategy perfect? No, again for the reckless it is not good advice. Instead of seeking the excitement of risk-taking, it’s better to focus on the tranquility of prudence.

Ultimately, you don’t face your fears because it’s fun. It’s fun because you’re improving yourself. It’s the difference between empty excitement and the kind of joy that means something.

# Why

## Quote

What man actually needs is not a tensionless state but rather the striving and struggling for some goal worthy of him.  
id: 1797

## Theory

One of the central ideas of Stoicism is that it’s not things in themselves that cause us suffering, but our opinion of them. As the Roman philosopher Cicero explains:

"The mere fact that men endure the same pain more easily when they voluntarily undergo it for the sake of their country than when they suffer it for some lesser cause, shows that the intensity of the pain depends on the state of mind of the sufferer, not on its own intrinsic nature."  
id: 1721

It’s negative thinking patterns that have little semblance of reality that cause us suffering, not reality itself.

Someone who has a purpose behind their actions can motivate themselves and make sense of what happens to them. Cicero gives the example of a soldier in battle – he can withstand pain because he’s fighting for a greater cause.

He expects adversity – it cannot be avoided in battle. He appreciates the joys and excitement of war but doesn't crave it.

What are you fighting for? What motivates your search for personal transformation? Bring that to mind and never lose sight of it.

As the psychiatrist Viktor Frankl said:

“What man actually needs is not a tensionless state but rather the striving and struggling for some goal worthy of him.”

At the broadest level, all of us share the same goal: to be excellent humans. Stoics do the ordinary – in extraordinary ways. They forge deep relationships, contribute to their communities, and show courage in ordinary life. 

Today, most of us are not called to fight in literal wars, but instead show our worth in the field of family life, friendship, community, and career. 

Perhaps you have specific purposes in mind – don’t lose sight of them. If you do, return to these common goals: to develop excellent character and show by acting with courage, discipline, generosity, and justice.

If you keep these goals in mind, you’ll be able to face your fears and adversity. Focus on your purpose. It will give you power over fear.

Let’s end with a quote from Seneca:

"If a man knows not which port he sails, no wind is favorable."
