# Intro

## Theory

Marcus Aurelius is one of the most famous and eminent Stoics. 

The British Historian Edward Gibbon held that he was the last of the five good emperors who oversaw a time of prosperity in Rome, before its downfall. The Historian Herodian said the following about him:

“Alone of the emperors, he gave proof of his learning not by mere words or knowledge of philosophical doctrines but by his blameless character and temperate way of life.”

This course is about the Roman emperor and his philosophy. 

After going through this week you’ll understand the essentials of Marcus Aurelius’s Stoic philosophy and be able to apply it in your life. He used philosophy not as a merely academic tool, but as a way to become more resilient and virtuous. You can do the same. 

Born in 121 AD Marcus Aurelius was adopted by the emperor Antoninus Pius. Pius was known for his competence and virtue. Indeed Marcus Aurelius describes him as follows:

“Remember Antoninus Pius' constancy in things that were done by him in accordance with reason, his equability in all things; how he would never give over a matter until he understood the whole state of it fully and plainly; and how patiently and without any resentment he would bear those who unjustly condemned him.”  
id: 1742

Marcus was known as a precocious and serious youth. He devoted himself to his studies and training.

Marcus Aurelius learned philosophy from his tutors. It was his teacher Junius Rusticus who shared Epictetus’s *Discourses* with him. We find paraphrases of Epictetus in Marcus Aurelius’s *Meditations*. 

Like many today, Marcus Aurelius practiced his philosophy by memorizing and reminding himself of Stoic maxims.

Incredibly, Marcus Aurelius’s writing was not meant for public consumption. His *Meditations* are essentially reminders that he wrote to himself to live well. 

Marcus Aurelius may have lived a satisfying and meaningful life, but he did not lead an easy one.

He was marked for the role of emperor when he was 17\. 

By all accounts, he did not fit the standard mold of the emperor. He would have preferred to be a philosopher. But his role was to be greater.

Despite wishing things were otherwise, Marcus fully devoted himself to running the empire well. The same cannot be said for every Roman emperor.

His reign was prosperous. He maintained the reputation of a fair, generous, and competent emperor. 

Yet his rule was also full of one disaster after another. Whether it was Germanic peoples invading Rome, uprisings from rebellious generals or widespread plague, Marcus’s life offered no respite. The historian Cassius Dio wrote:

"He didn't have the luck which he deserved . . . but was confronted, throughout his reign, by a multitude of disasters. That is why I admire him more than any other, for it was amidst these extraordinary and unparalleled difficul­ties that he was able to survive, and to save the Empire. "

Such a life no doubt benefited from the solace of Stoic philosophy.

This is evident in his *Meditations*.

The ancient Stoics saw humans as fundamentally rational and social creatures. 

With the rational part comes the demand to pursue the truth. It is false beliefs that cause suffering and poor decisions. Marcus Aurelius’s focus on believing what is true, no matter the harm to his ego, is revealed in comments like:

“If it is not right, do not do it: if it is not true, do not say it. For let your impulse be in your own power.”  
id: 1055

This attitude was necessary for his political role, where bad decisions could lead to ruin, not just for Marcus, but millions of other people.

He took the social aspect of our nature equally seriously. To himself, he wrote:

“First, do nothing inconsiderately or without a purpose. Second, make your acts refer to nothing else but a social end.”  
id: 1056

It was Marcus’s dedication to acting intentionally and prosocially that enabled him to fully devote himself to serving the empire and treating others as well as he could – regardless of how they treated him.

In many respects, Marcus Aurelius is a model Stoic.  He was a thoughtful friend, advisor, and general. During his reign, he faced enemies in battle, managed a deadly plague, and served as a widely regarded judge. Unlike other emperors, he didn’t abuse his power, but instead forgave political opponents. He devoted his wealth and life to the Roman people.

It’s true that he certainly made mistakes. He possessed vices that many men of his time did.

But he exemplified virtues of courage, justice, moderation, and wisdom as far as imperfect beings like us can. 

With his writings, we’re given insight into Stoic philosophy and how one man used it to live well.

In this course, we will learn more about how Marcus Aurelius applied Stoic philosophy to live better ourselves.

# Hero

## Theory

Humans are fundamentally imitative animals. We copy others.

If we’re surrounded by virtuous people, it will be easier to become virtuous ourselves through emulation. However, if we’re surrounded by mean and unhappy people, we may copy traits that are not good.

This means that we must ensure that we’re surrounded by people we want to copy.

Part of this involves being intentional about one’s social and physical environment. Seek out positive role models and learn from them.

Marcus Aurelius took the previous emperor Antoninus as a role model. Marcus wrote that it was Antoninus who showed him, through the example of his life, that an emperor could be respected because of their character and not merely their power. It was through watching Antoninus work that Marcus learned this lesson.

Later in life, He reminded himself to:

“Remember Antoninus Pius' constancy in things that were done by him in accordance with reason, his equability in all things; how he would never give over a matter until he understood the whole state of it fully and plainly; and how patiently and without any resentment he would bear those who unjustly condemned him.”  
id: 1742

Not all of us are fortunate enough to be surrounded by people like Antoninus. Yet we may find inspiration in surprising places and people.

It’s not only important to shape our physical and social surroundings, we must control what we think also. We have control over our internal and external environments. Another way to take advantage of the human tendency to copy is to meditate on figures that are not physically present.

To this end, Marcus Aurelius would regularly contemplate Stoic sages like Socrates and Diogenes.

Marcus Aurelius himself was reputed to be a competent, thoughtful, and kind ruler. He serves as a role model for many past and present Stoics. 

In order to properly meditate on role models we must understand who they were and how they exemplified their virtue in ordinary life.

So, let’s consider positive stories from Marcus Aurelius’s life. How did he exemplify his virtue?

Marcus Aurelius completely devoted himself to his role as emperor. This could not be said for all Roman emperors. He was known as a disciplined and dedicated ruler. 

As an emperor he was generous. When his sister married, Marcus gave away the inheritance his father had left him. In fact, it was a regular occurrence for him to give away any inheritance he had to those he thought needed it more. During his reign he even auctioned off imperial treasures to replenish the treasury of the state.

Many Roman emperors sought to destroy and murder their opponents, but Marcus Aurelius granted clemency instead. Indeed, even when his general Avidius Cassius launched a coup, Marcus Aurelius insisted that Cassius be allowed grace. By doing so, Marcus Aurelius offered clemency to his enemies. Yet Marcus Aurelius was not a pushover. He prepared for war with Cassius, if that’s what it came to. 

Despite his sincerity, Marcus Aurelius was also personable. In the letters to his tutor and friend Fronto, he does not preach or focus on cerebral philosophical topics, but is instead concerned with the day to day. He prays for Fronto’s health and happiness. Marcus Aurelius even sends him a birthday greeting. Despite the demands of the state, Marcus Aurelius remained committed with his friends.

So, we have a thoughtful, devoted, generous, and forgiving character in Marcus Aurelius.

As you contemplate models, remember people like Antoninus Pius, Marcus Aurelius, and the excellent men and women who came before and after.

# Training Program: judgment

## Theory

Marcus Aurelius encountered Stoic precepts through his Stoic tutor, Junicus Rusticus. This teacher leant Marcus a copy of Epictetus’ *Discourses* from his personal library.

By doing so, he shaped Marcus Aurelius’s life, for the philosophy would always stick with him.

And so the slave-philosopher Epictetus influenced the Roman empire.

Epictetus talks about 3 Stoic disciplines:

* The Discipline of judgment  
* The Discipline of desire  
* The Discipline of action


judgment is about seeing the world as it is. Desire is about relegating our desires to what is under our control. And action is all making virtuous decisions. 

We find notes on these three disciplines throughout *Meditations.*

Each is captured in Marcus Aurelius’s line:

"Objective judgment, now, at this very moment. Unselfish action, now, at this very moment. Willing acceptance—now, at this very moment—of all external events. That’s all you need."  
id: 193

Let’s focus on the discipline of judgment today and consider the other two in following routines.

The discipline of judgment concerns what ideas we should assent or agree to.

When we perceive the world, we do so in stages. First, we have an initial sensation. Then, after the initial sensation, we move to make sense of it and form a judgment.

The discipline is perfectly captured in an example that Marcus Aurelius writes: 

"Don’t tell yourself anything more than what your primary representations tell you. If you’ve been told, “so-and-so has been talking behind your back”, then this is what you’ve been told. You have not however been told that “Somebody has done a wrong to you”"  
id: 1656

The initial sensation is just the experience of someone telling Marcus that someone is talking behind his back. At this point, Marcus must decide to see the world as it is. He reminds himself that he does not need to take personal offense. Someone has been talking about him behind his back, that is all.

In many of these situations, however, we go above and beyond what our initial experience gives us. We add details like the fact that the insult harms us or that we’ve been wronged.

The discipline of judgment is about only agreeing to what is true. We should not lose ourselves in fictional narratives or stories. When we do this, we suffer and are distracted from what matters.

If someone is talking behind our back that is unfortunate – it would be preferable if they didn’t do such things – but we must understand this without making unnecessary value judgments about the matter. Some things are dispreffered indifferents – we do not want them, but we shouldn’t think of them as terrible. For they do not harm us.

Epictetus gives another example when he tells a story of someone being sent to jail:

He was sent to jail. What happened? He was sent to jail.  But "He is unhappy" is added by oneself.  
id: 1743

This is the key idea that the value judgment that brings emotions is added by ourselves. That part comes from us, not the world.

This is why the Stoics say:

“It’s not things in themselves that harm us, but our opinion of them”

We’ve each formed habits about how we respond to events. If someone insults us, we automatically believe that we are harmed. If we lose something, we immediately react like we’ve been injured. But we have not been hurt by the world, but our interpretation of it.

To change this we must separate events from our judgments about them and reprogram our mental habits. 

Mindfulness meditation is a crucial practice for this skill. By bringing our attention to our mind we can better see that sensations and our judgments of them are separate things. And we can reprogram our mental moves so that we do not immediately react to the world by agreeing with false ideas.

Let’s practice this today.

# Training Program: Desire

## Theory

The discipline of judgment is about embracing reality. This means assenting to what is true no matter how uncomfortable that may be. It also means not adding unnecessary narratives to reality. Instead, we positively affirm reality as it is.

Our minds naturally add stories to whatever happens to us. Often these stories do not reflect reality. When we do this we suffer.

When this happens, you can think of the mind as overflowing beyond what is given to us.

This is not the only way that our mind overflows beyond its given limits. What we desire often extends beyond what is under control. Worse, we make ourselves dependent on the world by wanting things that are not up to us. When we do this, we circumscribe our freedom.

The discipline of desire is about limiting our desire to what is under control. 

Fundamentally the only things which are up to us according to the Stoics are judgments and decisions. Everything else is not.

Yet we constantly concern ourselves with thoughts about the past and future. And we constantly want things beyond what we’re given. 

Consider a game of chance, like poker. Occasionally, people kick themselves for not having played a bad hand when it turns out that they could have won big with it.

This is nearly always a mistake.

Excellent poker player’s focus on playing the game well. This means thinking about one’s longterm strategies and not overweighting things that are out of their control. Regularly playing bad hands will result in you regularly losing money.

Playing well is not about focusing on what cards are dealt. It’s about how you play what you’re given. 

We should think of ourselves like poker players. This means focusing on being an excellent person and making the best judgments and decisions we can. Everything else is not up to us.

To do this, Marcus Aurelius reminds himself to stay confined to the present:

“If you apply yourself to living only that which you are living – in other words, the present – then you can live the rest of your life until your death in peace, benevolence, and serenity.”  
id: 1744

Do not become distracted by past or future matters that are not up to you.

Instead, embrace what happens:  
					

“The proper characteristic of the good man is to love and to greet joyfully all those events which he encounters, and which are linked to him by Destiny.”

id: 1745

This is what the discipline of desire is all about: embracing whatever happens to us and simply wanting to make excellent decisions and believe what is true.

This requires living in the present and understanding what is our responsibility. 

That is what we will practice today. If you can limit your attention to the present and understand what is up to you will live well.

# Training Program: Action

## Theory

Marcus Aurelius was renowned for his service to the empire. 

He spent most of his life aiming to better the lives of the Roman people.

As a Roman Emperor, he was given the power to shape millions of people’s lives and circumstances. 

Most of us are not in that position today. Yet, we each should ensure that we are serving others and living well with them nonetheless.

This is the focus of the discipline of action. This final discipline focuses on acting virtuously. 

With the discipline of judgment, we practice seeing the world objectively. With the discipline of desire, we limit our desires to what is up to us. 

Desire and judgment are not enough, Stoicism is ultimately a philosophy of action. This is where the discipline of action comes in.

Marcus Aurelius reminds himself of his proper role:

"It is a proper work of a man to be benevolent to his own kind”  
id: 1746

We should take this role seriously, as Marcus said:

“In the first place: nothing at random, and nothing that is not related to some goal. Second: do not relate your actions to anything other than a goal which may serve the human community.”  
id: 1747

Our time is limited. We will not always be able to do right by ourselves or others.

The opposite of acting well is acting with vice. 

Many of us do not seek to act poorly. But we find ourselves doing it time and time again.

We focus too much on selfish aims, instead of seeing others for who they are and acting in a prosocial way.

The French philosopher Pierre Hadot wrote that:

“The vice which is opposed to the discipline of action is thus frivolity. It is the opposite to that seriousness or gravity with which all human actions should be accomplished.”

In his view, so many of the mistakes we make are a result of failing to take things seriously. 

Of course, being serious does not mean taking on a stiff or aloof demeanor. We find even the serious Marcus Aurelius recounting pranks and displaying a softer side in his letters.

Taking things seriously amounts to giving things their due. Treating others and ourselves seriously means living with excellence in the limited time we have.

We only possess one life, only one chance to do right by ourselves and others.

Because of this Marcus Aurelius writes:

“Carry out each action of your life as if it were the last, and keep yourself far from all frivolity.”  
id: 1748

# Gratitude

## Theory

Marcus Aurelius spends pages and pages thanking his friends, teachers, family, and role models in the first chapter of *Meditations*.

This fact highlights how important gratitude was for Stoics. It’s a central part of the philosophy.

When we see the world clearly, we see that we have much to be thankful for.

When we do this, we reorient our orientation towards what is good: truth, beauty, and other people.

By remembering what he learned from others, Marcus Aurelius reminds himself of the wisdom he acquired, relationships he formed, and checks his ego.

Gratitude is grounded in the knowledge that others have influenced us positively.

We learn how to live from everyone around us. Not all of those lessons have been positive, but undoubtedly many of them have.

By noting who has shaped us, we emphasize our social nature.

We are not purely independent beings. Whatever we do, we’re bound up in networks of other people.

Happily, many of these people serve as fine examples for us – even if they are not perfect.

As Marcus Aurelius wrote:

"Accept whatever comes to you woven in the pattern of your destiny, for what could more aptly fit your needs?”

id: 60

We’re fortunate to be able to live in a world that gives us the possibility of living well.

Let’s remember that others have helped us in that effort today.

# View From Above

## Theory

Well done finishing this week on Marcus Aurelius. You should now have a grasp of the Roman emperor’s philosophy and practice. Stoicism is about believing what is true, desiring what is up to us, and making excellent decisions.

Marcus Aurelius reminded himself of the central challenge of Stoicism with the words:

"Objective judgment, now, at this very moment. Unselfish action, now, at this very moment. Willing acceptance—now, at this very moment—of all external events. That’s all you need."

id: 193

We’ll finish by introducing one more meditation.

A key aspect of Stoicism is living in accord with nature.

This means recognizing our nature as social and rational beings. It also means that we must see our lives in relation to the whole and not merely its parts.

Pierre Hadot wrote that the sage is someone who:

"never ceases to have the whole constantly present to his mind. He thinks and acts within a cosmic perspective. He has the feeling of a whole which goes beyond the limits of his individuality."

This skill involves being able to look at the big picture, without dissociating from what is in front of us.

Many people have far too narrow a picture of the world. They only see parts of their life without the whole. This may cause them to be too short-term, overreact to whatever happens, or chase novelty. There’s another kind of person who is rarer, but makes the opposite mistake. These people dissociate. They stay in the clouds and never come down. This kind of person is too abstract and impersonal. They spend too much time thinking about the big picture and forget what is in front of them.

The sage is someone who combines both perspectives.

We can see Marcus Aurelius aiming to do this in his Meditations.

For example, he takes reminds himself to stay in the moment with lines like:

"Wipe out the imagination. Stop pulling the strings. Confine thyself to the present."

id: 587

Yet Marcus also reminds himself of the big picture. He takes the view from above.

He does this by meditating on the huge expanse of space and time:

"How small a part of the boundless and unfathomable time is assigned to every man\! For it is very soon swallowed up in the eternal. And how small a part of the whole substance\! And how small a part of the universal soul\! And on what a small clod of the whole earth you creep\!"

id: 1061

He sums up Stoicism in the pithy maxim: 

“The universe is transformation, life is opinion.”

id: 910

One must understand that everything is impermanent, changing, and much larger than any individual. Yet we must think well and take responsibility for what is up to you.

Let’s take a larger perspective today and meditate on what’s ultimately important.
