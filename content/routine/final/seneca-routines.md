# Introduction

## Theory

Seneca is one of the wisest and most complicated Stoic philosophers.

He was a political advisor and investor. He saw emperors come and go. At one point, he was rumored to be the richest man in all of Rome.

He was also a playwright and philosopher. It’s not too much of an exaggeration to say that he was a combination of Shakespeare, Socrates, and Thomas Jefferson.

His writings and life contain deep lessons about how to live well. 

He possessed traits worth emulating and yet he also made decisions that are, at best, lessons in how to avoid ruin.

He was born in 4 BC in modern-day Spain. He moved to Rome and served as a senator. He was renowned for his skill in oratory. Eventually, he was expelled from Rome on grounds of being involved in an affair with an emperor’s sister. This charge was likely fabricated by his political enemies. 

He was called out of exile by the ambitious and strategic Agrippina, the mother of the Roman emperor Nero, to serve as Nero’s tutor.

This role placed Seneca in the powerful, yet precarious position of advising Rome’s strongest man. Unfortunately, despite Seneca’s lessons, the emperor Nero was neither the sanest nor most virtuous emperor.

Nero’s early years were prosperous ones. It’s surmised that at this point in time, the Roman empire was essentially run by Seneca, Agripinna, and another one of Nero’s tutors, Burrus.

Yet Nero’s later reign was disastrous. Though some historians have argued that he’s painted in far too negative of a light, he was by most accounts greedy, impulsive, and unstable. 

Senators were pressured to play the sycophant to Nero’s childish behavior. Not all did. Those who didn’t faced exile or death.

In time, Nero’s rage turned to Seneca. Nero ordered Seneca to commit suicide in 65 AD.  
And so ended the life of a Stoic philosopher, advisor, and playwright.

He lives on through his writings. Seneca’s works have provided advice and insight to millions of people after him. Statesmen, entrepreneurs, and artists have learned from his works.

His most famous works include an essay On Anger and his Moral Letters. The moral letters are written to a friend, Lucilius, someone seeking counsel. Seneca’s talent as a writer and advisor is on full display. For that reason, they are exceptionally useful and practical.

They’re a key resource for Stoics and philosophers today. Seneca was an astute philosopher and political player. His understanding of human psychology was exemplary. He has useful insights into how Stoics manage politics, friendship, crowds, time, and much more.

In today's meditation, we’ll contemplate some of Seneca’s most popular lines.

# Reflection

## Theory

Most of our life is determined by forces outside of ourselves. 

Our social environment is set early in life.  Who we’re surrounded by is determined by chance. Luckily we have the power to proactively seek out positive influences and develop new relationships. We can always step beyond our immediate environment and look to the past for guidance.

Seneca used this tactic when reviewing each day.

He imagined that he was facing a panel of advisors and heroes. He would present how he lived his day to them. Then they would evaluate how he lived. Thus he took advantage of the wisdom of those who were not present.

In order for this exercise to work, we must have a detailed picture of our model’s character. This is required in order to understand how they would handle different situations.

This can come from countless sources. From books, movies, and courses to the practical affairs of ordinary life. There is much to learn from people past and present.

Once we have a good sense of how a role model would influence, advise, and evaluate us we can take advantage of their knowledge and apply it to our own life. 

Imagine yourself facing a jury of your models. You are placed at the center with a ring of advisors surrounding you.

This is a high stake situation. Your heroes would undoubtedly press you to be better. Remember that they are on your side. They want you to succeed and will give you the best advice on how to get there. Use that as motivation.

Start by simply reviewing what happened. Do this in an objective way. Like a historian simply trying to determine what happened in detail – without coming to the story after making up their mind already.

Then move to detail what your heroes would pick out as excellent behavior. Note it whatever it is. You can take joy in what you’ve done well and gain motivation from the fact that others will think of you well for having done it.

Then consider where you missed the mark. Note times when you didn’t live up to who you wanted to be. Be scrupulous, honest, and careful.

What issues would your role models pick out?

Once you’ve done that, bring to mind how the panel would advise you. What would they want you to do? What would they expect you to have done when the panel reconvenes?

We shouldn’t put too much stock in what others think about us. Yet, when others are virtuous heroes, we should care. If an excellent person thinks well of us, that’s a sign that we’re on the right track. Because excellent people take joy in watching others act virtuously. However, if a person of good character does not admire you, that too is information. You do not want to be disapproved of by the virtuous, because they know what is good.

Use this as motivation to act well.

Let’s end with a line from Seneca

If you wish to set a value on yourself, put away your money, your estates, your honours, and look into your own soul.  
id: 1412

# Time

## Theory

Seneca reminded his friend Lucilius:

“While we are postponing, life speeds by.”  
id: 467

Time is a central focus of Seneca’s writing.

Nothing is properly ours except the time we have. Yet so many of us waste time on trivial matters.

For this reason, Seneca advises us that to save time and use it thoughtfully. He asks:

“What man can you show me who places any value on his time, who reckons the worth of each day, who understands that he is dying daily?”  
id: 1191

Our time is limited. 

This is an obvious fact, yet so many of us live like we deny it.

If you only had a year left to live, would you spend it differently? You likely would. You would focus more on what matters, take advantage of the good that you can, and put even more effort into being who you were meant to be.

Would you do the same if it were two years? three? five? ten?

The fact of the matter is that no one knows how much more time we have.

“Time is the one loan which even a grateful recipient cannot repay.”  
Id: 1754

The first step to using time well is to account for it.

If we know what we spend our time on, we can better budget it. In that way, it is no different from a financial budget.

After a full accounting, we must be sure to waste less time. We cannot balance our time budget immediately, but we can make serious improvements each day. 

Seneca ends his letter *On Saving Time* with the following note:

“I do not regard a man as poor, if the little which remains is enough for him. I advise you, however, to keep what is really yours; and you cannot begin too early.”  
id: 1755

# Anger

## Theory

Seneca’s *On Anger* is one of the most important pieces of ancient philosophy.

Thousands, perhaps millions, of people have used it to reduce their own anger and live better.

Seneca frames the work as a letter to his brother. His brother is a stand-in for an audience of elite Romans. Today that audience is even larger.

In this letter, Seneca brings together history, psychology, and philosophy in order to address the most vicious and savage of emotions.

Some defend anger saying that it has its purposes. 

Seneca does not believe this.

In his view, anger is a monstrous emotion. We just need to look at what anger does to people’s faces – they transform themselves into ugly and contorted positions.

Even worse anger is the root of so much stupidity and tragedy. In Seneca’s words, when you look at the results of anger:

“You’ll see slaughters, poisons, mutual mud-slinging of litigants, wreckage of cities, extinctions of whole races, lives of leading men sold at public auction, torches touched to buildings, flames not contained within walls but, held by an enemy host, gleaming over vast spans of territory.”  
id: 1757

Anger is the foundation for so much evil. For that reason, it must be reigned in.

But how does one do that?

Start by understanding what anger is. Seneca declares that anger is the desire to punish a perceived wrong. It’s the feeling that takes over when we think someone has mistreated us. If we let it, wrath can consume us and we can only see the world from the standpoint of feeling wronged. Indeed, we must be careful to not let initial feelings of anger grow into a rage. Such a strong emotion has the capacity to enslave someone and drive every feeling, desire, and thought to its own ends.

How do we stop this from happening?

We should address any feelings of perceived wrongdoing as soon as possible. It’s easiest to manage extreme emotions at the early stage. Before it has grown and taken us over.

This is true both within ourselves and in relationships with others. Anger always feeds on itself. Do not let it.

To accomplish this, Seneca lists many different strategies.

First, you can delay. Do not let yourself act on rash impulses. If you simply wait, you will often find that vicious emotions relent all on their own. 

Second, you can decrease your ignorance and change expectations. So much anger is the result of not expecting wicked people to do wicked things. Why do we do this? We live in a world with imperfect people. Hence, we should expect to be wronged. Always keep in mind the possibility that someone will mistreat you. Then handle it when it arises, without losing your cool.

Third, Seneca suggests looking in the mirror. This is a useful distraction, but more importantly, it can show the ugliness of anger.

Fourth, we must value tranquility. As Seneca writes:

“There is no proof of greatness so sure as when nothing that can occur can provoke you. The higher and more ordered part of the universe, the part near the stars, is not forced down toward the clouds nor driven into a storm nor whipped into a whirlwind; it is completely without turmoil, while lower levels get the thunderbolts. In the same way, the lofty mind, ever calm, situated in a tranquil resting place, keeping down below itself all things that anger it, is moderate and reverend and well ordered.”  
id: 1758

Finally, remember that we and others will die. Seneca writes:

"Why do we concern ourselves with conflict and plotting? That man you are angry with – can you wish for him anything worse than death? He is going to die without your doing a thing."  
id: 1727

In sum, Seneca gives us five strategies for managing anger: delay, setting expectations, looking in the mirror, valuing tranquility, and remembering our mortality. Choose one of these and put it into practice today. 

Let’s end with a line from Seneca:

“Let’s not be a source of fear or danger to anyone. Let’s cast scorn on injuries, harms, insults, and taunts; let’s put up with brief annoyances. As they say, the moment we turn and look behind us, death stands right there.”  
id: 1759

# Ambiguity

## Theory

Seneca was a complicated character.

In some sense, he was a great man. His writings reveal a thoughtful and careful mind. His political successes demanded diplomatic skills. Unlike many other Roman political players, he did not actively round up and purge his enemies, but sought to restrain Nero’s worst impulses. When he was at the height of his power, Rome was run well.

Yet Seneca had failings too.

Seneca undoubtedly profited from his association with Nero. Critics constantly point out the irony of one of Rome’s most successful men counseling not putting too much value in material goods. 

He did not join the Stoic Opposition. This group was formed to oppose the tyrannical Nero. Instead, Seneca worked with him.

Seneca played a role in dubious political machinations. Seneca legitimized Nero’s assassination of Agrippina, Nero’s mother, by writing a letter justifying her death to the Senate. Seneca was not able to keep himself clean. 

He was undoubtedly motivated by considerations of justice, but he was likely also striving for power. 

Unlike other Stoics, he did not always hold to his principles with resolve. In that way, he is like the rest of us.

Seneca himself realized that he was imperfect. In his 27th letter to Lucilius, he writes that he speaks not as someone who is cured, but as someone who is also sick, lying next to us in the hospital. 

Indeed, many people are attracted to Seneca because he seems like an ordinary and complicated figure. Other ancient Stoics may be less approachable, as they are exceptionally virtuous for their time.

Nonetheless, we must not forget that Seneca paid for his mistakes. Not just with his life, but with the life of his loved ones who Nero hunted down.

We have learned from Seneca’s admirable traits in other lessons. In this one, we should remember that his faults have something to teach us.

None of us are perfect, but all of us can be better.

Let’s end with a line from one of Seneca’s plays:

“No one has had so much divine favor   
That they could guarantee themselves tomorrow.  
God keeps our lives hurtling on,   
Spinning in a whirlwind.”

# Death

## Theory

It is only by facing the reality of death that one can live well.

Seneca once wrote the following in a letter to Lucilius:

“I enjoy my life thus far because I don’t spend too much time measuring how long all this will remain.”  
id: 1761

Indeed, he often meditated on our mortal nature, a practice the Stoics call memento mori.

The point of such an exercise is, ironically, to focus on life and see it for what it is.

Seneca writes:

“It’s not years nor days, but the mind, that determines that we’ve lived enough. I, my dearest Lucilius, have lived as much as is enough.”  
id: 1762

By remembering that we will only live for a brief instant, we can focus on what matters. It’s not extending life per se, but making excellent judgments and decisions that bring joy.

Seneca writes:

“Make haste to live, Lucilius, and think each of your days to be an individual life.”  
id: 1763

Each day should be complete and focused on what is good. We do not know which day will be our last. 

Marcus Aurelius makes the same point when he writes:

T​​hink of yourself as dead. You have lived your life. Now take what's left and live it well."  
id: 1764

Life will come and go of its own accord. 

What matters is ensuring that you live well while you can. All that is required is acting well.

Let’s end with a line from Seneca:

“He who has learned to meet death has unlearned how to be a slave.”  
id: 1704

# Virtue

## Theory

Well done. You have reached the end of the Seneca course.

We will finish by considering role models.

Seneca advised his friend Lucilius to find a model to guide our behavior.:

"Choose therefore a Cato; or, if Cato seems too severe a model, choose some Laelius, a gentler spirit. Choose a master whose life, conversation, and soul-expressing face have satisfied you; picture him always to yourself as your protector or your pattern. For we must indeed have someone according to whom we may regulate our characters; you can never straighten that which is crooked unless you use a ruler."  
id: 1222

The Roman Cato was known for resolutely holding to his principles – even in the face of death. Indeed, he committed suicide rather than surrendering to tyrants. Laelius is a less intense man, but is one known for upright character nonetheless.

Models can serve as motivation and guidance for our lives. 

We’re deeply imitative creatures. To take advantage of this part of our nature, we must collect positive models.

The Stoics advised regularly pausing and contemplating the sage. We should imagine what they would do in our place, how we would act if they were to observe us, or what kind of advice they would give us.

Seneca himself said that he would imagine a panel of sages questioning and advising him at the end of each day.

This highlights a key Stoic technique, but it also brings to light the purpose of Stoic philosophy.

Stoics, along with the other ancient schools, were obsessed with virtue. For them, a good life is one of virtue. A good person makes excellent decisions and judgments. They form deep relationships and treat themselves and others well. 

To live well, find models, real or fictional, who express our values. Learn from their decisions and thoughts. Let them serve as exemplars.

You may also use anti-models. People whose mistakes offer lessons on what we must avoid.

Seneca himself can serve as both a model and anti-model.

He was dedicated, diplomatic, and disciplined. Yet he also collaborated with vicious people, either out of the hope that he could change their trajectory or out of the desire for power. Perhaps both.

As you live, build a portfolio of role models and anti-models. Use them as motivation and guides for action.

Contemplate this today.
