1. #  Day One {#day-one}

## Theory {#theory}

Welcome to Stoa’s 3 Week course on the fundamentals of Stoicism.

Stoa is an app built to help you build resilience and become virtuous. Thousands of people have used it as a tool to manage negative emotions and live with courage and discipline.

The app is grounded in the ancient greco-roman philosophy of Stoicism. 

This philosophy has shaped people from all walks of life: entrepreneurs, psychologists, artists, and athletes.

The purpose of this course is to pack the most valuable lessons and exercises of this ancient philosophy into 3 weeks.

As a result of this course you will have:

\- Possess a  solid foundation in Stoic principles

\- Know the key Stoic exercises and techniques

\- Be better at managing negative emotions

\- And see positive results in your ordinary life

If none of this happens for you, we will happily refund any payments you’ve made for this course or app.

Each day consists of a quote, lesson, meditation, and commitment. 

Use the quote to center your day and begin the routine.

Listen to lessons to learn about how to think and act like a Stoic. 

Then apply the philosophical concepts during meditation. Meditation is where we practice how to think well. It is an isolated environment for building our mental muscles. It is only through regularly putting in the time that you’re able to build up strength. This is true for the mind and the body. 

Through meditation and contemplation, you can bring the fruits of the Stoic techniques into ordinary life.

The last step of the routine is a commitment. We suggest a single action that you can do outside of the app. Be sure to commit each day and then follow through. It’s through training and action that you will build resilience, cultivate calm, and shape yourself into an excellent person.

In today’s meditation, we’ll introduce a visualization exercise.

The core insight of Stoicism is that how we experience the world is caused by how we think.

People who think well are able to respond to the world and live well. Those who cannot, find themselves reacting to the world and losing control of their life.

How we think determines how we live.

The good news is that we can change how we think. Because we can change how we think, we can transform who we are and how we live. 

This is true no matter your station in life. Living well is not a magical ability, but an ordinary skill. Everyone can improve.

Let’s do that today.

Before moving onto the meditation, let’s consider a line from the emperor and philosopher Marcus Aurelius:

“If you are distressed by anything external, the pain is not due to the thing itself, but to your estimate of it; and this you have the power to revoke at any moment.”

2. #  Day Two {#day-two}

## Theory {#theory-1}

The Roman Emperor and philosopher Marcus Aurelius once wrote: 

“Today I escaped anxiety. Or no, I discarded it, because it was within me, in my own perceptions — not outside.”  
id: 225

He wrote this as a private reminder to himself. His book *Meditations* is actually a collection of notes from his private journal. This work is an amazing resource.

With it, we’re able to see how one of history's most powerful and thoughtful men implemented his philosophy.

In several locations, he reminds himself of the Stoic idea:

"It is in our power to have no opinion about a thing and not to be disturbed in our soul; for things themselves have no natural power to form our judgments."  
id: 962

The key idea behind this is that how we think determines how we experience the world. The very same sensation can be treated as nervousness or excitement. The very same event can be treated with joy or despair, depending on our perspective.

This insight is what inspired contemporary psychologists like Albert Ellis. He said the following about his modern therapy:

“Many of the principles…are not new; some of them, in fact, were originally stated several thousand years ago, especially by the Greek and Roman Stoic philosophers”

But how is this insight useful? How can we actually apply it?

Like Marcus Aurelius, we can remind ourselves of the Stoic maxims everyday.

Whenever we notice a negative emotion we can ask – is it true? Can I reframe it to my advantage?

With the use of vigilant attention and careful judgements, we improve how we think. Through honing our thoughts, we can transform how we live.

Let’s practice this in the meditation exercise today.

3. # Day Three {#day-three}

## Theory {#theory-2}

The Stoic philosopher Epictetus once wrote:

"Some things are within our power, while others are not. Within our power are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever is of our own doing; not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not of our own doing." 

id: 806

This line captures what has become known as the dichotomy of control. The insight is captured theologian Reinhold Niebuhr’s serenity prayer, a piece which has been used in both religious and secular contexts:

“Grant me the wisdom to accept the things I cannot change; Courage to change the things I can; And wisdom to know the difference”

On one hand, this is a simple insight. Of course, some things are up to us, while others are not\!

But Epictetus has a radical view here.

What he is saying is that only our decisions and judgements are up to us. Everything else is not. Neither our wealth, health, nor social status are properly under our control.

Everything outside of our judgements and choices is given to us like a hand of cards. It’s our job to play the hand well, not wish that we received another one.

The transformative insight here is that we fundamentally are our considered thoughts and decisions. That’s it. They are expressions of our character. This is perhaps what the philosopher Heraclitus meant when he said: “Character is fate.”

We are not our body. We are not our career. We are not our external success and failures.

Only our mind and its movements define who we are.

This does not mean that everything in our mind is under our control. 

What is up to us are our reflective judgements and character. Initial sensations or thoughts arise without our control – but we can always decide how to react to them. It's our considered thoughts or judgements that we have control over.

The accumulation of these decisions is what determines who we are. This is the true meaning of the dichotomy of control.

Let’s end with a quote:

“If you think that only which is your own to be your own, and if you think that what is another's, as it really is, belongs to another, no man will ever compel you, no man will hinder you, you will never blame any man, you will accuse no man, you will do nothing involuntarily (against your will), no man will harm you, you will have no enemy, for you will not suffer any harm.”

id: ​​233

4. # Day Four {#day-four}

## Theory {#theory-3}

The last few lessons have capture two of the most important lessons in Stoicism:

* How we think determines how we feel  
* Only our decisions and judgements are up to us.

The first lesson is understood by both ancient philosophers and modern scientists. As the Roman statesman and philosopher Cicero explained:

"The mere fact that men endure the same pain more easily when they voluntarily undergo it for the sake of their country than when they suffer it for some lesser cause, shows that the intensity of the pain depends on the state of mind of the sufferer, not on its own intrinsic nature."   
id: 1721

Combine this with the insight from modern neuroscientist Lisa Feldman Barret:

“You are an architect of these experiences. Your river of feelings might feel like it’s flowing over you, but actually you’re the river’s source.”  
id: 1724

The first lesson of Stoicism: think well.

The second lesson is that only our decisions and thoughts are up to us. Everything else doesn’t matter intrinsically. They are external. It’s how we use these things that matter, not the things in themselves.

These two lessons were put into practice by admiral James Stockdale.

During the Vietnam war, Stockdale’s plane was shot down and he was captured by the enemy. At this moment he said that he was leaving the world of technology and joining the world of Epictetus.

Epictetus once summarized Stoicism as the maxim persist and resist. Everyone has the ability to endure and live well no matter the circumstance.

However, some of us have a harder time than others.

Stockdale applied his philosophy to his experience as a prisoner of war. It enabled him to lead fellow prisoners, survive torture, and return to lead a rich civilian life. 

He wrote: 

“Stress is essential to leadership. Living with stress, knowing how to handle pressure, is necessary for survival. It is related to man's ability to wrest control of his own destiny from the circumstances that surround him or, if you like, to prevail over technology.”  
id: ​​1725

Few of us will be prisoners of war. But we all have battles to fight. All of us will face stress.

The beauty of Stoicism is that it gives us a framework for approaching every situation.

Persist & resist.

5. # Day Five {#day-five}

## Theory {#theory-4}

The enlightenment philosopher Spinoza once said:

"Do not weep; do not wax indignant. Understand"

This captures the goal of Stoicism.

The virtuous person sees the world as it is. They embrace reality. They derive energy from the pursuit of knowledge.

According to Stoicism, all you need to live well is virtue. Excellent decisions and judgment. That’s it.

Such a life is grounded in knowledge.

What does this mean in practice?

As the emperor of Rome, Marcus Aurelius could not afford to make any mistakes. Past emperors had let the power of the throne go to their heads. 

They followed their greedy ambitions and crushed political rivals. Through their terrible decisions, they created new enemies. Many of them were assassinated. They were playing a fool’s game and, as such, ended their lives as fools.

To avoid becoming intoxicated with his office, Marcus Aurelius reminded himself that his royal robes are nothing more than sheep's wool dyed with shellfish:

“When you have savouries and fine dishes set before you, you will gain an idea of their nature if you tell yourself that this is the corpse of a fish, and that the corpse of a bird or a pig; or again, that fine Falernian wine is merely grape-juice, and this purple robe some sheep's wool dipped in the blood of a shellfish.”  
id: 1726

How many of our desires would stand up to such scrutiny? When seen correctly, not all desires are grounded in reality.

Yet it is when we live in ignorance that we fail to live well. That is why the Stoic seeks the truth, even when doing so is uncomfortable.

We should live according to our nature as rational and social beings. Our rational nature calls us to knowledge. Our social nature demands that we cooperate with others.

This is what Marcus Aurelius strove to do in every decision. Other emperors did not. Because of this, they did not rule or live as well.

We should embrace reality, not delusions. We should live with others, not against them.

Let’s end with a line from Marcus Aurelius, that summarizes the philosophy in a simple maxim:

“Objective judgment, now, at this very moment. Unselfish action, now, at this very moment. Willing acceptance—now, at this very moment—of all external events. That’s all you need.”  
id: 193

6. # Day Six {#day-six}

## Theory {#theory-5}

Performing Stoa routines everyday helps you be vigilant about your thoughts. If you pay attention to how you think, you can change your thinking patterns. 

And if you change your thoughts, you’ll transform how you live.

That is what this course is made to help you do.

We cannot live well by chance. We must do it intentionally. 

To become physically fit, we must eat well, be intentional about our workout regimen, and surround ourselves with people with similar goals. We must make good decisions in the moment and step back and ensure we’re on the path to building strength.

Similarly, to cultivate mental strength, we must watch what we think, practice thinking well every day, and surround ourselves with positive media.

The Stoic philosopher Epictetus said:

"When faced with anything painful or pleasurable, anything bringing glory or disrepute, realize that the crisis is now, that the Olympics have started, and waiting is no longer an option; that the chance for progress, to keep or lose, turns on the events of a single day."  
id: 845

It is important to cultivate mental strength now. We can get better at this through meditation. 

We must also regularly step back and reflect on our progress. By pausing and noticing what is going well and what isn’t, we can improve our judgements and decisions.

The Roman philosopher Seneca once wrote:

"I shall keep watching myself continually, and – a most useful habit – shall review each day. For this is what makes us wicked: that no one of us looks back over his own life. Our thoughts are devoted only to what we are about to do. And yet our plans for the future always depend on the past."  
id: 1419

The idea is to regularly review our progress in order to become who we want to be. 

With serious philosophical reflection and action, we can chisel our character into being in the same way a sculptor forms a statue from a block of marble. 

Highlight what is important and chip away what isn’t. If you do this, you can live a resilient and virtuous life.

7. # Day Seven {#day-seven}

## Theory {#theory-6}

Life is short.

People say this often. But their actions reveal that they have not internalized the lesson.

There simply is not enough time for trivial things.

Marcus Aurelius once wrote:

"Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good."

id: 915

We simply do not know how long we will be here. 

This fact should bring forth a sense of urgency. We do not have enough time to waste. We must start living well now.

It should also bring clarity. So many things that we think matter are obviously trivial when we remember that we’re going to die.

Seneca once said:

“Why do we concern ourselves with conflict and plotting? That man you are angry with – can you wish for him anything worse than death? He is going to die without your doing a thing.”

id: 1727

Insults, frustrations, and minor anxieties fade away when we remember that they’re merely temporary.

We must focus on what is truly important, seeing reality as it is and developing meaningful relationships.

Life is short. 

Take time to reflect on who you must be today.

# Day Eight {#day-eight}

## Theory {#theory-7}

Excellent work making it to week 2\. You’ve covered key Stoic ideas and practices. I hope you have started to see positive results in your life.

This week we will go deeper into the dichotomy of control. This will enable you to better manage your negative emotions and focus on what we can control.

2

What we want determines how well we live. If you have unreasonable desires, your wants will always be frustrated. If you only want trivial things, you will lead a trivial life.

A key part of living well is desiring what is good.

Hence, we should relegate our desire to what is under our control.

This means attaching little importance to anything that is out of our grasp. Stoics called these things externals. What are the externals? In Epictetus' words:

"Our body, our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not of our own doing."

Status, health, and wealth are externals. They are preferable, but they are not needed for a good life.

Imagine how resilient you would be if you truly internalized this message.

Instead of preoccupying ourselves with externals, we must focus on what is up to us: our judgements and decisions. It is these things that make up who we are. Our character makes the difference between acting with courage, self-control, and wisdom instead of cowardice, indiscipline, and foolishness.

Transforming who we are is a skill. It requires seriously committing to the change and following through.

Years down the line you may try junk food again – and find that it is disgusting. 

It’s possible to change each of your desires to good ones. This takes reflection and practice. It requires setting yourself and your environment up for success. 

This is true whether you’re changing your desire for junk food, decreasing your want for status, or increasing your impulse for virtue.

What really matters is taking concrete steps towards forming your character in your ordinary life. This course is part of this project.

## Theory {#theory-8}

Helvidius Priscus used Stoicism to act well and face down emperors. 

Epictetus tells a story of the emperor Vespasian commanding Helvidius to stay away from the senate. But Helvidius was a senator. So he said the following to Vespasian, “It is in your power not to allow me to be a member of the senate, but so long as I am, I must go in.”

Vespasian, as many powerful rulers have done before and after, raised the stakes. If you speak your mind I will have you put to death. Helividus responded:

“When then did I tell you that I am immortal? You will do your part, and I will do mine: it is your part to kill; it is mine to die, but not in fear: yours to banish me; mine to depart without sorrow.”

Helvidius is lasered in on what is up to him. He has a role, that of being a good senator.

Whether or not the dictator Vespasian punishes Helvidius is not up to him. What is up to him, is whether he attends the senate and performs his role as an honest and just politician.

This is a perfect application of the dichotomy of control. It’s not just about decreasing our stress so that we can win a soccer game or perform better at our career. It’s about focusing on what matters.

“Take heed, then, to the appearances of things. Keep yourself watchful over them. He encourages us to watch over what is under our control because it is through our decisions and judgment that we realize ourselves. Everything else is external.

Do not sacrifice your character for anything. To do so, is to sell yourself cheaply. Focus on what matters instead.

Let’s end with Epictetus’s statement of the dichotomy of control:

"Some things are within our power, while others are not. Within our power are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever is of our own doing; not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not of our own doing."

Bad feelings are not bad.

The key lesson is that we shouldn’t let bad feelings stand in the way of how we live.

# Day Fifteen {#day-fifteen}

## Theory {#theory-14}

Excellent work making it to week 3\. In this final week, we’ll focus on the ultimate purpose of Stoic philosophy. 

The term “virtue” is not exactly in fashion. It has certainly been misused by hypocritical moralizers.

But that doesn’t make it any less important.

Fundamentally, it just means character.

Don’t think of it as simply following rules, think of it as a matter of developing skills and habits. 

When we’re virtuous we are who we were meant to be. When we act without virtue, we express poor character and make mistakes. 

A climber develops their ability until they are an excellent climber. With practice, they transform their identity. After years of work, every move they make is perfect and second nature.

We ought to practice to become virtuous people in the same way. If we put in the effort, making correct decisions won’t be difficult – it will just be who we are.

One can get a grip on virtue by bringing the classical virtues to mind: moderation, justice, courage, and wisdom.

The ancients believed in the motto “nothing in excess”. Moderation is the key to self-discipline. Someone who lacks discipline acts impulsively. The concept of the Aristotelian mean is useful here: define virtue as the space between two vices. Self-control can be understood as the state between the vices of extreme self-denial and excess. 

An excellent climber exemplifies moderation by devoting themselves to their craft, without letting climbing turn into an obsession that harms their relationships. 

The virtue of courage falls in between recklessness and cowardice.

The reckless proceed without fear and blunder their way through life, making clumsy mistakes. The coward stays on the sidelines, never entering the fray.

Imagine three people climbing to the top of the mountain. The reckless will proceed without fear and hurt themselves — perhaps others too. The fearful will never start and will never see the beauty of the summit. The courageous will work through their fear and proceed with prudence and purpose. They’ve prepared for the climb, brought the right equipment, and know their route..

Justice concerns how we treat others. We’re social creatures and, in the Roman emperor and Stoic Marcus Aurelius’s words, our proper task is to treat others well.

Often, We’re too selfish to bother treating other people the way they deserve. We’d rather do what is convenient for us. To make this easier we come up with stories to justify our behavior. Acting with justice demands giving others and yourself their due, and therefore thinking and acting in a way that achieves that.

The last classical virtue is practical wisdom. Wisdom is knowing how the world works. Knowing what matters and acting on it. The wise are confident in who they are. They know their role in life. They do not make foolish mistakes. They are able to combine practical knowledge with a sense of the bigger picture.

Satisfaction in life is a matter of acting with virtue. 

Imagine if you realized these four virtues every day in every decision. You, and those around you, would be great in a way that many conquerors, artists, and athletes have failed to be. Such a life is the one we are meant to have.

Let’s end with a line from the Latin writer Publilius Syrus:

"Would you have a great empire? Rule over yourself."

Think of self-control as moderation and discipline. It’s about knowing what is needed and what is enough.

In his book the *Mundanity of Excellence*, Daniel Chambliss tells the story of Mary Meagher:

# Day Twenty {#day-twenty}

## Theory {#theory-19}

Today we will reflect on wisdom.

What is wisdom? 

It’s the ability to know what is good and apply it in action. People who are wise are able to see situations from multiple angles and choose the correct course.

It is the most difficult virtue to describe. 

In a way, most of our life is spent as a march toward wisdom. 

It is difficult to attain. Yet most of us see glimpses of the virtue, in ourselves and those around us.

A key Stoic role model was Socrates. The oracle of delphi pronounced that he was the wisest of all men.

This surprised Socrates because he did not know what his wisdom consisted in.

So he asked others deep questions about virtue, the good, and wisdom. Again and again, he found that many people claimed to know what they did not know. 

People’s professed beliefs and values fell apart after simple questioning.

Then it hit him\! He was wise because he did not say he knew things he didn’t. 

Socrates did not claim knowledge when he didn’t have it. Instead, he had the honesty and humility to claim knowledge only when he did. 

Wisdom doesn’t consist in knowing everything. It consists in knowing enough and acting on that knowledge.

Unlike others, Socrates did not go around making unearned pronouncements of knowledge. But he did act on the principles he was confident in. 

He aimed to act virtuously even when it was hard. So when his services were required in war, he served bravely.

And when the city of Athens persecuted him for his philosophy, he did not give in. He refused to forsake his quest for knowledge.

In this way, Socrates’s wisdom consist of acting with humility when he did not possess knowledge, and acting with firm resolve when he did.

Seeking wisdom is a lifelong project. Never stop practicing. Whether it’s with the Stoa app or other tools in your life. Most importantly, apply these lessons in your life.

Epicurus expressed this idea as:

“The knowledge of error is the beginning of deliverance.”
