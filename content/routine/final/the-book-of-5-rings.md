# **Content: The Book of 5 Rings \- Theory**

PT: The Book of 5 Rings  
PD: An introduction to Warrior Zen philosophy

PS: Japanese samurai Miyamoto Musashi teaches us about the philosophy of Warrior Zen

### **CT: Who was Musashi?**

CD: Learn about the famous warrior philosopher. 

What if there is something that connects all great people? Something that they all have in common about how they approach life, from great entrepreneurs and artists to community leaders and philosophers. Yes they are all good at different things, but what if we could narrow in on what all great people have in common, and focus on developing that in particular?

Zen has an answer to that question. What connects all great people is an understanding of the Way \- a direct indescribable knowledge of the nature of the current moment and how to navigate it successfully.  

Now, you can’t use words to describe something that is indescribable. Even if I understood it myself, I couldn’t tell you what the Way is, any more than I could give you a set of words that instantly make you a great artist, parent, or entrepreneur.

You have to understand it yourself through study and practice. However, 400 years ago a Japanese samurai, undefeated in over 60 duels, retired to a cave to write down his understanding of the Way. He knew his book couldn’t give people the answer, but it might help them get there quicker.

This course is about that book, Miyamoto Musashi’s famous Book of 5 Rings.

Zen is a type of Buddhism that emphasizes practice, particularly meditation and contemplation, over the study of theory or scripture. But it is also heavily influenced by the Taoist traditions, with a focus on finding ‘the way’ through life, the thing that connects all exceptional people, and runs through each domain of life. 

To understand ‘the way’ of anything, is to understand how to live well. However, Zen also argues that this way is indescribable. We can’t just talk about it and write it down, we have to come to understand it through practice.

The zen warrior is someone who pursues ‘the way’ through the art of combat or battle. This is their practice or craft, along with meditation, that enables them to understand how to live.

So, someone practicing Zen aims to live well, but is skeptical of how far theory can take them, so they begin a practice. The Zen warrior involves combat, conflict, and battle as a key part of that practice. 

Some listening to this may be professional soldiers, or combat athletes, but not many. So why should you care about Warrior Zen if you do not think of yourself as a warrior? 

As we will see, Warrior Zen is a life philosophy that focuses on using high-stress or challenging  conflicts in our life and turning them into a way to live better. It is about mastering strategy, learning how to succeed in difficult situations, and then applying that strategy back to other parts of your own life.

We will study Warrior Zen through the eyes of Miyamoto Musashi. Musashi was a Japanese swordsman who lived in the 17th century. Known as perhaps the greatest sword fighter of all time, he was undefeated in over 60 duels, many of which were to the death. 

He was also renowned for his innovative style of fighting, which often broke convention, and his use of two swords, instead of the more common single sword style. If all Musashi had done was fight with a sword, he would still be well known today. But he was also a philosopher.

Months before his death, at nearly 60 years old, he isolated himself in a cave and wrote down his philosophy of swordsmanship and life in the Book of 5 Rings.

There are many lessons in this book, about how to fight and how to live.

It also has many parallels to Stoicism. Remember that the Stoics also compared living well to a kind of combat.

Marcus Aurelius wrote that: 

“The art of life is more like the wrestler’s art than the dancer’s, in respect of this, that it should stand ready and firm to meet onsets which are sudden and unexpected.”

Zen does not always use the same language as western philosophy. It does not talk about pursuing virtue, or eudaimonia. Instead, it focuses on achieving what is called “The Way”. Zen philosophers aspire to life in accordance with the Way \- even if it is not easily describable. 

One key theme of Musashi’s writing is that we can learn about ‘The Way’ through whatever craft we pursue excellence in, although as a zen warrior his craft is battle.

So there is a ‘Way’ in swordfighting, and by understanding that, Musashi gets closer to understanding how to follow ‘The Way’ in life. 

This is similar to the Stoic’s craft analogy. Living well is a skill to be learnt like any other, so our other skills teach us about living. 

Keep this in mind when studying Musashi. You may not be a fighter, but you do have other skills. They are all connected. Learning about ‘The Way’ of swordfighting will teach you about how to draw, run a business, or be a better friend.

### **CT: Ring 1 \- Earth**

CD: The foundation of strategy.

Have you ever thought about how some part of your life has prepared you well for another? How your experience as a parent has helped you at work, or how your sport has made you more resilient for life? 

Musashi is obsessed with these kinds of connections. He takes them as evidence that there is a “Way” \- something that connects all people that are excellent at their crafts, and all people that live well.

The Book of 5 Rings is broken into 5 books, Earth, Water, Fire, Wind and Emptiness.

Musashi’s first chapter is the Book of Earth. It outlines the foundation of Musashi’s strategy for fighting. 

But he doesn’t start by talking about a samurai or an army general.

Instead he starts the chapter by talking about a carpenter. 

We can learn about how to fight well, but understanding how a skilled craftsman builds a house. Carpentry and fighting both require ‘skill’, and we can learn about skill by looking at anyone skillful. 

A head carpenter must have a detailed plan for the house, and know how to best put to work a team to execute that plan. 

They must understand the relative strengths and weaknesses of their team members. They must understand the materials they have available to them. This type of wood is strong and so can be used here. This type of wood is relatively weaker, and so must be used in a different way.

They must be efficient, vigilant, and attentive to the smallest details. They must understand how to direct the morale of their team, and inspire energy in those carrying out the plan.

All of this, Musashi points out, can be said of the general in war. If a head carpenter is like a general, then a junior carpenter is like a soldier. They must follow instructions to play their part in the larger plan. They must become skilled in their specific tools, understand them well. They must study the head carpenter closely, so they can take on their role one day. 

The soldier or general can learn from the craft of carpentry. Obviously the material of each craft is different. But, when you push a level deeper, the ideas that connect excellence in any craft are the same.  

This idea \- that all crafts are connected in the same way \- is crucial to Musashi’s thinking.

A consequence of this idea is that Musashi is skeptical of any school of fighting that argues you ‘must’ practice their exact style to be successful. Musashi writes:

“You should not discriminate against or develop special likings for certain weapons, or anything else. Too much of any one thing is the same as not enough.” (Page 72\)

He follows this by saying:

“Without imitating other people, choose what is appropriate for your own size and shape…For generals or footsoldiers, to like or dislike certain things is bad.”

In other words, when trying to fight or live well, we should not get too attached to one answer or approach. 

This is because ‘excellence’ is found in the things that connect all crafts and styles. The attention to detail, understanding of team morale, careful planning and execution. 

The expertise of the fighter is found in those abilities \- and those are not dependent on any style. As we’ve seen, the carpenter has them just as well as the samurai. 

Like Stoicism, Musashi frustratingly rejects hard and fast rules. He will not tell his students exactly what weapon to use, or exactly how to swing it. Their development as warriors depends on developing their own sensitivity to the correct way to approach each fight, based on themselves and their situations. This doesn’t mean there is no wrong way to fight or live. The argument is not that you cannot be a poor carpenter or warrior, only that what it means to become excellent will be dependent on yourself and the situation. 

Musashi moves between the topics of carpentry and war because he believes that excellence is transferable across domains. Whether it is the craft of the samurai, the carpenter, or the craft of living well, the rules are the same \- have a plan, pay attention to the details, understand your own tools or talents and how to use them, and know how your team, the people around you, fit into that plan. 

There is a “Way” that connects all things. When we pursue mastery in any craft, we do not just learn about that particular skill. We learn about the “Way” as well.

### **CT: Ring 2 \- Water**

CD: Strategy for combat.

In the Book of Water, Musashi turns his attention directly to sword fighting. His second book outlines the specific intricacies of his style of combat.

Musashi talks about the state of mind required to navigate a fight to the death. In this lesson we will focus on this idea, how to prepare the mind to do great things.

Musashi says:

“In daily life or in strategic moments, without changing it even the slightest bit, make your mind broad and straight. Without trying to rein it in tightly, or letting it go slack at all, and in such a way that it does not lean in any particular direction, centre your mind exactly, and set it quietly vibrating.” 

Musashi says we prepare for battle with a quietly vibrating mind that does not lean in any one direction. One theme is this idea of centering the mind. Not allowing it to be pulled too strongly by anything external, or lean too far. In another passage he says:

“At quiet times, your mind should not become quiet, and when for some reason you are hurried, your mind should not become even a little rushed. Your mindset should not be affected by your body, nor your body by your mind.”  

This is similar to the Stoic idea of the passions. When we experience extreme emotions, the Stoics think our souls literally expand or contract away from the objects we fear or desire. Emotions are the movement of the soul or mind. For another example, we are all familiar with the physical effects of strong emotions. The anxious feeling of being unrested; the quickening of our heart or the trembling that accompanies fear; the hyperfocus of anger or the sensation of being weighed down by grief. 

The mind that does not lean in any particular direction is one that is not pulled or moved by any extreme emotions.

Musashi also tells us to set our mind quietly vibrating. So the ideal mind is one that is not moving too much, but constantly moving a little. This part is, at least in part, about attention.

To use a sport metaphor, in hockey they tell you on defence to keep your legs bent and stay moving. Don’t stand still even if the puck is on the other end of the rink. That way, if the other team gets the puck and rushes you, you are ready to react, and not caught flat footed.

Quiet vibration could be like this. Not leaning in any direction, but ready to react. 

In your day to day \- pay attention to what you notice, and what pulls your mind out of the centre. Notice what gets you frustrated or puts you in a bad mood. Then, pull your mind back to the centre. 

Musashi’s point is that all our success in sword fighting or life, begins with, and depends on, the state of our mind. In his words:

“Pay close attention to the state of your mind, and do not worry about your body — fill any places where your mind is lacking...”

To be strategic, we must start with a mind that is centred but ready for action.

### **CT: Ring 3 \- Fire**

CD: Strategy for high-stakes moments.

Book 3 is the Book of Fire, and it is concerned with the moment of combat. If Book 1 is on general strategy, and Book 2 is on swordsmanship in particular, then Book 3 is about applying those skills in the moment of life or death battle.

You might be thinking, ‘well I don’t plan on getting in kill or be killed fights’. And that is probably for the best\! But think of this book as being about performance. The moment when your preparation is put into practice and it pays off, or you fail. Remember, because living well is a craft, we can learn from studying people excellent at any skill.

Musashi opens with very specific fighting advice. Keep the sun at your back so it blinds your opponents. Take the higher ground. Keep up the intensity so your opponent cannot assess their surroundings.

The rest of the book contains other strategies: Break your opponent completely before moving on; never try the same technique more than twice; instill fear in the enemy.

Fire is about strategic moments \- the moments that determine success or failure. The most important lesson in the book, the one that captures the essence of fire, is the notion of ‘getting across’. Getting across is the idea of extreme focus during pivotal moments. It is a metaphor, based on a ship’s captain crossing a particular dangerous body of water.

Musashi writes:

“In strategy, during battle, “getting across” is crucial. Adapting to the opponent’s condition, understanding your own abilities, using principles to get through: this is just the same as a good ship’s captain making a sea crossing. Once you have “got across”, then you can relax again.”

Musashi’s point is that while we practice self-improvement our entire lives, some moments matter more than others. You may practice Stoicism for years, but applying it at the grocery store is not the same as pivotal moments of tragedy, or insult, or temptation. Our personal philosophy matters most when a loved one dies, we face a major setback, or there is a dilemma that asks us to be at our best. Or, in Musashi’s case, when we have a duel to the death.  
Real pivotal moments are temporary. They can require a degree of focus and attention that is not sustainable long term. Like a swordsman in battle, or a captain navigating a treacherous part of the sea \- you need to summon almost super human focus to handle these situations well.

However, to be ready for these moments you must train your mindset during everyday, normal life.

As Epictetus said: 

“Whenever you encounter anything that is difficult or pleasurable, or highly or lowly regarded, remember that the contest is now: you are at the Olympic Games, you cannot wait any longer, and that your progress is wrecked or preserved by a single day and a single event.”

If you treat the small things like they are important, as important as competing in the olympics, then when it is time to get across, you will be able to. 

In summary, some moments in life will require you to summon the very best of yourself \- your best attention, focus, and morale. Musashi reminds us to notice these. 

But what is in our control is to prepare for these moments in the mundane. By taking our lives seriously, we will be prepared for serious moments.

### **CT: Book 4 \- Wind**

CD: Learn from other schools and disciplines.

Book 4 is the Book of Wind. It covers other strategies of fighting. To understand how others approach combat is to better prepare yourself to face them, and to understand what is unique about your own style.

In ancient philosophy, this would be like studying the Epicureans or the Cynics, in addition to the Stoics. Both to learn from them, and better understand what sets Stoicism apart. 

Musashi argues that other fighting styles fail because they privilege something as good, and become dependent on it.

Some schools argue the long sword is better than any other weapon. Some say it is best to always strike with as much force as possible. Some argue for each student to memorize a specific list of techniques, stances and footwork.

Musashi argues that these are mistakes. If the goal is to win, then the best way to win always depends on the circumstance. To commit to any one sub-style \- to become dependent on a long sword for example \- is to limit yourself.

He writes:

“In our Way, we do not think of forcing anything even a little; having the wisdom of strategy, the heart of it is to understand how to win in any way at all. You should work through this carefully.”

That is the key to Musashi's style. Do not force anything. To take the best option at that moment. 

A jazz musician is an example of someone who knows how to not force things. Jazz musicians are some of the most technically proficient musicians out there. They are experts in theory and performance. But they are also able to improvise and innovate because they aren’t rigid or focused on only playing the exactly perfect notes.

In Stoicism, this improvisation connects with the idea of the indifferents. The Stoic aims to live a good life by having virtue. That is their goal. So everything else is indifferent. We cannot call health good in all circumstances, or call sickness bad. We can’t make a rule that you need money, or you need to avoid being insulted.

What matters is if in your particular life, in your particular situation, do these things help you show good character or not.

It might be right to donate a kidney to a loved one. That damages your health, but it is the right choice. It might be right to take a stand for what you believe in, and then be insulted for it. You are choosing insults in that situation.

Musashi’s argument in the Book of Wind is that, when we make absolute rules, we limit our options. If your goal is to win, take any path that gets you to victory.

Likewise, if your goal is to live well, make any choice that supports that. Don’t get caught up in rules.

### **CT: Book 5 \- Emptiness**

CD: See the true way.

The final book, the fifth ring, is the Book of emptiness. This book is about the things that cannot be known. 

This is a short and esoteric book, focused on describing what cannot be described. 

It is meant as the culmination of Musashi’s work and moves beyond the idea of sword fighting, to living well in all things, or following the true Way. 

For this lesson, we will focus on one key idea. Musashi writes about how \- in trying to understand the world \- we distort it:

“From the perspective of the true Way, the major “models” and standards in the world, seen together, are all the biases of individual minds and, based on these distortions, go against the true Way.”

Musashi’s point, in part, is that when we attempt to describe the world in a theory or model, when we try to define something, we color, distort, or influence it. In our attempt to see the world as it is, we necessarily put it through a filter. There is no objective perception.

To make an analogy to Stoicism, the Stoics talk about impressions, or the representations of the world we perceive. 

Like Musashi \- the Stoics argue that these impressions are often not accurate, they are distorted by our own perspective. A significant part of self-improvement is removing those distortions, and seeing things as they really are. 

For example, if you have the impression that someone was cruel to you and then judge them to be a terrible person who deserves to be punished \- that is one perspective by which you can view the world.

But that person likely views themselves as the hero in their own story, or if they acknowledge their wrong doing, probably see themselves as a victim of circumstance. They only treated you that way because someone else was cruel to them, they might say.

That is another perspective. Another angle we can look at the same situation from. Neither is objectively accurate though according to the Stoics. You are not a victim of their actions any more than they are the hero. These are stories we tell ourselves. These stories distort things and often hurt us.

A large part of Stoic practice is achieving an objective perspective. Identifying the bias in your impressions and working to change that perspective. This is why the Stoics practice shifting their perspective to a more objective one.

But Musashi’s idea goes a bit farther. He calls out the models of thinking themselves.  
   
Remember his quote:

“From the perspective of the true Way, the major “models” and standards in the world, seen together, are all the biases of individual minds and, based on these distortions, go against the true Way.”

So Musashi even questions Stoicism’s ability to make sense of the world. Can it help us? Or does it just risk giving us a different distorted model to think within? Are these ancient philosophies just other ‘styles’ that we adopt \- ways of seeing the world \- that distort things but cannot represent the truth?

Regardless, Musashi’s point is about recognizing that in trying to define the world, we are adding something to it. Things are not how they initially appear, and they almost never fit cleanly into the boxes we make for them. You are neither a hero nor a villain, and if you seek the Way you’ll have to resist easy answers. That is a valuable reminder.

### **CT: Putting it all together**

CD: Bring Warrior Zen into your own life.

We’ve covered in this course several of Musashi’s best lessons from his Book of 5 Rings: Earth taught us to be flexible in trying to win, Water \- to have a constant attention to what is going on. Fire taught us to channel a fierce focus in key moments and Wind to never force anything. Emptiness reminded us to watch out for distortion and bias.

All of these are valuable lessons on their own about how to win in fighting and in life. But the key thread that connects them is to always pursue the way \- this is Zen.

If you consider yourself a philosopher, then you are already after the way, although western philosophy doesn’t frame it like that. Western philosophy instead focuses on wisdom, understanding the truth of the world so you can live in accordance with it. Philosophy literally means the love of wisdom. 

But time and time again, Musashi has seen people lose their focus on this target. Instead of the truth of how to win in a specific moment they become more concerned with demonstrating a certain style, or following a set of rules.

They cannot see the way in the moment because they are so concerned with this focus on a ‘style’ \- a specific notion of how one should fight or live.

Stoicism, in many ways, is a style. It says there are certain things to do or not do.

But if you consider yourself a philosopher, then you must be after the truth. 

Marcus Aurelius writes in his meditations:

"If anyone can refute me—show me I’m making a mistake or looking at things from the wrong perspective—I’ll gladly change. It’s the truth I’m after, and the truth never harmed anyone."

Apply this humility to your entire life including your own life philosophy.

At its worst, Stoicism can become just like any other style. It becomes a rigid framework that limits our ability to see things as they really are. The Stoic talks about virtue, passions and impressions the same way another school talks about always using a long sword or taking this particular stance with your feet in battle. These are useful tools, but should not become impediments to living well.

Musashi would say to use them to your benefit, retain what is helpful and discard what is not. But always focus on the truth or the Way. This is the Zen approach to life.

But as a final point, it is important to remember that Musashi was not just teaching Zen \- he practiced Warrior Zen. Warrior Zen focuses on using high-stress or challenging conflicts in our life as a method to learn about the Way.

As Musashi wrote:

“As members of the warrior class, you should learn the Way of Strategy correctly… — the Way that warriors practise never becoming even a little darkened, your heart never becoming lost, never neglecting your training for a single morning or any other time, polishing the twofold heart of unmoving spirit and active will, honing the dual eye of perception  
and seeing — without the slightest cloud in your vision.”

We are not samurai like Musashi, but we can all approach our lives as warriors.
