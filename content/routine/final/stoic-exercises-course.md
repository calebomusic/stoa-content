# The View From Above

## Quote

“Look round at the courses of the stars, as if you were going along with them; and constantly consider the changes of the elements into one another; for such thoughts purge away the filth of the terrene life.” 

Sentence\_id: 1091

## Theory

This course is about practice. It consists of a variety of exercises the Stoic adopted, developed, or perfected. 

All of them are aimed at self-transformation. The goal is to change who you are, how you think, and what you feel. 

First, you learn the ideas of Stoicism, but then you must digest them, make them a part of yourself. Philosophy without practice is inert.

You can use this course in two ways. First, as a survey of the fundamental Stoic exercises. Progress through the course each day to learn about an exercise, practice it once, and then, once you’ve finished, dig deeper into the ones you like best. On Stoa we offer courses that deep dive into many of the exercises discussed here, like Memento Mori and The Dichotomy of Control.

Second, this course should be a resource to be returned to again and again when looking for a refresher on Stoic practice. For that reason, each lesson, except for this introduction, will be designed to stand alone. 

With that context in place, we will introduce the first exercise of the course: the view from above.

Most Stoic exercises begin by changing the way we think about something. They call to our attention a flaw in our reasoning, or a truth we keep forgetting. At Stoa, we say they work by changing our *perspective*.

Perspective is about what we focus our attention on. Whether we focus on things in our control, or outside of it, ways we have been harmed, or opportunities for improvement, the difference is perspective.

One perspective mistake is to get pulled into the mundane aspects of our lives. We overemphasize the struggles of the day to day. We take seriously what should be handled lightly. 

Marcus Aurelius made the same mistake. As an antidote the emperor of Rome proposed adopting the view from above. 

When we are struggling with perspective, we should, in Marcus’ words:

“Look round at the courses of the stars, as if you were going along with them; and constantly consider the changes of the elements into one another; for such thoughts purge away the filth of the terrene life.” 

This is taking the view from above.

This exercise entails viewing your experiences and life from a broad, cosmic viewpoint, seeing events as a small part of the universal nature and as interconnected to other things. 

It helps to diminish the perceived importance of trivial matters and to reinforce your connection to the larger natural order.

For example, when stressed about a job interview, a break up, or a fight with a friend, you might imagine looking down on your city from up in the sky, only to realize that you are one of millions of people, each going about their lives, each thinking their particular story is both more important, and less connected to others, than it really is.

The view from above exercise reminds us that the things that can seem most important to us, the stresses of the day to day, are maybe not as significant as we supposed. And, it reminds us that we are part of a larger community and universe. We can find meaning and perspective in that, even when struggling individually.

# Premeditatio Malorum

## Quote

“With regard to whatever objects give you delight, are useful, or are deeply loved, remember to tell yourself of what general nature they are, beginning from the most insignificant things. If, for example, you are fond of a specific ceramic cup, remind yourself that it is only ceramic cups in general of which you are fond. Then, if it breaks, you will not be disturbed. If you kiss your child, or your wife, say that you only kiss things which are human, and thus you will not be disturbed if either of them dies.”

Sentence\_id: 20-23  
Source: Handbook

## Theory

The Stoics aimed at equanimity, a pleasant and even keel emotional state. It is a way of being that allows for happiness without emotions that are too extreme. Someone with equanimity will respond to situations with urgency and intensity when they demand it, but they will never be overwhelmed.

One way to cultivate equanimity is to reduce the number of times you are surprised. When we are caught off guard, unexpecting, we often overreact. This is why time does such a good job of reducing many extreme emotions \- the situation has not changed, but it no longer surprises us. We’ve had time to process. 

The exercise of Premeditatio Malorum is like processing a situation in advance. This latin phrase means to think about bad things, malorum, before they happen \- premeditatio. 

The purpose is to lessen the impact of these events if they do occur. 

Epictetus provides an extreme example in the handbook. He says:

“If you kiss your child, or your wife, say that you only kiss things which are human, and thus you will not be disturbed if either of them dies.” 

Sentence\_Id: 23

I have ready stories of professors, scholars in other fields, who have rejected Stoicism because they were disgusted by this line. 

But Epictetus is not telling to pull away, or avoid kissing your loved ones. He is asking you to remember that they are mortal, and that mortal things can die.

It is unnecessary to be surprised by things that are inevitable or extremely likely. We are likely to be laid off at some point, to have a loved one get sick, to struggle with our social lives. We are all certain to either die or see our loved ones die. 

But these things surprise us because we aren’t willing to look them in the face, and so their impact is doubled. Reflect on these things beforehand, not to be pessimistic, but to be realistic. 

At a more advanced level, the Stoics would also argue that many of these perceived misfortunes are not actually misfortunes at all. 

For example, maybe I fear romantic rejection. But by considering it beforehand, I can examine what it is really like. If I ask a person out, and they say no, what has really happened to me? Well, I have gained information, I have learnt that I am not compatible with that person. And that is not such a bad thing to experience when you consider it in detail.

Many seemingly bad situations are indifferent. They are external situations that we can navigate well if prepared for. By considering them beforehand, we are more likely to face them well.

# Voluntary Discomfort

## Quote

Set aside a certain number of days, during which you shall be content with the scantiest and cheapest fare, with coarse and rough dress, saying to yourself the while: “Is this the condition that I feared?" (Seneca, Letters on Ethics, Letter 18\)

Sentence\_id: 50648

## Theory

A key tenent of Stoicism is that we can learn from hardship. So why not, the Stoics ask, introduce some hardship into our own life?

This is the exercise of voluntary discomfort. 

This exercise involves intentionally taking on things that are challenging, or uncomfortable. This could be physical, such as a cold shower, exercise, or eating plain food. But, it could also be things you find socially or emotionally challenging, such as public speaking or exposing yourself to something you fear. 

In his Moral Letters, Seneca uses the example of eating and dressing simply:

“Set aside a certain number of days, during which you shall be content with the scantiest and cheapest fare, with coarse and rough dress, saying to yourself the while: “Is this the condition that I feared?" Sentence\_id: 50648

The benefits of this exercise are multifaceted. As Seneca describes it, part of the benefit of doing uncomfortable things is that you often realize they are not as bad as you expected them to be. We fear many things because we do not know what they are like in reality. We have never actually experienced them. But we are more afraid of the potential than the reality. 

Imagine Seneca in the Roman court, surrounded by people willing to betray each other, to act viciously, to retain their power and money. But if you’ve lived in a plain apartment, and ate plain food, it must seem absurd. Is this the experience everyone fears so much? Is this what they are trying to avoid when they betray each other? 

So voluntary discomfort is on one hand focused on learning about the world, and your capacity to handle the hardest parts of it well.

But life is full of hard things already. The other benefit of voluntary discomfort is picking circumstances that are challenging but ultimately low stakes. You pick situations that give you opportunities to practice your Stoic training safely.

A cold shower is not the same as losing a loved one or becoming sick. But that is the point. It is a safe place to practice.

Think of voluntary discomfort like introducing opportunities to test Stoicism into your life. The main benefit is not the challenge, we all face challenges in our lives, but the controlled setting that provides a chance to evaluate our progress.

# Daily Reflection and Self-Scrutiny

# Quote

When the light has been removed and my wife has fallen silent, aware of this habit that's now mine, I examine my entire day and go back over what I've done and said, hiding nothing from myself, passing nothing by.” 

Quote\_id: 1954  
Source: On Anger

## Theory

Athletes are always measuring their performance.The runner tracks their mile time. The weight lifter tracks the weight they can lift. The musician tries to nail that scale at a faster and faster tempo. They practice like this because it works. What gets measured gets managed.

As Stoics, our craft is living well. So, part of our practice should be measuring how well we actually live.

For us, it will not look the same as measuring the time of a run, or a new lifting personal best, but we can still measure our progress.

The exercise I am calling daily reflection and self-scrutiny involves engaging in self-dialogue, meditating on Stoic principles, and reviewing one's actions, judgments, and progress against those principles. The aim is to internalize Stoic ideas so that they become a part of your character. 

Marcus Aurelius’ *Meditations* is an example of the emperor committing to this practice. By all historical accounts the *Meditations* was not meant to be read by an external audience. It was Marcus writing to himself, processing Stoic ideas, and reprimanding himself where he noticed he had fallen short.

Seneca was another famous Stoic who made this a part of his daily routine. As he wrote in On Anger:

 “When the light has been removed and my wife has fallen silent, aware of this habit that's now mine, I examine my entire day and go back over what I've done and said, hiding nothing from myself, passing nothing by.” 

Quote\_id: 1954  
Source: On Anger

What gets measured gets managed, and the Stoic measures their ability to live up to Stoic ideals.

This could look like doing guided meditations on Stoa. These are often built with reflection in mind. It could look like keeping a journal, or even talking with friends about how your Stoic practice is progressing.

Regardless of the form, the point of this exercise is to regularly turn your attention from the principles of the Stoics to yourself. Ask yourself: How well are you doing at applying the 4 virtues? How often can you keep in mind the dichotomy of control? How focused is your attention on the truth? Where do you find your emotions get the best of you? Which desires for unhealthy things do you struggle with the most?

Ask these questions regularly, because this exercise works when you are practicing something specific. Stoa courses, such as our course on 7 effective Stoic habits, can give you inspiration for what to work on. Once you’ve picked what you practice, measure it. Note your progress and adjust your training accordingly.

# Memento Mori

## Quote

**Quote:** Death, for instance, is not terrible, else it would have appeared so to Socrates. But the terror consists in our notion of death that it is terrible. (Epictetus, Enchiridion 5\)

Sentence\_id: 30-31

## Theory

All of us will die. It is one of the few things everyone has in common. But because we ignore this fact, we both underplay and overplay the importance of death.

We over emphasize how terrible death is when we are surprised or caught off guard by it. Many of us live as if death won’t affect us. This, the Stoics point out, is a mistaken judgement. Death is necessary and natural. But if you don’t accept this, when it does happen you’ll be unprepared to manage it, either for yourself or for those you love.

The Stoics taught that death was indifferent, that means that it is not inherently bad. What matters is how we handle it. As Epictetus said:

Death, for instance, is not terrible, else it would have appeared so to Socrates. But the terror consists in our notion of death that it is terrible. (Epictetus, Enchiridion 5\)

A happy life involves interrogating that fear of death, and preparing ourselves to face it well. 

However, we also underplay the importance of death when we do not live with the urgency it demands. It is cliche but our days are numbered and each day could be our last. But we don’t live like it. So few of us have that urgency within ourselves. 

Memento Mori, literally ‘remember that you will die’, is the exercise of recentering death as a part of our lives. By remembering that you must die, you can live with less fear and more energy.

In practice, there are many ways to do this. You could spend time in a hospital, or work with the elderly. You could visit a graveyard, and think about how all of these people had lives, dreams, and hopes, just like you. You could talk about how you want to die, what you want your funeral to look like. You could build and tackle a bucket list with intention, as a genuine list of things that are important to do before you inevitably die.

All these strategies work by targeting our judgements. If you think something is terrible, you will be anxious and fearful. But death is not a terrible thing, only an inevitable one.

It is important, but not evil. 

Memento Mori is the practice of remembering and accepting this.

# Expanding Concern to Others (Oikeosis)

## Quote

**Quote:** “You will discover that there is no reason to take any trouble that these men may have this or that opinion about you. However you must be well disposed towards them, for by nature they are friends.” 

Sentence\_id: 1435

## Theory

We all are quick to help ourselves. In fact we spend most of the day doing that. Most of us are also thoughtful for our family and closest friends.

But what would it be like to share that same degree of concern for a stranger? What about a supposed enemy? Someone from a different political group or country? Could you even extend your compassion to the person you hate most of all?

The Stoics think we all naturally are born with a desire to protect and help ourselves. Ethical progress involves expanding your circle of concern, initially from yourself to your immediate family, then to friends, your neighbours, and ultimately to all human beings as fellow members of a universal shared community. 

The Stoics called this process of expanding concern oikeosis (oy-key-o-sis) \- Oikos in Greek means a house or home. So this is the exercise of treating more and more people like family, like they are a part of your home. As you advance in this exercise, you extend the same goodwill you have for yourself to more and more people.

Practicing Oikeiosis is more like an ethical goal than a simple exercise. In today’s language, we might call it working to expand who receives your ‘compassion’. 

We all know what it is like to immediately care about our friends and family. This exercise is the process of taking someone typically outside your sphere of concern (a stranger, a homeless person, someone suffering in a foreign country, someone of a different political affiliation or religion) \- and extending to them that same concern and consideration. It is treating a stranger like they are part of your community, because you recognize that they are \- we are all humans.

Marcus practices this exercise in the meditations. When thinking of a group of men in the court he does not like, he writes to himself: “You will discover that there is no reason to take any trouble that these men may have this or that opinion about you. However you must be well disposed towards them, for by nature they are friends.” (Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 9.27)

This is what it means to expand your sphere of concern. You must recognize that even our enemies are humans. And that they are by nature connected to us.

Like many Stoic exercises, this is done by training our perspective. The underlying point is easily argued for \- we shared something in common with all humans and that they are worth our consideration. The challenge is keeping that in mind every moment.

# Dichotomy of Control

## Quote

**Quote:** Some things are in our control and others not. Things in our control are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever are our own actions. Things not in our control are body, property, reputation, command, and, in one word, whatever are not our own actions. 

Sentence\_id: 1-3

## Theory

We often overestimate our responsibility. We think that it is our job to fix things \- our relationship, our career, our looks, our social status. And then, when these don’t go as planned, we feel disappointed, heartbroken or confused.

But these are not our job \- our job is only to fix just the things that are up to us.

In the first paragraph of Epictetus’ Handbook, he outlines the Dichotomy of Control:

**“**Some things are in our control and others not. Things in our control are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever are our own actions. Things not in our control are body, property, reputation, command, and, in one word, whatever are not our own actions.(Epictetus, Handbook 1\)

This is probably the most famous Stoic exercise. In practice the exercise involves identifying what is "up to us" \- namely our judgments, impulses, what we desire, what we want to avoid \- versus what is not “up to us” \- namely external events, other people's actions, health and reputation. The goal is to both value and focus on the things up to us. People that can do this are practically more effective and less emotionally tumultuous.

There are two aspects to this exercise. 

First, there is a knowledge piece. Can you identify what is up to you and what is not? Sometimes this is easy. I don’t control who won the 2016 American presidential election. I do not control the weather tomorrow. But sometimes it is less clear. Are my grades in school up to me? Do I determine what people think of me?

To get this part right, you must understand the Stoic idea on a deeper level. The Stoics argue that the only thing truly up to you is your character, understood as the choices you make and how you respond to the world in the moment. Nothing else is up to us, not even the things we have strong influence over. 

This is because what you choose to do is the only thing no one else can stop. You can have a strong degree of influence over things external to you, but they are never really up to you. 

This is why Epictetus would use the example of being chained up \- you can be thrown in jail by a tyrant. You can be tortured and they can take away everything you have. But they can’t control what you think and how you act \- so this is where your true freedom lies.

Once you can make that distinction consistently and correctly, then you need to focus your attention and value on the things up to you. 

In practice, there are a few ways this can work. When anxious, you can make a list of everything causing anxiety. If any of them are up to you, focus on those. If they aren’t, remember that they are nothing to you.

When you feel guilt or shame, ask yourself what is up to you? You can’t change the past, but you can make amends, apologize, or forgive yourself.  

When you feel angry because someone has harmed you, ask if you’ve really been harmed? Have they done something to your character? Or did they just do something to something external to you? Your reputation or your possessions? And if you cared about those less, would you be less angry?

You should practice these strategies, not because they just make you feel better, but because you agree with the Stoics. Our lives are only made better or worse by the choices we make.

# The Reserve Clause

## Quote

**Quote:** When you are going about any action, remind yourself what nature the action is. If you are going to bathe, picture to yourself the things which usually happen in the bath: some people splash the water, some push, some use abusive language, and others steal. Thus you will more safely go about this action if you say to yourself, ‘I will now go bathe, and keep my own mind in a state conformable to nature.’ 

Sentence\_id: 24-26

## Theory

There is an apparent conflict in Stoicism. On one hand, we should only care about the things up to us. But on the other hand, Stoicism tells us to fulfil our roles of parent, citizen, friend or otherwise.

But being good at these roles involves making plans that do not seem up to us. For example, shouldn’t an athlete want to win their game, even though it is not up to them? Shouldn’t a parent want healthy, successful, happy children?

How are we supposed to have these plans, without being disappointed or crushed when they fail? The secret is the reserve clause.

The reserve clause is when you make an earnest specific plan, add into that plan something about failure or a broader goal. Epictetus might say \- I will go shopping at the mall today, and not let the crowds bother me.

Epictetus shows us the reserve clause in Handbook 4, when he writes:

“When you are going about any action, remind yourself what nature the action is. If you are going to bathe, picture to yourself the things which usually happen in the bath: some people splash the water, some push, some use abusive language, and others steal. Thus you will more safely go about this action if you say to yourself, ‘I will now go bathe, and keep my own mind in a state conformable to nature.’” (Epictetus, Handbook 4\)

The reserve clause here is “And keep my own mind in a state comfortable to nature”. It is added to the plan of going to the bath house.

Another way to approach this is as conditional planning. When forming a plan include a conditional phrase like "If God wills it" or “As long as I can maintain my character”. For example, an athlete might say “I will do everything I can to win this game, so long as I maintain my character.” The reserve clause stops the athlete from cheating or fouling. 

It also prevents disappointment if you do not achieve your original plan, because the plan was not to win the game, but to do the best that your current character and skill allows. This allows you to set ambitious plans, but also calmly adapt as needed.

The Stoics taught that if we don’t get what we want, we will necessarily be disappointed. To want something is to be upset not to have it. This is what the dichotomy of control warns us about. 

But, we have complex desires. We may want a romantic partner, if they are the right person for us; to be successful in business, so long as we are able to balance a good family life. We want to do everything we can to win a sports game, so long as we don’t have to cheat, or to become powerful so long as we remain the kind of person that will use that power for good. 

Or, for a less grand example, we can want to spend the whole day outside, if it doesn’t rain. 

The reserve clause adds that ‘if statement’ to your desires. Epictetus wants to go to a bathhouse, IF he is able to still act well and not get upset. By adding this nuance to your plans and ambitions, you stop unnecessary disappointment and remember what is most important.

This is the key to being ambitious and Stoic.

# Obstacle is the way

## Quote

**Quote:** “That which rules within, when it is according to nature, is so affected with respect to the events which happen, that it always easily adapts itself to that which is and is presented to it. For it requires no definite material, but it moves towards its purpose, under certain conditions however; and it makes a material for itself out of that which opposes it, as fire lays hold of what falls into it, by which a small light would have been extinguished: but when the fire is strong, it soon appropriates to itself the matter which is heaped on it, and consumes it.” (Meditations 4.1)

Sentence\_id: 495-496

## Theory

Stoics are not inert, like statues. They are dynamic and adaptive. They are anti-fragile. Setbacks provide information they can use to improve, identify mistakes, and correct strategies.

They embody the idea that the obstacle is the way.

This exercise involves re-framing challenges as opportunities to grow, learn, and display the character you have already developed. It is related to the dichotomy of control. If your goal is to develop and display an excellent character, then any obstacle or challenge you face becomes an opportunity to do that. This makes challenges not something you passively endure, but something you grow from. 

As Marcus says: 

“That which rules within, when it is according to nature, is so affected with respect to the events which happen, that it always easily adapts itself to that which is and is presented to it. For it requires no definite material, but it moves towards its purpose, under certain conditions however; and it makes a material for itself out of that which opposes it, as fire lays hold of what falls into it, by which a small light would have been extinguished: but when the fire is strong, it soon appropriates to itself the matter which is heaped on it, and consumes it.” (Meditations 4.1)

The obstacle becomes the way, because what stops your external goal advances your internal goal. Like a fire, you consume what is thrown into you and adapt it for your own benefit and strength.

The opportunity in any challenge always exists, so this exercise is about reframing things to focus on it. 

This involves two steps. First, set internal goals. Second, focus on how the situation can advance your internal goal \- the development of excellent character or the achievement of happiness.  

To use a simple example, when you have an external goal, such as ‘make it to the airport by 6pm’, then something outside your control can genuinely stop you achieving that goal \- like traffic. But if you have an internal goal like ‘become the kind of person that does not get angry at inconveniences’ \- then traffic is no longer an obstacle, it is an opportunity to practice your Stoicism.

A more impactful example might be genuine failure. To use a personal example, I wasn’t successful as an academic philosopher \- I did not land a job immediately after my PhD. That felt like real failure at the time, and was a very painful moment for me.

But that failure made me ask what I really wanted out of philosophy. And what I wanted was to help share philosophy with as many people as possible. I wanted to share what we already knew about the stoics, but was inaccessible or presented as philosophical jargon. I liked teaching and living philosophy, more than hashing out the latest academic debates. This led to me co-founding Stoa \- something that suits my interests more than purely academic philosophy.

What was an obstacle to a specific goal \- become a tenured professor \- became an opportunity for the more general goal \- to live a happy life. 

These opportunities exist all the time. This exercise is about learning to notice them. Train yourself to be like the fire that consumes whatever is thrown into it to make itself stronger.

# Mindful Attention (Prosoche) 

## Quote

**Quote:** “Work, therefore to be able to say to every harsh appearance, "You are but an appearance, and not absolutely the thing you appear to be." (Epictetus, Handbook 1\)

Sentence\_id: 10

## Theory

The Stoics had an incredible psychological insight: When we first experience a situation, we don’t control our first impression, but it is up to us if we take that impression to be true. There is a gap between impression and belief, and in that gap we have the freedom to choose what we believe. 

This process of choosing which impressions are true, which are false, and which require additional information they called assent, and it is one of the things the dichotomy of control says is up to us. Prosoche, or the careful examination of impressions, is the exercise of making sure you evaluate these impressions carefully, and only assent to what you know to be true.

Imagine you are walking and someone bumps into you. Your first impression might be that they are incredibly rude, and to be angry and offended. But prosoche demands you slow down. Did they not notice you? Are they in a rush because of something important? Have they already turned to apologize for an honest mistake? Or worse, did you bump into them\!? 

Prosoche asks us to slow things down and be intentional. 

The Stoics taught that what separates non-rational animals from humans is ability to assent. When we talk about an animal running on instinct, that is because they receive impressions and they react. They don’t get to decide how to react, or to deliberate. 

When we are not mindful, the Stoics say we are living like animals. But this is not necessary. We don’t have to run on instinct. We can determine what we let affect us and what we believe to be true. This is the most important freedom of all.

This is why Epictetus says:

“Work, therefore to be able to say to every harsh appearance, "You are but an appearance, and not absolutely the thing you appear to be." 

As Epictetus reminds us, the world is not what it appears to be. But if you do not pay attention, then you might not have the chance to notice.
