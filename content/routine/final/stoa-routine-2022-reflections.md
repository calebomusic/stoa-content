## **Reflections**

## **Life \#1**

#### **Theory**

Ancient philosophy teaches us to clarify our values. It challenges us to understand what we value in life and how to live in accord with those values. It makes a great deal of difference to our lives if we think what is most important is good character, family, financial wealth, or professional success. There will be times when we have to choose between family time and professional success, or financial wealth and our character. 

If we are asked to do something we do not agree with, in exchange for money, what is the right decision? What if our children or family need that money for their health or success?

Life is filled with difficult decisions like this. And the only way to address them properly, is to come equipped with an understanding of who you are, and what you value.

The famous Temple of Apollo in Ancient Delphi, which was the home of the Delphic Oracle, had the words “know thyself’ inscribed on its entrance. It was crucial to the ancient Greeks that individuals understood who they were, what matters to them, and what kind of life they want to live.

This same Oracle was once visited by Socrates, the teacher of Plato and Aristotle. Even though he lived 100 years before the founding of Stoicism, he was admired so much by Stoics that the early Stoics were sometimes called followers of Socrates.

Socrates once said:

“The unexamined life is not worth living.”  
id: ​​1740 

In other words, for Socrates, reflection about the world and yourself is a necessary part of a good life. Take this idea with you. The precondition to practicing philosophy or stoicism is a genuine curiosity about the world, your values, who you are and who you want to be.

## **Life \#2**

#### **Theory**

No doubt, We all have vices, unhealthy habits, and poor behaviors. And philosophy helps us eliminate these. The Stoics themselves  describe philosophy as a cure for the illnesses of the soul. 

But philosophy is not just a cure for sickness, it is also like a fitness routine. It doesn’t just eliminate the bad, it also strengthens the good. 

When Stoicism teaches about virtue, it does not mean just the absence of vice. We don’t become courageous by avoiding cowardly acts, we become courageous by doing brave things.

In discussing how to console an upset friend, Seneca once said:

“This, too, will help – to turn the mind aside to thoughts of other things and thus to depart from pain. Call to mind what honourable or brave deeds you have done.” (Seneca, ID 1406\)

When we think about our own lives, we must also reflect on the good things we have done, and the things we like about ourselves. A good life is one filled with excellent actions and virtuous choices, not just an absence of mistakes. 

So even though Stoicism asks us to spend time considering role-models, and our own opportunities for self-improvement, we must be sure to balance that out with a recognition of our own progress, and victories in life. Ignorance of our strengths is just as harmful as ignorance of our faults.

## **Death**

#### **Theory**

Stoics work to accept death as a natural part of life. In fact, this is the only part of life that every human has in common. But many of us ignore death. We do not consider its inevitability, until it is forced into our attention, and affects ourselves or someone close to use.

The Stoic goal is not to avoid death, but to try and die as well as possible. The Stoic asks “Since I must die, how can I die as well as possible?”.

In the words of Seneca: “Just as I shall select my ship when I am about to go on a voyage or my house when I propose to take a residence, so I shall choose my death when I am about to depart from life.” (ID: 1351\)

In 2011 there was a nuclear crisis in Japan, when an unprecedented earthquake led to a tsunami that flooded the Fukushima Nuclear power plant. The surrounding area was contaminated with dangerous nuclear waste that can lead to life ending cancer. Amazingly, over 200 retired Japanese senior citizens volunteered to clean up the waste. 

Their reasoning was that the radiation acquired while cleaning would take time to produce cancer in their bodies, and they would either already have died by then, or would be close to death.

This selfless act is a beautiful example of how accepting the inevitability of one’s death can liberate you to do incredible things. If these senior citizens had not accepted their death, or recognized that they are likely closer to it than someone who is thirty, then they would have never offered to help.

So reflecting on death is not always a morbid exercise. It can help us live with courage, and enable wonderful actions that help others.

## **Assumptions**

#### **Theory** 

We all go through life with certain assumptions. We can take for granted how our life will go, for example that we will live forever, or get a chance to take that trip, or make that apology. 

We can make assumptions about the way the world is. For example, we can accept notions of ethics and right and wrong that others have told us, and never really question the deeper meaning of these ideas.

Western philosophy and Stoicism began with questioning assumptions. Socrates would explore the streets of ancient Athens, confronting those who claimed to know things with certainty.

One person would claim they understood justice, another would claim they understood piety, or virtue. Socrates would question these individuals and push them to explain, and question these assumptions until he discovered the belief informing them. When Socrates pushed hard enough, he would often realize there was no evidence at the bottom of this line of reasoning, only an assumption. This method of questioning was called the “Socratic Method”.

Socratic questioning can be annoying, and difficult, but it is the foundation of skeptical enquiry. It is invaluable because knowledge requires skeptical enquiry. Thinking well requires the willingness to push beyond the surface level, just like Socrates.

## **View from Above**

#### **Theory**

To properly reflect on our life, we must assume a diverse number of perspectives. Sometimes we should think about just ourselves, who we are and the kind of life we want. But sometimes we need to assume a larger perspective. We are an individual, but we are also a small part of a larger city, country, and universe. 

Marcus Aurelius once wrote in his Meditations: “One who would converse about human beings should look on all things earthly as though from some point far above, upon herds, armies, and agriculture, marriages and divorces, births and deaths, the clamour of law courts, deserted wastes, peoples of every kind, festivals, lamentations, and markets, this intermixture of everything and ordered combination of opposites.” (ID: 1659\)

Marcus, emperor of Rome, wrote his Meditations while at war. If he had only looked at this war from his perspective as an individual, it would have been much more difficult for him to make the correct decision on how to act before his troops, how to position his army and whether the war was worth fighting. Looking at a situation from just one perspective is necessarily a narrow view. It focuses our attention on certain things at the neglect of others.

Just like Marcus Aurelius worked to expand his perspective, when we feel confused or stuck in our own lives, we should try reflecting from a different view. By taking a broad perspective, we are able to imagine new possibilities, empathize more broadly, and change our paradigm, instead of approaching the situation from the same angle.

## **Character**

#### **Theory**

Of all the parts of our identity, the Stoics thought our character was the most important. But this still leaves open the question of what a good character looks like. In response to this question Seneca once wrote: “How are you to know what character is desirable, unless you have discovered what is best suited to man? Or unless you have studied his nature?” (ID: 1536).

Seneca’s idea was what a good character is depends on the kind of thing a human being is. What is excellent is determined by our nature.

In philosophical terms, this is called teleology, the idea that human beings all share a ‘natural’ function, goal, or ideal state that is dependent on our nature. Teleology was a major part of ancient ethical thought. 

To understand the argument, we can use the example of another animal besides humans, such as a dog. The Stoics would argue that we can make true or false statements about what is better or worse for a dog. But these statements depend on the nature or essence of a dog. Dog’s are social, so to isolate one and not pay attention to it would be harmful. Dog’s don’t live underwater, so to put one there would be harmful. Dog’s like to play, so to play with them is beneficial. 

None of these statements apply to all living things. If we took another animal, like an octopus or an eagle, we would get a different answer. So what is good for something, depends on its nature.

When we apply this reasoning to humans, the Stoics come up with a unique answer. The Stoics define humans as thinking things, because we alone possess the ability to be self-reflective. Because we have this incredible ability to self-reflect, the best kind of life for a human is one where they use that power well. But this does not mean an academic life of books and study. Rather, to think well, or to live rationally, means to think carefully about making the right choices about how to live, given the situation and information available to you. 

It is worth thinking about what you think your nature is. Because, according to Seneca, you cannot understand what it means to live well, unless you really understand the kind of life best suited for human nature.

## **Models**

#### **Theory**

When we reflect on the kinds of people we want to be, we can’t underestimate the value of an example. For Christians, this example can be Jesus. For Buddhists, it is the Buddha. But the Stoics have no single example of virtue that every Stoic must look up to. A Stoic gets to choose their own role model.

Seneca once wrote:

“Choose therefore a Cato; or, if Cato seems too severe a model, choose some Laelius, a gentler spirit. Choose a master whose life, conversation, and soul-expressing face have satisfied you; picture him always to yourself as your protector or your pattern. For we must indeed have someone according to whom we may regulate our characters; you can never straighten that which is crooked unless you use a ruler.” (Seneca ID:1222)

We use models as a ruler, or measuring stick. They are something to compare ourselves to. They allow us to recognize our potential, and to acknowledge when we have failed to live up to that potential. 

We can choose a model from any excellent person we know of. It can be a family member, a historical figure, or even a fictional character, like the protagonist from your favorite book.

What matters is that the measuring stick is straight, the model is a good person, and that you have that image on hand. They don’t need to be a perfect person in every sense, like a Buddha or Jesus Christ. They only need to be a target to which you aspire.

Models are particularly valuable because they reveal to us what we value. They are a concrete manifestation of the traits and virtues we aspire too. They do not just provide an example to aspire too, but also clarify what we think is worth aspiring for. And that is incredibly valuable.

You cannot begin progress without a destination in mind. So let the excellence of others serve as both a destination, and a reminder of what is possible.
