# How to Handle Tyrants

## Quote

But it is a man's own opinions which disturb him: for when the tyrant says to a man, I will chain your leg, he who values his leg says, Do not; have pity: but he who values his own will says, If it appears more advantageous to you, chain it. 

Sentence\_ID: 11512

## Theory

How do you deal with bad people who have power over you?

In Epictetus’ time, he called them tyrants. Today, we might call them something else, but they still exist. The terrible boss, the bully, the corrupt politician. 

First, Epictetus says to recognize that they are not as powerful as they seem to be. They might have a puffed up ego, but even with all their money or authority they don’t have power over the important things. They can’t make themselves a good person. They can’t make you a bad person. They only have power over a few things, namely external things, indifferents.

Second, recognize that if you fear them, it is because you also value things outside your control \- these indifferent things. Epictetus says to his students:

“But it is a man's own opinions which disturb him: for when the tyrant says to a man, I will chain your leg, he who values his leg says, Do not; have pity: but he who values his own will says, If it appears more advantageous to you, chain it.” 

Sentence\_ID: 11512

Epictetus' point is that if you desire things other people control, you will find them terrifying. If you desire a promotion, you will be focused on appeasing and kissing up to your terrible boss.

If you want to be popular, you will have to laugh along with the jokes of the bully.

The Stoic focuses on virtue. The Stoic does what they think is right with the things up to them, and accepts that the Tyrant will do the same. And if it seems right to the tyrant to chain up or exile the Stoic, they will do that.

Tyrants control the domain of externals. And if we worship externals, the things not to us, then they will control our happiness and our emotions. 

Epictetus gives the example of a man who has been elected to an important position in Rome: 

“Has a man been exalted to the tribuneship? All who meet him offer their congratulations: one kisses his eyes, another the neck, and the slaves kiss his hands. He goes to his house, he finds torches lighted. He ascends the capitol: he offers a sacrifice on the occasion. Now who ever sacrificed for having had good desires? For having acted conformably to nature?”

Epictetus is right here. We claim to be Stoics but celebrate a promotion or raise. 

We should focus on celebrating our internal victories. Elsewhere in the Discourses, Epictetus compares these moral victories winning the Olympics. The moral dilemma, the moment of bravery, the time you did what is right, that is your Olympics.

So ask yourself, when someone has scared you or seemed terrible, what external success or reputation are you holding onto? 

Tyrants on their own are not as powerful as they seem. Don’t give them power over you by valuing the things they have the ability to give or take away.

# Examine appearances

## Quote

It is the chief and the first work of a philosopher to examine appearances, and to distinguish them, and to admit none without examination.  
Sentence\_Id: 11576

## Theory

We all have a craft that we take seriously. 

If we love to cook, we stress over getting the ingredients right. If we are a fan of a sports team, we track every statistic and know every player. If we love music, then we hunt for the best albums, or the best equipment to listen to it. 

For a Stoic, their craft is knowledge, or understanding the world as it really is. As Epictetus says:  
“It is the chief and the first work of a philosopher to examine appearances, and to distinguish them, and to admit none without examination.”  
Sentence\_ID: 11576

However, while many of us say we want to be Stoics we don’t actually show this in our actions. We are not fanatical about our craft in the way we focus on our other interests.

Epictetus makes the comparison between his students seeking the truth, and the attention a banker puts into spotting counterfeit coin: 

“You see even in the matter of coin, in which our interest appears to be somewhat concerned, how we have invented an art, and how many means the assayer uses to try the value of coin, the sight, the touch, the smell, and lastly the hearing…. In like manner, where we think that to be mistaken and not to be mistaken make a great difference, there we apply great attention to discovering the things which can deceive. But in the matter of our miserable ruling faculty, yawning and sleeping, we carelessly admit every appearance, for the harm is not noticed.”

So you can tell how much someone cares about a goal just by observing the effort you put into getting it right. Imagine if we judged impressions as carefully as a banker evaluates a counterfeit coin?

Alternatively you can also judge someone’s commitment by how they react to the idea of failing at that craft. Epictetus’ student claim to want to be Stoic, but they feel more fear at damaging their bodies than their characters: 

“When then you would know how careless you are with respect to good and evil, and how active with respect to things which are indifferent (neither good nor evil), observe how you feel with respect to being deprived of the sight of the eyes, and how with respect to being deceived, and you will discover that you are far from feeling as you ought to do in relation to good and evil.”

The lesson of Discourses Book 1, Chapter 20 is this: You need to be focused on the craft of philosophy to be an excellent person. You have to want to be a good person as much as, or even more than, an athlete, a musician, or an actor wants to excel in their field. You have to fear failure as much as getting sick. And a Stoics craft is examining impressions, and getting to the truth of the matter.

Epictetus holds us to a high standard, so I will offer another lesson along with his. We get good at what we think about, care about, and practice.  

You would not be listening to this if you didn’t already care. So keep at philosophy until it becomes a part of you. Until you can call yourself a philosopher, like others call themselves runners, artists or musicians.

# On Admiration

Who are they by whom you wish to be admired? Are they not those of whom you are used to say, that they are mad? Well then do you wish to be admired by madmen?  
Sentence\_ID: 11600-11601

## Theory

I remember once reading an interview with David Foster Wallace, author of the famous and excellent book Infinite Jest. The journalist asked him if he was proud of all the awards Infinite Jest had won after years of working as a relatively obscure author. In response Wallace spoke to a paradox.

The paradox was that in order to become a successful author, Wallace had to inundate himself from the desire to win awards. He had created these mental justifications for why awards don’t matter: Those that judge the awards don't know what they are talking about; the game is rigged and political; who wins awards is random; real authors don’t care about awards.

To survive the challenge of being a struggling writer he had adopted these paradigms. And now that has written this great book, and received these awards, he is not able to enjoy them. He no longer desires them the way a fresh young David Foster Wallace might have.

Some might say that these were survival mechanisms, borne out of a need for psychological protection. Others would say David was naturally moving towards a truth.

Discourses book 1, chapter 21 is about how we measure success. True philosophers measure success internally. They don’t want things outside of themselves.

But many of us try to impress people. We strut around. We show off. We want to be admired. We want other people to comment on how successful we are.

There are two things wrong with this. First, you are placing success externally, which makes you vulnerable. It breaks the idea of the dichotomy of control. 

But there is an important second risk: you are letting people who are not qualified be the measure of success. This is not a job they have earned. They don’t practice philosophy, they don’t have the authority to measure success as well as you do.  

As Epictetus says about the philosopher who shows off: 

“Who are they by whom you wish to be admired? Are they not those of whom you are used to say, that they are mad? Well then do you wish to be admired by madmen?”  
Sentence\_ID: 11600-11601

You should not need to be admired. That is not the metric of success for a philosopher. But if you do look for feedback, make sure it is from people who have authority, people whose character you actually want to emulate. 

If David Foster Wallace’s approach to awards has validity, it is because it gets close to this Stoic idea: we shouldn’t care about the opinions of people we don’t respect.

# Everyone’s a Hero 

## Quote

When then does the contradiction arise? It arises in the adaptation of the preconceptions to the particular cases. When one man says, He has done well: he is a brave man, and another says, Not so; but he has acted foolishly; then the disputes arise among men.  
Quote\_ID: 11605-11607

## Theory

The American author John Barth once wrote that everyone is necessarily the hero of their own story. The Stoics would agree with this but add their own twist. They would say that no one intentionally does evil. Everyone thinks what they are doing is the best course of action available to them. If they thought differently, they would act differently. 

The Stoics had several arguments for this, but one was that we are born with a natural understanding of certain concepts, like good and bad. The Stoics called these natural understandings preconceptions \- ideas that did not require instruction. 

Preconceptions are shared across all humans. Every person just knows, from birth, that good things are to be pursued, and bad things are to be avoided. These are examples of preconceptions.

We can’t do bad willingly because our bodies won’t let us. Our minds cannot choose things we think are bad, because we have an inherent understanding that bad things are to be avoided. 

The obvious counter argument to this is through observation. Look at the terrible people, the murderers and thieves and robbers. How can the Stoics say they do not do bad things willingly?

That’s because those people think differently about what is good and bad than you do. They apply the judgement of good and bad in a different way. 

As Epictetus says, people disagree about the application of these value judgements, not their meaning:

“When then does the contradiction arise? It arises in the adaptation of the preconceptions to the particular cases. When one man says, He has done well: he is a brave man, and another says, Not so; but he has acted foolishly; then the disputes arise among men.”

There are many lessons from this \- but let’s focus on two, one for yourself and one for other people.

First, for yourself pay attention to how you apply preconceptions to particular cases. This is the Stoic idea of making good use of impressions. Watch what you call good or bad, who you call brave and foolish. These ideas cut across all people but their application doesn’t. Apply them carefully and truthfully. 

Second, remember that everyone is the hero of their own story. Everyone thinks their actions, at least in the moment, are good or at least the best they can do. No one wants to be bad, they are only ever mistaken and ignorant. This will help you empathize and understand others. You don’t have enemies, only people who have applied their preconceptions differently than you.

So apply your preconceptions carefully, and watch out for how others apply theirs.

# Against Detachment

## Quote

“But Epicurus…ventures to say that we should not bring up children. But a sheep does not desert its own offspring, nor yet a wolf; and shall a man desert his child?”  
Sentene\_id: 11671-11672

## Theory

The Epicureans were the enemies of the Stoics. They argued that pleasure was the highest good, not virtue.

In practice though, the life they encouraged was not too dissimilar to Stoicism. The Epicureans recommended a virtuous life because they argued that a virtuous person would feel the most pleasure. The virtuous person is free from jealousy, desire, anger and other painful emotions. Not to mention they would have no enemies out to destroy them. 

However, there was one major difference with the Stoics. The Epicureans thought we should separate ourselves from society. They encourage us to avoid having children, and live in a small commune of close friends. Politics is dangerous they would argue. It presents too many opportunities for suffering. Today the Epicurean might be compared to the person living off the grid \- self-sufficient and peaceful, but disconnected.

Discourses Book 1 Chapter 23 is a criticism of this Epicurean tendency for isolation.   
Epictetus sees this as weakness. You aren’t virtuous enough to navigate politics well, so you avoid it. You are not calm enough to raise your child, so you abandon them.

Epictetus says to his students:

“Epicurus…ventures to say that we should not bring up children. But a sheep does not desert its own offspring, nor yet a wolf; and shall a man desert his child?”

His point is that we criticize the sheep as dumb, and the wolf as vicious, but both still raise their children. Both still perform their social duties.

The Stoic lesson is this: living with other people is hard. It will lead to mistakes. You will lose your temper and patience. Work will challenge you, and your family will challenge you. 

But this is why we need to study philosophy and practice our virtue. Anyone can avoid getting angry by removing themselves from social situations entirely. Our goal is to be a great member of a community, which is a much higher calling.

The Stoics are often criticized for being detached \- much more often than the Epicureans. But this is an interesting example of what Stoicism actually asks of us. Stoics do not detach themselves from politics and social pressure so they can feel good. They detach themselves so that they can contribute more effectively, so they can help others. 

The Stoic detaches themselves from their child's tantrums, so they can be a better parent. It is the Epicurean who deserts their difficult child.

# Why we need difficulties 

## Quote

It is difficulties which show what men are. Therefore when a difficulty falls upon you, remember that God, like a trainer of wrestlers, has matched you with a rough young man. For what purpose? you may say. Why that you may become an Olympic conqueror; but it is not accomplished without sweat.  
Sentence\_ID: 11678-11682

## Theory

One Stoic superpower is that they always can find a silver lining to any situation. Because Stoics are always trying to get better, then anything bad becomes a learning experience. 

Marcus said that the obstacle becomes the way, that we should be like a fire that burns brighter the more that is thrown into it. Nassim Taleb 2000 years later described this idea as antifragility. Like the Hydra who grows two heads where one is cut off, we want to become stronger the more we experience.

Epictetus used the craft analogy. We are like athletes, except our sport is living well. So like athletes, we learn from our opponents.

In Discourses Book 1, Chapter 24, Epictetus says to his students:

“It is difficulties which show what men are. Therefore when a difficulty falls upon you, remember that God, like a trainer of wrestlers, has matched you with a rough young man. For what purpose? you may say. Why that you may become an Olympic conqueror; but it is not accomplished without sweat.”

I wrestled in university so these examples always stand out in particular to me. There is a frustrating truth in the wrestling room: You get better by losing. The best athletes seek out the people that can beat them, the ones they can learn from. The lesser athletes try to protect their egos.

Epictetus wants his students to frame life this way. We learn from the challenges in our lives. But even better for us, we don’t need to go to the gym. We don’t need to put on our running shoes.

These difficulties will come to us\! So embrace them when they inevitably happen. Remember that we now have a chance now to measure our progress. Don’t be discouraged. Instead, look for the meaning and purpose in what you are doing.

# Apply What You Know

## Quote

Produce your preconceptions, produce the proofs of philosophers, produce what you have often heard, and produce what you have said yourself, produce what you have read, produce what you have meditated on; and you will then see that all these things are from God.  
(Quote\_ID: 11739\)

## Theory

Is this goal of philosophy to have the exact answer to everything calculated for you, like a math problem? Is there something wrong with Stoicism, if it can’t give you an immediate and definitive answer to each situation in your life?

I hear this complaint a lot. People say that Stoicism only offers concepts, it can’t tell you exactly what is right or wrong in a given situation, only general guidelines. Some people don’t want ambiguity. They think philosophy should be binary. 

And this was true in ancient times as well. Epictetus’ students would often ask him for more direction. They would ask to be told exactly what to do in a given situation. 

But Stoicism is less like a math equation to be processed, and more like a skill to be put into action.

As Epictetus said to his students:

“Produce your preconceptions, produce the proofs of philosophers, produce what you have often heard, and produce what you have said yourself, produce what you have read, produce what you have meditated on; and you will then see that all these things are from God.”

We know the arguments. We understand their justification. At this point it is up to us to apply them.

Epictetus then goes through a list of examples of how his students break these Stoic principles: If we covet our bodies or possessions, if we flatter the powerful and rich, if we judge quickly and carelessly, if we fear being hated or publicly shamed \- in these moments we break our Stoic principles. 

Our job is to apply Stoicism, not memorize it. That’s what makes it a skill and craft we have to put the principles into action and apply them carefully to our own lives.

We don’t have a modern day Epictetus to tell us what to do, but Epictetus wouldn’t even play this role for his students.

Stoicism can provide the principles, but it is up to you to apply them.
