1. # What is high-performance Stoicism?  

## Quote 

“How much longer will you wait before you think yourself worthy of the best things and transgress in nothing the distinctions laid down by reason? You have received the philosophical precepts that you should assent to, and you have given your assent to them. What sort of a teacher, then, are you still waiting for, that you should delay setting yourself right until he comes?”  
(Handbook 51 \- Trans. Hard)  
Sentence\_ID: 302 \- 313

## Theory

There are few things more impressive, for the Stoic, than someone who lives in accordance with what they value. The wise person has no gap between what they say and what they do. They set a plan for themselves, and they immediately follow through on each part that is up to them with focus and purpose. 

But this is not how most of us live. Most of us fail to rise to our potential, even if we see it clearly. This was as true 2000 years ago as it is today. Epictetus asks his students:

“How much longer will you wait before you think yourself worthy of the best things and transgress in nothing the distinctions laid down by reason? You have received the philosophical precepts that you should assent to, and you have given your assent to them. What sort of a teacher, then, are you still waiting for, that you should delay setting yourself right until he comes?”

This course is about using Stoicism to reach your potential, to be excellent in a specific domain.

You will learn from the Stoics how to hold yourself to the highest standards, master what is up to you, and demand the best from yourself with urgency and intensity. 

We will focus on the characteristics of people who are able to achieve great things, including consistency, resilience and focus. 

You might think that it is not Stoic to care about being excellent, but this is a misinterpretation.   
It is not Stoic to think you are owed external success. I won’t ever be an NBA player and it is silly to be bitter or angry about that fact. But there is nothing wrong with demanding to be excellent at the things up to you \- I could aspire to be the best basketball player I can be, or to be a great coach or trainer. 

George Washington often cited a line from Joseph Addison’s play *Cato, a Tragedy* \- based on the life and death of the Stoic Cato. The line goes: “’Tis not in mortals to command success; But we’ll do more…we’ll deserve it.”  In any domain outside philosophy, we can’t guarantee our success, but we can do everything in our power to deserve it. 

Remember what Epictetus tells his students:

“From this moment, then, you should consider yourself worthy of living as a grown man and one who is making progress; and let whatever appears to be best be an inviolable law to you. And if you meet with anything that is burdensome or sweet, or glorious or inglorious, remember that now is the time of the contest, and the olympic games have arrived, and that you cannot defer things any longer, and that it rests on a single day and single action whether your progress is lost or maintained.”

So now you will not delay any longer. Beginning today, you will think yourself worthy of the best things. To help you get there, in this course we will cover Stoic strategies and techniques for performing at your best.

In the following exercise we will set a goal \- the target that you are aiming \- so that you approach this course with intention and focus.

Good luck.

2. # Be Realistic

## Quote

In every act consider what precedes and what follows, and then proceed to the act. If you do not consider, you will at first begin with spirit, since you have not thought at all of the things which follow; but afterwards when some consequences have shown themselves, you will basely desist from that which you have begun.   
Sentence\_ID: 14479-14480

## Theory

The Stoic does not commit to anything frivolously. They understand the entirety of their decision and choose to commit anyway.

In our last lesson, we set a goal or focus for this course. A specific domain we want to be excellent at. But before we can be great, we must be realistic. 

Epictetus once had a student who wanted to win the Olympics. An ambitious goal that many still share today. Here is what he said to him: 

“Consider here what precedes and what follows; and then, if it is for your good, undertake the thing. You must act according to rules, follow strict diet, abstain from delicacies, exercise yourself by compulsion at fixed times, in heat, in cold; drink no cold water, nor wine, when there is opportunity of drinking it. In a word you must surrender yourself to the trainer, as you do to a physician. Next in the contest, you must be covered with sand, sometimes dislocate a hand, sprain an ankle, swallow a quantity of dust, be scourged with the whip; and after undergoing all this, you must sometimes be conquered.”

Epictetus’ point is that when you say you want to be Olympic champion, it makes no sense to describe that as a single thing, like a moment in time. To become a champion is a series of decisions or actions. You will have to diet, train, and get injured along the way. And after all that you might still lose. You must realistically assess all of these aspects of the commitment, before committing yourself to it.

Becoming wealthy often means working a challenging job other people cannot or will not do, and then living below your means for decades. Or it requires taking on an exceptional amount of risk to start a business or take a new job that might fail. Becoming well-read means actually having to sit down and work through difficult texts, setting a consistent habit. Becoming fit means having to go to the gym consistently, and cutting out bad foods. 

You can’t just want the end point, you have to understand and accept the entire journey. You have to be realistic.

Epictetus continues:

“After considering all these things, if you still want to, then go to the athletic practice. If you do not consider these things first, observe you will behave like children who at one time play as wrestlers, then as gladiators, then blow a trumpet, then perform as an actor, when they have seen and admired such things. You act the same way: you are at one time an athlete, then a gladiator, then a philosopher, then a rhetorician; but with your whole soul you are nothing.”

The biggest risk of not keeping the whole journey in mind, is that you are surprised and thrown off track. Because you never accept the challenge realistically, in its entirety, you are demotivated when the difficult parts come. 

How often have you started a new hobby or lifestyle change with a moment of enthusiasm only to fail to stick to the habit? When we do this, we are acting like children who every six months set themselves on a new interest. They never commit themselves fully to any pursuit.	

The high performing Stoic is grounded in reality. Anyone can set a target or goal for themselves. But if you want to be excellent, to commit your entire soul to being a great athlete, partner, parent, entrepreneur or anything else, then you must consider and accept every part of that role. 

In today’s exercise, we will practice taking a more realistic view of our goal, so we can become the kind of person who commits to it entirely.

3. # Be Resilient 

## Quote

We must make the best use that we can of the things which are in our power, and use the rest according to their nature.   
Sentence\_ID: 10702

## Theory

If you want to be excellent at something, you must be resilient. When we talk about excellence, we don’t necessarily have to mean something you even want to do. Excellence is shown in any situation where you navigate a difficult situation well. Sometimes you will seek out challenging opportunities. Sometimes you are called to do great things that you’d rather have avoided, like be a single parent, take care of a loved one, deal with a chronic illness or injury.

So how can we be resilient through these exceptional challenges in our lives, both chosen and given to us?

In the book *Good to Great*, author Jim Collins describes a conversation he had with Admiral James Stockdale. Stockdale was a prisoner of war in Vietnam, and a student of philosophy who studied Stoicism. 

The conversation turned to which prisoners were able to survive those conditions, and which could not:

Jim asks, “Who didn’t make it out?”

“Oh, that’s easy,” Stockdale said. “The optimists Jim. They were the ones who said, ‘We’re going to be out by Christmas.’ And Christmas would come, and Christmas would go. Then they’d say, ‘We’re going to be out by Easter.’ And Easter would come, and Easter would go. And then Thanksgiving, and then it would be Christmas again. And they died of a broken heart.” 

There are a few ways to read Stockdale’s lesson. It’s partly about the dangers of unrealistic positivity, or failing to practice premeditatio malorum. But it is also about the risk of focusing on the outcome instead of the process. 

The high-performer is persistent. They do the right things, day after day, without getting discouraged or knocked off course by short term results.

Achieving something great is not done in a day. Yes, there are critical moments. There is the big game, or the important pitch**.** But the success of those moments depends on the decisions that have come before.

Stoic resilience comes from being focused on the process, not the outcomes. This is a simple, but not easy, application of the dichotomy of control.

Epictetus wrote: 

“We must make the best use that we can of the things which are in our power, and use the rest according to their nature.”

Pessimism is harmful when you underestimate your agency. You give up because you fail to see what you could do. Optimism is harmful when you are no longer grounded in reality, and you assume that something outside of your control must happen.

Sometimes hard work is not enough. Sometimes you will fail even if you feel prepared. Sometimes life will be unfair and you will be denied opportunities you feel you deserve. 

If you hold too closely to outcomes, you will be knocked off course and discouraged when you inevitably fail. 

Anyone who achieves something great focuses on the process. From the inventor who had to watch 100 inventions fail before one worked, to the writer who has a drawer full of rejected stories, to the prisoner of war who kept their focus and made it home alive.  
   
They didn’t get their hopes up. They kept their minds focused on the part that was up to them. We can’t always control the outcomes of our effort, but we can do what is in our power to maximize our chances of success.

4. # Be Consistent

## Quote

Philosophy teaches us to act, not to speak; it exacts of every man that he should live according to his own standards, that his life should not be out of harmony with his words, and that, further, his inner life should be of one hue and not out of harmony with all his activities. This, I say, is the highest duty and the highest proof of wisdom,—that deed and word should be in accord.   
Sentence\_ID: 50764-50765

## Theory

If you think about the people you admire most, they probably share this one quality. The walk the walk. They follow through on what they say they are going to do. 

Performance is very rarely a knowledge problem \- it is almost always an execution problem. We usually know what we have to do to improve, but struggle to take steps to do so. 

That is why it is so impressive when we see people who can follow through with their plans.

Stoicism hones in on the execution problem. Seneca, in his 20th letter on ethics, writes:

“Philosophy teaches us to act, not to speak; it exacts of every man that he should live according to his own standards, that his life should not be out of harmony with his words, and that, further, his inner life should be of one hue and not out of harmony with all his activities. This, I say, is the highest duty and the highest proof of wisdom—that deed and word should be in accord.”

The highest duty of philosophy is that we practice what we preach. That we bring into alignment what we claim to value and how we actually act. 

This requires some soul-searching, and goes back to the early point about being realistic. Sometimes we don’t act in accordance with our stated plans *because we don’t actually want those things*. 

Think of the teenager who claims they want to be a doctor but then spends all their time partying, or reading fiction, or learning to code. That person does not actually want to be a doctor. So if you notice a large gap between your words and your actions, reflect on if you actually want what you say you do.

But if you are certain, that you do want the things you have planned for, then the next step is to *take any instance of inconsistency seriously.* Identify any of these moments where you are failing to practice what you preach. Interrogate them, and focus on them, and build the contrary habits. 

As Epictetus says:

“First tell yourself what sort of man you want to be; then act accordingly in all you do. For in almost everything else we see this to be the practice. Athletes first determine what kind of athlete they want to be, and then act accordingly.”

If you want to be a good athlete, you just need to do the things good athletes do. Train consistently, manage your diet, care for your injuries, and follow your coaching.

If you want to be a good friend, you need to do what you know good friends do. Be available and thoughtful. Reach out to those you care about.

If you want to be an excellent person, then you probably already know most of what that involves. Simply thinking of people you admire should give you most of that content. 

The Stoics are telling us that excellence doesn’t come from some trick, 99% of it comes from just consistently executing on what we already know we have to do.  

Seneca calls this consistency the highest proof of wisdom. It is not easy, but it is that simple.

5. # Be Focused 

## Quote

The Iliad is nothing else than appearance and the use of appearances. It appeared to Alexander to carry off the wife of Menelaus: it appeared to Helene to follow him. If then it had appeared to Menelaus to feel that it was a gain to be deprived of such a wife, what would have happened? Not only would the Iliad have been lost, but the Odyssey also.   
Sentence\_ID: 11913-11916

## Theory

At the start of this course, you set an intention to become excellent or perform your best at a certain task or role in your life.

In order to do that you must focus on the things that are up to you, or in your control. From the student, to entrepreneur, to the good friend, anyone who is excellent is focused on doing the best with what is up to them.

Epictetus reminds us of the importance of focus. He tells us that the key to living well is doing just one thing that is up to us right: making good use of our impressions. Nothing more, nothing less. Excellence does not come from anything complicated.

It is just to make good use of impressions. If you do that, the rest will follow. 

Impressions are how the world seems to us in a given moment. Specific ideas, thoughts, or representations of the world that present us with choices. The Stoics pointed out thousands of years ago that we don’t perceive objective facts. The world is filtered through our subjective point of view, which can sometimes be true, and sometimes be false. 

If you are giving an important presentation or speech, that is a fact. But it is an impression that the audience seems a bit bored. It is an impression that you need to slow down how fast you are speaking. It is an impression that if this next joke doesn’t land well, then you will have no chance of leaving a good impression.

Impressions are not permanent. They are specific moments, specific thoughts, that you have the ability to react to. Stoicism, and good performance, is focused on the micro. Excellence is achieved step by step. 

To emphasize this point, Epictetus makes an analogy to the Illiad, Homer’s famous epic poem about the battle at Troy. 

Epictetus tells his students:

“The Iliad is nothing else than impression and the use of impressions. It appeared to Alexander to carry off the wife of Menelaus: it appeared to Helene to follow him. If then it had appeared to Menelaus to feel that it was a gain to be deprived of such a wife, what would have happened? Not only would the Iliad have been lost, but the Odyssey also.”

The entire Illiad, Epictetus says, is reducible to how people made use of impressions. We cannot underestimate the importance of the small choices we make. 

A stoic executes on their plan by nailing what is up to them. They can’t control the world, but they can do everything in their power towards their set goal. 

But what is in our power is just making good use of impressions.

Showing discipline is just correctly navigating an impression around something pleasurable or painful.

Being courageous is correctly handling an impression of something fearful.

Being kind is making good use of impressions about people who need our help. 

Excellence can be reduced to a series of good individual decisions over a long timeframe. So today, notice the impressions you have and do everything in your power to handle them well.

Becoming the person you want to be is not easy, but it really is that simple.

6. # Be Antifragile

## Quote

That which rules within, when it is according to nature, is so affected with respect to the events which happen, that it always easily adapts itself to that which is and is presented to it…when the fire is strong, it soon appropriates to itself the matter which is heaped on it, and consumes it, and rises higher by means of this very material. 

Sentence\_ID: 495-496

## Theory

At the start of this course, Epictetus reminded us that even though you may train to be an Olympic champion for years, after all that work, you might still fail.

Success is not up to you. Even if you do everything in your power. Even if you are realistic, consistent, inspired, resilient and focused \- you can still fail any external goal.

If you are a runner, unless you are world champion, you only won your race because someone better than you did not register. And you only came in last because no one worse than you was there.

The Stoic is not discouraged by failure, or made cocky by success. Instead, they use success or failure as feedback. It becomes information, an input into the process. 

Marcus wrote in his meditations:

“That which rules within, when it is according to nature, is so affected with respect to the events which happen, that it always easily adapts itself to that which is and is presented to it. For it requires no definite material, but it moves towards its purpose, under certain conditions however; and it makes a material for itself out of that which opposes it, as fire lays hold of what falls into it, by which a small light would have been extinguished: but when the fire is strong, it soon appropriates to itself the matter which is heaped on it, and consumes it, and rises higher by means of this very material.”

To paraphrase Marcus in more plain language, the philosopher pursues a goal or purpose and turns whatever failures or hardship it encounters into material that benefits that goal.

Their character is like a fire that has achieved enough size and power to consume whatever is thrown into it, instead of being snuffed out. 

Inspired by the Stoics, the contemporary author Nassim Taleb described this ability as antifragility. The antifragile person is made stronger by disruption, failure, and challenge \- they are the opposite of the fragile person, who is ruined by chaos.

High-performers are antifragile. They incorporate failure into the process. They learn from their mistakes. Being antifragile, like all things, required effort and focus. You must practice finding the lesson in each failure. 

But it also requires a flexibility of purpose. If your goal is to make the 2032 Olympics, or get accepted into a specific medical school, or have a specific amount of money by a certain age \- you can’t be antifragile about these goals. They are binary. You either reach them or you fail. They have no flexibility.

The Stoic’s ultimate goal is to make the right choice in every moment with what they have available to them \- so their purpose is maximally flexible. 

But \- that goal is a bit abstract. Specific goals can be framed more flexibly too. Instead of  making the olympics, aspire to achieve your potential as an athlete. Instead of being accepted at medical school, aspire to become someone that helps people with their health. Instead of a certain net worth, aspire to be financially responsible. 

Antifragility comes from practice and focus, and reframing your goals as a purpose rather than a specific achievement. 

Today, consider how you can make your goal a purpose \- so that you can be antifragile to anything outside your control.

## Theory

At this point in the course, we’ve focused on the characteristics of excellent or high-performing people that overlap with Stoicism. 

The Stoics teach us that to be excellent in life we need to be realistic, resilient, consistent, focused and antifragile. And this is all true. But none of this matters if we are not motivated.

To be successful is to do something extraordinary. To be extraordinary requires a sense of intensity and focus that is hard to sustain. 

But what makes someone driven and passionate about what they do?

Some people think this drive and intensity is something we can’t control. They say that this kind of focus comes built into you, you either have it or you don’t.

But that evidently is not true. Our motivation can fluctuate. Marcus Aurelius was a great man who lived a full life \- but even he wrote about struggling to find the motivation to get out of bed in the morning some days. 

The Stoics teach that our drive and intensity comes from perspective. How you feel depends on how you think. Change your mind and you will change how you live. 

To avoid complacency, the Stoics would focus on the inevitability of death. We are incredibly lucky to have this opportunity to live, and try to be good. In his journals, Marcus wrote to himself:

“Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good.” 

Alternatively, you do not have to focus on death itself, but instead the fact that your time is limited, and so this opportunity is special. As Marcus wrote:

“Remember how long you have been putting off these things, and how often you have received an opportunity from the gods, and yet do not use it. You must now at last perceive…that a limit of time is fixed for you which if you do not use for clearing away the clouds from your mind, it will go and you will go, and it will never return.”

The shortness of life can shock us out of apathy. But the fleeting nature of opportunities should inspire us to seize the moment. Now might be the time to start that business, make a new friend, write your book, step outside your comfort zone. 

If you find yourself with a moment to act, with your goal in reach, take that as an opportunity from the gods.

It is much better to think of how fortunate we are to have these chances, than to put pressure on ourselves or worry about failing. 

If you don’t take that chance now, it might go, and never return.
