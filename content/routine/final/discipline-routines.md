1. # Introduction

## Quote

“If pleasure is how we should measure how attractive things are, then nothing should attract us more than self-control; and if pain is how we should measure what we should avoid, then nothing should be more painful than a lack of self-control.”  
Id: 1113

## Theory

No matter what kind of person you want to be, discipline is what will get you there.

Your identity is built from action. You are what you do. 

The runner must run, the painter must create, the parent must nurture, and the Emperor must rule.

If you are to become anything, you must have the discipline to put this role into action. 

For the Stoics, this ability to act consistently was called discipline, or self-control. It was one of the four virtues, or strengths of character, needed to flourish and be happy, along with justice, wisdom, and courage.

Discipline is the virtue that helps us have a life that is consistent with our aspirations. It’s what makes us the kind of people who are able to follow through when we say we are going to learn a new skill, change our diet, or finally quit a harmful, destructive habit. 

We all want things we can’t have in the moment, and so we create plans to achieve them.

We take on a risk, and start a business or side project that may not succeed for a few years, if ever. We want to improve our relationship with a harmful vice, whether that is food, alcohol, the internet or something else, and feel in control of how much we engage with it. Or maybe we just want to be a little bit better everyday. Not change who we are, but reach our full potential in the job, relationships, and hobbies we already have.

But, as we’ve all experienced, that initial motivation or inspiration to achieve our goal will waiver. What was exciting becomes difficult or boring. What seemed possible now feel impossible.

Discipline is the difference between being the kind of person that follows through on these plans, and the kind of person that struggles and fails. This is why the Stoics thought it was necessary for happiness.

Without it, becoming the person you want to be is simply not possible. 

The Stoics were obviously not the first ones to recognize the importance of discipline. But they did have a unique philosophical insight: The Stoics saw that changing our values, how we think, is how we begin to change our behavior. 

Musonius Rufus, the gruff teacher of Epictetus, once said: 

“If pleasure is how we should measure how attractive things are, then nothing should attract us more than self-control; and if pain is how we should measure what we should avoid, then nothing should be more painful than a lack of self-control.”  
Id: 1113  
Source: Musonius Rufus  
Location: 

For Musonius, if we are going to become disciplined, then we must learn to love self-control, but we recognize it is how we get the things we want. We must also learn to hate its opposite. We need to recognize that we experience what we want to avoid because of a lack of discipline. 

Changing our behavior starts with changing our perspective. 

Discipline and self-control is built through training, like any other skill. 

In this course, we will cover Stoic strategies for developing discipline, as well as explore the important role the Stoics think discipline plays in a good life.

But for this lesson, we will start by building self-control through meditation.

2. # Cultivate Purpose

## Theory

You might think a Roman emperor would have no problem feeling inspired to work hard. But we know from Marcus Aurelius’ journal to himself that he struggled to get out of bed. He also wanted to sleep in, and waste the day away.

But Marcus has a strategy for getting over this. Marcus wrote the following in his Meditations:

“At dawn, when you have trouble getting out of bed, tell yourself: I have to go to work—as a human being. What do I have to complain of, if I’m going to do what I was born for—the things I was brought into the world to do? Or is this what I was created for? To huddle under the blankets and stay warm?”  
Id: 1734  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 5.1

Focus on your purpose. This was Marcus’ strategy for getting over his ambivalence to the day. Marcus wasn’t meant to lay in bed, and we weren’t meant to waste our days either.

Discipline comes from having a goal, or target you are aiming at, and then taking steps towards it. Discipline is ignoring the short term pleasure of a comfy bed, for the long term goal of being a great leader.

If you’re having difficulty motivating yourself to do something hard in the moment, you either don’t have a purpose or you’ve lost sight of it.

The first Stoic lesson for developing discipline is to establish a clear goal or purpose, one you really believe in, and to bring that goal to mind in moments of wavering commitment. 

For some, this will be simple. If a clear purpose is difficult for you the Stoics have a simple answer. We can look at our roles in life to establish a purpose. We all have many roles, such as a sibling, a parent, a coworker, a neighbor, or a teammate.

Pick one of these roles and make an actionable goal of it. You want to be a better son or daughter. Achieve this by being more disciplined about contacting your parents. You want to be a better teammate. Achieve this by training hard and doing your part during the game.

We all have a purpose we can find in our roles. This purpose will give us motivation.

To paraphrase Neitzche, those who have a why to live for can withstand almost any what. 

Make sure you know your why.

3. # Focus on yourself

## Quote

“Would you have a great empire? Rule over yourself.”   
Id: 1664  
Source: Publius Syrus  
Location: 

## Theory

The famous Roman writer, Publius Syrus, once said:

“Would you have a great empire? Rule over yourself.”   
Id: 1664  
Source: Publius Syrus  
Location: 

There’s a key Stoic idea here. If you want to be great, focus on the things that are up to you.

This echoes Epictetus’ famous dichotomy of control. That as Stoics we should pay more attention to the things up to us than the things that aren’t. 

This insight also applies to discipline. 

When we start to intentionally build discipline, we can make a common mistake.

We can measure success by external metrics. Instead of asking how disciplined we have been in eating less, we compare ourselves to models. Instead of asking if we improved our habits around spending and saving, we compare ourselves to those that are rich.

This is a mistake. Discipline is doing what is up to you to contribute to your goals. Your choices alone are up to you. Your success is not. So for the Stoics, being a disciplined person is about making consistent good choices, not necessarily being successful. And developing discipline is about making better choices, not being perfect.

Your success is only influenced by your choices, not determined by it.

You might have a worse physique than a model, or less money than someone you see driving a nice car. 

That has nothing to do with your discipline.

This idea applies just as much to vice as it does to success. If you are trying to break a bad habit, don’t compare yourself to others.

If you struggle with wasting time on the internet, drinking, or some other bad habit, don’t look at other people.

Instead, ask yourself, has my behavior improved over the past day, month, year? Am I making better decisions? Am I gaining a greater mastery over the domain of things up to me, my choices and decisions?

These are the questions someone cultivating disciplines asks. 

So if you are to have a great empire, rule over yourself. This means focus on yourself and not external results. Start with something small, actionable and purposeful. This alone is up to you.

4. # Accept yourself

## Quote

Id: 159  
Source: Meditations

## Theory

In the last lesson, we said that when we are working towards a goal the actions we take alone are up to us, not our external success.

No one can make you disciplined but yourself. 

But the Stoics realized that we cannot snap our fingers and become perfectly disciplined. They did not think we needed, or even could, immediately transform ourselves.

Change takes time and practice. We will fail to achieve the plans we set for ourselves. We won’t stick to our schedule. Sometimes we will sleep in, miss a practice, or break a diet. Sometimes we will fall back into a vice we wanted to avoid. 

In other words, it is not in our control to be perfect. But we can accept our imperfections. If mistakes are inevitable, the only appropriate response is to expect, accept, and move on from them.

This doesn’t mean that Stoicism makes excuses for failure. Instead, it causes us to redefine what failure is. It is foolish for beginners to expect perfection. Even as someone becomes intermediate or advanced they will make mistakes. True failure is giving up on trying to hit th mark. This reality is what Stoics call us to embrace.

Marcus wrote in his Meditations:

“When force of circumstance upsets your equanimity, lose no time in recovering your self-control, and do not remain out of tune longer than you can help. Habitual recurrence to the harmony will increase your mastery of it.”  
Id: 159  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 6.11

In other words, when things outside of your control get difficult, and you lose your discipline, the best thing you can do is accept you made a mistake and snap back into focus. 

It is as much of a skill to regain our lost focus, as it is to keep focus in the first place.  
Remember that the Stoics ask us to see the world as it really is, not as we want it to be. And even though we want to be perfectly disciplined, none of us are. 

We do not want to easily knocked off course by our mistakes. Like an athlete who always keeps their focus on the ball, if we make a mistake in one moment, we shouldn’t allow this to distract ourselves from what needs to be done next.

 If you lose your discipline, practice the skill of regaining it.

5. # Voluntary Discomfort

## Quote

“Set aside a certain number of days, during which you shall be content with the scantiest and cheapest fare, with coarse and rough dress, saying to yourself the while: “Is this the condition that I feared?”  
Id: 1236  
Source: Letters  
Location: 18.5

## Theory

So far in this course we’ve talked about discipline as doing difficult things. It is breaking your bad habits with food or substances. It is quitting your job to pursue a side project. It is showing up everyday at your maximum potential for your current job and relationships. 

But discipline is just as much about avoiding temptation. For the Stoics, discipline was the art of enduring short term pains and resisting short term pleasures in service of a long term goal.

We can train both of these skills by practicing what is called voluntary discomfort.

Seneca recommended it to his friend Lucillius as follows:

“Set aside a certain number of days, during which you shall be content with the scantiest and cheapest fare, with coarse and rough dress, saying to yourself the while: “Is this the condition that I feared?”  
Id: 1236  
Source: Letters  
Location: 18.5

In other words, we get better at doing hard things, by experiencing hard situations. We get better at going without, by living minimalistically. 

When people hear voluntary discomfort, they often think of cold showers and plain food. But voluntary discomfort is not just about self-denial. It has a few steps to make it useful.

First, make sure it is related to what you actually need to practice. Cold showers won’t help you find the discipline to finish your homework on time. 

Second, make sure it is leveled correctly. Too easy and you are not practicing. Too difficult and you are just hurting yourself for no benefit. It needs to be at a level that feels comfortable but attainable, like running just a bit faster each workout.

Third, it needs to be repetitive and progressive. A single night on the floor will not change you. But a year leaving your bed immediately when your alarm goes off will.

Finally, it needs to be connected to what you value. You need to practice being more like the person you want to be. Seneca didn’t want to be anxious about losing money, so he practiced living without a fancy bed. 

There are two ways to experience discomfort. You can either add something uncomfortable or remove something comfortable. You can add in exercise or cut out dessert. You can have a cold shower in the morning, or cut out your morning coffee. 

The best choice depends on your personal circumstances. Do you have more trouble enduring hardship, or giving up pleasure? 

Either way, do hard things related to what you find purposeful and important, and you will experience the benefits.

6. # Moderation In All Things

## Quote

“Which is recorded of Socrates, that he was able both to abstain from, and to enjoy, those things which many are too weak to abstain from, and cannot enjoy without excess. But to be strong enough both to bear the one and to be sober in the other is the mark of a man who has a perfect and invincible soul.”  
Id: 101  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 1.16

## Theory

Just like the Stoics recommend reflecting on role models, Marcus Aurelius once wrote in his journal something he admired about Socrates:

“Which is recorded of Socrates, that he was able both to abstain from, and to enjoy, those things which many are too weak to abstain from, and cannot enjoy without excess. But to be strong enough both to bear the one and to be sober in the other is the mark of a man who has a perfect and invincible soul.”  
Id: 101  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 1.16

Marcus is talking about moderation. To be able to endure pain without passing into self-denial, and enjoy pleasure without passing into excess. This makes the perfect and invincible soul. 

Discipline is not just about finding the middle path, or saying no to excess. It is not about every experience in life being a 5 out of 10 in terms of intensity. 

If discipline is the ability to exhibit self-control around impressions of pain and pleasure, then part of that is knowing when to indulge or go wild, and when to pull back.

The person with real self-control is the person who can say yes to going out with friends one night, and say no the next weekend because they have to get up early. 

It is the person who can eat cake on their birthday, but get back to their diet the next day.

The person who can skip a workout because they feel off, without it impacting the rest of their training.

Most of us struggle saying no to pleasurable impressions, and that’s why the Stoics recommend the practice of voluntary discomfort, which we discussed in our last lesson.

But once you master doing difficult things, practice also doing pleasurable things without going to an extreme.

Seneca once said:

“He is a great man who uses earthenware dishes as if they were silver; but he is equally great who uses silver as if it were earthenware. It is the sign of an unstable mind not to be able to endure riches.”  
Id: 1823  
Source: Letters  
Location: 5.6

In other words, there is no virtue in abstaining from nice things unnecessarily. As disciplined people, we should be able to go to a fancy restaurant or use silver plates without it going to our heads. 

The stable mind can endure riches as well as hardship.

The Stoic shows moderation in all things.

7. # Staying disciplined around others

## Quote

“It shows courage to remain dry and sober when the mob is drunk and vomiting; but it shows greater self-control to refuse to withdraw oneself and to do what the crowd does, but in a different way – thus neither making oneself conspicuous nor becoming one of the crowd. ## Theory

Seneca wrote in his letters to Lucillius:

“It shows courage to remain dry and sober when the mob is drunk and vomiting; but it shows greater self-control to refuse to withdraw oneself and to do what the crowd does, but in a different way – thus neither making oneself conspicuous nor becoming one of the crowd. For one may keep holiday without extravagance.”  
Id: 1235  
Source: Letters   
Location: 18.4

The Stoics care deeply about freedom. Discipline matters, because it gives us the freedom to be our authentic selves, no matter what the people around us are doing. It stops us from becoming a part of the mob. 

Some people resist the mob by standing in opposition to it. Imagine the defiant member of a counter culture, who just does the opposite of whatever is popular or normal.

Other people resist the mob by standing outside of it. Imagine the person who seeks enlightenment in a cabin or a retreat.

But the Stoics had a challenge for discipline. Can you be your authentic self while living in the city? Neither automatically reject the opinions of your community, nor leaving that community. But acting how you want to act, while fulfilling your roles to others. 

This is the final Stoic challenge for discipline and is an advanced task. Don’t think extreme discipline requires rejecting or pushing away others. Show that discipline around others. 

To paraphrase Seneca, if you don’t drink, it shows discipline to remain sober at a party or skip the part all together. This is better than breaking your habits. But it shows greater discipline to figure out how to be the life of that party, without drinking.

This is easier said than done, and so it should only be practiced when you feel comfortable. There is a reason athletes spend time around other athletes, and recovering addicts remove themselves from their previous communities.

Our environment matters. It makes a huge difference in developing and maintaining discipline.

But the end goal of discipline is always to be discipline in any environment. 

This is what the virtue of discipline promises us:

To be an example for others in how you act without judging others. To show your philosophy in what you do, not what you say. To be authentic to yourself without making others change. And to become the person you want to be without leaving others behind.
