# Introduction

## Quote

Most men ebb and flow in wretchedness between the fear of death and the hardships of life; they are unwilling to live, and yet they do not know how to die.

## Theory

Seneca wrote that

"Most men ebb and flow in wretchedness between the fear of death and the hardships of life; they are unwilling to live, and yet they do not know how to die."  
index  
sentence\_id: 1963

For Stoics, the philosopher knows how to live and how to die.

So much of our focus today is on how to live well. According to the Stoics, this requires dying well too.

What Seneca is calling out here, when we’re not living well – when things aren’t going the way we want, when our life is amiss – we’re often driven by fear. This can be seen in the fear of life such as avoiding commitment, taking no risks, isolating oneself, or shrinking away from major opportunities. It is also present in the fear of death as in a midlife crisis, health scare, or grief at someone else passing.

As Seneca says, the foolish are unwilling to live and yet do not know how to die.

As the Stoics say – our time is limited. It’s time to figure out how to do both.

In this course, we will practice daily reminders of death. In Latin, memento mori. This practice brings about urgency, meaning, and gratitude that can improve every day. Most importantly, it brings out lessons that we can use to live a deeper, more philosophical life. Many parts of today’s world sanitize life and sweep the fact of mortality away into a corner. But we can’t avoid it forever. This course is about facing up to mortality and using it to live better. 

It’s not about dwelling or ruminating on the tragedy of death. It’s about becoming someone who is willing to live.

# Urgency

## Quote

Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good.

## Theory

Life is short.

This is something everyone knows, but is difficult to internalize.

Part of doing this course or any other memento mori practice involves reminding ourselves of this fact. Otherwise, the distractions of our daily lives cause us to forget it.

Is it such a bad thing if we forget this?

The Stoics thought so. To live in a state of forgetfulness, to fail to remember our mortal condition, will result in us acting like we will live forever. Like we have the time to procrastinate. Like we have the time to put off becoming who we were meant to be. 

But we do not.

As Marcus Aurelius wrote:

"Remember how long you have been putting off these things, and how often you have received an opportunity from the gods, and yet do not use it."

Some day, there will be no more time. And we do not know when that time is. So, act with urgency. 

Marcus Aurelius counseled himself:

"Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good."  
sentence\_id: 540-541

In this way, we can see the Roman emperor practicing memento mori. 

He also shows how the practice is a positive one. He isn’t simply morbidly reminding himself that he is going to die. He is reminding himself to be good. 

By doing so, he’s reaffirming the existence and oppurtunity of everything good in the world. We just need to see it, seize it, and become it while we can.

Today, savor what is good around you. Take advantage of opportunities to be excellent. And be good. 

We do not know how many more tomorrows there will be.

# Meaning

## Quote

In everything that you do, pause and ask yourself if death is a dreadful thing because it deprives you of this.

## Theory

One of my favorite lines from the Meditations is this line from Marcus Aurelius:

"In everything that you do, pause and ask yourself if death is a dreadful thing because it deprives you of this."  
sentence\_id: 1619

There are different ways of interpreting it. Perhaps Marcus Aurelius is suggesting that death is nothing serious, because we spend our time on so many trivial things. Or he's posing a challenge: if you wish to avoid death, then live a life worth holding onto.

To my mind, he is setting a standard for everything that he does. Each action must be worth it.

Time should be spent doing what matters. 

In a way, this is easy to do. Even the small things are meaningful. The everyday interactions, simple pleasures, and beautiful encounters with nature. We live in a world that gives us the possibility of a good life. We just need to recognize it.

So, don’t live failing to see the value of everything around you.

But equally, you must prune everything that isn’t worth it. Ruthlessly eliminate time wasting activity from your life. 

That’s what Marcus Aurelius is ultimately urging himself to do. 

Catalog how you spend your time. What activity truly matters? Which ones are simply empty striving, wasteful pleasures, or worse?

Do what you can to eliminate the time wasters. 

Paradoxically, coming to terms with death involves making our lives great. Some philosophers suggested that people made their lives worse because they couldn’t face up to the fear of death. Subconsciously they didn’t want death to deprive them of something wonderful. So they made their life meaningless. 

This is a backwards strategy. A good book doesn’t need to be long. It needs to be good.

Likewise in life. A long life isn’t necessarily a good one. In fact, Seneca is known for saying “There is no life that is not short.”

For the Stoics, a good life is one where someone becomes who they should be. They achieve happiness by becoming an excellent, virtuous, and good person. 

As you go throughout your day, remind yourself of Marcus Aurelius’s line. 

And ensure that you’re spending your time well.

# Philosophy

## Quote

You wish to live; well, do you know how to live? You are afraid to die. But come now: is this life of yours anything but death? Gaius Caesar was passing along the Via Latina, when a man stepped out from the ranks of the prisoners, his grey beard hanging down even to his breast, and begged to be put to death. “What\!” said Caesar, “are you alive now?” That is the answer which should be given to men to whom death would come as a relief. “You are afraid to die; what\! Are you alive now?”

## Theory

It has been said that to philosophize is to learn how to die.

There are different ways to interpret this phrase.

A common one involves knowledge. We are mortal creatures. No one lives forever. As Marcus Aurelius would say: the only fame is oblivion.

As such, we must know that we are going to die. Of course, this is something all of us can say. But think about the difference between saying that you are mortal and receiving a mortal diagnosis. For many of us, it’s only when we receive an estimate of the time of our death that it becomes real. And yet we are dying everyday. 

To study philosophy means to come to terms with this fact. To know it, in a real sense. 

There’s another sense in which philosophizing requires overcoming the fear of death.

This too is another situation where what we say and reality come apart. Many people say they don’t fear death – but they turn into blubber during an emergency when death becomes a real possibility. 

Alternatively, some people are terrified by the possibility of their death. But when the announcement comes that their time is limited – they are filled with calm and acceptance. For the Stoics, fear of death is  just another species of irrational fear. We shouldn’t fear what we can’t avoid. Of course, we must also be prudent. We must act with caution and avoid recklessness. Indeed, a cavalier attitude towards death is often just another way of fearing it. We all know stories about the person who undertakes a risky lifestyle in order to escape parts of their lives. 

This points at what overcoming fear of death is ultimately about. It’s about overcoming the fears holding us back from living the life we’re meant to live. As Seneca wrote:

You wish to live; well, do you know how to live? You are afraid to die. But come now: is this life of yours anything but death? Gaius Caesar was passing along the Via Latina, when a man stepped out from the ranks of the prisoners, his grey beard hanging down even to his breast, and begged to be put to death. “What\!” said Caesar, “are you alive now?” That is the answer which should be given to men to whom death would come as a relief. “You are afraid to die; what\! Are you alive now?”

# Gratitude

## Quote

"The perfection of moral character consists in this, in passing every day as if it were the last.”  
sentence\_id: 1142

## Theory

Nothing lasts. Everything is ephemeral.

Part of internalizing this message requires pruning everything that doesn’t matter. Remove the trivial from your life. You literally do not have time for it.

The other aspect of this involves seizing what matters. As Marcus Aurelius said, be good while you can.

You can think of this as having two aspects: recognition and action. recognize what is good. Don’t let it pass you by. Then act. Be good.

These are intertwined. Today, let’s focus on the first part: simply feeling gratitude for what we have.

One way to practice this is to bring to mind what we’re grateful for. The people in our life. Our health, such as it is. Our standard of living. Our ability to engage with human art and the beauty of nature. 

Just bring these to mind – and then imagine your life as if they were gone. A life without the people we love, in poor health, without shelter or art could still be a good one. But it would be a difficult one. And there would be a real tragedy in losing all the people we love.

Take solace in the fact that this world is a fiction. But in another way, it is not. It’s a simple fact of life that everything is temporary. 

So, while you can, recognize what you have. Do not take it for granted.

Do not become the person who realizes how good they had it – when they are about to lose everything. Instead, take joy in what you have now. And take as much advantage of it as you can. 

Be present in your relationships. Treat your body well. Consume great art and appreciate nature. Do this before loved ones leave and your body begins to break down.

Let’s end with a line from Marcus Aurelius:

"The perfection of moral character consists in this, in passing every day as if it were the last.”

# Short / Long

## Quote

"Why of your own accord postpone your real life to the distant future? Shall you wait for some interest to fall due, or for some income on your merchandise, or for a place in the will of some wealthy old man, when you can be rich here and now. Wisdom offers wealth in ready money, and pays it over to those in whose eyes she has made wealth superfluous."  
sentence\_id: 2633

## Theory

A long movie isn’t necessarily a good one. In fact, we’ve all seen movies that could have been better if they were just a little shorter.

The same thing is true about our lives. A long life is not necessarily a good one.

In fact, it’s better to die well than live poorly. The Stoics really believed that. There are fates worse than death.

Indeed, all of us would prefer death over some lives. It’s a morbid thought, but the best of us would rather die than become an evil person or someone who suffers for no reason. 

What is the upshot of this?

Don’t focus on trying to live forever. It is impossible. Indeed, perhaps we shouldn’t even focus on trying to squeeze out years. Calorie restriction may improve most people’s lifespan. But a life focused purely on restricting calories is focused on the wrong thing. What are you extending your life for? If you have a good answer to that question, then you’re making progress.

Of course, just as a long movie isn’t necessarily a good one, a short one isn’t either. 

A good life demands prudence and caution. Not making pointless risks. I remember reading a tragic story about youths playing with electric fences. There is no point in this. 

The focus is on living well today. I hope that comes with relief. Set aside preoccupation with the past and the future. 

The good life is possible today. It doesn’t depend on external success. Just do your best now. 

As Seneca wrote:

"Why of your own accord postpone your real life to the distant future? Shall you wait for some interest to fall due, or for some income on your merchandise, or for a place in the will of some wealthy old man, when you can be rich here and now. # Death as the ultimate test

## Quote

And the person educated correctly, whoever it be, whether male or female, must become accustomed to endure toil, to not fear death, and to not become dejected in the face of any misfortune.

## Theory

The Stoic philosopher Musonius Rufus said that:

“And the person educated correctly, whoever it be, whether male or female, must become accustomed to endure toil, to not fear death, and to not become dejected in the face of any misfortune.”  
quote\_id: ?

For the ancient philosophers, death was the ultimate test. If you could face death, then you could face anything.

Think of some of the larger struggles you’ve faced in your life. The real adversity you have overcome. And bring to mind how that makes day to day struggles easier. People who have faced real tragedies and obstacles in their life and are able to live with them will find the day to day troubles of office politics, bureaucratic battles, health issues, and human conflict easy to handle.

Likewise, someone who has really come to terms with their death can do the same.  
Seneca provides an amusing example of this:

"Why do we concern ourselves with conflict and plotting? That man you are angry with – can you wish for him anything worse than death? He is going to die without your doing a thing."  
quote\_id: 1727

Memento mori makes the rest of life easier. 

That’s why Musonius Rufus includes it in the list of what’s required to become an educated person. If you don’t fear death, then fear will not hold you back from living a good life. 

It’s important to note that the fear of death doesn’t look like other fears. It's not the kind of fear that hits immediately and catches us by surprise, like hearing bad news from a doctor or turning down the wrong street after dark. Instead, it’s something that lives in the background of life. Only emerging in critical moments. In other words, it’s something that brings life to our other fears. The doctor’s news or the turn into the wrong neighborhood only catch us off guard because we’ve been pushing away the reality of death all our lives.

If we can embrace the fact of our own and other’s mortality, death will lose its sting. And so will many of the apparent evils of life. It’s easier to live a good life when fear isn’t holding you back.

So whatever fears are holding you back – let them go. You’ve spent the last few days meditating on the reality of death. You’ve taken steps forward overcoming it. You can make progress overcoming your fear of everything else too.

If you can face death, you can face anything \- office politics, difficult people, setbacks \- with the calm of someone who has faced the ultimate challenge.
