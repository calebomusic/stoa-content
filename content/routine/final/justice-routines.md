1. #  Introduction

## Quote

“He who does wrong does wrong against himself. He who acts unjustly acts unjustly to himself, because he makes himself bad.”  
quote\_id: 1928  
Sentence\_Id: 1386-1387

## Theory

How do you decide how other people deserve to be treated? 

If you promise to keep someone’s secret, and then they reveal they have done something wrong, should you keep their secret?

If someone asks for your help, and you have the power to help them, should you? Even if it comes at a great cost?

How should we respond to injustice outside our immediate control: wars, social movements, past wrongs?

For the Stoics, these are questions about Justice.

When we talk of justice today, it can evoke images of politics, protest, and court battles.

But the Stoics also think of justice as a personal virtue. It is something we act out on the day-to-day.

In 5th century Macedonia, Stobaeus compiled the original texts of the ancient Stoics and other ancient Greeks that would otherwise be lost to us. According to him, the ancient Stoics defined justice as the science concerned with distributing to individuals what they deserve.

The Stoics care about distributing to individuals what they deserve first and foremost because they care about other people. They believe we are all connected and part of the same community. Further, our community is not just our family, city, or country. We are, by nature, connected to all people, through our membership in the cosmopolis. Our community is the entire cosmos.

This view is grounded in their particular understanding of nature. As Marcus puts it:

“Injustice is impiety. For since the universal nature has made rational animals for the sake of one another to help one another according to their deserts, but in no way to injure one another, he who transgresses her will is clearly guilty of impiety toward the highest divinity.”  
Sentence\_Id: 1362-1363

We should be just, according to Marcus, because we were made to be. There is something inherently prosocial and cooperative about humans. When we act unjustly, we are acting unnaturally, because we don’t acknowledge that other people are part of the same community as us, connected to us.

In order to be just, we must be discerning and thoughtful. Justice, like all Stoic virtues, is defined as a kind of knowledge. To be just to someone, you need to know certain things. For example: Who is this person? What have they done? What is my relationship with them, and the responsibilities that come from that?

Justice requires an acute sensitivity to each situation. It’s not as simple as following a set of rules, or always maximizing happiness. An action that might be kindness for a stranger could be an unjust way to treat your family. A title justly earned by one person can be unjustly held by another. 

Because justice is a form of knowledge, the Stoics think we have selfish reasons to be just. They argue our happiness depends on it.

As Marcus Aurelius said:

“He who does wrong does wrong against himself. He who acts unjustly acts unjustly to himself, because he makes himself bad.”  
Sentence\_Id: 1386-1387

When we harm other people we harm ourselves by acting ignorantly. Acting justly is then always to the benefit of others and ourselves. The just person never loses compared to the unjust. 

In this course, we will break down each aspect of Stoic justice: Kindness, good fellowship, piety and fair dealing. Then we will describe how to be just and how to face injustice. We do this to help others, but also to make ourselves good.

2. # Kindness

## Quote

“No philosophical school is kindlier and gentler, nor more loving of humankind and more attentive to our common good, to the degree that its very purpose is to be useful, bring assistance, and consider the interests not only of itself as a school but of all people, individually and collectively.”  
quote\_id: 1929  
source: On Clemency  
location: 2.5  
translation: Kraver & Nussbaum, 2010

## Theory

Here is a simple story that demonstrates kindness.

You are walking with a group of friends, and notice someone struggling with a mechanical issue with their bike. They seem to be having a hard time, but are not actively calling out for help.

Your friend group keeps walking, you included. You have an event to make it to. But one friend stops and runs over. They introduce themselves to the stranger and offer to help. They had no reason but their own, an internal motivation to help.

That is kindness.

Kindness is a Stoic sub-component of justice. The Stoics broke their virtue down into these smaller parts, to provide a framework for becoming more just. By focusing on a small piece of justice, we can make noticeable progress, like an athlete drilling a specific technique.

Kindness was defined in a simple way. It is voluntarily doing good actions. 

One way to think of kindness then is like an ethical reaction time. When a kind person sees someone struggling to carry something heavy, looking like they could use a kind word, or trying to fix a broken bike, they spring into action. They notice opportunities to help, and are glad to take those opportunities.

Think of kindness like a kind of sensitivity to opportunities to help combined with a desire to do so.

You could have all reasoned with your friends that helping that person fix their bike was the best thing to do. Some of you might have even felt a tinge of guilt for not helping, either in the moment or later. But only one person sprang into action. That’s the person who had maintained a state of voluntarily doing good actions. They needed no more motivation.

Contrary to their gruff reputation Stoics viewed themselves as a very kind school. As Seneca puts it:

“No philosophical school is kindlier and gentler, nor more loving of humankind and more attentive to our common good, to the degree that its very purpose is to be useful, bring assistance, and consider the interests not only of itself as a school but of all people, individually and collectively.”

source: On Clemency  
location: 2.5  
translation: Kraver & Nussbaum, 2010

But the Stoics didn’t think of kindness as inherent. It was something we build along with the rest of our character. No one is inherently a kind or unkind person. This is a muscle you build through practice. 

If kindness is defined as a state of voluntarily doing good, you need two things to put it into practice.

First, you need to understand the value of the common good. You need to think it is worth helping other people. This is what makes your kindness voluntary. You need to cultivate a love for human beings as Seneca puts it.

Second, you need to pay attention to the common good. You need to notice opportunities to help others. Someone who values the common good may be persuaded to help someone, but they won’t necessarily spring into action. The kind person is paying attention. They are mindful. Like your friend who notices the man with a broken bike, kind people notice opportunities to help. 

So let’s practice being kind by building our attention and focusing on the common good.

3. # Good Fellowship

## Quote

“For we are made for co-operation, like feet, like hands, like eyelids, like the rows of the upper and lower teeth. To act against one another then is contrary to nature; and it is acting against one another to be vexed and to turn away.”

quote\_id: 1930  
Sentence\_id: 369-370

## Theory

Posidonius might not be as well known as Marcus Aurelius or Seneca, but he was an important part of Stoic history. He was the head of the Stoic school at Rhodes, and a key innovator in what’s called the middle period of Stoicism, the period between the founding of Stoicism and the famous Roman Stoics. Posidonius is always part of a story that demonstrates the Stoic sub-virtue of good fellowship.

Thousands of years ago, Pirates were terrorizing the Romans and threatening their grain supply. Pompey the Great was tasked with dealing with the problem. It would have been tempting to simply destroy the pirates entirely. But the Stoic Posidonius gives him radical advice. Instead of executing them all, Posidonius said to Pompey, remember what you have in common with the pirates. They are people, even if they are not Romans, trying to improve their position and find success.

Pompey heeded this advice. Instead of destroying them all, he executed a few and resettlement the others.

This story embodies the idea of ‘good fellowship’, a subcomponent of Stoic virtue.

Good fellowship is defined as a knowledge of what it means to treat members of a society equally. To treat people as their membership in the community deserves.

The Stoics took this idea of fellowship far. They were the first school to advocate for the idea of Cosmopolitanism. In ancient Greece, people were divided into city-states, like Athens and Sparta, each with its own citizenship. Athens and Sparta were each called a polis, which is the Greek term for a city-state.

Athens owed Athenians certain protections and benefits, but these rules didn’t wholly apply to visiting Spartans, and especially not to non-Greek foreigners.

Exile was so feared back then because it meant losing your community. You had no polis anymore. The Stoics argued that our polis, our community, was the cosmos. 

So when Pompey spares the pirates, it is not out of pity, or turning the other cheek. He merely treats them how he might treat misbehaving Romans: execute the main bad actors and give the others another chance.

He punishes the pirates, but does not exterminate them. He treats them like they deserve punishment, but not like he owes them nothing. 

But good fellowship is in Pompey’s interest too. The Stoics recognized there is something unnatural about treating people as if we had no connection to them. 

That is why Marcus says: 

“For we are made for co-operation, like feet, like hands, like eyelids, like the rows of the upper and lower teeth. To act against one another then is contrary to nature; and it is acting against one another to be vexed and to turn away.”

Sentence\_id: 369-370

We won’t always work together. We can’t also be functioning towards the same goal. But cooperation is natural. It is how things should be.

Treat everyone you come across as if you are part of the same community, because you are.

4. # Piety

## Quote

“Injustice is impiety. For since the universal nature has made rational animals for the sake of one another to help one another according to their deserts, but in no way to injure one another, he who transgresses her will is clearly guilty of impiety toward the highest divinity.”  
Quote\_Id: 1014  
Source: Meditations

## Theory

You might be surprised to see piety, respect for the gods, as a part of Justice. But the Stoics had an interesting argument for this. 

In the last lesson, I argued that the Stoics thought taking care of other humans was natural, it is the way things should be. We are all members of the cosmopolis and so we owe each other the kind of good fellowship we give people from the same hometown. 

But if cooperation is natural, then to fight is unnatural. And remember, the Stoics thought of God as nature, as the universe. So in a way acting unjustly is going against what nature wants, the natural way of things.

As Marcus puts it:

“Injustice is impiety. For since the universal nature has made rational animals for the sake of one another to help one another according to their deserts, but in no way to injure one another, he who transgresses her will is clearly guilty of impiety toward the highest divinity.”  
Quote\_Id: 1014  
Source: Meditations  
Location:

If impiety is disrespecting the divine, then we are impious when we are cruel to each other, because Nature made us to get along. 

Now not everyone believes in the Stoic god, and so maybe you are resistant to the idea of divine piety. But regardless of your belief in a Stoic god, Stoicism is about living in accordance with nature, both your human nature, and your place in the broader system of things.

As Stephen Hawking wrote in the conclusion of his 2010 book, The Grand Design: 

"The fact that we humans, who are ourselves mere collections of fundamental particles of nature, have been able to come to an understanding of the laws governing us, and our universe, is a great triumph."

According to Hawking, it is a great triumph that humans can understand the universe they are made from. Stoic piety is about celebrating that triumph and developing our understanding of nature.

Piety is the sub-component of virtue that reminds us to play our part in that system. To not be the part that creates tension, disarray, or pulls other people out of alignment. 

We respect nature by fulfilling our role. And our role is to work well with others.

5. # Fair Dealing

## Quote

If I can get them with the preservation of my own honor and fidelity and greatness of mind, show me the way and I will get them; but if you require me to lose my own proper good that you may gain what is not good, consider how inequitable and foolish you are.  
quote\_id: 1931  
Sentence\_Id: 130 

## Theory

Justice is simple when we think of how we act with strangers. Be kind to those you pass on the street. Let go of any insults or perceived slights. Work together to help others. But real life is messy. We have year-spanning relationships of different kinds: Lovers, friends, business partners, and family.

How do we treat these relationships fairly?

The sub-virtue of ‘fair dealing’ is defined as a knowledge of how to deal with one’s neighbours without incurring blame. In other words, it is about understanding what you have to do, to be a good person in normal interactions.

How do you conduct business fairly? What is an appropriate thing to ask of your family? Where do you draw a line with a partner? These are questions of fair dealing.

One Stoic insight is that when it comes to fair dealing, we always have to fulfill our side of the relationship. Epictetus, responds to a student as follows:

Is a brother unjust? Well, keep your own situation towards him. Consider not what he does, but what you are able to do to keep your own faculty of choice in a state comfortable to nature. For another will not hurt you unless you please. You will then be hurt when you think you are hurt.   
Sentence\_Id: 199-203  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 

The rules of fair dealing exist regardless of how others act. The decency we owe others is not changed by their behavior. We don’t trick cheaters, or lie to liars. We don’t bully bullies. Kindness and good fellowship apply in all circumstances.

Another Stoic insight is to keep fair dealing in mind as the limit of what is permissible. Anything is allowed when interacting with others, so long as it passes the test of being fair.

Many people think the Stoics oppose success, wealth, or fame. But they don’t. They oppose getting these things unjustly. As Epictetus puts it when referring to acquiring external things:

If I can get them with the preservation of my own honor and fidelity and greatness of mind, show me the way and I will get them; but if you require me to lose my own proper good that you may gain what is not good, consider how inequitable and foolish you are.

Think of fair dealing as the guardrails to your businesses and relationships. Breaking up is not unfair, but it is unfair to avoid a hard conversation with your partner and cheat. Prioritizing your business is not unfair, but treating your employees cruelly to make extra money is. 

The Stoics are fair in how they deal with others. They would never sacrifice their own values for external things. Remember, as Marcus said, to act unjustly is to harm the other person and ourselves.

If our character is what matters most, we don’t trade it for anything.

6. # Confronting Personal Injustice

## Quote

“Do these things befall me deservedly or undeservedly? If deservedly, it is not an insult, but a judicial sentence; if undeservedly, then he who does injustice ought to blush, not I.”  
quote\_id: 1932  
source: On Firmness  
location: Chapter 16  
translation: Stewart. 1889\.

## Theory

Stoic justice is about acting with kindness, good fellowship, piety, and fair dealing. 

But how does a Stoic confront being treated unjustly? How do we respond in a Stoic way to a world that does not live up to these ideals? 

The non-Stoic might say we respond to injustice with anger, or revenge. You need to get even, some would say, it’s only just.

But the Stoic doesn’t get angry at these things. 

The reason the Stoic stays calm is that they know most of the things that upset people don’t actually harm us. As Seneca puts it, whenever someone treats us in a way that seems unjust, we should ask ourselves:

“Do these things befall me deservedly or undeservedly? If deservedly, it is not an insult, but a judicial sentence; if undeservedly, then he who does injustice ought to blush, not I.”  
source: On Firmness  
location: Chapter 16  
translation: Stewart. 1889\.

Sometimes we deserve how we are treated. When we are excluded from a group, or lose a friend, or someone doesn’t return our call, sometimes we had it coming, and need to reflect on our own behavior. It is not an injustice just because we don’t want it to happen. It doesn’t deserve our anger just because we wanted things to be different. It is a judicial punishment.

But in cases of genuine injustice, prejudice, bullying or harassment, the Stoic remembers that the other person is harming themselves. They are destroying their character, not yours. They should blush and be embarrassed by how they’ve acted, but we don’t need to get angry.

Epictetus even reframes poor treatment as a benefit. He says, in conversation with a student:

Is my neighbour bad? Bad to himself, but good to me: he exercises my good disposition, my moderation.  
Sentence\_Id: 14629-14630

We don’t have to think of our rude or cruel interactions as harming us. They are encounters with sick, confused individuals, and we can turn those to our benefit too. We can treat these as opportunities to develop our kindness, good fellowship, piety or fair dealing.

For example, we can be like Epictetus, who, when he heard he had been insulted publicly responded that the insulter must not have known him very well, otherwise he wouldn’t have forgotten to mention all of Epictetus’ other faults too.

When we want to preserve our equanimity in the face of hardship, keep in mind this Stoic division from Seneca: If I deserve to be treated this way, there is no injustice. If I don’t deserve to be treated this way, then they have harmed themselves, not me.

## Theory

The last lesson explained how the Stoic faces personal hardship. Whether their treatment is deserved or undeserved, they don’t complain or lament. 

But the Stoic is not apathetic to broader injustice at the scale of a community, a city, or a country. Living with Stoic justice means putting kindness, good fellowship, piety and fair dealing into practice to improve the world. 

A Stoic approach to broader justice is based in role ethics. The Stoic reflects deeply on their own life, and where they can make effective change. The Stoics understand their role, however small, in making a more just society. As Epictetus said:

Remember that you are an actor in a drama, of such a kind as the author pleases to make it. If short, of a short one; if long, of a long one. If it is his pleasure you should act a poor man, a cripple, a governor, or a private person, see that you act it naturally. For this is your business, to act well the character assigned you; to choose it is another's.  
Sentence\_ID: 98-101

Maybe you are in a position of influence, like Marcus Aurelius or Seneca. If so, you might have a role in large-scale change.

Maybe you are only in a position of influence over your immediate family and community. Then maybe the way you enact change is by leading through example, teaching others, or just improving a single person’s day.

We are ineffective and unhappy when we try to exert change over domains we have no efficacy or power. When we focus our attention on the spaces we can’t control we suffer.

This can be at the macro or micro level. It is the same mistake to think we can easily change the behavior of a family member as it is to try and change the culture of a country.

Stoicism tells us to focus on where we are most effective. We must double down our efforts on where we can make the most impactful change. 

But this advice cuts both ways. We can overestimate our capacity to change things, but we can also underestimate ourselves. We often underestimate the difference kindness and fair treatment can have on those we interact with.

The Stoic is not nihilistic when approaching justice, but they are pragmatic. Some are in positions to make substantial change. But not everyone. There was only one Roman emperor in Marcus’ time. Epictetus helped others by being a teacher, not by trying to become emperor. If you can change your immediate circle for the better, if you can treat those around you how they deserve to be treated, that is enough for a just life. 

We all have different roles to play, but we can all play them justly.
