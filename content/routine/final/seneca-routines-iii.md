# Letter 8

### Theory

Ordinary life is hostage to chance. Men and women’s thoughts and dreams are at the mercy of Fate and randomness.

The Stoic Seneca is one of the most astute observers of this fact. In his 8th letter to Lucilius he writes:

“What Chance has made yours is not really yours.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 8.10

He continues:

“Fortune does not capsize us – she plunges our bows under and dashes us on the rocks.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 8.4

He means that we can achieve health, great relationships, and comfortable finances – only to lose any of these things in a moment. A single accident, random illness, or freak event can send our lives on a completely different path.

The things that we care about most are fundamentally not in our control. We can, and should, do our best to maintain our health, relationships, and vocation, but sometimes the world has other plans. 

If you fully invest your wellbeing in external things, then your wellbeing – your happiness – is not up to you. Given the randomness of life, you’re bound to be unhappy. Nothing lasts forever. Seneca constantly reminds his readers and friends of the power of fortune. 

That’s why the philosopher focuses on the internal. He does not put his hopes in avoiding pain or achieving external success. This is the meaning of the Stoic phrase: “Cease to hope and cease to fear.”

By investing their happiness in what is up to them, the Stoic robs chance of its power to hurt us.

A single accident can upend our lives, but nothing outside of our own choices can ever capsize us.

The Stoics counseled seeing everything as on loan from fate. Our health, comfort, and wealth are temporary. They don’t define us.

We can enjoy them when they are present, without believing that they are necessary for a good life. 

For Seneca, the only thing that’s necessary for happiness is good character. That’s something fortune cannot take away from you.

# Letter 9

### Theory

In Seneca’s Letter to Lucilius Letter 9 he writes:

“For what purpose, then, do I make a man my friend? In order to have someone for whom I may die, whom I may follow into exile, against whose death I may stake my own life, and pay the pledge, too.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 9.10

Stoic friendships are not transactional or opportunistic. They require trust, effort and sacrifice.

Some mistakenly think of Stoicism as a philosophy for the lone individual. After all, isn’t the focus on building an inner citadel? These people, even some Stoics, see Stoicism as a philosophy that tells you to build a mental barrier between yourself and other people – so that other people cannot harm you.

But this is not the right perspective.  
   
The Stoics recognized that we were rational *and* social creatures. We must live with other people. No man is an island. 

The point isn't to put up a barrier between ourselves and others, but to live well with them.

So, the Stoics saw friendship as a beautiful thing. Friendships improve our lives and are worthwhile for their own sake.

And, as Seneca says, the wise appreciate these:

“Precisely as he seeks an object of great beauty, not attracted to it by desire for gain, nor yet frightened by the instability of Fortune.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 9.12

Just as the philosopher is attracted to beauty in nature, so he recognizes what is good in other people too. When he does, he puts in the effort to build an excellent friendship.

But someone may ask: does this mean that the Stoic is placing their happiness into the hands of Fortune? If you care about a friend, you will be disappointed when they are hurt or make mistakes. That’s only natural. But isn’t that unStoic?

The Stoic view is that what makes a good life is up to you. Even when facing tragedy or injustice you can live well. However, that doesn’t mean you cannot care about others.

So when Seneca says:

“The Supreme Good calls for no practical aids from outside; it is developed at home, and arises entirely within itself. If the good seeks any portion of itself from without, it begins to be subject to the play of Fortune.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 9.15

He’s talking about our ultimate purpose – to be excellent people. The kind of happiness that comes from a flourishing character cannot be harmed by other people. It’s fully up to us. Others cannot take it away.

But we’re social creatures – and so we must cultivate friendships. That is what the ancient Stoics did and what Stoics do today.

Does that come with adversity and risk? Yes. Stoicism is about overcoming adversity and managing risk well.

So – keep that in mind as you go throughout your day.

The best friendships are realistic. They don’t strive for what cannot be. Instead, they’re two individuals present for each another – in whatever activity they choose.

# Letter 10

### Theory

Seneca once told a story about Crates.

Once Crates saw a young man walking by himself. He asked him what he was doing all along.

“I am communing with myself” – the young man replied. 

“Please be careful”, responded Crates, “You are communing with a bad man\!”

The moral of the story: in many cases, we cannot think well by ourselves.

When we’re stuck in our heads, we don’t get the feedback we need from others or the world. Isolation can breed rumination.

Thinking back to Letter 9 – that’s another reason why philosophers like Seneca insisted that we develop strong friendships.

Good friends call us out when we need it and keep us on the path. Surround yourself with people who can do that.

At the same time, self-improvement is ultimately self-directed. As CS Lewis said:

“Those who leave their manners behind them when they come home from the dance or the sherry party have no real courtesy even there. They were merely aping those who had.”  
id: ?  
source: four\_loves

The drive to always improve comes from within. It shouldn’t vary by circumstance. Other people help us channel that drive and help us become better people. But we need to foster that desire ourselves. 

The Stoic challenge is about building strong character that is enough on its own but also shows in how you interact with others.

In a way, the Stoic is like an athlete who recognizes that only they are responsible for playing their position well, but that that necessarily involves playing with others.

Just like the athlete we must take responsibility and improve ourselves – but also cultivate an environment of self-improvement.

We must steer between two risks: complete dependence on ourselves and dependency on others.

If we’re dependent on ourselves alone then we’re like the man that Crates called out. If you’re a master you can live by yourself – but most of us are not at that stage yet. Even if we were, we have lives that are already bound up with others. We need them and they need us.

But we can’t be fully dependent on others. The drive to improve must come from ourselves. Otherwise, we risk becoming like the person who CS Lewis describes: we’re virtuous because that’s what other people demand from us – but move us into another situation, we go back to our unhealthy ways.

The way through involves holding tight to the desire to be excellent – and then letting others help us improve.

# Letter 11

### Theory

Speeches could make or break a man in Rome.

Things haven’t changed. We’ve all heard how important first impressions are. Business leaders and influencers are quick to point out that those first moments when we meet someone – be they a friend, interviewer, or role model – are essential.

The internet makes this even more complicated. In addition to thinking about how we present to others face-to-face, we need to manage the randomness and chaos of our internet presence too.

Inevitably, sometimes we mess up. Not all first impressions go well. Whatever it is – a presentation, meeting, or brief exchange – we’re going to mess something up.

Seneca wrote about this very issue in his 11th letter to Lucilius.

To be exact, he writes about blushing while giving a big speech. Today, we could think about stuttering, misspeaking, and other signs of nervousness. As I said, a misstep like this, for a man aiming for Roman honor and prestige would have been exceptionally embarrassing.

And yet Seneca writes:

“Wisdom can never remove this habit; for if she could rub out all our faults, she would be mistress of the universe. Whatever is assigned to us by the terms of our birth and the blend in our constitutions, will stick with us, no matter how hard or how long the soul may have tried to master itself. And we cannot forbid these feelings any more than we can summon them.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 11.6

Seneca is saying that some of us are naturally more prone to stutter, put our foot in our mouth, sweat, or simply be nervous. We all have different talents and weaknesses to work through. This is natural.

Because we’re imperfect creatures – blushing, mispeaking, and such are unavoidable. This is something that even wise men and women must deal with.

In his letter, he cites the great men of Rome and their struggles. These were impressive military men who, despite their best efforts, gave speeches to the Roman Senate that sucked. 

Yes, first impressions are important. They matter for our careers and relationships. But none of us are perfect. It’s important to focus on the essentials, and not ruminate on past embarrassments or try to eliminate the possibility of future ones.

Dealing with these missteps is part of the path.

You can think of your own role models. They’ve had plenty of embarrassing failures. Use this as a reminder to relax whenever you make a misstep.

Whenever you start to ruminate, refocus your attention on moving forward. Come back to that inner drive to be excellent. To achieve happiness by taking responsibility for what is up to you – and taking joy in what is not. 

As you go throughout your day, call to mind a role model. Remember that they too suffered. If they are worth their salt they have a treasure chest of cringeworthy moments. But they move forward despite it all. Perhaps they even laugh when looking back.

# Letter 12

### Theory

Seneca wrote his letters to Lucilius in old age.

As such, they were an opportunity to reflect on his own mortality.

He notes how his memory is failing him and how those he grew up with are gone.

Many are surprised by death. Everyone knows it will happen, but its possibility isn’t felt until too late.

Stoics are different. Our mortality is top of mind for Stoics. It provides a sense of urgency and gratitude.

Philosophers keep the temporal nature of things close. They know nothing lasts. Appreciate life while you can. 

This is an energizing philosophy. Reject complacency. Capturing this attitude, Steve Jobs once said:

“Remembering that you are going to die is the best way I know to avoid the trap of thinking you have something to lose. You are already naked.”

Seneca finds this same sentiment in Heraclitus’s phrase:

“One day is equal to every day.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 12.7

Saying:

“Hence, every day ought to be regulated as if it closed the series, as if it rounded out and completed our existence.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 11.8

In other words, live every day with the mindfulness that it could be your last.

Life is a balance – you want to see things from the right perspective. You can’t live today exactly like it is your last, because you don’t know if it is. But obviously, you can’t act like you’ll live forever.

Seneca encourages us to act with the whole of our life in mind – while being present in the moment. Build your character now. Show your virtue by making decisions worthy of your whole life, not just the short term.

And at the end you can say:

“I have lived; the course which Fortune set for me is finished”

He knew that his life was at risk and he spent his final years away from court, writing letters for his contemporaries and posterity. 

Use his example as motivation today.

# Letter 13

### Theory

Today, we’ll continue meditating on the writings of Seneca.

Letter 13 To Lucilius contains one of his most famous lines:

“There are more things, Lucilius, likely to frighten us than there are to crush us; we suffer more often in imagination than in reality.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 13.4

One of the central teachings of Stoicism is that how we interpret the world shapes how we experience it.

Seneca continues:

“What I advise you to do is, not to be unhappy before the crisis comes; since it may be that the dangers before which you paled as if they were threatening you, will never come upon you; they certainly have not yet come. Accordingly, some things torment us more than they ought; some torment us before they ought; and some torment us when they ought not to torment us at all. We are in the habit of exaggerating, or imagining, or anticipating, sorrow.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 13.5

In other words, there are three ways our thinking causes us suffering.

We exaggerate harm, thinking things are worse than they are.

Not only that, we imagine fictional threats and disasters, causing ourselves suffering without reason.

Finally, we fret about the future. We forget that the future has not yet arrived, and when it does, we can meet it with the same resolve we possess in the present.

These strategies are similar to the teachings of cognitive behavioral therapy – a professional therapy inspired by Stoicism. 

A key part of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy involves identifying cognitive distortions. These are thought patterns that keep us from living the life we want to lead.

Consider these three: Catastrophizing, Fortune-telling, and Negativity Bias.

Catastrophizing is exactly what it sounds like. It’s what Seneca is talking about when he says “somethings torment us more than they ought.” When we’re focused on the worst case or always responding to setbacks like they are the end of the world, we hold ourselves back from living well. 

Fortune-telling involves catastrophizing the future. We think things must turn out for the worst – without significant evidence. Crucially, it involves ruminating on the future. In Seneca’s words: some things “torment us before they ought.”

Last, Negativity Bias causes us to disqualify the positive parts of our life. We focus on the negative and pay no mind to the positive aspects of ourselves and our circumstances.

Each of these involves exaggerating, imagining, or anticipating sorrow.

To improve, we can identify when our thinking has gone astray. Then return your attention to the present. See things as they are. Let your negative thoughts come, let them be, and let them go.

Don’t let them drive your life.

# Letter 14

### Theory

Seneca tells us that the practice of philosophy involves moderation. In his 14th letter to Lucilius he writes:

“Philosophy itself, however, should be practiced with calmness and moderation.”  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 14.11

Philosophers do not try to convert others to their cause. They do not brag about their practice. They do their best in every situation and focus on improving their mind. That’s it.

Sometimes philosophers withdraw from the world. They do not participate in the social or political causes of the day.

They practice the message from Robinson Jeffers’ poem *Times of Disturbance:*

“The best is, in war or faction or ordinary vindictive  
life, not to take sides.  
Leave it for children, and the emotional rabble of the  
streets, to back their horse or support a brawler.”  
id: ?

Seneca’s friend, Lucilius writes that many Stoics, however, did not always appear to be moderate. 

He brings up the Stoic hero Cato the Younger – a man who stood up against Caesar – making decisions that brought Rome closer to civil war. This was not the action of a man, a self-professed Stoic, who was quiet. He did not live unnoticed.

In other words, many Stoics lived in the arena. The domain of action. We can see this today too with artists, athletes, and entrepreneurs who practice Stoicism while practicing complete dedication to their craft and participating in the social and political debates of the day. The reach of Epictetus, Seneca, and Marcus Aurelius is long.

In response to these examples, Seneca notes that wise men can have different roles. In some circumstances, they must engage in politics. They must hold their nose and compromise. At other times, they resist or withdraw. 

He even says that perhaps Cato the Younger should not have expected so much from politics. Cato was not perfect. He didn’t triumph against his political enemy, Julius Caesar. In the end, Julius Caesar was not victorious either. The Roman Republic fell anyway. As a result of the Roman civil wars of the era, Rome would be ruled by emperors – emperors that Stoics advised, opposed, and eventually, in the case of Marcus Aurelius, became.

In the best case, those who dove into politics – and those who stayed away from it are driven by the same thing: the pursuit of excellent character.

The focus on virtue is the first and fundamental focus. It’s from this stance that someone decides to jump into the arena. That’s Seneca’s ultimate point. Often to clarify our purpose, we need to take stock of who we are and what is driving us. 

Regardless of the correct level of political involvement for you, you need to make sure you are acting for the right reasons. This takes philosophical work. Without examining our lives, we may end up pursuing someone else’s idea of success or justice. 

Seneca’s lesson applies to more than politics. It’s relevant to the idea of Stoic success. What does success look like for the Stoic? Political achievements? Entrepreneurial success? A good family? Artistic accomplishments? All of these things can be good – but ultimately, it depends on who you are and where you fit into the larger human story.

Seneca ends this letter with this:

“He who needs riches least, enjoys riches most."  
id: ?  
source: letters  
location: 14.17

The man who enters the arena has already has enough. They are not trying to prove or achieve anything – simply continue to play their role to the best of their ability.
