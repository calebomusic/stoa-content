#  2.6 – Your Life Is Sufficient

## Theory

Let’s continue reflecting on Marcus Aurelius’s *Meditations*.

In 2.6 he writes:

“Do wrong to yourself, do wrong to yourself my soul; but you will no longer have the opportunity of honoring yourself. Every man’s life is sufficient.

But yours is nearly finished, though your soul reveres not itself, but places your happiness in the souls of others.”

Marcus Aurelius wrote this, as a reminder to himself, while stationed with his troops. The warring Germanic tribes were not far off.

Roman defenses were strong, but occasionally they were overcome. Roman fortresses, positions, and men had been lost in the wars. Not only that, the plague was devastating. 

So, Marcus Aurelius was faced with stark reminders of death every day.

He knew that he did not have an infinite amount of time. In many ways, he may have considered himself lucky to be alive.

When he writes:

“Do wrong to yourself, do wrong to yourself my soul.”

He’s frustrated that he’s not living up to Stoic standards. There’s something humanizing in that attitude. Even the Roman Emperor and Stoic Sage recognizes when he errs and seeks to improve himself.

He is reminding himself to be good while he can, not to place happiness in the hands of others, but to find it in performing his role well.

We can take this lesson to heart. We don’t know how much time we have. While we live, we must set our souls on higher things.

Appreciate the small things in life and then let them go. Obstacles we will overcome. The good things in life – our character and relationships – that’s where we can find happiness.

And we can find it now. As Marcus Aurelius says: “Every man’s life is sufficient.”

He means that it doesn’t matter how short or long life is. It doesn’t matter what the circumstances are. What matters is whether the living find success in cultivating their own character.

As the Stoic philosopher Seneca writes, a short book can still be an excellent one. A long book can be a terrible one. It’s not the length of life, but its quality that matters.

So, make it excellent while you can. A beautiful life is possible.

# 2.7 – Focus

## Theory

Marcus Aurelius encouraged himself with these lines in *Meditations* 2.7:

“Do the external things which fall upon you distract you?

Give yourself time to learn something new and good, and cease to be whirled about.”

Even a Roman Emperor needed to remind himself to focus. All of us, no matter our station, must take control of our lives and become who we’re meant to be.

Marcus Aurelius knew his philosophical principles. It was up to him to apply them – without distraction.

Where are you on the path?

Marcus learned his philosophy at an early age. He benefited from excellent tutors and a studious mind. Not all of us have the same story.

Do you still need to explore and discover your values? Or, like Marcus Aurelius, do you possess your philosophy but need to remind yourself, from time to time, to apply it?

It’s a crucial distinction to make. If you know the right technique in a situation, simply apply it. If you don’t, figure out what it is. Either way – don’t be distracted from your craft. 

On the Stoic path, sometimes you need to figure out who you will be and what to do in a given situation. It may not be clear. At other times, it’s a matter of applying your knowledge in action.

Marcus Aurelius continues in his *Meditations*:

“But then you must also avoid being carried about the other way.

For those too are triflers who have wearied themselves in life by their activity, and yet have no object to which to direct every movement, and, in a word, all their thoughts.”

Some people focus, but on the wrong things. They are driven by their obsessions. They work hard and concentrate, but for nothing. 

So, even as you must avoid external distractions, you must also avoid concentrating on obsessions that will amount to little in the end.

What do you focus on? Building an inner citadel. 

For the Stoics, excellence is shown in character. It’s a constant battle not to lose ourselves in things external to us. What matters is how we respond to what happens, not what happens. What’s important is how we use what we’re given, not what we’re given.

Don’t lose yourself in distraction. Focus on what matters.

# 2.8 – Letting Go Of Other’s Opinions

## Theory

Today, we continue our reflection on *Meditations*.

These are the notes that Marcus Aurelius, the Roman emperor and Stoic philosopher wrote to become more Stoic. We can join him on the same path.

In *Meditations* 2.8, he writes:

“Through not observing what is in the mind of another man, a man has seldom been seen to be unhappy; but those who do not observe the movements of their own minds must of necessity be unhappy.”

First, Marcus Aurelius reminds himself of the fact that we spend so much time on what others think. If we didn’t, we’d likely be happier. No one has become unhappy by avoiding ruminating on what others thought about them.

This is connected to what moderns call the spotlight effect. We go through the world thinking that there’s a spotlight on us – others scrutinize and judge us – even spending their free time reflecting on their interactions with us. But in most cases, this is not so. We just don’t spend that much time thinking about other people – and scrutinizing their every decision – and other people don’t spend that much time thinking about us. Life’s too short. The old adage: “we wouldn’t care as much about what other people thought about us if we realized how seldom they do” has wisdom in it.

Second, Marcus Aurelius reminds himself that misery results from not attending to our own minds. If you’re meditating with Stoa, you’ve internalized the importance of attending to your own mind already.

One of the central goals of philosophy is to “know thyself.” If you don’t know who you are, you’ll make worse decisions and fail to develop your strengths.

So, we should pursue self-knowledge. We acquire this through philosophy, meditation, and experience.

This project is fundamentally connected to the quest for truth. It’s worth doing for its own sake.

It’s not a selfish project, but one of self-cultivation. 

This helps you act better for others. With self-knowledge, you can be a better friend, family member, and citizen because you know your strengths and weaknesses.

For the Stoics, it’s how we respond to the world that matters. We respond with every thought and decision. If we can master our minds and think clearly – we’re on the path to living well.

Focus on training your own mind and letting go of other people’s opinions.

# 2.9 – Play Your Part Well

## Theory

A fundamental Stoic framework is role ethics.

For the ancients, ethics was about living well. It is not just about how to treat other people, but how to live well. It’s about cultivating character and becoming a better person in all parts of life.

Stoics used role ethics to guide their decision making – for choices big and small.

Fundamentally, each of us plays the role of a human. We’re rational and social beings. So, we should pursue knowledge and live well with others. But we also have specific roles. Some of these we choose, others are given to us by our circumstances. All of these roles connect us to the larger whole.

Happiness is found by aligning ourselves with those roles. Who should we be? Excellent parents, children, siblings, spouses, citizens, neighbors, painters, musicians – the list goes on. It depends on who you are, the roles you’ve chosen, and the roles you’ve been given.

Marcus Aurelius possessed the role of emperor, but also adoptive son, student, husband, and friend. Some of these roles were prestigious. Others were ordinary. All of them connected him to the larger whole of humanity.

In *Meditations* 2.9 he wrote:

“This you must always bear in mind, what is the nature of the whole, and what is my nature, and how this is related to that…and that there is no one who hinders you from always doing and saying the things which are according to the nature of which you are a part.”

Our roles may seem small, but all of them connect us to the whole. Our friendships and careers connect us to our communities, our communities connect us to our cities, and our cities connect us to the world. Each interaction plays a role in shaping the network. This would have been obvious to someone like Marcus Aurelius – he was diligent in handling court cases because he knew one interaction with a single person would take him 15 minutes – but that people whose court cases he handled would remember those 15 minutes for the rest of their lives. All of us have parts of our lives like that.

We all have a part to play.

And, as Marcus Aurelius says, no one can stop you from playing that part.

All you need to do is focus on what needs to be done. What’s under your control. Action by action. If you’re free no internal or external circumstance can sway you from that.

# 2.10

## Theory

Stoicism is about self-mastery. It’s about changing our lives by changing what we want. As the ancients said, if you wish to make someone rich, subtract from their desires.

In *Meditations* 2.10 Marcus Aurelius contemplates a lesson from the Greek philosopher Theophrastus:

“He says, like a true philosopher, that the offenses which are committed through desire are more blameable than those which are committed through anger.”

Both kinds of mistakes reveal a lack of discipline. The central reason Theophrastus saw mistakes of desire as worse is that you are the only cause.

We become angry because of how we think and external stimuli. Someone, as they say, pushes our buttons. They couldn’t push our buttons if we didn’t expose them – that part is on us. But the other person needs to act too. In other words, anger takes two.

But mistakes committed out of desire only require one. They occur just because we want something. The problem is not caused by external stimuli, but by an internal lack, a desire for something. That is a pure failure of self-mastery.

As such, Marcus Aurelius writes that the person who lacks self-control:

“Is moved by his own impulse to do wrong being carried towards doing something by desire.”

Now Theophrastus was not a Stoic. Stoics wouldn’t strictly agree that mistakes due to pleasure are more blameable than those that result from anger. Fundamentally, both of them result from a lack of discipline.

Marcus Aurelius knew this. However, he is meditating on Theophrastus’s words because they contain an important truth.

The Stoic acts with discipline and moderation. They don’t let desires rule their life. Often, the source of our problems isn’t something external pushing our buttons – but something about us. How we think. That’s what we change through philosophy, meditation, and experience.

The ancients talked about living like we were at a banquet – we should take joy in the food and experiences we’re given – without gorging ourselves or craving more than we can have. Instead, we can simply let things come, let them be, and let them go. The best guests are not entitled. They enjoy what they’re given, don’t reach for more than they should, and recognize that there are some things they shouldn’t take at all.

The Stoic takes the same attitude in life. They don’t let their desires rule their life. Of course, they experience happiness and have a good time. But they do not grasp things that are out of their control and recognize that there are many things not worth wanting at all.

They keep their desires in check. Because this is the route to freedom. 

Meditate on these words today.

# 2.11a

## Theory

Throughout *Meditations* Marcus Aurelius urges himself to act well, while he can. Even a Roman Emperor needs to do that. For example, in 2.11 he writes:

“Since it is possible that you may depart from life at this very moment, regulate every act and thought accordingly.”

If you sit down and map out the events in life – the temporal limits of human life are obvious. You’ll spend such and such amount of time at school, at work, with your parents, your spouse, friends, and children. Sitting down and putting estimates for each of these is revealing.

The fact that these are just estimates – that they are what is likely, but what is necessary, brings urgency to our lives. 

As Marcus Aurelius says elsewhere:

“Do not act as if you will live for ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While it is within your power, be good.”

That’s in *Meditations* 4.17.

A simple and short message the Stoics always return to.

Mortality is a part of life. It’s natural. Not something to be feared. 

People who haven’t internalized Stoicism often fear making one of two mistakes: rumination or avoidance.

Those who ruminate on death fail to accept it. It is out of their control. They let their thoughts about its possibility interfere with their life now. Don’t let the fear of death stand in the way of life.

Those who avoid it, a more common tendency, simply shut it out. They give little thought to death. Until it shows up at their doorstep. Then regret and remorse arrive.

As the opening of an Emily Dickinson poem goes:

“Because I could not stop for Death—  
He kindly stopped for me—”

Stoicism is a philosophy for reducing regret and remorse.

Take each moment seriously – do your best to avoid foolish mistakes that result in remorse. And engage with the world, don’t regret sitting on the sidelines.

That’s what Marcus Aurelius is reminding himself to do.

Think well today. Act with virtue and cultivate joy.

# 2.11b

## Theory

In his *Meditations* Marcus Aurelius writes:

“But death certainly, and life, honor, and dishonor, pain, and pleasure, all these things equally happen to good men and bad, being things which make us neither better nor worse.

Therefore they are neither good nor evil.”

It’s a complex thought that he’s built up to over the course of Meditations 2.11.

Like other ancient Stoics, Marcus Aurelius, saw virtue as the ultimate good. He captured this beautifully in 6.24 of the Meditations too:

"Alexander the great and his mule driver died and the same thing happened to both. They were absorbed alike into the life force of the world or dissolved alike into atoms."

The fact that events – whether pleasant or painful, preferable or not – occur equally to all humans suggests that they are not truly good and evil.

Terrible things can happen to a good woman, while success can happen to an evil one. The mediocre man may experience luck, while the good one suffers hardship after hardship.

This is the way of Fortune. She smiles on some and scorns others – often changing her mood throughout our lives. All of us will experience good and bad luck.

Philosophies that value external things – like wealth, health, and status – make people dependent on chance. If you can only be happy if you’re healthy – well that renders you hostage to luck. It’s ultimately not up to you how healthy you are.

Stoicism offers a solution to this problem.

Happiness is up to you – no matter what happens. It’s an empowering message. 

That’s what Marcus Aurelius is reminding himself of. He is emphasizing that whatever happens to him is not truly bad or good. External events cannot make his life worse or better.

A good man’s life is not made better by the existence of prosperity or worse by the presence of pain. What matters is how he uses them.

These external things, life, honor, and pleasure are not where happiness is found. Likewise, dishonor, pain, and death are not the source of our unhappiness.

This philosophy defies fortune. Good people’s lives are robust against chance.

Happiness is found by responding to each well. In the Stoic’s words, living in accordance with nature.
