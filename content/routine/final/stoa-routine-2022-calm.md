## Intro

#### Theory

Picture a recent stressful moment in your life. It’s a part of everyday existence to experience anger, stress, anxiety, and any other negative emotion. Such moments cannot be avoided.

But picture that event again – the exact same thing happening – but without the negative emotion. Imagine encountering the stressful stuff of life with calm.

The Stoics believed that it is possible to face stressful events with equanimity. Indeed, you’ve probably gone through events in your life before with calm and poise, events that would have caused others to become nervous, overcome with anger, or lose their composure. Stoicism is the promise that this can happen more often.

Epictetus writes in his *Discourses*:

“Material things are indifferent; how we use them is not. How then may a man maintain not only steadiness and calm, but also the state of mind that is careful and neither reckless nor negligent?”

This is the key question. The Stoics developed a number of techniques to answer it. You’ll learn about those techniques on Stoa. Stoicism is not a collection of life hacks. It’s a worldview and life philosophy. It has a specific view about what is good in human life, namely virtue or excellent character.

Thousands of people who use Stoa have benefited by using Stoic techniques and also by adopting the life philosophy of Stoicism.

Of course, not everyone who uses Stoa is a Stoic. That’s OK. You don’t have to be Stoic to find value in philosophy. As you learn about philosophy, think about it critically. 

But what is this Stoicism? Epictetus mentioned part of it: Material things are indifferent. What does that mean? It means material things, like wealth or status are not what fundamentally matters. Instead, it is how you use them that matters. How you use them, the decisions that you make, express your character. Wealth is not good in itself. One can use it well, through effective philanthropy or investment. But equally, one can use it poorly. What ultimately matters is having a good character. 

This insight can bring calm. For much of our stress derives from placing too much weight on factors like wealth or status. We may care too deeply about what others think about us and not enough about our virtue. 

Stoicism allows us to let go of one source of stress and focus on what’s important – there’s peace in that.

## Non-judgement

#### Theory

So much of our ordinary thought is laden with value judgements. We’re perpetually classifying things as good or bad.

In its most extreme form, this can result in all or nothing thinking. This style of thought sees things as absolutely bad or good. Negative all or nothing thinking appears in many ways. Perhaps we respond to criticism by feeling dejected because we think it determines that we are failures. Or perhaps we find ourselves angry after taking a personal slight as an outrageous injustice. Or perhaps we give up on our habits because any mistake we make proves that we won’t be successful. Or perhaps you’re anxious about a meeting because you’re only focusing on the negative and stressful aspects of the situation. On the opposite side, positive all or nothing thinking can also arise in our lives: perhaps we treat business success as nothing but a blessing. Or perhaps unbounded optimism causes us to make promises we won’t fulfill.

Reality has more nuance. Every obstacle has its advantages and every success has its traps. There may be some truth in many negative judgments, but the reality is too messy to simply throw every situation into buckets of good and bad. 

Instead of seeing criticism as pure bad, see it as information. Likewise, instead of treating personal slights as nothing but an injustice, but it’s worth checking whether your beliefs about the situation are correct or if the issue is as big a deal as you think. 

When it comes to positive all or nothing thinking, things like business success bring wealth and prestige. But any form of success can also lead to complacency or an irrational ego.

For the ancient Stoics, the most important good is knowledge. The most virtuous know what is right and do it. All or nothing thinking does not help with this pursuit.

Yet it’s easy to fall into. For we’ve built up habits that we’ll need to reprogram. Experience is made up of an initial impression and then a value judgment. Suppose we’re taking a test and receive feedback that we got a question wrong. That’s the impression. This follows with a value judgment –we believe that we’re a failure and never learn. Many of us have different grooves of behavior just like this ingrained into us. We receive an impression and then immediately make an absolute judgment.

How do we deal with this?

Recast your impressions in objective language. For example, treat the fact that you got a question wrong as evidence. You misunderstood something – perhaps the course content or exam question. Address that misunderstanding and then move on. You don’t need to see the situation as all bad.

Seneca captured the Stoic opposition to all or nothing thinking

Someone once asked, what will you do if you fail? They responded:

“The same thing I’ll do if I succeed, work hard to improve.”

This can be reframed in the Stoic manner, whatever happens, focus on the next step that’s required for excellence. You’ll find that this can lead to a kind of tranquility. Absolute negative and positive judgments can make our lives into roller coasters – too much up and down. Some of us may have many more negative judgements than positive ones, rendering our lives darker than they need to be.

Life isn’t easy. There are many problems with ourselves and our societies.

But we can calmly see the world as it is. This requires transcending all or nothing thinking. Reality is far too detailed for that to work.

## Awareness

#### Theory 

The last lesson focused on the importance of seeing the world as it is. One thought pattern that gets in the way of that is all-or-nothing thinking. There are other cognitive distortions one must reprogram. This effort demands awareness. In order to change our mental habits, we must be aware of them.

This is why Marcus Aurelius write the following line to himself:

"Confine yourself to the present."

id: 74

Awareness is the ability to notice what is happening in the external and internal worlds. When we’re fully aware, we go through life knowing what is happening around us, instead of clumsily moving through without really knowing what is going on.

There are different ways to train awareness. Stoa will utilize many of them.

Today we will reflect on what happened recently, what went well, and what didn’t.

We’ll start by noting what happened during the last day. At this step, simply rehearse the events in your mind. Do this in an objective way, like a scientist simply observing what happened without judgment.

Then notice what went well. It’s important to clarify what this means to us.

Finally, notice what didn’t. The point is to let nothing slip under us.

This practice was suggested by Seneca:

"We should every night call ourselves to an account; What infirmity have I mastered today? What passions opposed? What temptation resisted? What virtue acquired? Our vices will abort of themselves if they be brought every day to the shrift."   
id: 395

If we bring each vice to our awareness, resolve to eliminate it, and plan to do so, we can become virtuous.

## Attention

#### Theory

Awareness is the capacity to notice what’s going on around you and in you. There’s a related ability that also matters: attention. Attention is the capacity to hold your mind at a certain point, without becoming distracted. 

Awareness and attention work together. If you’re aware, but unable to hold your attention, you may not focus on what matters or hold your attention when you need to. Attention without awareness results in tunnel vision – you may hold your attention to a single thing while ignoring everything else, missing things that matter. 

When attention and awareness work well together, we’re better able to focus on what matters: our decisions and beliefs. 

In Stoa, we train attention with mindfulness meditation. Practicing focusing on the breath can highlight just how hard it is to pay attention to something for long moments of time. Moreover it can build up the muscle for holding attention when it’s needed. 

Importantly, meditation is a way to practice. It’s the application of awareness and attention that matter. 

You want to be able to become aware of mistaken thoughts quickly, simply notice them, and then turn your attention to correct ones. Attention and awareness may work in concert – both matter. Some cultivate the ability to lose attention in an effortful manner. Their attention is broken by any distraction and they’re left in disarray. This is like the chess player who was intently focused, but loses that focus when a loud car drives by. They may find that they can’t get back into the zone.

But there’s another state, one that keeps stable attention and awareness of the outside world. When distractions arise in this state, they’re dealt with and then focus returns to the original activity. This is like the wizened chess player who is able to play well and focus with his grandchildren climbing over them. He even has enough awareness to gently catch one who falls off.

This is a tranquil state. See if you can attain it during these exercises. And bring it with you into your day.

## Model

#### Theory

Seneca once said that:

“For he who knows not to which port he sails, no wind is favorable.”

A lack of purpose may cloud our judgment. Worse we may wake up one day to realize that we were living the life of others – not defining our own purpose.

Because of this it is crucial to define what you value and what ultimately matters in life.

For the Stoics, what matters is virtue – making excellent decisions and believing what is true. Virtue can be cultivated in any life. 

But what does that virtue look like? What does it consist of?

One way to answer these questions is to look to role models. We can look at the actions in their life to uncover what they valued and how that shaped their decisions and thoughts.

Today we’re focusing on Stoic calm. A fine role model for our purposes is Marcus Aurelius.

Marcus Aurelius was a Roman emperor and Stoic philosopher. He ruled Rome during wars, the antonine plague, and one coup attempt. Needless to say, this could be stress inducing work.

To the best of our knowledge, Marcus Aurelius dealt with these events with more calm than the typical person would. Unlike many Roman emperors, he did not make rash decisions. Instead, he kept his head. He was dedicated to his work, adjudicating between many affairs of state and war.

As you go throughout your day, bring to mind Marcus Aurelius or your own role model. Picture how they would think and act. And then think and act well.

## PM

#### Theory

It’s imagination that haunts us. Seneca once said:

“We suffer more in imagination than in reality.”

Often we ruminate over the future. Perhaps there’s some stressful event coming up. But we don’t know how the future will play out. Our worries really are imagined, not reality. Nonetheless, it’s easy to become anxious about making future mistakes.

Planning for the future can be useful. In fact, it’s our duty to prepare for many things in life. However, Rumination over the future, going over the same things over and over,, is not.

One technique for dealing with this is *praemeditatio malorum*. This is translated as the premeditation of evils. 

One does this by imaging the worst case of different events. Perhaps you’re worried about lunch, whether it’s with an important executive or a date. Visualize how things could go terribly. There are many possibilities\!

Often when we pause and do this, we realize that our worries are not so extreme. Or perhaps we realize that we can prevent the worst-case now.

The exercise then has two benefits. First, we become psychologically prepared for the worst. Second, we can improve our plans to avoid the worst outcomes.

Instead of ruminating over the future, see what you can learn about it. When we picture the worst things that can happen, we learn that we may be overestimating how serious things are and find calm. We may learn that there are good ways we can improve our plans and build confidence.

This may seem like an odd exercise. Yet the Stoics used it to remind themselves that externals, everything that is not under our control, are not what truly matters. It’s making excellent decisions and thinking well that does. Once we’ve done this to the best of our ability, there’s truly nothing left to worry about.

Don’t let ignorance and imagination cause suffering. Instead, prepare for the worst, focus on what you can control, and act well.

## VFA

#### Theory

Our lives are only a sentence in the long story of history.

When we zoom out and picture our lives from a larger perspective, ordinary worries and desires fall away.

What matters from this perspective? Only acting and thinking well. And playing your role in the social web that has existed years before you ever came to be and will persist for years after you are gone.

Marcus Aurelius once said:

“Forget everything else. Keep hold of this alone and remember it: Each of us lives only now, this brief instant. The rest has been lived already, or is impossible to see. The span we live is small—small as the corner of the earth in which we live it. Small as even the greatest renown, passed from mouth to mouth by short-lived stick figures, ignorant alike of themselves and those long dead." 

Learn to remember this as you go throughout your days. When you’re disturbed, take the view from above. And then return, with a renewed focus on what matters. 

This exercise can have a cleansing effect. The detritus of peer pressure and worthless desires fall away when we take into account deep space and time. In Marcus Aurelius’s phrase:

“In a little while you will have forgotten everything; in a little while everything will have forgotten you.“  
id:  969

After letting distractions fall away, you can return to what matters: being an excellent friend, family member, citizen, and human.

Your role is to do that to the best of your ability. Factors that are out of your control, cannot harm you. It is not externals like wealth, power, and status that matter but how you use them.
