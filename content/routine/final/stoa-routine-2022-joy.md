## **Joy**

## Intro

#### Theory

It is a common misconception that Stoics do not feel joy. The contemporary stereotype of a stoic is someone who barely emotes, and seems indifferent to the world around him. But the ancient Stoics thought that joy, a stable sustainable kind of joy, was the result of Stoic training towards being a good person.

For the ancient Stoics, joy comes from a recognition of the good in the world, and in yourself. For the Stoics, the phrase ‘ignorance is bliss’ is false. Joy comes from mindful appreciation for and understanding of the world as it truly is.

Stoicism is a fundamentally optimistic philosophy. They thought that whoever practiced Stoicism would eventually gain an understanding of the beauty of the world, and an appreciation for the quality of their own character. The training Stoic would both develop virtue and begin to value their virtue above their other qualities. In doing so, they would be filled with a calm joy as they went about their whole lives. While this kind of joy was not a direct goal of Stoicism, the goal of Stoicism is virtue, it was something the Stoics thought every sage, or perfect Stoic, would experience.

Stoic joy is a calm type of joy. It is not at all like the erratic joy of a gambler, who feels highs when they win and then crashes when they lose. This kind of happiness is too fragile and transitory to be a Stoic goal. Instead, because Stoic joy is caused by an appreciation of the world, not external factors, it will be calm and constant, like a positive glow.  

As Seneca wrote in a letter to his friend:

“Above all, my dear Lucilius, make this your business: learn how to feel joy.” (ID:1246)

## **Choice**

#### **Theory**

Seneca once wrote: 

“True happiness is to enjoy the present, without anxious dependence upon the future, not to amuse ourselves with either hopes or fears but to rest satisfied with what we have, which is sufficient, for he that is so wants nothing.” (ID: 356\)

This passage is an excellent summary on the Stoic view of joy. Joy comes from an appreciation of the present, combined with no anxiety for the future, and no remorse about the past. 

But most people, even if they agree with this statement, still aren’t happy. It is not an easy state to achieve. So how do we achieve this true happiness Seneca talks about?

For the Stoics, happiness is achieved through choice. This does not mean we can just ‘choose’ to be happy in the moment. It is more complicated than that. Rather, happiness comes from choosing to work to become the kind of person who values certain things. It comes from understanding your own value system, and adjusting that value system when it places too much importance on the wrong type of things, or too much focus on the past or future.

If you choose to value good character and beauty of the world as it is, then you will find joy in your own character and the world in the present moment. If you wish you had acted differently in the past, or think that your happiness depends on some future event, then you will feel remorse or anxiety instead. 

To achieve Stoic joy, work on becoming the kind of person that values what you have in the moment: the ability to make good choices, and a life in a world that is full of beauty and wonder.

## **Acceptance**

#### **Theory**

Acceptance of the world as it is, not as we want it to be, is a crucial part of Stoic joy. So much of our stress, anxiety, and suffering comes from our expectations.

We want people to act differently than who they are, we want the past to be different than it is, or we want to control a future we cannot control. These are all expectations that lead to disappointment when they are not met. 

One strategy to maintain joy is what is sometimes called the ‘Stoic Reserve-clause’. The idea is to have expectations to achieve and do things, but with the reservation that you only want them if you can have them and keep your mind in a calm, virtuous, hopefully joyful state. 

So you can want to have a successful career, but always with the reservation that you will stop wanting it if it interferes with your ability to be happy and live in accordance with nature. 

Epictetus introduces this idea in that example of going to a bathhouse. 

He says “When you’re about to embark on any action, remind yourself what kind of action it is. If you’re going out to take a bath, set before your mind the things that happen at the baths, that people splash you, that people knock up against you, that people steal from you. And you’ll thus undertake the action in a surer manner if you say to yourself at the outset, ‘I want to take a bath and ensure at the same time that my choice remains in harmony with nature.’” (ID 811). 

So as a Stoic, it is fine to want things and set out life plans, but we should also accept that these plans do not always turn out the way we want them to, and that if these plans come into conflict with our happiness and quality of character, we should abandon them. This is how a Stoic maintains realistic expectations, by accepting that many times they will have to change their plans if they wish to preserve their character.

## **Identity**

#### **Theory** 

How we think of ourselves, how we define our identity, is a crucial part of Stoic joy. We are happy when we live in accordance with our nature. We feel content when our actions, who we truly are, and who we think we are, are all aligned.

The Stoics have a particular answer to the question of identity. Every person, first and foremost, is a rational being. We are, fundamentally, not our bodies or our reputations or talents, but our ability to make choices with the information and circumstances we have. 

If you agree with the Stoics, then what it means to be true to yourself changes. To be true to yourself is just to understand yourself and value yourself by your ability to make good choices with the situations you find yourself in. 

There are two upsides to looking at identity in this way. First, every person, no matter their circumstance, has the ability to be the best version of themselves, and feel the joy that comes with that. We cannot all be movie stars or professional athletes, but we can all make good choices, where we strive to act with courage, justice, and moderation. 

Second, when we attach our identity to something outside of who we really are, we are mistaken. We are not our jobs or talents, and if we think that is all we are, we will suffer, because we value the wrong things.

This is not a new phenomenon. Since the time of the ancient Stoics people have attached value to external things. In the words of Epictetus: “It is just charming how people boast about qualities beyond their control. For instance, ‘I am better than you because I have many estates, while you are practically starving’; or, ‘I’m a consul,’ ‘I’m a governor,’ or ‘I have fine curly hair.’” (ID: 853\)

Instead of acting like the people Epictetus describes. we should identify with something that is up to us, namely being an excellent choice maker. Grounding our identity in what we have control over is the foundation for secure self-confidence, and the happiness that comes from that.

## **Questions**

#### **Theory**

If you were to ask the Stoic Epictetus: “How can I be happy?” Here is how one Stoic, Epictetus, would answer:

“Your happiness depends on three things, all of which are within your power: your will, your ideas concerning the events in which you are involved, and the use you make of your ideas.” (Epictetus: ID 318\)

What did Epictetus mean by this? To understand we will go through each part in detail.

The will for Epictetus is where you direct your energy. This is the psychological drive to do something, or to avoid something, we all experience. The different people will feel a different drives about the same situation. When offered an invitation by a friend, I might have the will to socialize, where someone else may feel the need to stay at home by themselves.

Our will depends on our ideas concerning the events in which we are involved. If I judge an invitation to socialize as good, I will want to socialize. If I judge the invitation as a bad thing I will want to stay home. So our will, comes from the ideas we have about events in our life, and whether we judge them as good or bad things.

Finally, this entire process is within our power, because humans have the ability to decide what they think about the world around them. As humans, we have the freedom and ability to reflect on our impressions about situations, and make the judgment we think is best. This makes us different from other animals, who don’t have this to make use of ideas in the same way.

Let’s return to the quote one more time: 

“Your happiness depends on three things, all of which are within your power: your will, your ideas concerning the events in which you are involved, and the use you make of your ideas.” (Epictetus: ID 318\)

Remember this as you go throughout your day.

## **Attention**

#### **Theory**

Marcus Aurelius once wrote: “Such as are your habitual thoughts, such also will be the character of your mind; for the soul is dyed by the thoughts. Dye it then with a continuous series of such thoughts as these: for instance, that where a man can live, there he can also live well; now it is possible to live at court, so it must also be possible to live well at court.” (ID 939\) 

Marcus’ point is that the nature of our mind follows our attention. How our mind works is actually influenced, or ‘dyed’, by what we think about and what we focus on. If we focus on bad things, our mind will tend towards producing bad thoughts. If we focus on optimistic possibilities, our mind will find solutions and positives more easily.

This means that to feel Stoic joy, it is not enough to just limit negative thoughts to reduce suffering. We must be able to turn our attention to the positive in ourselves, in others, and in the world. We must make a consistent effort to ‘dye’ our minds with positive thoughts, by training our attention towards them.

Marcus did not say just “to live at court is not as bad as I thought”, because this kind of thinking only reduces the negative. Instead he said “it must be possible to live well at court”. This kind of thinking turns the mind towards the positive. 

The ability to bring our attention to the positive is a skill like any other. It must be practiced. This will be intentional at first. Then over time, it will become a habit, and your attention will turn to the positive with less effort. Meditation practice is the perfect time to start this training, but most people don’t meditate for long enough for this to be sufficient. It must also be practiced as you go about your day.

## **Thought-Patterns**

#### **Theory**

Once you have achieved joy, you must work to maintain it. 

Marcus Aurelius said in his meditations:

“The happiness of your life depends upon the quality of your thoughts: therefore, guard accordingly, and take care that you entertain no notions unsuitable to virtue and reasonable nature.” (Marcus ID:57)

This notion of ‘guarding’ your happiness against unhealthy or toxic thoughts is one that reoccurs throughout Stoicism.

Epictetus provides the example of the mind like a fortress, and thoughts like those seeking entry. You should be on guard against who enters, and be ready to turn away or throw out anyone that might aim to betray the fortress.

Seneca uses the metaphor too. He says:

“Always stand as it were on guard, and mark the attacks and charges of Fortune long before she delivers them; she is only terrible to those whom she catches unawares; he who is always looking out for her assault, easily sustains it.” (Seneca, ID: 1611\)

What all of these have in common is a proactive approach to negative thoughts. The Stoic focuses on the positive while guarding against negative thoughts that would disrupt this focus. 

The most famous way to guard against negative thoughts is premeditatio malorum, or premeditation of evils. This is where, like in Seneca’s example, the Stoic imagines negative changes of fortune before they occur, so that they are prepared for these situations if they come.

But Marcus and Epictetus’ example is different. In their examples, we guard against negative thoughts by dismissing them as unhealthy, or false, before they become firmly established beliefs. We should practice letting these kinds of thoughts go before they gain strength in your mind.

Whatever strategy you choose, the Stoic is proactive in creating a mind that can defend its joy.
