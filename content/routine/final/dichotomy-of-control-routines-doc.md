## Intro

#### Theory

The dichotomy of control is possibly the most famous single piece of Stoic philosophy. Epictetus gives us a clear explanation of it at the start of his Handbook. He says:

“Of things, some are in our power, and others are not. In our power are opinion, movement towards a thing, desire, aversion, turning from a thing; and in a word, whatever are our acts. Not in our power are the body, property, reputation, political offices, and in a word, whatever are not our own acts. And the things in our power are by nature free, not subject to restraint or hindrance; but the things not in our power are weak, slavish, subject to restraint, in the power of others.” (D 231\)

There are three key ideas to the Dichotomy of Control:

First, there is an idea about what it is like to be a human. As humans, some things in the world are up to us, and other things are not. This applies to all people.

Second, Epictetus tells us about the nature of the things in each category. The things up to us  cannot be restrained or controlled by others. Only things not up to us can be in the power of others.

Third, there is advice about what humans should do, given that these two categories exist. We should not confuse where we categorize something, because when we do, we suffer. When we treat something like our reputation and popularity, which does not depend on us alone but a number of other things in the world, as something that is totally up to us, we suffer.

We suffer when we try to control our reputation because we are trying to do something impossible. No matter how we act, how we are perceived is always the result of other independent people. It is never entirely up to us. So if we expect it to be, we will always be disappointed and frustrated.

Reflect on the basic categories throughout your day and in the daily meditation.

## **Choice**

#### **Theory**

A crucial part of the Dichotomy of Control is understanding that there are things that we do have control over, no matter the circumstance.

Our opinions and choices, and the motivations follow from that are things we determine and are responsible for. Because we determine our choices, and have responsibility for them, these are the things that should really matter to us. These qualities internal to us are what determines our quality as a human being.

As Epictetus says: 

“Where is the good? In our choices. Where is the evil? In our choices. Where is neither of them? In those things we do not choose.” (ID: 1675\)

When we call someone evil or good, we can only say that about their choices, because those choices, and the things that follow from them, are the only things up to them. We can’t call someone good because they are a talented athlete, or have a brilliant mind, or were born into wealth. Nor should we judge a person for being the opposite. 

We should only judge people based on the choices they make, with the circumstances they have. These choices are where we find good and evil.

That we should only judge someone by the quality of their character alone was quite a radical idea in ancient Greece and Rome. These were societies with strong ideas that where you were born determined how you should be treated. There were those from nobles families, and those who were slaves. 

Judging people by their choices rather than their circumstances has become a more popular idea today. Martin Luther King Jr. famously fought back against racism by saying that we should judge people by the contents of their character rather than by the color of their skin. His point was in the context of racism, but it was about focusing on what the individual could determine, their choices, rather than what they can’t, their ethnicity. However, we still often forget to apply this idea to ourselves and others.

In today’s daily meditation, we will train ourselves to focus on what is up to us, our choice.

## **Acceptance** 

#### **Theory**

One half of the dichotomy of control is about taking responsibility for the things which are up to you, which are your choices and responses to situations. 

The other half of the dichotomy of control, is about how to handle the things that are not up to you. We are to accept them, as they are, not as we want them to be, because ultimately they are not up to us.

Often external events are the things that we fear the most: for example we often fear or hate random accidents or illnesses, being betrayed by others, large economic changes, the eccentricities of our family, or the circumstances we are born into.

And in some cases you can modify these, or influence them, but you can only do what is in your power, you can only make different choices. Imagine a loved one or family member you want to be different than they are, or an idea you wish was more popular, or a problem you want the world to come together to solve. Your job is not to control or change that thing, your job is to make the best choices possible, and hope that your choices influence things for the better, and accept when it does not. 

Remember that Stoicism is not about passivity. Marcus Aurelius was an emperor of one of the largest powers in history. The Stoic is not passive, but they are accepting of what they cannot change.

Although not a Stoic quote, the serenity prayer captures this idea perfectly.  It goes: “God grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and wisdom to know the difference.” (ID: 1688\)

The dichotomy of control is the tool that gives us the wisdom to know the difference.

## **4\. Identity**

#### **Theory** 

The Dichotomy of Control, which teaches that some things are up to us and others are not, is not just about how to categorize other things. It is also a lesson about what we as human beings, essentially are. It is about our identity.

Our thoughts, behaviors, and choices are ‘up to us’, because we are considered by the Stoics to be, at a fundamental level, choosing and thinking things. Epictetus says that the body is not ‘up to us’, because Epictetus does not consider the body to be part of our essential identity. The body is a thing we possess, like our clothes, or a watch, or our house.

And like clothes or a house, we should take good care of it, but we should not be judged by our bodies, not should we think something bad has happened to us, as we fundamentally are, if our body is injured.

Human beings are minds, and the quality of our mind is how we should measure each other. But many people mistakenly extend their identity too far. They think they are their body, so they mistakenly think they are worse than others if their body is less beautiful, or athletic, or mobile. People also extend their identity to their property, or their career. They think they are worse off if they own cheap things, or a have less impressive job.

The Stoic perspective on such thoughts is that they are absurd and cause unnecessary suffering. You are to be judged only by the choices you make, because that is what you are. 

We should all strive to be like the character in the following lesson by Epictetus. Epictetus says that when a tyrant threatens to throw you in prison, you should respond “Correction \- it is my body you will throw there” (ID:599). A Stoic remembers what they really are, and that other people have the power to harm their body, but not their integrity or choices.

## **5\. Questions**

#### **Theory**

Stoicism is a philosophy to be lived, not just considered. The Dichotomy of Control is an interesting idea, but it is most useful when it is applied to our lives. To apply the Dichotomy of Control, we are to use it like a tool, to test and examine our first impressions about situations. Epictetus explains it like this:

“So make a practice at once of saying to every strong impression: ‘An impression is all you are, not the source of the impression.’ Then test and assess it with your criteria, but one primarily: ask, ‘Is this something that is, or is not, in my control?’”(ID: 808\)

So using the Dichotomy of Control begins with recognizing that our first impressions about situations may be mistaken and inaccurate. For example, we may incorrectly judge how harmful a situation is. To test if our judgment is correct, we measure our impression against the question “Is this situation up to me, or not?”

If it is up to you, take it seriously, and apply yourself to change it for the better. For example, if you have acted poorly, then you can and should apologize. You should be disturbed if you fail to apologize, because that is something up to you.

But if it is not up to you, do not allow yourself to be inappropriately disturbed by it. For example, if you have acted poorly but you have done everything you can to improve the situation and make amends, then you should not continue to be disturbed by your past vice. 

So the Dichotomy of Control is a tool for understanding the world around you. You put it into practice by asking thoughtful questions and constantly determining what is and is not up to you.

In this meditation, we will practice the Stoic skill of questioning our impressions.

## **6\. Attention**

#### **Theory**

In the last routine, we discussed how the Dichotomy of Control can be used to evaluate new situations as they happen, and question our first impressions of things.

But we should not just apply the Dichotomy of Control to new situations. Properly understood, the Stoic idea that we should focus on, care about, and treat as most important the things up to us, should be applied to all of our beliefs and judgements, even those deeply held from before our exposure to Stoicism.

Being a Stoic is not just about going through our day encountering new situations without worry. It is about fundamentally reshaping what we value, so that we value our character and integrity as more important than external circumstances.

A necessary part of reshaping what we value is a mindful reflection on what we give our attention to. Through mindful meditation, we can identify our hopes, worries, and fears. And then we can evaluate which of those sensations come from things up to us, and which comes from things that are not up to us. 

The Stoic then strives to give more attention and time to the feelings that come from things up to them. These are the things that ultimately matter, and so they are the things we should be focused upon.

In the words of Epictetus:

“There is only one road to happiness – let this rule be at hand morning, noon, and night: stay detached from things that are not up to you.”   
id: 1655

In this meditation, we will practice detaching from things not to us, and shifting our focus towards the things we can determine.

## **7\. Mantra**

#### **Theory**

The Dichotomy of Control is a tool we use to measure and evaluate new experiences and previous beliefs. If something is up to us, we should take it seriously, focus on it, and do our best to act properly in that situation. If something is not up to us, we should remember that we don’t have the ability to control it, and we should focus less on it.

But sometimes we can forget to use this tool. To ensure we are ready to apply it, Epictetus encourages us to always keep the Dichotomy of Control ‘on hand’, and accessible. This is done by considering Stoic ideas and keeping them clear and at the front of your mind.

In fact, Epictetus handbook, a short summary of Stoic lessons, was designed for this exact purpose. By carrying a small book around, you could immerse yourself in Stoicism at any moment, and have these ideas with you always.

Today, few people carry around books, but we often have our phones or other devices. These serve the same purpose. By turning to Stoic ideas often, we keep these lessons at the forefront of our mind. The closer the tool, the easier it is to use, and the more likely we are to use it. This is why toolbelts exist.

Through meditation and repetition, you can add the dichotomy of control to your philosophical tool belt. It can become always accessible to pull out and measure each situation with. This should be one of the primary goals of Stoic meditation training.

As Epictetus said about difficult situations:

“What should we have ready at hand in a situation like this? The knowledge of what is mine and what is not mine, what I can and cannot do.” (ID 597\)
