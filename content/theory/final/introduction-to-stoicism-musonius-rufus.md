**Introduction to Stoicism: Musonius Rufus**

Hi everyone, my name is Michael Tremblay, and welcome to this introduction to the life and thought of Musonius Rufus, Stoic philosopher, and teacher of Epictetus. In this lesson, we will cover what is known about his life, and three key ideas from his unique approach to Stoic philosophy. 

Musonius Rufus was born in an area to the north of Rome between 20 and 30 AD, around 30 years after Seneca. Like Epictetus, we know relatively little about Musonius’ life compared to the comparatively famous Seneca and Marcus Aurelius. But we do know that he gained enough fame in Rome as a teacher of Stoic philosophy to gain attention from powerful people. In 65 AD, he aroused the suspicion of Emperor Nero and was banished to a remote Greek island.  He eventually returned to Rome when Galba, the Emperor following Nero, took power around 68 AD. It is likely during this time that he took on Epictetus as a student. He was exiled one further time, under Emperor Vespasian in 75 AD. Musonius returned to Rome sometime after Vespasian’s death in 79 AD and likely remained there until his death.

Epictetus, once just a student of Musonius, would go on to open his own school of Stoic philosophy, but, in Epictetus’ Discourses, we often find references to his teacher Musonius. In one section, a young Epictetus tries to argue that making mistakes in logic homework is not that important. “It is not as if I have burned down the capitol”, Epictetus says. “Idiot”, Musonius replied, “The mistake here is the capitol.” Epictetus, then a student, was acting in the way we might expect a teenager to say today: ‘It doesn’t really matter if I pay attention when I do my homework, there is no real consequence to being lazy, I’ll pay attention when it matters’. But Musonius reminded Epictetus, in a way that stayed with him into his adult life, that thinking with discipline matters in every moment.

This gruff teaching style, and an intense commitment to taking your actions seriously, are recognizable in Epictetus’ own teaching style and are passed down, in part, by Musonius.

We don’t know if Musonius wrote anything for publication, but, like Epictetus, his students would collect and summarize his teachings. We still have Musonius’ writings today because Stobaeus, a 5th-century Christian, compiled together these collected lectures and sayings from Musonius’ students. It is because of this collection by Stobaeus, preserved and translated, that we have any direct references to Musonius’ lecture at all with the exception of some fragments. It is worth remembering that what we have of the Stoics today is only a fraction of what they wrote and taught. 

In total, this collection is around 60-80 normal-sized English pages. It is less than Seneca, Marcus, or Epictetus, but enough to get a sense of Musonius’ unique ideas and focus.

Musonius is remembered today and was considered worth preserving in his own time, because of his unique strand of applied ethics-focused Roman Stoicism. This was an approach to philosophy that he passed on directly to Epictetus and then to Marcus Aurelius who read Epictetus. 

The remainder of this lesson will provide an overview of three key parts of this unique style:

* First, his focus on putting philosophy into practice  
* Second, his emphasis on minimalist living.  
* Third, his unique arguments that both men and women should be able to practice philosophy as equals 


**First, we will begin** with Musonius’ focus on putting philosophy into practice. An idea we find fully developed in Epictetus is the essential divide between studying Stoic theory and putting your philosophy into practice. Epictetus argues that both are necessary for virtue, but neither is sufficient on its own.

In other words, if you only read about Stoicism all day, you won’t be able to act well when it matters. But if you go around acting non-reflectively, and don’t understand the WHY behind what you are doing, then you stunt your progress as well. By combining action and reflection, Epictetus asks us to take a balanced approach to self-improvement.

Musonius likely inspired this way of thinking in Epictetus. Philosophy is a pursuit that can, at its worst, lead to idle debates about ideas, rather than transformed character. This is what happens when you treat philosophy as just a theory, rather than a craft or skill.

Musonius was quick to point out that philosophy is indeed a craft, and so we should value skilled action over pure theory. He posed the following question to his students:

“Suppose there are two doctors. One of them can talk about medical matters as if he had the greatest possible acquaintance with them, but has never actually cared for sick people. The other is not able to talk about medical matters, but is experienced in healing in accordance with medical theory. Which one would you choose as your doctor if you were ill?”

The answer is obvious. We prefer the doctor who has experience applying their craft. So too with philosophy. We need to get that experience by acting on our lessons. We need to do things that scare us, go without things we desire, practice kindness to others, and do things because they are right even if they are uncomfortable. This is how the Stoics practiced their craft, not by more reading and debate.

Musonius provides another example of a musician. They need to both understand musical theory and  practice their scales. The same is true for a philosopher. If you are to become a better person, you must learn more about philosophy, like you are doing right now, listening to this lesson. But you also must do your metaphorical scales and practice in life. Musonius was the first Roman stoic to fully establish the implications of philosophy as a craft.

**Musonius’ second unique idea is his focus on a minimalistic life.** He argued that our surroundings shape our character, and it is better to be challenged by too little than spoiled by too much.

The reason for this, Musonius argues, is that hardships train and shape our soul. As Musonius says:

“We will train both soul and body when we accustom ourselves to cold, heat, thirst, hunger, scarcity of food, hardness of bed, abstaining from pleasures and enduring pains…Through these methods and others like them, the body is strengthened…and the soul is strengthened as it is trained for courage by enduring hardships and trained for self-control by abstaining from pleasures.”

In other words, when things are tough you learn transferable skills. If self-control is the ability to go without something we want in the short term, then minimalism teaches self-control. If courage is in part the ability to face hardships, then when things get tough we practice courage.

You can think of Musonius as channeling the spirit of modern athletics trainer, who argues that sport teaches discipline in children because it asks them to do difficult things. 

But Musonius’ point is not just about the benefit of difficulty, but also the harm of luxury. In another lecture, he says to his students:

“I myself would therefore choose to be sick rather than to live in luxury. Being sick harms the body only; living in luxury harms both soul and body, by making the body weak..and the soul undisciplined and cowardly.”

So whether or not discomfort benefits us, it certainly does not corrupt our characters. But luxury can harm us internally, by spoiling ourselves. A proper bed is preferable to a sleeping bag in terms of comfort, but it not might be best for us overall, if too many comfy beds make us undisciplined or dependent on that comfort. 

Musonius is not arguing that we should all sleep on the floor. He is not an ascetic, someone who argues that the only path to virtue is through physical suffering. But he is pointing out, rightly I think, the connection between our environment and our character. How we live affects who we are. So be sure to live in a way that makes you better, not worse.

The **third and final** key feature of Musonius’ approach to Stoicism is egalitarianism or his commitment to equality between all people. In his lectures, Musonius Rufus argued that women could be excellent Stoics and achieve virtue. While this is not controversial today, it is important to note how unusual this would have been in ancient Rome. Even the word virtue, comes from Vir, the Latin word for man. To be virtuous meant, in one sense, to be manly, or be the things an excellent man should be. 

But Musonius Rufus shifted the focus away from gender, towards something all people share in common, our minds and the ability to reason. In his lectures, Musonius says:

“Women have received from the gods the same reasoning power as men…likewise the same senses as the male…likewise too each has the same parts of the body…in addition, a desire for virtue and an affinity for it belong by nature not only to men but also to women…Since this is so, why would it be appropriate for men but not women to seek to live honourable and consider how to do so, which is what studying philosophy is? Is it appropriate for men to be good, but not women?” (King 28\)

In Stoicism, to be a good person means to make virtuous decisions and live in accordance with nature. Musonius points out that this is achievable for both men and women. If we defined excellence or greatness instead as something conditional on our biology, physical strength for example, then maybe there would be a difference between men and women. But virtue is about our minds, so both men and women are equal as philosophers.

It is worth noting that Musonius was not trying to abolish traditional gender roles. He thought virtue was defined as excellent choices in accordance with nature, and that women had a different nature than men. When he gives examples of virtuous women, they are traditional examples. He points to women who can:

* take care of the house  
  * manage the estate  
  * carefully look after their husband and children.

Musonius clearly ascribes to traditional gender roles. However, he thinks that studying philosophy can help both men and women excel in those roles. To quote Musonius:

“There is no way that I would expect women who pursue philosophy, or even men for that matter, to cast aside their appropriate tasks and concern themselves with words only, But I saw that they should pursue the discussions they undertake for the sake of action.”

Whether you agree with traditional Roman gender roles or not, Musonius’ contribution stands as this. Philosophy is a craft, defined by action. Its goal is to make us into excellent people. And so philosophy belongs to every person because all people should strive for excellence. 

In summary, this lesson introduced you to the thinking of Musonius Rufus, and three teachings from his unique Stoicism:

* First, we put philosophy into practice, because philosophy is more like the crafts of medicine and music than it is an academic pursuit.  
* Second, we should focus on minimalist living where possible, because there is a relationship between our environment and our characters.  
* And third, we should remember that because excellent Stoicism is about making good choices, both men and women are able to practice and excel at Stoic philosophy as equals
