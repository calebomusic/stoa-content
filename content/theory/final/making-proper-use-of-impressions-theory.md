PS: The most important Stoic practice

PD: Michael Tremblay provides a deep-dive into what Epictetus says is the ‘main task’ of a philosopher \- How to handle impressions like a Stoic. 

### **CT: The key to living well**

CD: Why making proper use of impressions is the key to Stoicism

Stoicism is a philosophy for living well. But what does practicing Stoicism actually mean? What is a Stoic supposed to do all day? 

This course provides the answer – a single practice that you can and should do every day to be Stoic.

The Stoics taught that happiness comes from virtue, and virtue is knowledge. To live well is to understand the world as it really is, and to live in accordance with nature.

But having knowledge is the end goal, it is not a practice. It gives us, those developing our Stoic practice, a target but not a way to get there. It is the destination, not the path. 

So to find the Stoic path, people turn to exercises. We start applying the dichotomy of control, or practicing negative visualization. We journal, meditate, and practice mindfulness.  We undergo voluntary discomfort, and take the view from above. We read the words of ancient Stoics, and attempt to digest some of their wisdom into how we see the world. 

But what do these all have in common or share? What about these exercises makes them Stoic?

Epictetus had an answer. He spent his life teaching Stoicism, observing which students took on the philosophy and which failed to see a change. This experience taught him the difference between a successful Stoic practice, and an ineffective one. He developed a way to sum up all Stoic practice and training in one sentence: Making proper use of impressions.

In his Discourses, he tells his students plainly that:

“The nature of good is a proper use of appearances.”   
Sentence\_id: 11585  
Location: 1.20.17

Elsewhere, he says:

“Now reason, for what purpose has it been given \[to us\] by nature?. For the right use of appearances.”   
Sentence\_Ids: 11565-11566  
Location: 1.20.5

In Epictetus’ view, we have received reason for the purpose of making proper use of impressions. And our good consists in doing this well. Not only that, but this is the task of those educated in philosophy.

As Epictetus says, when we receive impressions of all kinds, quote:

“ …in all these cases to form a right judgment (to hit the mark) is the office of an educated man.”

Sentence\_Ids: 11842  
Location: 1.27.2

The key to living well for Epictetus, the path to Stoic virtue, is making proper use of impressions. But why this focus? 

Well, because Epictetus thinks that making proper use of impressions is the most important thing we do for two reasons.

First, because how we act and what we feel depends on our responses to impressions. It is a basic stoic principle that humans are psychologically required to act and feel in response to how they judge the world. Our emotions, fears and desires follow our cognition. 

This is why Marcus says the quality of our life is determined by the quality of our thoughts. And why the Stoics say that virtue and happiness depend on knowledge. Our emotional life and character depend on how we perceive the world. The kind, courageous, just, happy person is the person who understands the world properly.

Epictetus makes this clear through his analysis of Homer’s epic work the Illiad. He says to his students:

“The Illiad consists of nothing but impressions, and the use of those impressions. An impression prompted Paris to carry off the wife of Menelaus. An impression prompted Helen to follow him. If, then, an impression has caused Menelaus to feel that it was an advantage to be robbed of such a wife, what would have happened? not only the Illiad would have been lost, but the Odyssey too.” 

Sentence Ids:   
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.28.11-13

The point is that even the most complex stories and politics can be reducible to one thing: how people make use of the impressions they receive. 

The second reason making proper use of impressions is our chief task is because, going back to his famous dichotomy of control, it is the only thing up to us. The moment between impression and assent, between how things seem to us and how we believe them to be, is our moment of freedom. 

It is the moment where we can exert reason’s reflective power and change our minds. We can say “It seemed to me I should be angry here because I was insulted, but upon reflection, I really have no reason to carry a grudge. I haven’t been harmed at all. That person has harmed themselves, by being so bitter and angry, and it is better that I forgive or pity them than take on anger myself.” That is making proper use of an impression.

Every part of Stoic training is reducible to this single task for Epictetus, because the moment we confront impressions is the moment where progress is won or lost. It is the moment when we successfully make use of our training, or show that it was for nothing.

Why do we read books? Why do we practice mindfulness meditation? To make better use of impressions. Why do we journal, and introspect? All to make better use of impressions. 

In the rest of these lessons, we will cover what it means to make proper use of impressions, and how we can do it with confidence and consistency. We will also discuss how Stoic theory like the dichotomy of control or memento mori, play a crucial piece in helping us know how to handle impressions.

Because If we haven’t done this, achieved our chief task, then in Epictetus’ view we haven’t made any progress at all.

### **CT: Preconceptions**

CD: Understand what you value

If we accept Epictetus’ argument that all Stoic progress can be reduced to making proper use of impressions, that the essence of human good and happiness lies here, then the next question is obvious: How do we actually make better use of impressions?

To understand, let’s use an example from a friend of mine. My friend went to a halloween party. When she showed up, all of her friends were wearing very cool, attractive outfits, and ready to go out to the club dancing. My friend, unfortunately, had misunderstood the vibe, and did not realize they were planning on going out. She had dressed up as a giant inflated M\&M, the circular candy. 

She had a decision to make at that moment. She could be embarrassed, take the costume off, make an excuse, maybe leave the party and certainly skip the dancing.

But instead she laughed it off, embraced the silliness of the situation and went out with her friends.

Realizing you’ve shown up mis-dressed for a party, that you stand-out from the crowd unintentionally, that is an impression.

What you do next is how you make use of it.

So what went right here? My friend didn’t judge the situation as bad. Instead she judged it as silly, or an opportunity for a good story. In other words, what mattered was what category she applied to the situation. If she applied the category of bad, or shameful, or embarrassing, she would have felt that. But she applied a different category instead.

These categories are what Epictetus calls preconceptions. Preconceptions are ways of talking about values all humans share. ‘Good’ and ‘Bad’ are preconceptions. All humans share the idea that you should pursue good things, and avoid bad things, whenever possible. 

The key point here is that when we argue with people about what is bad, we might think we are arguing about the idea of badness, but we are not. We agree that bad things are to be avoided. We are arguing about if that preconception properly applies.

As Epictetus puts it:

Preconceptions are common to all men, and preconception is not contradictory to preconception. For who of us does not assume that Good is useful and eligible, and in all circumstances that we ought to follow and pursue it? And who of us does not assume that Justice is beautiful and becoming? When then does the contradiction arise? It arises in the adaptation of the preconceptions to the particular cases. When one man says, He has done well: he is a brave man, and another says, Not so; but he has acted foolishly; then the disputes arise among men.

Sentence\_Ids: 11602-11607  
Location: 1.22.1-3

To return to our previous example, if you show up to a party in the wrong kind of costume, is that a bad thing or is it a silly thing? That is a question about the application of preconceptions. All humans will feel something negative if they think a bad thing has happened. What matters then, is if we apply that category accurately, if this is actually an example of a bad thing happening. 

Preconceptions are important parts of Stoic psychology. They are why certain beliefs carry motivational force. Our minds are inherently designed to pursue and desire what we apply the preconception of good to, and avoid what we apply the preconception of bad to. If you remove the preconception, you remove the impulse and desire as well. 

It is for this reason that judgements around preconceptions become really important to get right. When you first read Epictetus, you might think we need to make proper use of *all* impressions, even those around basic facts. As the Stoics say, we should assent to true impressions, dissent from false ones, and suspend judgement when we aren’t clear. 

But Epictetus calls our attention to something more particular. It doesn’t really matter if we make a factual mistake that does not involve a value judgement. We can get away with these mistakes. A stoic can make a math error, or think it is a different time than it is.

When we really suffer and experience extreme passions, when we lose our Stoic progress, is when we make errors around the application of preconceptions around value. 

To get the time wrong is a mistake, but not a Stoic one. To judge your costume as shameful is a mistake according to the Stoics.

My friend embodied Stoicism because she accurately saw that it was in fact a silly situation, not a bad situation, to be dressed up at a club in a giant costume. She lived in accordance with nature at that moment, by not overvaluing what other people think – and got a great story out of it. 

As Epictetus says:

“Education has no goal more important than bringing our preconception of what is reasonable and unreasonable in alignment with nature.”

Sentence Ids: 601  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.2.6

If we can apply our preconceptions of good and bad accurately, then we can make proper use of impressions.

### **CT: A Standard of Judgement**

CD: Apply your preconceptions

We make all kinds of mistakes. Sometimes we lack knowledge around what is really good and bad. Maybe we’ve been raised to care what people think, and so when we stand out or are judged, then categorize this as a bad thing. 

Unfortunately, people are not very good at applying preconceptions concerning value. If we were, we wouldn’t need Epictetus to scold us, or Stoicism to teach us.

Sometimes, we just don’t think at all. An impression strikes us, and we go along with the room, the peer pressure, the energy of the moment. We are not reflective but passive, and the impression overpowers us. 

We accept what we are told, or how things seem to us, without pushing further to the truth of the matter.

Epictetus is particularly disappointed at the kind of errors that come from not reflecting. He thinks it indicative of a world where we value and give our attention to so many things besides philosophy and living well. As he says:

When we intend to judge of weights, we do not judge by guess: where we intend to judge of straight and crooked, we do not judge by guess. In all cases where it is our interest to know what is true in any matter, never will any man among us do anything by guess. But in things which depend on the first and on the only cause of doing right or wrong, of happiness or unhappiness, of being unfortunate or fortunate, there only we are inconsiderate and rash. There is then nothing like scales (balance), nothing like a rule: but some appearance is presented, and straightway I act according to it.

Sentence Ids: 11951 \- 11954  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.28.28-30

And Epictetus’ point is strong here. Often we do live unreflectively when it comes to what is really good or bad for us. People do have opinions on what makes a good car, or good fashion or a good restaurant, but if you asked them to define a good life they would likely struggle. Not because there is no answer to that question, but because they haven’t thought about it as much as they have thought about the other topics. 

We develop and apply standards for judging all kinds of things. It is a real shame when we don’t do the same for how to live. To be fair, how to live well is a harder question than how to measure if something is straight or crooked. But, Epictetus would argue that only gives us more reason to focus on it.

So how do we fix this issue of not thinking carefully about the right way to live? How do we apply our preconceptions accurately and fulfill our primary task?

Well, fortunately for us, Epictetus has a two part system.

First, to apply preconceptions concerning what is good or bad, you have to suspend judgement long enough to reflect on your first impression of a situation.

Epictetus says, when we confront impressions: 

But in the first place be not hurried away by the rapidity of the appearance, but say, Appearances, wait for me a little: let me see who you are, and what you are about: let me put you to the test.

Sentence\_Ids: 13165  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 2.18.24-25

By suspending judgement, you create a gap between impression and belief. You give yourself time to determine and adjust your reaction. If you don’t do this, you are living on autopilot. Sometimes you’ll judge things correctly, sometimes not, and you don’t have any ability to influence the process.

Second, once you’ve suspended judgement you need to apply a standard of judgement that you have tested and believe in. For example, Epictetus’ standard is that good things must be stable. They can’t be the kind of thing that comes and goes without our control.

So, he applies this standard to the idea of pleasure:

Pleasure (for example). Subject it to the rule, throw it into the balance. Ought the good to be such a thing that it is fit that we have confidence in it? Yes. And in which we ought to confide? It ought to be. Is it fit to trust to any thing which is insecure? No. Is then pleasure any thing secure? No. Take it Then and throw it out of the scale, and drive it far away from the place of good things.

Sentence Ids: 12712-12722  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 2.11.19-21

Maybe you aren’t a die-hard Stoic. Maybe it isn’t obvious to you that good things have to be stable. The point is not the content of the standard, it is that Epictetus has a standard. He has a way to judge if something is good or bad, just or unjust.

And then he applies that standard consistently. 

That’s why he says:

And to philosophize is this, to examine and confirm the rules; and then to use them when they are known is the act of a wise and good man.

Sentence\_Ids: 12729  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 2.11 

Philosophy is about questioning how you define what is good or bad, developing a solid criteria for those preconceptions and then applying it faithfully and consistently, even in situations where it may seem unintuitive.

Here is an example of the two part process in action: When someone insults you, on first impression this feels bad, maybe you begin to feel a prickle of shame or embarrassment. But instead of feeding into that feeling, you pause to test the impression. Then you can apply the standard. An insult is just the judgement of somebody else. And these can’t be bad things, because they aren’t up to us, they are often false, they often have more to do with the individual than reality. 

So you don’t apply the preconception of bad to the insult. You don’t accept that impression as true, and your feelings are not hurt in any deep way.

This is the two-part process. Examine the impression and apply a consistent standard.

But as Epictetus points out, we need to be consistent in how we apply standards. So, likewise, when someone flatters you, you need to apply the same logic. A compliment is not good, for the same reasons an insult is not a bad thing, they are just the words of another person. So again, you apply the standard. An inflated ego from compliments and shame from insults both come from a poor standard for judging the value of the words of others.

Anybody can look for tools to soften the pain of an insult. But someone who softens the pleasure of a compliment, because it is the true and consistent application of the philosophical principles they believe in, would make Epictetus proud.

This is making proper use of impressions.

### **CT: Precepts \- A tool for judging** 

CD: Put this into practice

To progress as Stoics, we need to apply standards of judgment consistently to impressions, so that we can correctly apply our preconceptions.

Epictetus’ 2-part system for correctly applying preconceptions is to pause and apply a standard of judgement.

But there is a problem here. What if your standard of judgement is false? For that matter, what standard of judgement should we use at all?

This is where theory comes in. Stoic theory is a tool to apply in your practice. Do not think then, that reading theory is sufficient to become more Stoic. You’ve got to use it. This is why Epictetus compares books to training weights. They are not the end, but the means to the end.

Theory provides us the standards of judgements that we use to evaluate preconceptions. It is the scale we measure things on. 

Let’s go over three of the best Stoic ideas with this framing in mind: that these are standards of judgements to be employed when evaluating impressions. 

First, there is the dichotomy of control. This is Epictetus’ idea that all things are either fully up to us or not, and that the good lies in the things that are up to us.

When you first learn about the dichotomy of control, it can seem like a call for where to focus your attention: focus on the things up to you. And that is true, to an extent. But it is also a standard to determine what deserves to be called good or bad.

If a tyrant demands obedience and immoral action in exchange either for a bribe of money, or to avoid the threat of exile or poverty, that is a complex situation.

How do you know what is good or bad here? How do you know how to act to and feel? You apply the standard. The immoral action is up to you, so it can be categorized as bad. But the money, exile, and behavior of a tyrant are not up to you. They then, can’t deserve the category of good or bad. 

So the answer, for the Stoic, is a simple one. I will not do a bad thing just to receive a bribe that is not actually good for me, or to avoid a threat that is not actually harmful. 

Employ the dichotomy of control as a standard when you need to determine what really matters in a situation.

Second, we can use the view from above as a standard of judgement too. As Marcus Aurelius wrote:

Observe the movements of the stars as if you were running their courses with them, and let your mind constantly dwell on the changes of the elements into each other. Such imaginings wash away the filth of life on the ground.

Quote\_Id: 111  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 7.47

The view from above is a standard to employ when trying to get a sense of scale. One way to think about it is like this: “Do I still categorize this situation the same, if I expand my sense of scale outside of myself, and the here and now?”  

For example, maybe you made an error, you made a mistake that was up to you. You did something unjust. So this was a bad thing, but how bad of a thing?

Apply the view from above. Change your perspective. Does the situation deserve the same categorization we reserve for major catastrophizes? If so, maybe you should feel as bad as you do. If not, maybe you’ve lost perspective and a sense of scope.

Third, we can develop a standard from the idea of Memento Mori. Marcus says: 

Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live while it is in your power, be good.  
Sentence\_Ids: 540-541  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 4.17

Unlike the dichotomy of control, or the view from above, which are about what really matters in a situation, memento mori can be seen as a piece of theory specifically developed to target a single problem.

More than any other error, across almost all philosophies and cultures people will tend to incorrectly apply the preconception of bad with death. 

The Stoics think there are a number of reasons we make this error, but one of the biggest is because we tend not to want to think about death. We don’t want to wrestle with it, with its inevitability, with how it will touch ourselves and those we love. So when the strong impression of death reaches us, we are unprepared and we apply our preconceptions incorrectly. 

Instead, we should use memento mori, both in preparation and as a standard. For example, the Stoic reasoning might be something like this: Death seems like a bad thing, especially for those you love. But death is part of our essential nature. It will happen to all people. Can our essential nature be bad? No, the Stoics think, because to be good is to live in accordance with nature. So death can’t be bad. It is the fear of death, caused by forgetting how natural it is, that makes it seem bad. 

All three examples, the dichotomy of control, the view from above, and Memento Mori, are Stoic ideas that have a lot to teach us. The point of this lesson is to think of them differently. Not as concepts to be wrestled with, but tools to be used. Scales to be brought in when evaluating impressions. Think of them as standards of judgement to apply your preconceptions. 

This is, for Epictetus, what all Stoic theory is working towards. It is worthless unless put into practice. And it is put into practice when it changes how we think about what deserves the categories of good and bad, just and unjust, courageous and cowardly. 

As Epictetus says, the key to living well is making proper use of impressions.
