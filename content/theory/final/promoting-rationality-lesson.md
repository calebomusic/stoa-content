**Promoting Rationality:**

**Story:**

I want to start our discussion of rationality with a story inspired by the book, the Scout Mindset, by Stoa Conversations guest Julia Galef.

The Dreyfus affair is an infamous military situation in late 19th century France. It is 1894 at the German Embassy in France, and the cleaning lady is actually a French spy. She discovers a torn-up note in the waste-basket which contains French military secrets that had been given to the Germans by a member of the French military. In searching for the culprit, the French military accused Captain Richard Dreyfus.

Dreyfus was the only Jewish member of the army’s general staff, and he was not well-liked. Besides a series of anecdotes, the only substantial evidence against him was that his handwriting resembled the note, although one of two handwriting experts who had been consulted disagreed that it was identical. 

Dreyfus, despite declaring his innocence, was found guilty of treason, stripped of his military rank, and imprisoned for life on incredibly poor evidence. That very well could have been the end of it, except the notes continued after Dreyfus’ arrest, and apparently the new spy, whoever it was, seemed to have identical handwriting to the note pinned on Dreyfus. A French Colonel in charge of counter-espionage, noticing the similarity in hand-writing, wondered if the second spy might have also written the previous, and if Dreyfus could be innocent. When he brought this evidence to the French handwriting experts, these experts concluded that the second spy must have been trained to write in an identical manner to Dreyfus.

The Colonel fought against push-back from the French military, who did not want to admit they had convicted the wrong man. And over 10 years he led a campaign to exonerate Dreyfus which was eventually successful. Dreyfus was free, but almost spent life in prison for a crime he did not commit. 

**Intellectual \= Ethical:**

There are two points about rationality I want to take from this story. The first is that just because our thinking involves reasoning, does not mean that it is correct, beneficial, or good. Once the French military had already decided they wanted Dreyfus to be guilty, and that they didn’t want to admit they were mistaken, then they used reason to construct more and more complicated explanations for why Dreyfus was guilty despite the evidence to the contrary, such as new notes appearing in the same handwriting. It is reasoning that constructed the argument that someone else must have been trained to write in the same manner as the obviously guilty Dreyfus. 

So what matters when we use reasoning, the goal itis serving. Are we using reason to pursue the truth? Or are we using reason to justify a conclusion or result we want to be true?

Reason alone is not always a good thing. In the case of the Dreyfus affair, it can be a tool to save face in a military scandal, or to justify racist beliefs. To paraphrase the 16th century philosopher Immanuel Kant, you might think of intelligence as a virtue, but you do not want a very intelligent serial killer. Intelligence and reasoning, if not directed properly, can easily become a vehicle for harm.

The second key lesson in this story is that it shows why, for the Stoics, reasoning is deeply connected to Ethics. According to Stoicism, we all do what we think is right. The anti-semitism demonstrated in the Dreyfus affair is a rational failure. It is a false belief that led at least some of his accusers to genuinely think that the Jewish Dreyfus was less loyal to France than other French Citizens, and thus more likely to have commited the crime. And if he commited the crime, then imprisoning him would help their country and protect the people they care about. 

The action was not senseless, or born from an necessarily evil soul. Accusing Dreyfus served a purpose that was logically consistent within their world view. Their cognitive failing, their failure to recognize and understand the very good reasons why Dreyfus may be innocent and how their prejudice was obscuring those reasons, became a moral failing when put into action.

So rationality is not just an intellectual skill. It is also an ethical skill. And that is why the Stoics thought that virtue was wisdom. If you knew what was right in each situation, and really understood why it was right, then you would pursue that correct course of action.

For example, for the Stoics, the virtue of Courage was defined as the knowledge of what is to be avoided and what is to be confronted. We should avoid unnecessary harm, like crashing your car or jumping from a plane, but we should not avoid other situations like standing up to a bully or a corrupt government.

Note that this Stoic conception of knowledge has to be actionable. It has to motivate you. Superficially knowing that stopping a bully is the right thing to do, is different than a thorough knowledge about why this is the case. It is that thorough knowledge, achieved through careful thought, study, and practice, that motivates action.

Since reasoning is also an ethical skill, it is so important to learn how to reason effectively, and avoid the traps others fall into. I will now go over five Stoic lessons and tools designed to improve our rationality:

**Stoic Lessons:**

1) Slow Down Judgment

The first lesson for good reasoning is to slow down judgment. The Stoics teach that when we encounter a situation, we receive a mental representation of what is the case. This is called an “impression”. So if I hear about a potential spy being captured, I may have an “impression” that he is guilty. However, it is only when we ‘assent’ to that impression, or accept it as true, that it becomes a belief.

What separates humans from animals is that we have control over this process of assent. We can examine our impressions, test them, and decide if we want to assent to them as true or not. 

However, we only have this ability if we slow down our judgment. If we let every impression become a belief without examination, then we might as well not have this ability to reason at all. So the first step to proper judgment is to not accept your impressions as true, but to slow down and examine them before they become beliefs. You must identify your impressions as impressions, not facts, and learn to interrogate, or test them for truth.

2) You don’t have to have an opinion.

Once you have identified your impression, the second tool for good reasoning is to recognize that you do not have to have an opinion. Yes, it is in our power to believe the spy is guilty or not guilty, but it is also in our power to suspend judgment until we have more evidence.

This is a third option which many people forget they have. This is not the same as being skeptical, or saying there is no truth of the matter, or that everything is only opinion. The idea here is to recognize where you have the expertise to make a correct judgment about your impressions, and where you lack that expertise or the relevant information.

Suspending judgment is crucial because beliefs have consequences. Beliefs shape our actions, our emotions, and the other beliefs we come to hold. So we do not want to believe things carelessly. We should always remember, just as we can judge an impression, we can also leave it alone.

3) Use a consistent standard of judgment

When we do want to judge an impression and form a belief, then we should remember the third Stoic tool for good reasoning: To always use a consistent and appropriate standard of judgment. 

A standard of judgment is the criteria by which you decide if something is true or false. For example, if I don’t believe something my friend says in an argument, I might search for an article that disproves them online, and take that as evidence they were wrong. That would be my standard of judgment for arguments with friends. 

There are two key ideas here. First, be intentional about what your standard of judgment is in each kind of situation, and make sure it is a good one. We want a standard that actually gets us closer to the truth.

Second, when you do pick a standard of judgment, be consistent. Often we change standards depending on how we feel about the situation. You might immediately accept good news or flattery as true, but be incredibly skeptical of criticism. You might think one article disproves your friend’s argument, but not take a single article as enough evidence to disprove your own.

These kinds of inconsistencies demonstrate an approach to reason that is defensive, and in pursuit of a predetermined conclusion, rather than in pursuit of truth. It was this kind of reasoning that led the same people to consider gossip as sufficient evidence that Dreyfus was guilty, but not consider a note in the same handwriting found after he was imprisoned as evidence that he was innocent. 

4) Reconstruct your opponent's position

This brings us to the 4th tool of good reasoning: Always reconstruct your opponent’s position, or the position that would prove you wrong, as well as possible., there is the idea of a straw-man argument. This is when you set up your opponent’s argument in a deliberately poor manner to make it an easy target to knock-down, like how a straw-man in a field is easier to knock-over than an actual enemy soldier. 

When reasoning, you want to always avoid straw-men, no matter how pleasing they might be to knock down. You avoid straw-men by using the principle of ‘charity’. To charitably reconstruct a position, is to construct it in its strongest form possible. 

A charitable interpretation of the Dreyfus accusation might be that Dreyfus had the ability to commit the crime, and anecdotal evidence that others suspected him to be of poor character.

The argument against Dreyfus fails even when constructed charitably, so that is how we can be confident it was a poor argument. Never knock down the straw-man, because if you are correct the position is false, then there is no need to.

5) Make decisions based on what is true, not what feels good. 

The fifth and final Stoic tool of good reasoning, is to remember the proper goal of all judgements. All judgements should be aimed at the truth, not what feels good. Often we become emotionally attached to our beliefs. They form an identity, or they make us feel comfortable. Beliefs can be self-serving too. They can make us feel good about ourselves.

But good reasoning pursues the truth, not what feels good or comfortable in the moment. We have to be very careful, intentional, and reflective about why we are making the judgements we are making and only make them if we take them to be genuinely true after fair and honest investigation.

**Summary:**

So to summarize these five Stoic tools for reasoning, they are:

1) Slow down judgment  
2) Remember that you don’t have to have an opinion.  
3) Use a consistent standard of judgment  
4) Always reconstruct your opponent’s position charitably  
5) Make decisions based on what is true, not what feels good.

Stoicism teaches us about the importance of reasoning, because our rationality is not just an intellectual skill, but an ethical one. What we think, determines how we act, how we feel, and who we are. In this way, there is nothing more important for a Stoic than reasoning correctly. 

On this note, it is appropriate to end with this passage by Marcus Aurelius: “If anyone can refute me…I’ll gladly change. It’s the truth I’m after, and the truth never harmed anyone. What harms us is to persist in self-deceit and ignorance.” (6.21)
