# Managing Impressions

Have you ever felt the satisfaction of knowing that you made an excellent decision? This is the feeling that arises when you’ve thought through a matter carefully and chosen the right path.

Alternatively, have you felt the anxiety or disappointment that comes when your thinking is confused? These negative emotions appear when we are overwhelmed by the problems that life throws our way and feel like we can’t handle them.

Stoicism is a philosophical program that helps us experience the emotions of success and confidence more often.

Fundamentally it is about thinking well.

By changing how we think, we can change how we experience the world and who we are. We can manage our negative emotions and transform into better people.

The essential part of this art is managing impressions.

Marcus Aurelius once captured this in his advice:

“If you can cut free of impressions that cling to the mind, free of the future and the past…and concentrate on living what can be lived (which means the present) . . . then you can spend the time you have left in tranquility. And in kindness. And at peace with the spirit within you.”

Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 12.3

Impressions are our initial sensations or appearances. They are how things appear to us. Consider the line: 

“Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space we find our freedom.”

Impressions are the stimuli. Hence, how we manage impressions is of central importance to the Stoics. Epictetus encourages us to see impressions as they are –as impressions, not reality. We are not our thoughts and our thoughts often do not reflect reality.

Responding to impressions well makes the difference between a happy and unhappy life.

Someone who is always reacting to events is not acting freely. They are like a puppet controlled by factors outside of their control. But someone who pauses and doesn’t let their first impression knock them off their feet can live a full and meaningful life.

This is why the Stoic philosophers called our ability to use impressions well “the most beautiful thing.” It unlocks an excellent life.

To manage your impressions well, focus on seeing things as they are. Believing the true, rejecting the false, and suspending judgment in the face of uncertainty. Develop the mental skill to change your mind when you should and act with resolve when you must.

The Stoics saw Socrates as the ultimate role model of someone who thought well. He didn’t accept things automatically. Instead, he lived critically and philosophically. He put his beliefs to the test – he didn’t accept them on faith or convenience. 

For the ancients, philosophy was a matter of theory and practice. When an interlocutor asked Socrates if he knew the definition of justice, he replied that “he displayed justice in his actions.” His actions were a result of taking philosophical life seriously. In a way, he lived an ordinary life for an ancient Greek. He served his city in the military, played a political role as a citizen, was a father, husband, and neighbor, and taught fellow Athenians in his leisure. But he did these ordinary things in an extraordinary way. That’s why we still know his name today.

We can follow in Socrates footsteps by improving how we think too.

In this series, we’ll cover some of the essential ideas and practices from Stoic philosophers for thinking better – all with the aim of living lives of confidence, success, and happiness.

# Stoic Mindfulness

When someone asks us “how was your day?” We respond by listing things that happen to us. External events that were outside of our control. 

We naturally think that life happens to us and outside us – and whether those things are good or bad determines how well our day goes. If we received a raise or a compliment, then we had a good day. If we missed the raise we deserved or someone insulted us, then we had a bad one.

But life does not happen to us. Life is what goes on inside.

Stoic mindfulness is an essential skill for living well. It is all about acting with presence and principles. For a Stoic, whether or not we did that is what makes a difference between a good day and a bad one.

Marcus Aurelius reminded himself:

"Objective judgment, now, at this very moment. Unselfish action, now, at this very moment. Willing acceptance—now, at this very moment—of all external events. That’s all you need."  
id: 193

Stoic mindfulness captures the way of being that the Roman emperor was seeking.

It is made up of two parts: presence and principles.

With presence, we attend to the present and are aware of our surroundings from the right perspective. You may know or have met those with this trait. They have presence – a way of being that shows they live in the now, even when they do the ordinary acts of life. They understand what’s happening and are in tune with the people around them.

We can understand this idea further when we think of people who do not exhibit presence.

Many are too distracted. They can’t hold their focus on what matters for a sustained period of time. You’ve probably met people like this too. They begin to pay attention to you in conversation but impulsively lose their focus. 

There are other people who can focus for a long time, but they aren’t aware of what surrounds them. They stay glued to their obsession, even when their life is falling apart around them. 

Finally, some people lack perspective. They live in the present, but cannot step back and see how their life fits into the whole. 

Stoic mindfulness involves balance. The ability to pay attention to what matters while maintaining awareness and perspective. Life is complex. It requires an agile mind to navigate well.

However, presence is not enough. One can be present, but not happy. Happiness requires making excellent decisions and thinking well. This is where Stoic principles come into play.

Every circumstance life presents us with is a question. How we respond is the answer. Stoic principles are what we use to respond well. They’re essential for managing our impressions well.

With presence, you can be aware of the stimulus. With principles, you can respond with excellence.

Excellence means acting with virtues like courage, moderation, justice, and wisdom. It means keeping your cool under pressure. It requires self-control – knowing the right amount of things. It involves treating others well, as they deserve. Finally, excellence needs wisdom, the wisdom to know what is right and how to do it. These are the principles by which you choose to respond to the world and shape it.

Both aspects of Stoic mindfulness: presence and principles, can be cultivated through meditation.

By focusing on the breath you can build attention. With practice, you can hold it for longer periods of time. Apply this skill outside of meditation and you’ll have built the general skill to focus. Awareness too is necessary. In meditation, notice whatever sensation is arising and embrace it. Let everything in. Finally, practice viewing things from the right perspective. With practices like the view from above you can situate your life as part of a greater whole. 

Meditation is a way to practice each of these parts of mindfulness. Once you truly feel what equanimity feels like, you’ll be better suited to experience it in ordinary life. With constant practice, you can develop a capable mind ready to handle what life throws your way.

By visualizing acting well and reflecting on your values in meditation you can refine your principles too. By learning about Stoic philosophy, you’ll have a better sense of the tactics for living an excellent life.

Remember, to pursue the truth and act with virtue. Everything else is not up to you.

# Critical Assent

Stoicism is a way to be all in on life. It’s a way of life where you’re always present and applying your principles.

A key strategy for doing this is critical assent. This is a central component of Stoic mindfulness. It is Stoic mindfulness in practice.

The ancient Stoic philosopher Epictetus described it as this:

“But first of all, don’t allow yourself to be dazed by the rapidity of the impact, but say, ‘Wait a while for me, my impression, let me see what you are, and what you’re an impression of; let me test you out.’ And then don’t allow it to lead you on by making you picture all that may follow, or else it will take possession of you and conduct you wherever it wants. But rather, introduce some fine and noble impression in place of it, and cast out this impure one.”

These are instructions for mental movement that we can apply to any area in our lives.

The steps are simple:

1\) Identify you received an impression without agreeing to it.  
2\) Examine the impression.  
3\) Introduce a Stoic principle  
4\) Make a decision.

The first step involves noticing your first sensations or thoughts. Imagine you see something or hear someone say something. The first step is like saying to yourself, "Okay, I just had this thought or feeling, but I haven't decided yet if it's true or important."

The second step involves reflecting on the idea. Understand what it is. Examine it.

Then introduce a Stoic principle. For example, "What happens outside of me isn’t up to me, but how I respond is." Apply this kind of thinking to your thoughts or feelings. This helps you see things in a more clear and calm way.

Finally, only agree with the thought or feeling if it withstands this critical process. Decide how to respond and act accordingly.

For example, if someone bumps into you in the subway, you may notice the sensation of heat. Perhaps thoughts that you’ve been wronged. Before becoming angry – bring your attention and awareness to these thoughts and feelings. Then apply the Stoic idea to pursue the truth. You don’t know whether you’ve been wronged. You need more information. Decide to calmly turn around in order to take stock of the situation. Gather information until you are ready for a decision.

This skill is powerful because it moves our focus on what is up to us. It requires real mindfulness. Steps one and two are all about noticing and examining what’s happening in the present. 

This attentiveness can help one feel more alive. Don’t sleepwalk through life. See it as if you’re looking at it for the first time.

Then apply your principles and values. As Epictetus encourages us:

“It is in accordance with this plan of action above all that one should train oneself. As soon as you leave the house at the break of day, examine everyone whom you see, everyone whom you hear, and answer as if under questioning. What did you see?... Apply the rule. Does this lie within the sphere of choice, or outside it? Outside. Throw it away.”

With critical assent, you build the skill of thinking well. Remember, our first thoughts and feelings are not necessarily true. Slow things down to get perspective and see things as they are. Start by keeping a single Stoic principle in mind like the idea to take full responsibility for what is up to you, your judgments and decisions, and embrace everything else. 

Train this skill in meditation. Then exhibit it in your life.

# Systems 1 and 2

In *Thinking, Fast and Slow* Daniel Kahneman, a Nobel prize-winning psychologist and economist, suggested that we have two kinds of modes: system 1 and system 2\.

This distinction is essential for thinking well.

​​System 1 is fast, intuitive, and automatic. It’s responsible for quick, snap judgements.

System 2 is slow, effortful, and analytical. It’s responsible for reflective deliberation.

Stoicism is all about thinking well. That means using systems 1 and 2 well. Before jumping to that, let’s note some examples of the two systems.

System 1 tasks range from activities like turning towards a loud noise, driving a car on an empty road, or detecting emotion in someone’s voice. They are automatic. Like when we think 2+2= (pause) 4, of course, the answer arises of its accord.

Alternatively, system 2 tasks range from filling out a complex form, reasoning through a philosophical argument, or comparing two washing machines for their overall value. This is difficult work, the kind of thinking that can leave one tired.

So – what are these systems good at?

In *Thinking, Fast and Slow* Daniel Kahneman catalogs the ways in which System 1 is often misled. Our intuitions can be accurate – but often they’re biased or only roughly accurate. Take the emotion of anxiety for example.

Seneca once said:

“We suffer more in imagination than in reality.”

Evolutionary psychiatrists agree.

In other words, we fear something when in reality there’s nothing to be nervous about. However, this strategy can make evolutionary sense. If you’re deathly afraid of spiders, you’ll squash everyone you see and survive – even if only 1% of spiders are dangerous. If someone isn’t afraid of spiders they run the risk of being bit by the 1% of spiders that are dangerous. 

So, in a sense, we’re more anxious than we should be, but this is for the best.

The issue is that evolution didn’t program us to live well. Evolutionary fitness isn’t the same as happiness.

Likewise, many of our intuitions are not designed to track reality. As such, even when they are roughly accurate, they cause us to make mistakes.

However, we can’t just offload all our thinking to System 2\. We don’t have the time or energy to reason through every problem we face. Moreover, System 2 uses data provided by our intuitions. We’re all familiar with the tendency to justify something we think is intuitively true. In many cases, System 2 is driven by System 1\.

Some psychologists have even surmised that the primary purpose of System 2 style reasoning wasn’t to solve problems but to justify our decisions to other people. In other words, System 2 serves the role of personal PR while System 1 is the true CEO\!

If you’re trying to think better, this paints a challenging picture. System 1 is often biased. But system 2 is too expensive to employ all the time and is itself hampered by biases. How do we think better?

We can better train our intuition and integrate deliberative thinking. 

Stoicism is about managing our impressions well. Some of these impressions are going to be produced by system 1, others by system 2\.  We need to use both systems as best as we can.

How do you use system 1 better? Think of System 1 like an athlete. An elite athlete’s responses are fast and intuitive. This is the result of training. To train well, you need to expose yourself to situations where you get fast and accurate feedback. This is the purpose of drilling. So, to train system 1, put yourself in the arena and get the experience you need to succeed.

We need to recognize when this strategy works and when it doesn’t. In some situations, the feedback you get isn’t accurate or doesn't happen immediately. In such circumstances, we can’t rely on system 1 alone and may even be wary of relying on it too much. Our intuitions about long-term investing or political questions are not reliable because these are abstract, complex issues, where the results of our decisions happen years after they are made. These are the kinds of domains where you need to sit down and think through matters rigorously.

And how do you use system 2 better? First, ensure that you set aside time to use it. Whether in the morning or evening, pause and reflect on your life.

When you're making large decisions. Break things down into more simple problems. Always go through the basics. Consider a complex investing decision. Simplify it as much as you can. Understand the math. Walk through your reasoning with others. 

And don’t forget to set yourself up for success. It’s harder to be a Stoic when we are tired, stressed, fatigued, and hungry. Why? Because System 2 requires energy. Train your ability to think and focus through meditation. With practice, the effort required to focus your attention will decrease. And when you are exhausted, don’t tackle hard problems. Set them aside until you take them on with energized focus.

What does using systems 1 and 2 well look like in Stoic life?

A practitioner found this distinction was essential for him at work. Because of his experience, he was able to rely on his intuition when dealing with different people or situations. But he needed to step back time and again and ask whether he was making the best decisions and managing his emotions well. By creating a focused time to pause and reflect, he was able to build confidence in his decisions and better handle what life threw his way.

In summary, recognize the domains where you can trust and train your intuition – and places where you need more sustained reflection. Integrate both systems into your life well. With training and meditation, you can improve your thinking and shape yourself and your life into a better one.

# Decomposition

Marcus Aurelius knew the corrupting nature of wealth and luxury. To retain his character, Marcus Aurelius reminded himself:

"When you have savouries and fine dishes set before you, you will gain an idea of their nature if you tell yourself that this is the corpse of a fish, and that the corpse of a bird or a pig; or again, that fine Falernian wine is merely grape-juice, and this purple robe some sheep's wool dipped in the blood of a shellfish."  
id: 1726

This is the Stoic strategy of decomposition. Step back and break things down into their parts. This helps us see things as they are. 

So much of life involves the stories we tell ourselves. Ancient Romans attached huge importance to the toga. But in the end, all it is is an article of clothing, some sheep’s wool. What we believe isn’t necessarily so. When we break things down into their parts, we can see that clearly.

The Stoics held that the good life was the virtuous one. It’s not determined by wealth, poverty, status, pleasure, or pain, but by how we respond to these things. 

The decomposition exercise is a powerful tool to use these things well.

For instance, take wealth. So much of the value of opulence is social, not material. If we look at material goods and see them as they are – well we realize that cars, houses, boats whatever are just physical things. Of course, many upshots of wealth are important. It can help you better care for yourself, friends, and family. But, in the end, many of the pleasantries of wealth just come from the stories we tell ourselves about it. If we take those stories too seriously, we risk using our wealth poorly. We can say the same for poverty. As Seneca remarked: “he is rich who wants little.”

Many of these questions come down to status. Humans are social creatures. We care deeply about what others think about us. Often other people’s opinions are informative and useful. We learn about the world and how we should act in it from other people. But we can overvalue our reputation. Many of us care about what others think for the wrong reasons. Here too we can use the decomposition technique just as Marcus Aurelius does when he asks himself:

“What then is worth being valued? To be received with clapping of hands? No. Neither must we value the clapping of tongues, for the praise which comes from the many is a clapping of tongues.”

What matters is whether we’re virtuous. Living well with others requires taking their opinions into account. We shouldn’t completely ignore them, nor should we slavishly devote ourselves to them. As the ancient Greeks said, nothing in excess. In the end, fame is oblivion.

So, the central use of this decomposition technique is a reminder to see things as they are. Don’t let the unnecessary value judgments we make about things capture your thinking. 

# Socratic Method

Stoicism, at its core, is about mastering the art of thought. This philosophy teaches us that by changing how we think, we can reshape our experiences and ourselves. By reflecting well and making excellent decisions, we can live a calmer and happier life.

So much of our culture today promotes the message that we need external things to be happy. We hear that it is wealth, reputation, influence, and achievement that are the route to happiness. Even when we know deep down that these messages are not true, they are difficult to ignore. Many of us devote our lives to pursuing these externals, rather than improving what is internal: our character.

In his pursuit of wisdom, Socrates stands as the ultimate Stoic role model. He embodied the perfect mix of theory and practice. 

He didn't simply accept the ideas and values of his society; he questioned them, rejecting blind faith and convenience. He tried to think as well as he could – whether he was acting in his role of a soldier, citizen, father, husband, or neighbor. Using his method of Socratic questioning, we can do the same.

The Socratic method is a systematic process of inquiry. Traditionally, it’s performed in dialogue. However, we can work through it introspectively too. Let’s quickly go over each step and then work through an example.

First, start with a thought or idea.  
Second, develop the full implications of that idea.   
Third, determine if there are any contradictions between the initial idea and its implications.  
Fourth, refine or reject the idea to make it more accurate.  
Fifth, repeat the process until you recognize your ignorance or possess a truly useful idea.

Let’s work through an example. In one dialogue with Socrates, he and his friends are debating the definition of justice. One person proposes the idea that justice is giving what other people are owed. If you borrow something from someone, then you should give it back. This is the idea.  
In the next step, Socrates thinks through the implications of that idea. Typically, when you borrow money from someone, you should pay it back. But does that mean that you should give back the money you borrowed from someone when you know they’ll use it to commit a crime?

At this step, we’ve identified a contradiction. The proposed idea of justice conflicts with what justice actually is – it doesn’t describe it correctly. Because we do not think that we should return borrowed money to people who will use it to commit horrible crimes.

So, Socrates' friends propose a new definition of justice. This discussion and inquiry continues. You can find it Plato’s *Republic* – one of the classics in philosophical literature. 

How can this method impact our own lives?

By working through the implications of our ideas, we can improve them.

For instance, we often have thoughts like:

* If I achieve this promotion, then I’ll be satisfied  
* My work isn’t meaningful

If we can summon up a questioning Socrates to work through these ideas, we can address anxious thoughts and make better decisions.

Do deep satisfaction and happiness emerge from career success? No. I’m sure you can bring to mind examples of dissatisfied and unhappy people who are wealthy and famous.

Is your work truly not meaningful? What does meaningful work look like? Can you define it? Does your job share any characteristics with those meaningful jobs? If it does, then isn’t it meaningful? 

By asking yourself these questions you will clarify your thinking about meaning in work. You may realize that your work is meaningful – our negative emotions are produced by imprecise thinking and cognitive distortions. Or you may find that your work isn’t meaningful in the way you want it to be – and be spurred to change your life.

Working through these questions may not be easy. The Athenians didn’t always appreciate Socrates’ questions. But as it is said, the quality of your questions determines the quality of your life.

People often react impulsively to events, becoming slaves to external forces. However, those who pause and maintain their composure can lead fulfilling lives. The Socratic Method is one tool we can use when we’re able to pause. Instead of accepting your ideas immediately, refine them, reject falsehoods, hold onto what’s true, and withhold your judgment when you must. Cultivate the mental agility to ask better questions, the confidence to alter your beliefs, and the decisiveness to act when required.

# Suspending Judgment

You don’t always need to have an opinion.

In our quest for knowledge, we often fall into the trap of believing that we must always have definitive answers. 

However, an insistence on certainty is limiting. The fact of the matter is we don’t always know.

The Stoics held Socrates up as a model of a good thinker because he knew his limits. In other words, he was aware of his ignorance. He didn’t proclaim knowledge when he didn’t have it.

The Stoic Cato used to say:

“I begin to speak only when I’m certain what I’ll say isn’t better left unsaid.”

Much anger and suffering is caused by people assuming they knew when they didn’t. A simple example – Noah is furious because Chris failed to show up at the coffee shop for a business meeting. He looks for him, with no success. He steams about how rude it is to set a meeting and then not show up to it. A few minutes later, an attractive woman already sitting in the corner for some time approaches him and asks, “Hi, are you Noah? I’m Chris.” 

Seneca once said we suffer more in imagination than in reality. That’s exactly what happened to Noah. The naive assumption that Chris was a man caused misery for no reason.

Unfortunately, our false beliefs cause us to suffer in more serious and weighty manners too.

Conflicts of life and death are caused by ignorant assumptions. Important decisions that shape our lives are led astray by the knowledge we thought we had but lacked.

Our lives would be better if we avoided false beliefs – that’s why the Stoics advocated suspending judgment. You don’t need to have an opinion about everything. Question your assumptions.

This practice requires conscious effort and a shift in perspective. Agnosticism, the position that we don’t know, is often the best place to end up.

For important and complex decisions, bring to mind the best arguments for opposing positions. When we deliberately explore the best cases for and against a proposition, we often discover that we simply don’t know the right answer.

That’s ok. Many issues don’t require an answer from us. For those that do, we can carefully seek more information.

Suspending judgment isn’t the same thing as avoiding decision-making. Suspending judgment is a deliberate, temporary state that allows for deeper contemplation and understanding. In contrast, avoiding decisions can sometimes stem from a reluctance to engage or take a stand on an issue. While it's essential to withhold judgment when necessary, it's equally important to be decisive when action is required.

In the process, we learn to hold our beliefs lightly. This doesn't mean we lack conviction but rather that we remain open to the possibility that our current beliefs may change. Holding beliefs lightly is a mark of adaptability and skill. 

Moreover, it makes it easier to change our minds when the world requires it. 

See things as they are. When you can’t do that, don’t make up stories. Know the limits of your thought and practice suspending judgment. 

# Praemeditatio Malorum

Seneca once wrote: 

"Everyone approaches courageously a danger which he has prepared himself to meet long before, and withstands even hardships if he has previously practised how to meet them. But the unprepared are panic-stricken even at the most trifling things. We must see to it that nothing shall come upon us unforeseen."   
id: 1492

This statement encapsulates the essence of 'praemeditatio malorum,' or negative visualization, a vital aspect of Stoic philosophy. The practice involves picturing potential challenges and setbacks to fortify the mind against the vicissitudes of life. The Stoics didn’t practice this to foster fear or ruminate but to think better. 

There are two fundamental reasons this exercise improves thinking.

First, it reflects the core of Stoic value theory. What matters is what is up to us: our decisions and judgements. In other words, how we respond to the world.

Normally, at the end of the day, you may ask yourself how it went. You may list out things that happened to you. Annoyances, problems, pleasures, experiences – whatever it is. 

That’s not how the Stoic thinks about things. The quality of their minds determines the quality of your days. It’s not what happens to them, but how they respond that matters.

So, when a Stoic contemplate adversity, they do not assume that external events will destroy their happiness. Instead, they remind themselves that contentment stems from our character. No event or person can take that away from you. By visualizing 'indifferents' \- things outside our control \- we prepare ourselves to handle them effectively when they arise. This is an optimistic message. Genuine happiness is rooted in the quality of our character and that is within our control. 

Secondly, anticipating potential misfortunes enables us to better prepare for them. By visualizing problems that may arise or ways our plans may be foiled, we can improve our plans. This is a key exercise that people take on – in business, athletics, and war. As Harry Truman once quipped:

“Plans are useless, but planning is essential.”

Things will go wrong – they won’t go according to plan. But you can prevent many common ways things do go wrong, identify mistakes in your assumptions, and, by doing so, think and act better throughout your life. This is a proactive approach to life's unpredictability.

So, how do you do it?

To practice 'praemeditatio malorum' effectively, consider the following guidelines:

Challenge Your Assumptions: Regularly question the beliefs and expectations you hold. Are they based on solid evidence, or are they mere speculations? Imagine a world where your plans don’t work – what’s the most likely reason they fail? Can you address that now?

Be Realistic: Envision plausible scenarios where things might not go as planned. This exercise is not about pessimism but about preparedness.

Don’t Ruminate: don’t envision tragedy after tragedy, disaster after disaster for its own sake. If you’re not ready to visualize intense challenges, start with small problems. 

Avoid Fatalism: While acknowledging potential difficulties, do not view them as inevitable. Focus on identifying ways to prevent or address these challenges proactively. 

Remember What Truly Matters: Central to this practice is the understanding that your character is the key determinant of happiness. External events are 'indifferents'; it is your response that truly matters.

'Praemeditatio malorum' is, in essence, about shielding our minds from suffering. It's about seeing life's challenges realistically and preventing them when we can. By anticipating adversity, we can strengthen our resilience and maintain our composure in the face of life's trials. It calls us to see things realistically. Problems will arise. No matter. We can navigate life with wisdom and tranquility.

# Surprise

Thinking well is a matter of reducing surprises.

Marcus Aurelius, the Stoic philosopher and Roman Emperor, once wrote: 

"Remember: you shouldn’t be surprised that a fig tree produces figs, nor the world what it produces. A good doctor isn’t surprised when his patients have fever, or a helmsman when the wind blows against him."   
id: 1703

Sometimes people see Stoics as advocating that we lower our expectations. This is not so. Stoics urge us to set our expectations appropriately. That is, they urge us to see things as they are.

According to the Stoics, surprise stems from either a false belief or ignorance. By understanding and anticipating the nature of things and people, we can maintain composure and resilience in the face of life’s unpredictability. 

Here’s Marcus Aurelius reminding himself about this:

"When you run up against someone else’s shamelessness, ask yourself this: Is a world without shamelessness possible? No. Then don’t ask the impossible. There have to be shameless people in the world. This is one of them.”

He’s calling himself to his senses. Why are you taken aback by adversity? Don’t you know the world you live in?

The philosopher Alain de Botton brilliantly captured how unrealistic expectations drive negative emotions like anger. As he says:

“We typically think of anger as a dark and pessimistic state of mind. But behind anger lies a surprising emotion: optimism. The angry are, beneath their ranting, possessed of some recklessly optimistic notions of how life might go. They are not merely in a destructive fury, they are in the grip of hope.”

So, when you find yourself shocked or taken aback by the actions of others or by certain situations, it indicates a lapse in your understanding or expectations. For instance, being surprised by varied behaviors in people, encountering rude drivers in traffic, or facing an economic downturn reflects our unrealistic expectations of the world.

Seneca, another Stoic philosopher, discussed the development of emotions, particularly anger. He noted that while the initial sensation or impression of anger is often quick and involuntary, it is our endorsement of this impression that fuels the emotion. For example, if someone bumps into us, the initial annoyance is natural, but it is the thought, "No one can disrespect me like that," that escalates this feeling into anger. By removing the element of surprise and accepting that such incidents are part of life, we can better manage our reactions.

Marcus Aurelius practiced this Stoic approach in his own life, maintaining composure even amidst the folly and errors of others. He understood that foolishness and mistakes are inherent aspects of human nature. By anticipating these, he was not thrown off balance by them. Of course, he wasn’t perfect. That’s why he had to remind himself of Stoic principles in his *Meditations*. We can do the same.

It’s essential to remember that we’re not perfect. We can’t expect to never make mistakes. That would just be another kind of unrealistic expectation\! So, when you catch yourself becoming surprised due to false beliefs or ignorance, see that surprise as an opportunity for learning. 

Ultimately, the Stoic aim of removing surprise is about seeing the world as it truly is. It involves a deep understanding of oneself, others, and the world at large. 

Stoic practice is a journey towards better thinking and skill. Each day presents an opportunity to enhance our understanding and reduce the likelihood of being taken by surprise.

Let’s end with another reminder from Marcus Aurelius: 

“The foolishness of people who are surprised by anything that happens. Like travelers amazed at foreign customs.”

This sentiment encapsulates the essence of Stoicism: the strength of mind that comes from preparation and understanding. By anticipating and accepting the inevitable challenges and inconveniences of life, we can face them with equanimity and grace, undisturbed by the ebb and flow of external circumstances.

# Seek Truth

For the Stoics, knowledge is what ultimately matters.

By seeing the world as it is we understand who we ought to be and how to transform ourselves to be that person. Thinking well and doing the right thing are tightly connected.

That is, we understand and are motivated to do the right thing. Theory and practice are, in the end, one.

So, as an essential principle: never flinch away from the truth. Remind yourself that it is necessary to become virtuous. 

A commitment to the truth is required to live well. To shy away from the truth is to hinder one's moral and philosophical development. Therefore, one must not only accept the truth but actively seek it out. 

This requires curiosity. Not the aimless kind, that is more interested in the process than results.

Curiosity, according to Stoic thought, is inherently self-eliminating; it exists to find knowledge. As one philosopher once wrote:

“If in your heart you believe you already know, or if in your heart you do not wish to know, then your questioning will be purposeless and your skills without direction. Curiosity seeks to annihilate itself; there is no curiosity that does not want an answer.”

Socrates was admired because he sought the truth – not just because he asked a lot of questions.

This quest, however, is not always a comfortable journey. Truth can challenge our ego. It can expose our flaws, mistakes, and vices. This confrontation with reality is a fundamental reason why Stoics likened philosophy to visiting a hospital. Just as medical treatment can be unpleasant yet necessary for healing, facing uncomfortable truths is essential for moral and personal reform. Each truth, no matter how unsettling, can be seen as an opportunity for improvement, a treasure that aids in living a better life.

Epictetus advised his students

"If you want to improve, be content to be thought foolish and stupid with regard to external things. Don't wish to be thought to know anything; and even if you appear to be somebody important to others, distrust yourself."

The pursuit of truth is not merely an intellectual exercise but a practical tool for personal transformation. By embracing truth in all its forms, we allow ourselves to be shaped by it, aligning our actions with virtue and integrity. By suspending judgment when we cannot have it, we avoid making poor decisions and acting with vice.

Embrace the discomfort of self-examination and transformation, recognizing that the path to virtue is with sincere and joyful self-reflection.

Let’s end with a reminder from another Stoic, Musonius Rufus:

“Knowledge concerning life is what philosophy is."
