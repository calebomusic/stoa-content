# Introduction

Stoicism is a philosophy of action. It teaches us how to live, provides a picture of the good life, and asks us to live up to our potential. This is very different from much of today’s ‘Academic’ philosophy, which can be abstract or concerned with very specialized arguments and have little to do with day-to-day life. 

Both Stoicism and contemporary academic philosophy are called “philosophies”, but they seem to aim at very different things. 

To encapsulate this difference Stoic scholar Pierre Hadot coined the phrase “Philosophy as a way of life”. Stoicism is a philosophy as a way of life. Its target is to make us live better. It directly challenges what we think, feel, and do.

Contemporary logic, metaphysics, philosophy of language, or even many contemporary ethics are not philosophies of life. They may put value in discovering truth and clarifying concepts but despite being called philosophy they are a different kind of thing. 

If you are using Stoa, it is because you care about philosophy as a way of life. You want to know what Stoicism has to teach you about improving some aspect of yourself, from your emotions to your perspective, to your behavior towards others. You care in some way about living a good life, about achieving happiness, and about being a good person. These are the central concerns of philosophy as a way of life. 

This is not just an intellectual pursuit. Philosophy as a way of life cares about what you do, not just what you know. A brilliant ethics professor can be a terrible person, abuse their students, bully their family, steal, lie, and cheat, and they could still be considered a good academic philosopher. This is not the case for Stoicism. A Stoic who is mean, angry, and cruel is a bad Stoic, no matter how much theory they can recite or books they have read. Philosophy as a way of life’s standard for praise or contempt, above all, is how you actually live.

This is an amazing part of Stoicism, but it is not exclusive to it.  There are many schools of thought that also think about philosophy in this way. In fact, the time of Stoicism was a kind of Golden Age for this. The Stoics found themselves entrenched in debate with many rival schools, such as the Epicureans who valued experiencing pleasure and limiting pain, the Skeptics who hated overconfident beliefs, and the Cynics who valued being free from societal conformity.

This course will provide a brief introduction to other philosophies as a way of life. Even if you side with the Stoics, it is worth exploring other Philosophies as a way of life for the following reasons:

1. First, as Seneca says in his Letters to Lucillius, we should always be eager to find a good idea even if it comes from the enemy camp, not as a deserter but as a scout. Even if you disagree with these other schools, they have something to teach each of us about how to live.  
2. Second, studying other philosophies will deepen your understanding of Stoicism. The Stoics exist in conversation with other philosophies of life, and it is easier to understand Stoic arguments if you know what kinds of lives they are responding to or defending against.   
3. And third, they might actually be right about some things the Stoics get wrong. As Marcus Aurelius says in his Meditations, if someone could prove to him Stoicism was wrong he would gladly change his mind. He is not after being a Stoic, he is after the truth. it is a good exercise, regardless of your beliefs, to consider if someone else might be right.

In the lessons that make up this course, we will explore different philosophies of life. Some are like the Stoics, and some are quite different, but each has something to teach us about the best way to live. 

**Epicureanism:**

As a philosophy of life, the Epicureans were all about pleasure. This school, named after its founder Epicurus, argued that the good life consisted of a life that maximized pleasure and minimized pain. They were literally hedonists. Hedon is just the Greek word for pleasure, and a hedonist is someone who values pleasure as the best thing in life. 

At first glance, this might evoke images of salacious parties, filled with wine, food, and drugs. Epicureans seem like the kind of people you invite to a party, not the kind of people you emulate to live a fulfilling life. But upon deeper inspection, the life the Epicureans recommend is surprisingly similar to the Stoics. 

Epicureans viewed the ideal, most pleasurable state not as the high of the party, but what they called ‘ataraxia’, literally a life free from disturbance. Their pleasure then was one of a relaxing weekend afternoon on vacation with some close friends. The kind of day where you feel like you have everything you need and not a care in the world. 

So any sort of extravagant life that caused pain was off the table. Partying might be fun, but doing something we regret while drunk, or having a hangover, is not fun, and only a foolish hedonist ignores the pain that comes with drinking to excess. Stealing might get you what you want in the short term, but being in jail, or fearing getting caught, does not make this long-term a pleasurable experience. It’s better to be honest and guilt-free, according to an Epicurean than dishonest and worried about getting caught.

As Epicurus said:  “No pleasure is in itself evil, but the things which produce certain pleasures entail annoyances many times greater than the pleasures themselves.” (Principal Doctrines 8\)

The Epicureans were also astute philosophers. Like the Stoics, they noticed that a lot of our suffering comes from the stories we tell ourselves. Sure there is the physical pain of a hangover, but there is also the mental pain of regret, shame, anxiety, and guilt. If we can target and remove these kinds of pain, then we can go a long way towards living a tranquil life. 

So why aren’t we happy already? Why don’t we each find ourselves living lives of maximum and Calm pleasure?

Like the Stoics, the Epicureans identified that our desires as the source of our suffering. If we desire too much, if we are always craving more, then we will never achieve ataraxia. So we should limit our desires to things that are easily achievable. Enough food to be full, but not a fancy meal.

Naturally, the Epicureans recommended healthy types of pleasure, things which, if we desire and pursue them well, will bring happiness. Friends, Epicurus taught, are some of the most important sources of long-lasting pleasure. We should cultivate strong friendships and keep them close.

Physical pleasure matters too, but we should be careful to moderate our desires. A good meal, a full stomach, and a place to sleep are all the physical comfort and pleasure we really need, so long as we stop ourselves from feeling like we need more or comparing ourselves to others. 

Intellectual pleasure was very important to the Epicureans. Not just the pleasure of solving a good sudoku or puzzle, although surely that counts, but the pleasure that comes from inside ourselves. For example, good memories, the Epicureans argued, were a source of constant joy that we can return to whenever we need to.

The Epicureans were self-oriented but not vicious. They cared about virtue but only in so far as it contributes to ataraxia. They agreed with the Stoics that we should be wise, courageous, just, and temperate, but for different reasons. For the Stoics, we should be virtuous because that is what matters for a good life. For the Epicureans, we should be virtuous because virtuous people tend to be happier people.

In summary, let’s consider what an Epicurean life would look like: You would prioritize pleasure and minimize pain wherever possible. You accept pain, only when you think it will lead to pleasure later. Like removing a splinter, working a difficult job, or apologizing for something we feel guilty about, sometimes we have to do painful things to maximize the joy of our lives. Additionally, you would pursue pleasure that is sustainable, rather than destructive and short-lived. Sustainable pleasure comes from virtue, friendship and having enough physical goods to meet our needs, so you would try to maximize these things in your life. Finally, you would focus on identifying and addressing the mental sources of suffering, not just the physical ones. This means limiting unnecessary desires, anxieties, phobias, or fears that cause harm but don’t lead to more happiness.

From this description, it is worth noting that many Modern Stoics might actually be closet Epicureans. If you want above all to be happy, to feel good, and see Stoicism as the way to get there, then you might be an Epicurean adopting the tools of the Stoic. The Stoic pursues virtue, with happiness as a pleasant side-effect. The Epicurean pursues pleasure and sees virtue as the best way to get there. 

# Skepticism

One of Socrates’ most famous phrases is that he is wiser than other people because at least he “knows that he knows nothing”

The real line is a bit longer. In Plato’s Apology (21d), Socrates describes a realization he had when debating philosophy with another:

“I am wiser than this man, for neither of us appears to know anything great and good; but he fancies he knows something, although he knows nothing; whereas I, as I do not know anything, do not fancy I do. In this trifling particular, then, I appear to be wiser than he, because I do not fancy I know what I do not know.”

The skeptics are a school of ancient philosophy who took this idea to be the most important part of philosophy. While the Stoics think the greatest harm is vice, and the Epicureans think it is pain, the skeptics argue that the greatest vice is a false belief. 

Skeptics think our lives go poorly, and we suffer because we hold onto false beliefs as if they are true. In this way, they have a lot in common with the Stoics. Both the Stoics and Skeptics agree that our beliefs determine our emotional lives and our behavior. We feel angry because we think we know what Justice is, and that someone has broken that rule. We feel anxious because we believe that someone else dislikes us. We fight to protect our reputation and pride because we believe we will be worse off without it. 

When the Stoic sees these facts, their answer is a simple one: discard the false beliefs and keep those that are true. It is wrong to feel angry when someone insults us because it is literally not true that we have been harmed. But it is fine to desire virtue or think it is unjust to be bribed because these statements are true.

However, the skeptics are more, well, skeptical. They put forward a simple three-part argument:

* First, nothing can be known, not even that we know nothing.  
* Second, it is very bad to assent to something that is false  
* And Third, Therefore, No one should assent to anything.

This made them go even a step further than Socrates. He claimed to know his own ignorance, but the Sceptics were not even comfortable claiming that\! 

But why, you might naturally ask, do the skeptics think it is bad to assent to something that is false? Well, that is because false beliefs lead to disturbance. Summarized, Skepticism's **ethical** position was as follows:

* The goal of a good life is peace from disturbance.  
  * Disturbance comes from desires and beliefs.  
  * Therefore, Suspension of belief provides freedom from disturbance.

Putting aside the obvious irony that comes with a skeptic making any sort of argument,  Skepticism is therefore defined as a kind of defensive philosophy. Things go wrong for us when we form false desires and beliefs, and there is no way out of this because we can never confidently know which are true beliefs, so we must suspend judgment and avoid our tendency to think we understand the world. 

So in practice what would a modern skeptic's life look like? Well, it is hard to say for sure since the Skeptics don’t prescribe specific actions or behaviors as the ‘best’ life beyond adopting a skeptical attitude, but there are some things we can say with confidence. First, the modern skeptic would avoid extreme positions. They would not be quick to anger from the news or social media, nor would they be apathetic or passive. They would consider all sides in political and social debate and avoid extremism. 

Second, their life, I suspect, would be one that is open to change. Since the modern skeptic entertains other ideas they would probably change their opinion about which is most likely to be true a number of times. They are unlikely to act the same way they did last year, and would not classify certain cultures or choices as off limits. They would avoid any absolute claims or ‘limiting beliefs’ about their own life. They would not define themselves as a certain kind of person who can or cannot do something. All of these things are achieved by suspending absolute judgment. 

Now you might be naturally wondering how it is possible to live as a skeptic. Maybe these are helpful tools for avoiding false beliefs, but how can we act in the world as true skeptics? Won’t we just walk off a cliff or into traffic if we don’t think the car is real, or that dying is a bad thing?

The skeptic solution to this is to act on ‘probability’. The skeptic has impressions that seem reasonable to them, but they never endorse them as true, and so never fully commit to them and experience the stronger feelings that come with belief. So they go about acting on what seems reasonable or probable, but never take anything for certain. It is probable that this is a car and that dying is a bad thing so the skeptic avoids it. 

Just like the Stoics practice memento mori or the view from above, the skeptics had exercises to help them live like a skeptic. First was the repetition of phrases to remind ourselves of our ignorance. My favorite skeptic phrase is this: “Perhaps, it is possible, maybe”. Again, the skeptics didn’t think everything around us was false, only that we can’t tell what is true or not. So anytime we encounter a situation, we should repeat to ourselves “Perhaps, it is possible, maybe” to avoid feeling too strongly about it either way.

Another exercise is the argument for and against. The skeptics thought that for any argument you make, there is just as compelling of a counterargument. The argument for or against exercise is where you construct that counter argument to your own beliefs. Carneades, the head of the Academic skeptics, famously demonstrated this exercise on a trip to Rome. He delivered an amazing speech on Justice to the admiration of all present. The next day, his speech was a criticism of his previous position. It was just as eloquent and persuasive, leaving everyone stunned.

Whether this is a persuasive argument for how to live or the skeptics admitting the limitations of their own school I will leave it up to you to decide, but it reminds me of Epictetus’ ancient criticism of the skeptics. To paraphrase, ‘you claim to be a skeptic, but if I throw this pot at your head you will duck every time.’

# Cynicism

There is one story that captures the spirit of cynicism. Diogenes was the founder of the Cynic school, and was famous for living in Athens while ignoring social norms: For example, he intentionally had minimal possessions and slept in a barrel in the middle of the city. One day is sunbathing in Ancient Greece. Alexander the Great, the most powerful man in the world, is walking by and notices Diogenes. Alexander has heard of him and admires his astute commitment to philosophy and walking his own path. Alexander walks up to Diogenes, casting a shadow over him, and asks if there is anything he, the most powerful man in the world, can do for him. Diogenes responds “You can get out of the way of the sun.” 

This captures the spirit of cynicism. Strong individualism and confidence in what one wants, but also minimalistic and natural desires. A cynic has the freedom to tell Alexander the Great what they really think, because they don’t want anything from him, not his wealth or his approval, nor do they fear anything about him, not his anger or punishment. 

In his Meditations, The emperor Marcus Aurelius compares people to bees. We are all small parts of a larger hive, working towards a greater common goal in the interest of all. If the Stoics are bees, then Cynics are stray dogs, living outside any system, but living in a way that is true to themselves. This metaphor is direct from ancient Greece \- Cynic comes from the Greek word for dog.

Although the Stoics place a strong emphasis on our social roles and what it means to be a neighbor, sibling, or citizen, the cynics reject these roles. Or, more precisely, the Cynic carve out their own role on the edge of society, not held to the rules that govern the behavior of others. In this way, the Cynic acts as a foil to the social conventions we have constructed, causing us to question them the same way Socrates asked people to question their beliefs. Diogenes famously would walk around naked, urinate and defecate in public, and sleep in a barrel, not for the joy of offending others, but in part to show the *possibility* of a happy life that includes these behaviors. By doing this, he made Athenians question the necessity of their social conventions and their definitions of a good life.  
   
Of all the ancient schools of thought, the cynics are the most respected by the Stoics. Zeno, the ancient founder of Stoicism, was a student of the Cynic Crates of Thebes, was a pupil of the Diogenes mentioned earlier. In fact, after Socrates, Diogenes the Cynic is the most referenced person in Epictetus’ discourses. If there was a family tree, cynicism is Stoicism’s cousin.

The Stoics, or at least Epictetus, saw the Cynics as playing a crucial inspirational role for others. To paraphrase the scholar Malcom Schofield: *Epictetus regards the cynic as a scout and a messenger. As a scout the cynic discovers whether certain ways of life pose any danger to mankind; as a messenger he reveals to others the result: that there is no real danger, as good and evil are always within our power.* 

The cynic shows us that we can live in a barrel and still be happy. Where the Stoics differ from the cynics though, is that the Stoics think Stoicism is for everyone, but cynicism is for a select, dedicated few. The Stoic follows social norms but does not allow them to trump virtue. The Cynic intentionally rejects social norms. The Stoic accepts wealth, comfort, and success when available to them, the cynic avoids these. The Stoic cares about their family and personal connections, the cynic tends to live alone. 

As a philosophy of life, it is hard to imagine what ‘practicing cynicism’ might look like, since cynicism is a very intense philosophy that is not for many. However, I can say with confidence that being a cynic does not resemble our modern-day definition of cynicism, which is characterized by a distrust of others and a focus on one’s own interest at the expense of others. Ancient Cynicism is not about hating others or being jaded. It is about an intense self-knowledge about what you need to be happy and the confidence to live in accordance with that even if it goes against social norms. So practicing cynicism might look something like this:

1) First, Adopt a ‘cynical’ stance towards social norms. Recognize them as social constructs that are arbitrary and can differ between societies. Begin practicing actions that go against these norms (but don’t harm others), to practice unlearning your socialized conformity to these rules.  
2) Next, question what is required in terms of possessions to be happy. Do you need a bed? Do you need hot food? Practice going without these things gladly to unlearn your beliefs about their importance.  
3) Third, when doing these things don’t leave society but stay in the middle of it. At the beginning, it will be an opportunity to perfect your cynic practice by avoiding temptation, and when you become advanced it will be an opportunity to inspire others. We do, after all, still talk about Diogenes today. 

From the description I just gave it is not surprising we don’t have many modern-day cynics. But I want to end with an important clarification. Cynicism is not about rejecting social norms. That is reactionary. It is about pursuing the truth, which the cynics thought naturally required going against social norms, as social norms are often false or developed to promote cohesion rather than map onto reality. So the Cynic pursues truth, not a shock factor. But that genuine pursuit of truth can often seem quite shocking. 

# Existentialism

When was the last time you felt authentic? Like you were living life by your own rules, the way it was meant to be lived, and not because of someone else’s rules? Some of us may have more of these moments than others, but we all know what it feels like. 

We also know what it feels like to live inauthentically. To do things others told us to do. To close ourselves off to the possibility of self-expression because we are afraid. To not take a chance, or say what we think, because it would bring us into the unknown. 

Existentialism argues that inauthenticity is the largest threat to a great life. However, many of us choose to be inauthentic, because it is easier. 

We want other people to tell us what to do, to avoid the responsibility of freedom. Here is an example:

During the Second World War, the famous French existentialist Jean-Paul Satre is corresponding with a pupil living in occupied France who is stuck with a personal dilemma. “Should I move to England and join the free French fighting against Naziism? Or should I stay and take care of my Mother?”. What kind of person am I? The student asks. What is the morally right thing to do? He wants his wise philosophy teacher to tell him, and free him from the burden of this choice.

Sartre responds to that student: “You are free, therefore choose \- that is to say invent. No rule of general morality can show you what you ought to do: no signs are revealed in this world.” (Pg. 38). 

All of us are like Sartre’s pupil. We each have difficult choices to make. This freedom is challenging so we try to pass it off to someone else: Our genetics, cultural norms, what our parents or friends think, or maybe even the opinion of our enlightened philosophy tutor.   
But to be existentialists we must reject that tendency, embrace the challenge of freedom, and make our own choices.

Existentialism, unlike Stoicism or Epicureanism, does not refer to a specific school of philosophy with a single founder. Rather, it refers to a broad philosophical movement, most popular in Europe, that peaked throughout the 19th to 20th centuries. While the term is controversial and not everyone agrees who is or is not an existentialist, some major figures are associated with the history of the term.

The major figures could include the Danish Kierkegaard in the early 1800s, author of the famous Fear and Trembling and Either/Or. Kierkegaard wrote about how to live in a state of doubt about the existence of god. 

Another might be the German Nietzsche, author of Beyond and Good and Evil, and Thus Spoke Zarathustra. Nietzsche wrote about moving beyond pre-established Christian moral norms, into a morality of your creation.

Finally in mid-20th century France we have figured that people agree to be existentialists: Albert Camus, Simone de Beauvoir, and the previously mentioned Sartre. Simone notably extended these themes to realities of living as a woman in the 50s, and how social norms and expectations for women further limited authentic living.

Existentialism has also always had a close connection with art, and was not afraid to embrace that medium to share and explore philosophy. Sartre and Camus both wrote several novels, the most famous perhaps is Camus’s The Stranger. Dostoevsky is primarily known as a famous Russian novelist but is often considered an existentialist for the themes explored in his work.  
Not everyone called an existentialist agreed with each other. But there are some core commitments that all existentialists share. These do not apply unanimously, but they will give you a general sense of existentialism as a school.

First, existentialism rejects the idea of god providing inherent meaning to the world, or at least considers the possibility of a world without inherent meaning. This is what Nietzsche meant when he said “God is Dead, and We have killed him”. In a world without inherent meaning, there are no absolute moral truths to discover. Taken alone, this would be a radical philosophical position in Christian Europe. 

The next, and arguably most important, Existentialist claim, is that one consequence of the ‘Death of God’ is that our existence precedes our essence. What this means, is that humans are not inherently good or bad, or anything at all. We have no necessary essence or soul. It is our job, throughout our life, to define that essence. This is why Sartre says “Man first of all exists, encounters himself, surges up in the world and defines himself afterwards”. This is a rejection of Stoic morality entirely. The Stoics say we have an essence defined by nature (which is also god). 

That essence is to be rational pro-social beings. All of Stoic ethics is learning to achieve that nature. To become the person you were meant to be. Existentialists reject that foundation of morality.

Finally, existentialists argue that the only thing left to do in a world without inherent meaning provided by god is to live authentically. This means to bravely acknowledge this lack of meaning and creatively choose a life for ourselves that we think is worth living. The existentialists create their picture of the good life, they don’t discover it by interpreting the will of god or the words of a sage. We live inauthentically when we avoid this process because it is difficult or scary.   
So these are three core commitments of existentialism: if god does not provide inherent meaning to the world, then our existence precedes our essence and we must choose to authentically define ourselves.

To look at how an existentialist would live following these ideas, we can return to our story of Sartre’s pupil. The young French man has an existential dilemma between two very different kinds of life. Should he join the army, or stay in France and take care of his mother? 

The most tempting path is to pretend like the dilemma is not one. Psychologically, it is comforting to turn a complicated situation into one that is black and white. A normal person might search for an argument that justifies one choice over the other. Or they might talk to a friend who will tell them what they want to hear and that the choice is simple. We have a natural tendency to want things to be simple or clear-cut. In these cases, we don’t make free choices because we don’t recognize our freedom. We are not authentic. 

But the existentialist must live authentically. They must recognize the challenge in what is a challenging choice. Sartre reminds his pupil that there is no right or wrong answer for him as an individual because he has no essential essence. He is neither essentially a fighter nor a caregiver. 

So, to live authentically, the pupil must make a creative choice between two types of lives, while acknowledging that this is his free choice to make. He must bravely choose path A or B himself. No one else can choose for him, or explain why one is necessarily better than another.   
This is to live as an existentialist. It is to bravely create for yourself your essence and meaning, in a world where there is none to be discovered, only created.

# Buddhism

We all desire something: Money, respect, authority, happiness. And this desire dictates our lives. It is why we do the things we do. We want to satisfy this desire.  
But anyone who has gotten what they desire has realized that life is not that simple. When you get what you want You almost always just want something else. 

If you try to break this cycle, all you’ve done is change the object of desire. You haven’t stopped desiring, you just desire to desire different things or to be someone different. 

At its worst, this can seem to be an inescapable curse of being human. We desire. We try to satisfy that desire. And suffer when we fail to get what we want and we suffer when we succeed and the target moves. For Buddhists, life is suffering.

Buddhism takes this as the central problem of happiness. Its promise is a solution to this cycle. Buddhism does not offer us what we want but offers a way to think differently about desire and craving altogether.

Buddhism is named after Siddhartha Gautama, the original Buddha, which means “enlightened one”. He was born in the 5th or 6th century BC in what is now Nepal at the border of modern-day India. It is always interesting to note that depending on the Buddha’s exact age and date of birth, he likely was alive at the same time as Socrates.

Buddha’s life is one of various extremes. He was born into a rich powerful family, but after seeing the suffering of common people on a trip outside the palace, he renounced his wealth and dedicated his life to ascetic meditation. This included self-mortification, or intense physical denial such as living on a diet of leaves and roots. After years of self-denial, Buddha realized this focus on the body was not the correct path to spiritual enlightenment and he abandoned asceticism, turned his attention inward towards his mind, and achieved enlightenment. For the remainder of his life, Buddha spread the message of Buddhism through northeastern India, before dying and passing into nirvana.

In Buddha’s time, people pursued happiness either through wealth or asceticism. They either tried to satisfy their cravings for material possessions or entirely denied it. Buddhism represents the “middle way”, a way to achieve happiness without either extreme wealth or suffering by turning your attention inward to your mind to dissolve craving.

Before I discuss what this middle way looks like, I should note that Buddhism is a complex tradition and that there are at least three major schools. I will focus on what these schools share in common, the four noble truths and a focus on craving and its associated suffering.  
Buddhism has four key doctrines, known as noble truths, that describe the state of the world.  
First, that life is suffering second, that the root of suffering is craving. Third, that craving can cease. Fourth, the way to cease craving is to follow the Buddhist practice of the eightfold path, which includes eight aspects of living that we must master, including action, speech, understanding, and mindfulness.

The first noble truth, that life is suffering, sets Buddhism apart from many other philosophies. Buddhism is in many ways a *negative* philosophy. Life is suffering, period. The goal of philosophy then becomes to understand that suffering, and how we might escape it.   
This brings us to the second noble truth \- that the root of suffering is craving. Like Stoicism, Buddhism recognizes that how we perceive the world affects our emotional life. If we desire something, we will be happy we get it, and suffer when we lose it. If we desire to avoid something, like death or change, we will suffer when it inevitably happens. 

Buddhism identifies three types of cravings. The first is the craving for sensual pleasure. This is a traditional kind of hedonism, a desire to feel good. Second, is the craving for being, to become something. This is a craving for status or power. For example, we have this kind of craving when we want a job title, or to achieve a personal development goal we set for ourselves. Third, there is the craving for non-existence. This is when we want to avoid the unpleasant things in life. We crave for non-existence when we want something to not happen. 

Buddhism is not the only philosophy to place desire and craving at the center of self-improvement. In Stoicism, we master desire by desiring the right things, namely virtue or excellent character. Stoics teach us to desire to perfect ourselves, not the outside world.   
But unlike Stoicism, Buddhism sees even that kind of craving as a trap that can lead to disappointment, it is the second kind of craving, the craving for being.

This understanding of craving leads us to the third and fourth noble truths. Suffering can cease, says Buddhism, and it can cease by following the eightfold path. At the final stages of this path, through a combination of mindfulness, meditation, and practiced awareness, you will be advanced enough to dissolve your craving without replacing it with another.

So if this is a description of the Buddhist path, what does this path look like in practice?   
A Buddhist’s life involves a strong awareness and questioning of the biases of our brains and our cognitive traps. We tend to crave things. We tend to attach our identity to things like our work, our appearance, or our social life. We tend to tell stories about ourselves and have a strong sense of personal identity, or ‘who we essentially are’. A Buddhist will be both aware and skeptical of these cognitive tendencies. 

Awareness is not easy, so they will also have a mindfulness practice to cultivate this self-awareness. Self-awareness allows feelings and thoughts to come and go without attaching value judgments to them or attributing them to their identity. 

Finally, a Buddhist will also turn this attention outward. They might ask themselves, what can I do for others when I am not obsessed with satisfying my desires? How can I be a better member of my family or community, or help those in need? As their focus on their cravings diminishes, they will have more time to focus on others.

If this life sounds similar to the Stoic, it is because in many ways it is.   
Both Stoicism and Buddhism are about mastering desire. The first of the Stoic disciplines is the discipline of desire.

But Mastery looks different. For the Stoics, to master desire is to point desire toward the one thing that is actually good, our character. We can see this strategy in how the Stoics define the minimal self. Stoics identify themselves as only their minds and character, the thing that is up to them, and dedicate their lives to making that as good as possible. We are our character, and so we should only desire to perfect our character.

Buddhists go one step further. They dissolve even themselves and any desire at all. The stronger we hold on to a sense of self, or that feeling that we need to become a certain kind of person, the more we will struggle and suffer.   
In other words, Buddhism practices letting go. Stoicism practices holding onto only what is most important.

# Aristotelianism

- Aristotle is arguably the most the important ancient philosopher

- Teleology: Everything has a purpose of function, and it is achieves excellence by fulfilling that function  
  - So ask yourself, what is my function?   
    - For Aristotle, we are rational, social animals. We achieve our function by achieving excellence across the rational domains (acting well), the social (reputation), and the animal (rich, comfortable, well fed), parts of ourselves.  
      - A stoic focuses on the rational. For Aristotle, an excellent person is great at all three.   
- Virtue: Still, rational excellence is a big part of our lives. Aristotle defined this as acting well. A good person has three components to their action:  
  - Right thing \- Practical Knowledge  
  - Right reason \- Theoretical Knowledge  
  - Right feeling \- Emotional Habituation  
- Golden Mean: Finally, Aristotle thought that virtuous action was a mean between extremes. We are not courageous or cowardly (dichotomy). Instead courage is the right balance of recklessness and cowardice. The kind person is the right balance of being a push-over and being a jerk. The humble person is the right balance of egoistic and self-deprecating.   
  - Virtue is about the right balance, and where that balance falls will depend on context and situation you find yourself in.
