**Stoicism and Emotions 101**

When you think of a “Stoic” person, what is the first thing that comes to mind? Is it their happiness? Their virtuous character? For most people, especially those beginning to study Stoicism, the key feature of a Stoic is their ability to remain calm under pressure, to not succumb to extreme emotions, and to not complain in the face of adversity. In other words, most people connect Stoicism with a mastery of emotions. A stoic is not quick to anger, does not succumb to fear, and cannot be manipulated by others into feeling a certain way. 

There are lots of characters from film who display Stoic qualities. Consider James Bond, who is cool under pressure, and able to execute his plan even when the stakes are high. Or Yoda from Star Wars, the wise teacher who reminds an emotional and frustrated Luke Skywalker “Do or do not, there is no try”. 

A lot of people start learning about Stoicism because they want to be like these characters. They want to be cool under pressure. They don’t want to feel anxiety or fear or grief. They don’t want to feel like their anger controls them.

But Stoicism was not just about being cool under pressure. It was an entire system for how to live. And there are risks to trying to be Stoic without understanding this system. 

First, many people who want to be in control of their emotions, just end up repressing or ignoring what they feel. This often does more harm than good. While it can look the same from the outside, there is a big difference between repressing anger, and genuinely not being angry. Stoicism offers the promise of cultivating an inner citadel, a fortress that is impervious to the stresses of life. But this requires a transformation of our emotional lives, not just putting up a wall around how we feel. A fortress cannot be built on shaky foundations or with low quality materials.

Second, emotional control is not valuable if you are not a good person. James Bond’s disposition to be calm under pressure is great as a hero, but we would not value the same qualities in the villain.

This Stoicism and Emotion 101 lesson is going to provide a basic overview of what Stoicism has to say about emotions and how these fit into an ethical, happy life.

## Cognitive View of Emotions

The first thing to do when tackling a philosophical question, is to go back to definitions. What even are emotions?

We talk about emotions all the time. We all describe ourselves as happy, sad, angry, nervous, or jealous, but not everyone has thought deeply about what they are referring to.

According to the third leader of the Stoic school, Chrysippus, emotions are value judgments that are followed by certain physical reactions. In other words, an emotion is when we make a value judgment about the world, that causes us to feel a certain way.

For example, imagine a bully pushes your friend, and they fall and hurt themselves, and this makes you angry. We can go through this example and try to identify where that anger comes from. The push itself didn’t cause anger. Friends push each other around all the time and it's part of playing around. Your friend being upset or hurt doesn’t cause anger. You wouldn’t be angry if your friend slipped and scraped their knee. You might be sympathetic to their pain, but not angry. Rather, anger has nothing to do with the external event at all, but arrives when we form a very specific kind of belief, namely that something bad has happened and it is someone else’s fault.

We are angry because we think our friend’s pain is bad, and we think this bully caused that pain. When we form this belief, we experience a number of physical and mental responses. Mentally, we might be quick to act, and prioritize short term revenge over long-term decision making. We might try and ‘get even’. Physically, our breath might quicken or we might experience a rush of adrenaline as we prepare to defend our friend.

This value judgment, this belief, *is* the emotion and coincides with the feelings we associate with anger.

In this picture, emotions are rational things. They are types of value judgements made by our intelligent human minds. This is called the cognitive view of emotions. It is an important distinction, because there is a tendency in modern times to think of emotions as being the ‘opposite of reason’. We use metaphors where we talk about reason wrestling with emotions, or we contrast the emotional individual with the cold-calculating rational person.

The Stoics say this is the wrong way to look at the relationship between thinking and feeling. Feelings, the physical sensations we associate with anger, grief, or fear, follow from how we reason and think about the world. These two things don’t compete against each other. People will feel differently depending on how they think differently. So emotion is not separate to, or opposite from, reason. 

This is what the Stoic Epictetus means when he says “It is not things themselves that disturb men, but their judgments about things…So whenever we are impeded or disturbed or distressed, let us blame no one but ourselves, that is our own judgments.”

All Epictetus is saying is that nothing upsets us until we form a judgment about it. Without that judgment, there is no extreme emotion.

A further nuance of this view worth discussing is that emotions are only a certain kind of judgment, namely value judgements. We experience emotions when we judge things to be good or bad. This is why a sports team losing, a fact about the world, can produce different reactions in different people. To a fan, the loss is a bad thing, and they feel anger or sadness. To the opposing team’s fan, the fact of the loss is a good thing, and they feel joy or pride. To the person who doesn’t follow sports, the fact is neither good nor bad. It is a piece of trivia, not something that elicits an emotional response.

So only value judgments are emotions. Non-value judgements are emotionally neutral. The fact that 2 \+ 2 \= 4 doesn’t make us feel anything. 

But we also know that our emotional lives are complex. We feel in different ways, not just positive or negative. Joy is a different feeling than anticipation, which are both different from gratitude, awe, or love. We experience different emotions because we can form different kinds of value judgments. Emotions are complex, because how we think about the world is complex.

The cognitive view of emotion is the first key lesson on the Stoics and emotions because if you do not understand this distinction then everything Stoics have to say on emotions, and emotional therapy, won’t make sense. It is the key Stoic view that the rest of their arguments are built upon.

## Taking Responsibility for Emotions

As we covered in the last session, the Stoics adopt a cognitive view of emotion. This means they think emotions are rational things. They are value judgments we make about the world that cause physical responses. 

But the cognitive view of emotions on its own doesn’t tell us anything about how we should feel. The next questions to ask are, for the Stoics, what does a healthy emotional life look like? What should we do with our emotions?

To better explain the Stoic view on emotions, consider the distinction between what I call a “Utility” approach, and a “reflective” approach to emotions.

Someone with a utility approach to emotions wants their emotions to be productive or useful for some other goal. They view emotions as tools to be harnessed to their advantage. They try to change their emotional life to only feel emotions that provide value.

In fact, I think many people first begin Stoicism with a utility mindset, myself included. Someone like this wants to become master of their emotions, so they can harness emotions to achieve their other goals. For example, someone with a utility mindset might try to get angry before a basketball game, so they can perform with greater intensity. If they are sad or anxious, they might try to become numb, so that they don’t have to experience the pain of sadness. They might try to force themselves to be happy, or engage with what is sometimes now called “toxic positivity”, where they refuse to consider any facts that may jeopardize that happiness.

The problem with a utility approach to emotions is that we end up looking for the beliefs that produce the feelings we want, irrespective of whether they are true. This may work in the short term, but never works in the long run. 

Instead of this, the Stoics ask us to engage with our emotions with what I call a ‘reflective’ approach. When we take a reflective approach to emotions, we don’t use our emotions as tools. Instead we recognize that our emotions reflect the way we think about the world. If we feel anxious or angry, the next step is not to repress or push away this emotion. The next step is to reflect and ask “why do I feel this way?”, or rather “what is this emotion telling me about my beliefs and how I view the world?”.

On the reflective approach, the feelings that come from emotions are not tools to be harnessed, but instead they are hard-wired physiological responses to certain kinds of beliefs. A healthy emotional life then does not come from controlling our emotional or irrational self, it comes from having healthy beliefs.

The Stoic takes responsibility for their emotions, because they understand that their emotions are just reflections of their beliefs, which they do have the ability to determine. We are responsible for how we feel, because we are responsible for what we think.

Taking responsibility for our emotions can be a counter-intuitive thing. We often describe people’s emotional dispositions as if they are necessary parts of them, like their hair color. “James is just an angry guy”, someone might say, as if James can’t change this. But the Stoic insight is that James being an “angry guy” really means that James has the tendency to form anger causing beliefs. Maybe James thinks people are out to get him, or that people don’t give him the respect he deserves.

The Stoics recentre the problem back to the individual. James is angry because of the way James relates to the world. And the way we relate to the world, unlike our natural hair color, is something we can change with effort, introspection, and practice.

This is not to say that people might not have reasons for being angry. I do not mean to say that every time we are angry or sad it is our “fault”. But we are the cause of that anger and sadness, because it is the result of our judgements. This mindset shift, from seeing emotions as something that happens to us, to emotions as something we do, actions we take, is empowering. Because we are empowered, and have the ability to change our emotional lives, we have a responsibility to change too.

To quote Lisa Feldman Barrett on emotions: “Sometimes we're responsible for things not because they're our fault, but because we're the only ones who can change them.” 

Lisa is a world leading Neuroscientist, who specializes in emotions, and argues for a few very similar to the Stoics. The point is that we may have a tendency towards anger because of our childhood, or past experiences we can no longer change, but we are still responsible for fixing that anger because we are the only ones who can. 

So what does a Stoic taking responsibility for their emotional life want to feel? The goal for the Stoic is to have the emotional life that follows from having true beliefs. This, and this alone, is the fundamental Stoic position on what emotions we should or shouldn’t have. We should strive to feel the way we would feel if we only had true beliefs, and got rid of the false ones.

The Stoics cared so much about their emotions reflecting the world, that they even had different terms for ones that did and those that did not. “Passions”, or Pathe in Greek, are emotions that derive from false beliefs. The Stoic goal is to eliminate all passions. But “Good-Feelings, or Eupathe in Greek, are those emotions deriving from true beliefs. The Stoics had no problem with Eupathe.

So the best Stoics, the sages, have no passions, but still a range of Eupathe, or good-feelings.This means they don’t feel the passionate anger that comes from a wounded ego, or the anxiety that comes from not having accepted their own mortality. But they can still feel the love that comes from having a good friend. Or a gentle-caution at avoiding outcomes that would be better to avoid. Even someone who has accepted their own mortality should be cautious not to fall from a high-place unnecessarily.

To summarize, the Stoics argue that emotions are not things to be controlled, because they aren’t things that happen to us, rather they are things we do. So when we experience emotions we don’t want to experience, rather than trying to control them, we should reflect on what judgments are causing them. Then, when we understand ourselves, we can take responsibility for these emotions and change them if we think they are the result of false or harmful beliefs.

## Why are Stoics so stoic?:

If Stoics are allowed to feel anything, so long as that feeling comes from a true belief, then why do the Stoics have a reputation for being so calm and collected? Why do they have a reputation for not complaining? This is for two reasons.

First, many extreme emotions come from false beliefs. 

One type of false belief is you might overvalue something. I used to get anxious before competing in sports, because I put too much value on the outcome. My life would be fine regardless of if I won or lost, but by thinking my happiness depended on winning, I naturally felt nervous. 

Or we might overestimate the likelihood that a bad thing will happen. Many phobias are like this. It is reasonable to fear being in a plane crash. That would be a bad thing for yourself and those involved. But what differentiates normal caution from an extreme phobia, is the person with a phobia of flying vastly over-estimates the chance of crashing, or focuses on that chance above others, such as getting in a car crash driving to the airport. In this case, the idea is right but the proportionality is wrong.

When we catastrophize or hold distorted views, this leads to distorted and extreme emotions. Although it is not always easy, if you can correct for these you will have eliminated many of your extreme, unhealthy, emotions.

But what about the extreme emotions that come from true beliefs? The reason Stoics don’t experience many extreme emotions from true beliefs, especially negative extreme emotions, is because Stoic ethics teaches that there are few things worth judging as extremely good or bad.

Up until this point, nothing I have said about the Stoics and emotions has connected to other parts of Stoic philosophy or ethics. The idea that emotions are value judgments, and that we should strive to have true beliefs and false beliefs, could be endorsed by people who disagree entirely with the rest of Stoic ethics. For example, the argument that reputation matters above all else, and that we should feel incredibly upset if our reputation is tarnished by gossip or the opinions of others, is not very Stoic at all. But it does not conflict with the Stoic position on emotions as I have described it so far. 

The final stage to understanding the Stoics on emotions then, is to also understand Stoic ethics. Stoicism is not just psychological theory. It is a philosophy of life, with interconnected views on ethics, human psychology, and the nature of the universe. It is this deeper system, and the Stoic arguments about ethics and what is really valuable in human life, that gives Stoics the reputation for being calm, cool, and able to do the right thing even when it isn’t easy.

If we approach emotions as Stoics, then we have to ask the ethical question of what is truly valuable and harmful? 

The only thing that is really good, in the Stoic view, is virtue or good character. Virtue is to be valued above all else, and no amount of money, fame, or external reward is worth compromising our characters for. It is this ethical argument that makes practicing Stoics so emotionally resilient.

A Stoic believes that good character is the most important thing for a good life. But they also know that virtue is accessible to everyone. We can always choose to act well, no matter how terrible the situation we find ourselves in. So the best thing in life is something that is accessible and up to us, or in our control.

Because the quality of a Stoic’s life is not determined by other people or external events, other people can’t make them inappropriately angry, sad, or scared, because they have no power over them. This is the inner citadel, the fortress of emotional strength that grants every Stoic a kind of freedom.

The Stoics compared those who lacked this freedom to slaves

The 1st century Stoic teacher Epictetus himself was a roman slave, so we can assume he does not speak of slavery frivolously. He put it this way: “The unrestricted person, who has in hand what they will in all events, is free. But anyone who can be restricted, coerced, or pushed into something against what they will is a slave.” (ID 1708). 

Epictetus’ point is that if someone wants something outside of themselves, they can always be threatened or bribed. If you think your happiness is fragile, and depends on others, or external things, then people can control you with those things.

But the Stoic does not think you can really harm or help them, at least not in the sense that is most important. The only thing that really harms or helps a Stoic is whether they are the best person they can be, given the situation they have. 

So this is why the Stoic’s have this reputation as emotionally calm and collected. A Stoic who only wants to be a good person, and avoid being a bad person, would not experience extreme emotions because of external circumstances. They would be calm under-pressure. They would be able to handle challenging situations well. They would not be quick to anger, but also not be easily intimidated.

This is the emotional life that Stoicism promises, but only if you commit yourself to a deep transformation of how you view the world, and what you consider valuable and necessary for a good life.

## CT: Managing Emotions

CD: Change your feelings

Now that you understand how the Stoics think about emotion, it is time to train your emotions like a Stoic. 

One strategy is a simple three step process Epictetus recommends. This is something to work on over weeks, to transform yourself. 

First, we non-judgmentally observe the feeling to understand ourselves. Here you try to understand what the emotion is and what might be causing it. 

Second, once you recognize that feeling, then you take steps to avoid feeding into that emotion. Here you want to avoid making things worse, or acting in a passionate way.

Third, you interrogate, or improve the belief causing the passion. This is done by testing our beliefs against stoic theory. 

In this lesson we will cover this three-step process to provide tools for the better management of emotion. 

The first step to managing your emotions is to stop, observe and understand. At this stage you want to non-judgmentally understand how you are feeling. Like a doctor diagnosing a patient, you don’t start cutting into them for surgery until you know what’s wrong.

We often skip this step and it leads to nasty results.

Emotions are complex because our beliefs are complex. What you think is sadness could be anger. What feels like frustration might be grief.

At this stage, don’t try to shut these feelings down. Non-judgmentally observe what you are experiencing.

This will give you insight into who you are, and what you think about the world.

Many mindfulness practices stop at this step, and encourage radical acceptance. They ask you to see your emotions as things that happen to you, not as something you produce. 

Mindfulness is essential for Stoic emotional improvement, and that is why we practice it on Stoa. But Stoics are discerning. They want to transform their life, not just accept it.

Stoics acknowledge their emotions, but we don’t accept them as inevitable or good. They move on to steps 2 and 3 to improve themselves.

If the first step is to stop and understand what you are feeling. The second is to drop the habit of feeling this way, or at least avoid feeding harmful feelings.

The Stoics knew that harmful emotions can create a feedback loop. 

Epictetus says, “when you have been angry, you must know that not only has this evil befallen you, but that you have also increased the habit, and in a manner thrown fuel upon fire.”

It would be lovely if we could snap our fingers and stop being angry people, or break ourselves out of sadness or bitterness. But we can’t, that is not how the mind works.

What we can do, through intentional effort in the moment, is avoid adding fuel to the fire.

There are a couple ways to do this in practice. First, you can pause when you notice a passionate response. When someone insults you, don’t yell something back or throw a punch. Just pause. 

Give it 10 seconds for the initial pull of the passion to passover. If you can make it through that time without doing something harmful, you’ve stopped the momentum of the passion.

Another way to avoid adding fuel to the fire is to recognize what sets you off. As Stoics, we know we can’t control our environment, but we can influence it. Try to avoid the things that you don’t react well to.

Maybe what sets you off is certain people, maybe it's the news, or certain topics. Avoid these things for now. Not to avoid them forever, but so that you can build yourself up to be able to handle them well later.

As Epictetus says, there is a real value to just avoiding passionate responses. He continues:

“If then you wish not to be of an angry temper, do not feed the habit: throw nothing on it which will increase it: at first keep quiet, and count the days on which you have not been angry. I used to be angry every day; now every second day; then every third, then every fourth.”

Stoic emotional development starts slow and steady. Everyday you do not become angry, or feed into your emotional patterns, is a success. It is a further step towards becoming a more Stoic person.

But just avoiding anger is not the same as being a calm person. Just like avoiding things that make you sad is not the same as being happy.

Finally, in the third step we take action to fix the problem.

Because our feelings are the result of our beliefs, we fix how we feel by thinking about what upsets us, carefully, intentionally, and with Stoic ideas in mind. Essentially, we try to change our own minds, by considering if the things making us upset are really true. 

Epictetus frames it as follows. When we encounter a situation that will carry away our emotions, quote: 

“...be not hurried away by the rapidity of the appearance, but say, Appearances, wait for me a little: let me see who you are, and what you are about: let me put you to the test.” (Disc. 2.18)

Once you’ve identified that you are reacting emotionally to a situation and either not been carried away by it or taken the time to reflect later, you’ve created a space to change your response. You’ve created a space to put your first impression to the test.

In that window, we have the freedom to change what we think and so change what we feel.

Here is a quick example: Imagine someone insults you and you have the impression that this is something worth getting angry about, that you have been harmed.

Step one is to identify that you are angry because you have the impression you've been insulted, and you take that insult to be a bad thing.

Step two is to avoid feeling anger in the moment, or, if that moment has passed, avoid feeding into your anger later, so that you are calm enough to reflect. Maybe you successfully paused in the moment and walked away. Or maybe, despite getting angry in the moment you’ve taken steps to avoid the kinds of people that insult you and make you angry, so that you’ve calmed down and have the time to reflect. 

Step three is to actually examine if your impression or belief that you have been harmed by an insult stands up to Stoic questioning.

You might ask yourself ‘Is being insulted really a bad thing?’ or, ‘Is what other people think of me up to me, or just something I can’t control?’

When we put our beliefs to the test, we will realize that it is not a bad thing to be insulted. Maybe that person means to harm us, but we haven't been harmed unless we let it get to us. 

This is the last stage of these three stoic steps for transforming your emotions. We can’t apply stoic theory unless we understand ourselves, and have avoided feeding into disruptive emotions.

Be sure to apply these techniques in your life. It is not enough to study Stoicism, it needs to be practiced as well. 

## CT: Avoid Unrealistic Optimism

CD: Pursue the truth

Now that you understand the Stoic theory of emotions, and have begun practicing managing your own emotions, you may notice something. It will start to work.

You will begin to have a different emotional life to your past self, and even to other people you know and love. 

But there is also a risk to Stoicism. Poorly practiced, Stoicism can become unrealistic optimism. 

Unrealistic optimism is when you do not acknowledge negative emotions or the possibility of poor outcomes, either for yourself or others.

An unrealistically optimistic person will never admit when something’s wrong. They will shame themselves for feeling negative emotions, and they will have little sympathy or time for the negative emotions of others. 

On the surface this seems to have a lot in common with Stoicism:

1\) Stoics will not consider many situations as bad or harmful, even when they seem that way to other people, including people losing their jobs, getting sick, or even death in the family. 

2\) Stoics want to eliminate passions, or any negative emotion based on incorrect beliefs

3\) Stoics, especially new practicing Stoics, often have trouble immediately sympathizing with the passionate responses of other people. 

But despite these similarities, Stoicism is not unrealistically optimistic. And understanding these differences will help you get a more clear picture about what a healthy Stoic emotional life looks like in practice. 

The first difference between Stoicism and unrealistic optimism, is that Stoicism aims for emotions that accurately represent the way the world is. It does not ignore aspects of the world. 

Consider this line from Seneca, writing to Lucillius after his friend Flaccus has just passed away

“Let not the eyes be dry when we have lost a friend, nor let them overflow. We may weep, but we must not wail.”   
Id: 1322  
Source: Moral Letters  
Location: 63.1

Seneca is not ignoring that there are bad things in the world. In technical Stoic language, there are things we would prefer not to happen: dispreferred indifferents. A Stoic recognizes and feels those things.

However, the Stoic does not exaggerate or add unnecessary judgements to those feelings. They don’t add the kinds of judgements that would turn weeping into wailing, that “my life is over”, or “I will never experience true friendship again,” or “I cannot live without Flaccus”. 

The Stoic tries to see the world as it is. It is a place where, while we would prefer to avoid death, especially for the young, it does happen and we are strong enough to deal with it. 

The unrealistically optimistic person ignores parts of the world they cannot deal with to remain happy. This is not Stoic knowledge but willful ignorance.

In terms of dealing with others, unrealistic optimists often do not sympathize or acknowledge negative feelings in others. Stoicism encourages you to sympathize, but stop short of fully empathy.

Empathy, in the way I am using it, is when you share in someone’s feelings by literally feeling the same thing. Their anger becomes your anger. Their sadness, your sadness. Stoics don’t do this, not even for their friends. But they are still caring friends and family members.

As Epictetus says:

“When you see a person weeping in sorrow either when their child goes abroad or when he is dead… So far as words then do not be unwilling to show him sympathy, and even if it happens so, to lament with him. But take care that you do not lament internally also.”   
Id: ?  
Source: Handbook  
Location: 16

For friends and family, Stoics understand that not everyone practices Stoicism. When someone we care about gets upset, we should meet them where they are at, and provide the kind of comfort they need to feel better. We should sympathize.

But that sympathy does not have to become empathy. We can console someone without lamenting internally as well. Not only can empathy be un-Stoic, it can often be unhelpful. 

Someone’s anger can become dangerous if you add to it with your own. Someone in grief often needs a recognition that something terrible has happened, but they also need the kind of calm support that comes from having a clear head.

Stoics care about others around them, and provide that care by sympathizing without empathizing. 

Instead of doing this, the unrealistic optimist treats emotions as a binary: you are either positive or negative. You are either contributing to the solution or part of the problem, and so there is little time for the negative feelings of others 

Stoicism understands that our emotional lives are a complex craft we build through years of practice. We can be better or worse at our craft, but a binary over simplifies this. And regardless, people still need help to navigate their emotions, even if you don’t agree with their judgements.

In summary, Stoicism well practiced is not unrealistic optimism, even as you try to eliminate the harmful emotions from your life. 

When you practice Stoicism for emotional development, remember to strive to see things as they really are, understand that you will make mistakes, and that you can sympathize with others without having to feel what they feel.

The Stoic approach to emotions takes work and time. And while you are putting in this work, avoid unrealistic optimism. It is a short-cut that does not take you to the same destination.
