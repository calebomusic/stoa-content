### **Content: Stoic Value Theory \- Theory**

PS: “Understand what matters most”

PD: “Learn about what the controversial things the Stoics call good, bad, and indifferent. Understanding this distinction is the key to putting Stoicism into practice.”

### **CT: Introduction**

CD: The controversial stoic view  
   
Since its inception Stoicism has been controversial.

What the Stoics believe about the good life is radical. 

The Stoics argue that someone can live well no matter their external circumstances. They could be happy even on the torture rack, the Stoics would say.

For the Stoics, virtue is the only good, vice is the only bad, and everything else is indifferent.

Virtue for the Stoics is a good character, or the knowledge required to make the right choices with the options presented to you. Vice is the opposite, ignorance and bad character. 

This is why Epictetus says:

Where is the good? In our choices. Where is the evil? In our choices. Where is neither of them? In those things we do not choose.  
Id: 1675  
Source: Discourses  
Location: ???

Tell a non-stoic who has just lost their job, or a pet, that the only evil is their own ignorance and you are more likely to make things worse than provide any kind of philosophical therapy. 

Tell a non-stoic who has won the lottery, or achieved a big goal, that nothing good has happened and you will come off like a nihilistic pessimist, not a wise philosopher.

Not only is the idea of virtue and vice as the only good and bad things radical, it is central to Stoicism. The stakes are high here. If you can show this to be false, then many of the practical tools of Stoicism fall apart. 

The Dichotomy of control rests on the idea that we should focus on what is up to us, because everything that is good is up to us.

As Epictetus says:

“The chief task in life is simply this: to identify and separate matters so that I can say clearly to myself which are externals not under my control, and which have to do with the choices I actually control. Where then do I look for good and evil? Not to uncontrollable externals, but within myself to the choices that are my own…”

Id: 1793  
Source: Discourses  
Location: ???

If the things outside of our control matter to our happiness, then it makes no sense to ignore them, and the dichotomy is false. The Dichotomy of control relies on the argument that good and evil are found in our choices. That’s why we should focus on what is up to us – because that is where happiness and unhappiness are found.

The Stoic goal to live in accordance with nature, is another idea that relies on this axiology. To live in accordance with nature means, in one sense, to live in accordance with the facts, to demonstrate knowledge our actions. 

If there are good or bad things besides virtue and knowledge, then maybe we shouldn’t live in accordance with nature.

If pain is bad, maybe it makes sense to delude ourselves and ignore hard truths. 

If Money is good, maybe it makes sense to take on a delusional level of self-confidence, if there is a slight chance it yields a better result in business.

Stoic ethics depends on Stoic value theory. For the stoics, it never makes sense to delude ourselves to achieve more external success, or avoid pain, because then you are harming what matters, the self, for the benefit of what doesn’t matter, things external to you.

So if you want to behave like a Stoic with confidence, you need to understand what the Stoics thought about the good, the bad, and the indifferent.

In this series of lesson we will cover how the Stoics justify these radical positions.

And this is not just a theoretical exercise. When you better understand the logic behind the Stoics argument’s, you will be better able to put the exercises built on these arguments into practice and improve your life.

### **CT: The Good**

CD: Why all good things are up to us

Think of examples of what a non-stoic person would call good. Maybe a nice meal, or a movie. Maybe the outcome of a sports game. That was a “good result” they would say, after their team one. Or, if things have gone their way, they might tell their friends they have some “good news” to share. Or if luck went your way a couple times, you might say you are having a “good day”.

What do people mean by this? Most people, when they say good, just mean something preferable at the moment. Something that nets positive. Maybe there is a certain bar of preferability that has to be cleared. Getting 50 cents is fine, getting 5 dollars is good. 

The Stoics did not mean this when they said good, they had a very specific definition. It is crucial to get this definition right, or else the rest of their arguments won’t make sense.

To the Stoics, what is good is that which benefits and must benefit. And what is bad is that which harms and must harm.

One way to think of good under this definition, is that a good thing would always be worth selecting. There is never a situation where I would be worse off for choosing it, unless choosing between other good things.

As Diogenes Laeurtius defines it:

“Good in general is that from which some advantage comes, and more particularly what is either identical with or not distinct from benefit.**”**

Id: ???  
Source: Lives  
Location: 7.1.94

This is the key idea: that a good thing is identical to a beneficial thing. For the Stoics something good can never fail to bring a benefit.

And, if you’ve been thinking like a Stoic for a while, it should be clear that many things fail to pass this test of “always benefiting.” 

Sometimes a nice tasting meal is not beneficial if I am on a diet, or a tight budget. An entertaining movie is not worth watching, if I am late for an appointment. 

By this definition, virtue alone becomes the only good thing, because it is the only thing that always benefits us, no matter the circumstances. Virtue, understood as correct action, is the only thing that is never the wrong choice. It is always in our best interest to make the right choice. 

When hearing this argument, there are some natural questions.

First, why do the Stoics define the good in such a specific and limited way? That’s not how normal people talk about good things. Most people talk about a ‘good’ meal as something they enjoyed in the moment, not something that has to always be beneficial. 

The reason the Stoics define good this way is connected with their view of human psychology. They argue that humans have minds that necessarily pursue what they perceive as good.

As Epictetus points out:

“For who among us doesn't assume that the good is beneficial and desirable, and that we should seek and pursue it in every circumstance?”

Id: 636  
Source: Discourses  
Location: ???

So, we can use whatever language we want, good, great, amazing, but there is a category of thing we will always pursue if we judge it to exist. For some people it’s money, others it’s fame, for some it is family, pleasure or comfort. But we all think something is good, and pursue it any chance we can get. 

This is that thing the mind thinks always benefits us. It is that category of thing that the Stoics call ‘good’. 

When they say a good thing is that which always benefits, they argue they are describing the way the mind works. 

The second question you might ask, is that if the Stoics are right, and all humans pursue the good necessarily, why do we have so much disagreement and arguing about how to live, both between cultures and individuals?

This happens, the Stoics say, because humans disagree about what falls into this category of good. For one person it is money, another it is family, another pleasure, another religion. Epictetus continues to explain further:

“For who among us doesn't assume that the good is beneficial and desirable, and that we should seek and pursue it in every circumstance?... When does contradiction arise, then? It comes about when we apply our preconceptions to particular cases ... Jews, Syrians, Egyptians and Romans. They don’t dispute that what is holy should be preferred above everything else and in every case pursued; but they argue, for example, over whether it is holy or unholy to eat pork.”

Id: 636  
Source: Discourses  
Location: ???

So people disagree, not because some pursue the good and some pursue the evil, but because we can’t agree on what the good is. We apply this mental category of good to different things. 

The project of philosophy then becomes wanting the right kinds of things. Desiring as good what is good and seeing truthfully the value of things.  

Virtue is what is good because it can never harm us and must benefit us. The value of everything else depends on the situation, even life itself. That was why the Stoics thought sometimes it was better to die than live, if there were no longer any virtuous options available for you. 

As the rest of this course will explain, the Stoics are not asking us to ignore the positive aspects of our lives. We shouldn’t grumble through a summer day, or correct a friend who says they have some ‘good news’ to tell us. 

The key idea here is one of focus. Where should we focus our time and attention? All people want a good life, so we need to understand what good is. Stoics do not become bad in pursuit of professional success. They don’t betray their friends to become popular. They don’t steal from others to maximize their pleasures. 

The stoics avoid these mistakes, because they are based on a confused understanding about what a good life is. Instead the Stoic focuses on improving their character and developing their virtue. Sometimes that comes with external success, sometimes it doesn’t. That’s not always up to us. But what is up to us is being a good person, and so a good life is available to anyone. 

### **CT: The Bad**

CD: Why only vice can harm us

In the past lesson, we discussed why the Stoics think that virtue is the only good thing.

Good things always benefit us, and virtue is the only thing that always benefits us. There is no example of virtue harming us. 

When it comes to bad things, the Stoics will unsurprisingly make a similar argument. Bad things will always harm us, and vice is the only thing that must harm us in any context. 

Take anything we normally call bad and you can find a context where it benefits: 

Getting sick may be beneficial if it stops you from being conscripted into an unjust war. Being socially ostracized is good, if the people bullying you are bad people you’d rather not be friends with. Being broke can help you appreciate and understand things about the world that someone who is well-off and naive will miss.

This is the Stoic argument for why vice is the only bad.    

But, while virtue was a compelling option for the only good, it seems more certain that there are other objectively bad things in the world, and other ancient philosophers argued for this. Maybe a poor-tasting meal is too contextual to always be a bad thing, but what about something like pain or death? Can’t something like pain necessarily harm us?

But the Stoics are not persuaded by these arguments. One reason the Stoics reject anything outside vice as necessarily harmful comes down to an argument around our identity. 

For the Stoics, our identity, who we fundamentally are, lies in the part of our mind that deliberates on impressions. Epictetus calls this the prohairesis, earlier Stoics focus on the broader part of our minds they call the hegemonikon.

Technical terms aside, all Stoics agree that we are not our reputations, our possessions, or even our body. We are our ability to make choices, an ability that is unrestricted and free.

This is why Epictetus says, when people complain about things external to them, that:

“Disease is an impediment to the body, but not to the will, unless the will itself chooses. Lameness is an impediment to the leg, but not to the will. And add this reflection on the occasion of everything that happens; for you will find it an impediment to something else, but not to yourself.”

Id: 244  
Source: Discourses  
Location: ???

An injured leg is not a harm to us because we are not our leg. It is a harm to the body but we are not the body, we are our minds. 

Because we are thinking things, our virtue, or excellence, is a rational kind of excellence. 

We are great people when we become great at thinking. Diogenes Laertius says, when describing the philosophy of Zeno, the Founder of Stoicism, that:

“Another particular definition of good which they give is "the natural perfection of a rational being qua rational."

Id: ???  
Source: Lives  
Location: 7.1.94

Remember that phrase. The good is the natural perfection of a rational being qua their rationality. Qua is latin for ‘in terms of’ or ‘in the manner of’ so Diogenes is saying the good is the natural perfection of a rational being in terms of their rationality.

Because we are thinking things, good thinking makes us excellent. Likewise bad thinking alone harms us.

Pain sits ‘outside’ ourselves, in the body. It only harms us if we allow it to distort or control our minds, for example when we judge pain to be a bad thing or allow fear of pain to drive us to poor decisions.

This explains why pain, illness, and death are not always bad. The athlete training hard is not harmed by the pain they feel while training, unless they view it as harmful. We are not harmed, even by death, unless we approach it with fear and distorted expectations. The Stoics famously were open to suicide in the right context. The door is always open, as Epictetus would say. 

Take any thing universally considered bad: Pain, illness, ruin, exile. All these things are all external to us. We can find benefit or harm in all of these situations. What matters to us is how we make use of them. 

It is hard to reframe our perception about what is bad. A certain skepticism about the good is often seen as wise, cautious and thoughtful. But those who dismiss the bad in the world or in other peoples’ lives can be seen as naive, or even callous and cruel. 

There is a passage in Epictetus’ Discourses that I have seen harshly criticized by non-Stoics. Some have even said it is the passage that has turned them off Stoicism as a philosophy.  Epictetus tells his students, when they tuck their children into bed, to remind themselves that their children are mortal and they will one day die.

But there is nothing cruel or callous in this exercise. Epictetus is right, all children will die one day. This is a fact of life. The Stoics asks how they want to respond to this fact. How would they want to handle the death or illness of a child, not as something necessarily bad or evil in the world, but a fact that applies to all mortal things.

These external things do not harm us on their own. They do not make us worse without our participation. And so they are not evil on their own, but how we deal with them makes it so. 

Marcus Aurelius captures this ideally beautifully when writes, in Book 4 of the mediations:

“That which does not make a man worse than he was, also does not make his life worse, nor does it harm him either from without or from within.”  
Id: 523  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 4.8

Externals cannot make us worse. Only Vice, or poor character can. This then is the only bad thing. Tyrants and luck can harm our possessions, our body or our reputation. But only vice can harm us.

### **CT: Indifferents**

CD: Navigating all the parts of life

So far in this series, we have focused on what kinds of things the Stoics think are good and bad. Good and bad is thinking and acting well or poorly, understood as virtue and vice. 

But what about all the other things in life we spend all our life pursuing? Friendship, money, pleasure, pain, fame, fashion, success? How do the Stoics make sense of all of these things?

The Stoics call these things ‘indifferents’. 

“Indifferents” is a bit of a clunky translation in english, but the easiest way to think about it is as things that don’t make a difference.

A difference to what? Well, they don’t make a difference, on their own, when judging a life to be happy or unhappy.

This seems a strange thing to say, but it should be clear what the Stoics had in mind when we remember their arguments around good and bad. If good and bad are internal states of character, then external things don’t make a difference.  

Put differently, imagine there are two people. One is rich and popular and powerful, has a nice car and beautiful hair, and the other  is broke, unsuccessful, has a broken down car and is balding.

Now if I asked you who is a better person, or has more good in their life, or is happier, what would you say?

The Stoics say that they don’t know. Epictetus would say that we haven’t been given any information about them, about who they really are. 

We’ve only been told a lot about things external to them. How can anyone say who is happier, or a better person, or has a better life, when we don’t know anything about them?

Which of them is more cowardly, kind, just or wise? How do they resist temptation, or face pain? How do they treat the people around them?

This is what the Stoic needs to know to say who is happier or better. 

That is the crucial stoic argument about indifferents. They don’t make a difference for happiness. Any number of them does not tell us who is happy or unhappy, or a good person or bad person.

As Seneca puts it:

“We mean by… the “indifferent” quality that which can fall to the lot of the bad no less than to the good – such things as money, beauty, or high social position.”

Id: 1521  
Source: Letters  
Location: ???

Now the Stoics did not think things were absolutely indifferent. They didn't think we had no means to differentiate between a paper cut and losing a hand, or scratching our car and winning the lottery.

As Seneca continues:

“Furthermore, there are vast distinctions among these qualities which we call “indifferent.” For example, death is not so indifferent as the question whether your hair should be worn evenly or unevenly.”

Id: 1417  
Source: Letters  
Location: ???

Just like death is not the same thing as how we style our hair, not all indifferents are equal.

The Stoics divided between “preferred” and “dispreferred” indifferents.

Preferred indifferents include: Wealth, pleasure, status, health, life.

Dispreferred indifferents include the opposite of these: Poverty, pain, poor social standing, sickness, death.

In the simplest terms, preferred indifferents are those we should tend to select, as long as there are no substantial reasons not to. So, all things being equal, we should choose wealth over poverty and pleasure over pain.

Consider the two people we discussed earlier, one successful and the other not, The Stoics would have no problems saying the successful person’s life is preferable, if that is all the information we have. We should after all aim to be successful, as long as it doesn’t make us vicious. 

But this person’s life is not good, just because they are wealthy and have a nice car. We can’t say that second part without more information. The less successful person’s life might still not just be better, but wonderful and good, depending on their character. 

One more Stoic example can help drive this concept home.

It is normal to pursue wealth and status. But to gain wealth and status by becoming a cruel mean person is never worth it.

It is normal to avoid physical pain, and it would be strange to not take care of our bodies or let ourselves become unnecessarily sick and ill. But just because we are ill, or have problems with our bodies, does not mean we have any less access to happiness, a good life, or being a great person.

Indifferents are the things external to us. They matter and some are preferred over others. but they alone can never make a life good or bad. 

It is not easy to detach from externals, and place a higher value on our character, but that is the Stoic task.

Let’s end with a line from Epictetus that really hammers this home.

If you want to know just how little concerned you are about things good and bad, and how serious about things indifferent, compare your attitude to going blind with your attitude about being mentally in the dark. You will realize, I think, how inappropriate your values really are.  
Id:  633  
Source: Discourses  
Location: ???

### **CT: Putting it into practice**

CD: How to live like a stoic day to day

So if we accept the Stoic argument that the categories of good and bad belong only to virtue and vice, what does this all mean for us? How does someone live with this knowledge?

Do we reject indifferents? Become monks? Isolate ourselves from the world? Sink into our minds? Sell all our possessions?

Not at all. There are are three reasons we should still live full lives, despite the good and the bad being internal to us.

First, indifferents are still preferred or dispreferred. If we can do something that benefits our bodies or our community, at no cost to our character, then we should always do this.

If virtue and vice can be found in any kind of life, then we should pursue it in the life that has the most preferred things possible. To paraphrase Epictetus, if you decide to be rich, that is fine as long as you do so with your character intact. 

The Stoics do not think the pursuit of virtue requires discarding indifferents not having or a normal life. 

Second, the Stoics argue that we have all kinds of roles to play. A common metaphor they use is to think of ourselves like actors in a play and in that play we have many different roles: Sibling, child, parent, mentor, student, boss, coworker, friend. We have been born into some of these relationships, and some roles we’ve chosen for ourselves. 

The Stoic goal is to understand these roles, and perform them well. Not to remove ourselves from the play all together. If a different character has to exit the stage, or performs their lines poorly, or fights against the director, this is no harm to you. It is external to you. It doesn’t change the fact that you have a role to play. 

For the ancient stoics, this role was decided by god. We had a fate to fulfill. But even a secular Stoic can take a lesson from this: A good life is one where our actions line up with the roles we choose for ourselves, and the natural connections we have with others. 

This connects to the third reason the Stoics thought we should still engage with the world of indifferents, despite good and evil not being found there. 

The stoics argue that we need indifferents, because we show virtue and vice by how we make use of indifferents. We show our character by how we treat others and navigate our community, job, and responsibilities.

As Epictetus says:

“The essence of good and evil consists in the condition of our character. And externals are the means by which our character finds its particular good and evil.”

Id: 648  
Source: Discourses  
Location: ???

The stoics don’t think we should retreat into ourselves. Or act like nothing matters. How we act both builds and demonstrates our character.

This can seem, at worse, like the Stoics are trying to have their cake and eat it too. They want to shield us from the ups and downs of life, but don’t want to admit how strange a life that only values our internal worlds would be. At worst, some may argue, like the philosopher Bertrand Russell, that Stoicism is built for hard times and difficult lives, but it is a terrible philosophy otherwise.

But the Stoics have a good reply to this. Consider the athlete. We praise the athlete for how they make use of the ball, for how well they score, but we don’t think a ball going into a net is a good thing on its own. 

We just set up a situation for the ball player to display their excellence.

Athletes care about the game, because the game allows them an opportunity to be at their best.

Good people are like good ball players. They know how to use the world around them gracefully to display their excellence. As Epictetus says:

“This is just what you will see those doing who play at ball skillfully. No one cares about the ball as being good or bad, but about throwing and catching it. … Socrates then knew how to play at ball”  
Id: 659  
Source: Discourses  
Location: ???

So moving forward, strive to be like the ball player.

Take the game seriously. Be earnest, genuine and try to win. But if the game doesn’t go your way, remember that it was a game. That the point of the game wasn’t to win, but to play well.

And we always have the freedom to play well. In that freedom, we can find our happiness.
