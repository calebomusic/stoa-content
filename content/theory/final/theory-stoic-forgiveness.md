### **Content: Stoic Forgiveness**

PT: How to Forgive  
PS: Stoic tools for releasing anger and finding peace

PD: Listen to this course to understand how the Stoics let go of their anger at others and the world.

### **CT: Introduction**

CD: Start here

In my Stoic journey, I have found it a lot easier to accept the things outside of my control, when they clearly aren’t any single person’s fault: bad weather, an illness or injury, a cancelled flight, a flat tire.

Stoicism has given me the tools to approach these things as challenges to be navigated. My task to rise up to. My olympic games to conquer and leave victorious.

But something feels different when it’s a person that has harmed you. This feels like a choice they made, something that is up to them, and so something that deserves anger.

You can’t have righteous anger against a storm, but you can feel very bitter against someone who has betrayed your trust, or lied to you. In the second case, there is someone to be punished.

In Stoicism, anger is never appropriate. So, instead of being angry, you must forgive what you weren’t able to accept. In this course, we will cover Stoic forgiveness, how to stop being angry with people that have harmed you, and why you shouldn’t be angry anyway. 

Forgiveness and acceptance are related but different concepts.

Forgiveness comes from old english, and it means to give something. When you forgive, you give a pardon. You relinquish your anger. 

Acceptance comes from latin, and it means to take something willingly. When you accept something, you take in the world as it is, without expecting it to be different. 

Giving and taking. Forgiveness and acceptance.

The Stoics teach acceptance of all things, seen in the concept of amor fati. But we aren’t sages, we are progressors, people trying to put Stoicism into action. We still get angry, frustrated, and feel hurt. And when we don’t accept something, when we are angry with it, then we must forgive.

In this course, we will focus on forgiveness. What do the Stoics teach us about how we can give a pardon to someone who has harmed us or done something that we can’t accept.

This to me, is one of the most complicated things to do, but also one of the most important steps to a Stoic life. At the start of this lesson it may be helpful to have a certain person or situation in mind. Something that you want to feel differently about. 

This could be a family member, or a partner, someone from school or work. Since forgiveness is something you give, it’s an action. It needs to be a decision directed towards a certain person.   
As you work through this course, think about who the action is for. 

This should be more than intellectual exercise, but a call to forgive. 

### **CT: They are confused**

CD: Don’t expect people to be different

Nobody is the villain in their own story. The Stoics knew that everybody wants to live a good life, and pursue what they take, subjectively, to be the best way to live.

The thief has someone to feed. Your cruel boss is just trying to advance their own career. Your parents thought they did the best they could with what they had. Your ex doesn’t see themselves as a liar, but someone who was stuck in a difficult situation. 

Nobody sees themselves as the villain, because no one ever does what they think is the wrong thing in the moment. 

However, the Stoics are not relativists. There are different kinds of ways to live a good life but we don’t get to make our own rules.

People that are vicious, that desire things outside their control, and that lie, cheat, and steal to get them, do not have good lives. They might think they do, but they are incorrect.

So, if there are better and worse ways to live, why do people act poorly? Well, because they are mistaken. The partner who cheats on you thought that was the best thing to do. The bully who picked on you also thought this would be the best way to live well.

It will take some empathy to see things from the bullies perspective, but everyone has their own working model of the best way to navigate the world. 

By empathy  I mean when we understand someone’s value system so that we can understand why they acted the way they did. 

It doesn’t mean we agree with that value system, or non-judgmentally or accept it as right. It is that we understand it. 

The bully picked on us because they wanted to feel strong, powerful, and secure by positioning themselves above us in the social hierarchy. We don’t need to accept that as a good way to live, we can still call that vice, but we should try to understand it.

Stoic empathy is recognizing that we all want to live well, but some of us choose wrong. We make mistakes. So the first Stoic reason to forgive others is because they are confused.

Epictetus was lecturing his students, young men who were arguing in favor of harsh punishment for robbers and thieves. They had little sympathy, and a righteous anger. Epictetus said this about the criminal:

“They have gone astray in matters of good and evil. Ought we, then, to be angry with them or to pity them? Do but show them their error, and you will see how they will amend their faults; but, if they do not see it, they have nothing higher than their personal opinion to rely on.”   
Sentence Ids: 11419-11422  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.18.3-4

We can’t expect, Epictetus points out, for people to be anything different than themselves. We all rely on our personal opinion to make sense of the world. And some of us are greatly mistaken. This deserves pity more than anger.

Epictetus then shares a personal example with his students:

“Now I had an iron lamp burning before my household gods, and hearing a noise at the window the other day, I ran downstairs to find that my lamp had been stolen. I thought to myself that the man who took it had been moved by a perfectly intelligible feeling. What of it?”   
Sentence Ids: 11443-11445  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.18.15

This is Stoic forgiveness and empathy in action. 

Epictetus sees the thief as a confused individual. They were struck by an impression, just like we all are. But they are not a philosopher, so they made poor use of it. The thief supposed that having the lamp is a good thing and that it would be best to steal the expensive lamp, and get the possession that might make them happier.

This argument follows from the Stoic idea that virtue is knowledge and vice is ignorance. Bad people are confused. This is a fundamental Stoic idea. Stoicism asks us to forgive ignorance, because it is unavoidable and necessary. There will always be confused people, and confused people will do bad things. People will only ever act based on their own conception of the good.

It doesn’t mean you shouldn’t have boundaries, or should trust them again. But we can’t be angry with the ignorant. To be angry is to expect someone to be different than they are. We expect them to act contrary to how everyone has always acted: Namely, in line with their own conception of the good. 

You might ask, shouldn’t they know better? But nobody wishes to be confused, the Stoics argue. If they had understood why they were wrong they would have made the change. It just is not clear to them yet. So, as Epictetus says, we should pity them instead.

Try applying this idea to someone you are angry with in your own life. Don’t ask how they harmed you, but what they were thinking at the time. Why did this seem like the right for them to do?

If you can understand that, then you can understand their confusion and mistakes. And to see that confusion will lead to pity, not anger.

### **CT: Why we get angry**

CD: Understand why struggle with anger

Something the Stoics point out is that often our anger comes from our own ignorance. We are angry at people because we desire the things they have taken from us. In other words, we are angry because we have something in common.

Epictetus told a story where someone walking by his house noticed his iron lamp and decided to steal it. We hate the thief for wanting the lamp, but if we are angry at a stolen lamp, it’s because we also love owning it\! We desire the lamp just as much as the thief. 

As Marcus Aurelius said:

"Whenever somebody wrongs you, ask yourself at once, ‘What conception of good and evil led him to commit such a wrong?’ And when you have seen that, you will pity him, and feel neither surprise nor anger. For you yourself still hold the same opinion about what is good as he does, or another not unlike it; and you are thus obliged to forgive him. Or if you no longer suppose that things of that kind are good or bad in themselves, you will find it easier to show kindness to one who is still in the dark."   
Sentence Ids: 1049-1053  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 7.26

Marcus’ argument is that people can only really harm things external to you, like your reputation, your possessions or your career. When someone harms something external, like your reputation, you either feel harmed or don’t. 

This is then the critical fork in the road. When someone damages your reputation to improve their own, do you feel harmed?

If you feel harmed it is because you value your reputation, just like they do. You and your enemy share the same kind of ignorance, and that realization should make it easier to empathize and forgive them.

If you don’t feel harmed, it is because you no longer value externals like reputation, only character. So then you should be able to show kindness to your enemy, who is suffering from bad character but has not harmed your own. 

Let’s use an example. Imagine you and someone at work are both vying for the same position, both hunting for the same single promotion. But, as a strategic choice to increase their odds, they lie about you behind your back to your coworkers. The lie works, people think less of you, and your rival gets the job over you. 

Marcus then presents his fork. If you feel angry, it’s because you also really wanted this job. And so you should be able to empathize with and forgive the kind of person who wants the job a bit too much and lies for it.

Or, if you are a Stoic about losing out on the position, then you should feel something more akin to pity at your coworker’s lies. You should see them for what they are, someone who thinks their career is more important than their character, and so harmed themselves to try and get a job.

It is a great argument, and one Epictetus uses as well:

“Know that a thief and an adulterer have no place among the things that are properly your own, but only among the things that belong to others, and which are not in your power. If you give up these things, and look upon them as nothing, with whom will you still be angry? But as long as you admire them, be angry with yourself rather than with others.”   
Sentence Ids: 11435-11436  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.18.12

As Epictetus said, the thief can’t steal what is yours, your character. They only have the capacity to harm, take, or disrupt the things that are not up to you. If you do not desire these things, you won’t have enemies. But if you do desire these things, you should be angry with yourself, for failing to be a Stoic.

Apply this argument to the example in your own life. What did the person you are angry with want? Have you ever wanted these things? Maybe not enough to lie, cheat, or steal, but still, you know what it is like to want something you think is required to be happy, but not have it. 

If you have done the same then you can forgive and show empathy.

If you’ve never wanted these things, for example if you’ve never understood people’s obsession with fame, popularity, cheating, drugs, or money, then you should be able to, from an objective position, see them for what they are. People that are confused about the good, and deserve pity.

### **CT: They are already punished**

CD: Know that everyone gets what they deserve

So far, we’ve considered anger from personal injustice. The kind you feel when you think “I hate this person for hurting me. They deserve to be punished.” 

And, we’ve looked at two Stoic reasons to forgive: First, because the person who harmed you is confused about how to live well. Second, because you yourself are only angry because you make the same mistakes as them, and desire the same things outside your control. 

But sometimes, our anger can extend beyond people. Sometimes, Epictetus noticed, people can be angry at the world itself.

The Stoics believed that nature was inherently good and that the universe was providential. This is part of the reason why we should strive to live in accordance with nature. We want to play our part well, our proper role, in the larger play because the story is a good one. 

However, despite the Stoic’s claim that the world is providential, it is also one obviously full of major injustices that extend beyond your personal experiences. For example corrupt people in positions of power, sick or poor children that rich people don’t wish to save, terrible people who have gone free or received no punishment for their crimes.

When seeing these things, we might lose our belief in Stoic providence. Even if you never believed the world was ordered or inherently good, but only neutral, you might start to believe the world is actually inherently bad. This is the kind of anger you feel when you start thinking things like:

What kind of world lets people get away with these things unpunished? What kind of society lets people like this become successful, and face no consequences?

In these cases, we are experiencing a kind of cosmic injustice. It seems like the universe can’t be just if people like that, bad people, face no consequences and have success.

The important difference here is that we become angry and cynical at the world itself, not just the individual. It is the world we have to forgive, for not being, in our view, a just one where the hero’s are rewarded and the villains are punished. 

This kind of anger can turn you bitter and jaded. Epictetus offers a simple argument against this cynicism. Unjust people are already punished. As he says in a fragment: 

”The man who blames providence because the wicked are not punished, but are strong and rich, is doing much the same as if, when \[this person\] had lost their eyes, he said that they had not been punished because their nails were sound. I for my part say that there is much more difference between virtue and property than there is between eyes and nails.”   
Quote\_ID:    
Translation: Hard, 1995  
Source: Fragments  
Location: 13

His point is that, if you are a Stoic, the worst thing that can happen to you is to have bad character. So, when someone achieves success, in business, popularity, or politics, at the expense of their character, they have been punished. A bad thing has happened to them.

They are like someone who has lost their eyes, in order to have pretty nails. This argument really tests how much you have internalized Stoicism. 

It is not a particularly complex idea, but it requires that you really believe in the Stoic idea that happiness is virtue, and that success is preferred but ultimately indifferent to happiness.

One way to help digest this is to imagine the person who is successful but terrible. Now ask yourself, would you want to be them? Would you willingly trade places? And if the answer is no, then really what have they won?

They are like the tyrant from Plato’s republic. They are controlled by their desires and ignorance. Yes, they have fallen into external success, but that is not a trade any Stoic would make. 

If you don’t find Epictetus’ argument convincing, you might respond as follows: No I wouldn’t trade places with them, but they should still be punished. The world is not just, one might argue, unless the bad also suffer, or at least fall from their success.

Epictetus had the same conversation with his students. They argued that robbers and thieves should be put to death. They should be punished further for their crimes.

Epictetus responded:

“What you are saying…comes to the same as saying, ‘Ought not this blind, or that deaf, man to be put to death?’ For, if the greatest harm a man can suffer is to be deprived of the most valuable things, and the most valuable thing in each man is a right moral choice, when any one is deprived just this thing, how can one still be angry with him?”  
Sentence Ids: 11425-11426  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 1.18.7-9

When the Stoic looks at the bad person, they already think the worst possible thing has happened to them. How can you be angry, Epictetus asks, with the person who has already received the harshest possible punishment?

Maybe that person still deserves punishment. The Stoics didn’t think we should abolish prisons or punitive measures. But the punishment shouldn’t be done to satisfy our anger at an unjust world. The world is already a just place where good people are rewarded and bad people are punished. 

In conclusion, all three arguments in this course, ultimately, ask for empathy. The key to Stoic forgiveness is to shift our perspective from ourselves, to the other person. 

Anger is the view that someone deserves punishment because they have wrongly harmed us, but it’s hard to keep that view when you actually empathize. When you empathize, you see someone acting in the way they must, because of their current ignorance. You see them as a part of nature. Consider how this applies to your own situation, or those you are angry with in your own life.

First, anyone who does wrong is confused. It does not seem that someone confused, acting on their own view of the world, deserves to be punished out of anger. 

Second, many times, those who do wrong are confused about the same things we are. A thief steals Epictetus’ lamp, and Epictetus is sad to lose the lamp, for the same reason: They both value it in a non-Stoic way.

So consider, when you feel righteous anger, if that person is like you, but just a bit more confused or a bit less Stoic. It’s hard to be angry with people we can see ourselves in.

Third, even if the person deserves to be punished, a Stoic thinks there is nothing worse that can be done to them. They have a bad character. They have to live with that bad character. That should be sufficient for you.

It’s not your job to go in and try to mess with their external success or their reputation..

If you are really Stoic, you already believe they have been punished. They have been deprived of a truly happy life.

And that is the root of Stoic forgiveness. Pity at those who have harmed their own character.
