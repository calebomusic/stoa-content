# **Diogenes**

**Opening Hook**

Epictetus, the gruff slave turned Stoic teacher, was not known for giving respect lightly. He is the most intense of the Roman Stoics, and berates his students when they fail to live up to the lessons standards of Stoicism.

Epictetus’ role models, the people he looked up to for inspiration, were few and far between.

But when you read Epictetus’ Discourses, the two most referenced examples of excellence are actually lived before the founding of Stoicism. The first is Socrates, teacher of Plato and Aristotle. The second most referenced philosopher is Diogenes the Cynic. 

Once, Diogenes was walking through his city in broad daylight with his lamp lit. When people asked him what he was doing, he said he was searching for an honest man. When one young man blushed, Diogenes responded that this was the complexion of virtue, because at least this man recognized his dishonesty and felt some embarrassment. 

Diogenes is famous for stories like these. He is almost a performance artist, shocking those around him out of philosophical complacency. Epictetus modeled his high standards off Diogenes.

And in this lesson we will learn from this famous cynic who inspired him and many other Stoics. 

**Biography:**

Diogenes was born between 412 and 404 BC in Sinope, a Greek Colony on the northern coast of modern day Turkey. 

At a young age he was captured by pirates and sold into slavery. 

At some point he gained his freedom and reached Athens. As a free man he learnt philosophy from Antisthenes, a student of Socrates who was present at Socrates’ execution in Athens. He is reported to have said that he earned his real freedom from Antisthenes, in the form of an education, and that after learning philosophy he could never be enslaved again.

Diogenes inherited two parts of Socrates’ legacy from Antisthenes. Like Socrates, he became a philosophical gadfly, irritating those around him by forcing them to think and question their assumptions. And second, maybe even more than Socrates, Diogenes did not care about reputation or the opinions of others. He was after the truth, at the expense of how he looked.

Diogenes quickly gained a reputation as the kind of person who would not hold his opinion back. He often fought with Plato, who was already a famous philosopher at the time. 

One time Diogenes went to one of Plato’s parties and trampled his carpet with dirty shoes, saying he was trampling the pride of Plato.

Plato responded “How much pride you show, Diogenes, by seeming not to be proud”.

These two great thinkers would bicker, Diogenes accusing Plato of being conceited and moving away from the humble roots of philosophy, and Plato accusing him of being just as proud as anyone else, but also a hypocrite about it. 

As a philosopher, Diogenes dedicated his life to pulling people out of complacency and getting them to question their assumptions. He did shocking things in public, like urinate, defecate and even masturbate. He would sleep in the streets of Athens in a barrel, point at things with his middle finger, and accost Athenians.

This earned him a reputation for being like a dog, being dirty and sleeping outside. In fact, the word Cynic is derived from the Greek word for dog.

Now all of this might sound shocking, and not particularly Stoic, for Stoicism today would not ask us to urinate in public.

But Stoicism is a direct intellectual descendent of Cynicism 

Diogenes the Cynics main pupil was Crates of Thebes. And Crates of Thebes was the philosophical teacher of Zeno of Citium when he first arrived in Athens. 

Stoicism as a tradition, is just two steps removed from Diogenes.

With that in mind, let’s explore what anyone, Stoics included, can learn from this controversial thinker.

**Key Lessons:**

The three key lessons we can learn from the life of Diogenes are:

1. First, We should be skeptical towards authority and the status quo  
2. Second, We are more self-sufficient than we think  
3. And Third, Freedom comes from self-knowledge

**Key Lesson 1: We should be skeptical towards authority and the status quo**

The first lesson from the life of Diogenes is a radical one. Diogenes teaches us to be skeptical towards authority when we want to pursue knowledge.

There is no shortage of examples of Diogenes push back against social norms, from living in a barrel to defecating in public. He was like a modern punk rocker, pushing back against social norms just to explore the consequences; only showing respect where it is earned, not demanded. 

But he also extended this to intellectual pursuits. 

Once, Plato at his Academy was working with his other thinkers to provide a succinct definition of what a human is. They came up with the definition of “A bipedal, hairless animal”. 

Diogenes, hearing this, stripped a chicken of its feather, carried it into the Academy and proclaimed “Behold, a man\!”. Since a chicken walks on two legs and has no hair. 

How many of us, in a class with a famous professor, would be unwilling to question something they said, even when it seems blatantly wrong to us?

How many of us are afraid to say what we think is true, because we fear the social consequences?

Diogenes’ first lesson is that sometimes those in positions of authority and power get it wrong, and it is our job to pursue the truth regardless of what they say, or what social norms are. 

This is not necessarily an anti-authority argument. 

To be anti-authority is to be reactive. If someone tells you to do something, you do the opposite. That’s anti-authority.

Diogenes is not strictly anti-authority (although obviously he seems to enjoy bugging people). He is pursuing truth, just through a different means.

He knows that there is a difference between earned authority or genuine expertise, and someone's reputation. 

There is a difference between the right decision, and just what is normal.

Is there anything wrong with sleeping outside in the street, or is it just not something we would feel judged for doing? Diogenes teaches us to push back on these assumptions.

**Key Lesson 2: We are more self-sufficient than we think**

The second lesson from the life of Diogenes, is that we are more self-sufficient and resilient than we think.

Diogenes practiced minimalism in all things. He had few possessions, slept outside, and ate a plain diet.

He saw minimalism as a path to freedom. If we desire fancy things, we can’t be happy unless we have them. Then we need a job to buy these things, we start worrying about how we come off to other people, and all of a sudden we are caught in a trap of living a very specific kind of life just so we don’t lose these possessions we desire. 

He was always pursuing ways to limit his reliance on external possessions. In one story, Diogenes walks to a river with his one possession, a small cup for drinking water. As he is filling the cup for a drink, he sees a small boy drinking from the same stream, except the boy is drinking from his hands, putting them together to form a small cup.

Diogenes is outraged. He exclaims “A child has beat me in the plainness of living\!”.

Something great about this story is that it shows that Diogenes above learning from other people. He was happy to learn from a child, when there was something worth learning.

Diogenes was so renowned for  his minimalism that he earned the reputation for living like a dog: sleeping in the streets, eating the scraps of others, bathing rarely. And this reputation stuck. The term cynic comes from the Greek word for Dog. 

To be a cynic, in many ways, is to live like a dog. Whether that sounds like a compliment to you or not will reveal a lot about whether you have natural cynic tendencies or not. 

In all things Diogenes was extreme, but his point remains. Even if we don’t sleep in a barrel outside, we don’t need most of the things we have. We don’t need a fancy bed, or exotic food. This is of course not true for all humans, some people do need more than they have for a basic living. But if you are listening to this on an Iphone or computer, you probably have access to more exotic food and certainly better technology than anyone in Ancient Greece.

You don’t need these things to be happy, so don’t be so anxious about losing them. That is the second lesson from Diogenes life. 

**Key Lesson 3:** **Freedom comes from self-knowledge**

The third key lesson from Diogenes is that freedom comes from self-knowledge.

If I had to describe the ultimate goal of cynicism, I would say that it is freedom achieved through understanding what we really are. 

We are rational, social animals on this planet for a short period of time. We need, and should desire, very little to make good use of that time. When we lose track of that and when we lose sight of what we really are, this is when we feel anxious and desire things outside our control. 

This is where Cynicism connects with Stoicism most clearly, we achieve freedom through self-knowledge and limited desire.

Diogenes was himself a former slave, so he knew a thing or two about freedom. He was captured by pirates and sold into slavery. And yet he always kept a perspective on his own freedom. When being sold at a slavers market, he is reported to have pointed at a man and said “Sell me to this man, he looks like he needs a master”.

Diogenes point is that real freedom is about how you internally relate to others, not who owns who according to the law.

Other anecdotes from the life of Diogenes show this radical freedom in action.

Finally, when Alexander the great, the most powerful man in the world at the time, traveled to meet the famous Diogenes, he came upon Diogenes on a sunny day lying in the grass. As Alexander stood over him, he asked Diogenes if there was anything he could do for him. Diogenes responded that Alexander could “Get out of his sun”. 

After this encounter, Alexander is reported to have said “Had I not been Alexander, I should have liked to be Diogenes”. 

Imagine for a moment the freedom Diogenes had in this moments. He felt neither desire nor fear when thinking of death, politics, or power. He could be his authentic self in front of Alexander the great.

Diogenes could do this because he knew what he was, and knew what he needed to be happy. And as it turns out, it is not that much. Not much more than a dog. A place to sleep, something to eat, and a community to exist in.

Some might find living like a Diogenes humiliating or unnecessarily painful. I know that I am not selling my bed for a barrel any time soon. But Diogenes really knew himself. And with that self-knowledge, and limited desires came a degree of freedom we should all aspire to.

Epictetus, himself a former slave, had this to say in admiration about Diogenes:

“Diogenes was free. How came he by this? Not because he was of free parents (he was not), but because he was free himself, had cast away all the weakness that might give slavery a hold on him, and so no one could approach or lay hold on him to enslave him. Everything he had he was ready to let go, it was loosely attached to him. If you had laid hold on his property, he would have let it go rather than have followed you for it; if you seized his leg, he would have let that go; if his whole poor body, he would have let his whole body go.” (Disc. 4.1)

**Conclusion**

Looking back at the life of Diogenes, I’d like to provide a word of caution. 

As Epictetus writes in his Discourses, the life of a cynic is not for everyone. 

Cynicism is an extreme philosophy that involves rejecting social norms in pursuit of freedom. Epictetus described cynics as the spies on the front line of a war. They put themselves at risk to discover the true nature of good and evil; they experiment to uncover what humans can put up with and still flourish.

We should be grateful for them, but not everyone should be one. It is a calling for a special few.

Stoicism is a more social, gentler form of cynicism. It keeps some of the best parts of Diogenes' lessons but doesn’t ask us to live in a barrel (even if Stoicism argues we would be ok if we had to). 

Stoicism and cynicism agree that we should be skeptical of authority that is demanded but not earned. We should understand our own self-sufficiency, and capacity to live well in difficult circumstances. And we should strive to understand who we really are, and enjoy the freedom that comes with that.

We don’t need to be Diogenes to benefit from the lessons of his life.
