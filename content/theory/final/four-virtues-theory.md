PD: “Learn about the four virtues that guide every Stoic action. Each lesson covers a different virtue, what it means for you, and examples of how to put it into practice.”

### **CT: Introduction**

CD: An overview of the four virtues

The goal of Stoicism is to be virtuous. This is what sets it apart from other philosophies. 

Some people aim at pleasure, others fame and fortune, some want glory. But Stoics think a good life depends on virtue only, just be a great person and do the right thing.

The ancient Greek word for virtue is Arete, meaning excellence. In modern English, virtue can seem like a moral idea, but the Greeks thought of it as broader than that. Everything has its own individual excellence. A house could have Arete if it is well built, or a book if it is well-written.

So for a human, the Stoics say that a good life is to achieve human excellence, to achieve virtue.

But saying the goal of life is to be a great person can be vague. It’s kind of like saying the goal of basketball is to be the best at basketball. 

Sure, it’s good to clarify that the goal isn’t to become famous or impress your friends.

But once we agree on setting this internal goal, what does actually becoming good look like? What are the parts? The essential skills? What do you need to know how to do well?

To answer these questions, and flesh out the idea of virtue, the Stoics broke virtue into four parts, each representing a part of a good character: courage, discipline, justice, and wisdom.

As Seneca said:

“We have separated this perfect virtue into its several parts. The desires had to be reined in, fear to be suppressed, proper actions to be arranged, debts to be paid; we therefore included self-restraint, bravery, prudence, and justice – assigning to each quality its special function.”  
Id: 1535  
Source: Letters  
Location: 120.11

The virtues tell us how we should change to be happy and flourish. 

In Seneca’s words, Discipline is for mastering desire, courage is for suppressing fear, wisdom is for planning the right actions, and justice is what ensures we pay our debts.

These virtues were not the invention of the Stoics. They had been around in this form since the time of Plato. The Stoics were responding to an established philosophical tradition here, and putting their own spin on ideas everyone would have known and used. 

Another important idea is that, despite this division, the virtues were thought to be all connected. You can’t have one without the other.

You can’t really be courageous without also being just, for example. Sure, someone who robs and threatens people might lack fear concerning physical danger, or getting caught by the cops, but they aren’t courageous because they are not afraid of things they should be, namely vicious actions and harming others. 

Their lack of understanding that it is wrong to harm other people, stops them being just and that prevents them from showing courage, which is knowing what is or is not worth fearing. 

Since the virtues are all connected, they shouldn’t be thought of as distinct things. Rather, they are best used as different angles for self-improvement.

Think of a guitarist. Their craft can be broken down into multiple pieces. How fast they can play, how well they can write music, their stage presence, their ability to play accurately. They are not a great guitarist unless they have all of these. 

But just because excellent guitar playing involves many skills, this doesn’t mean you can’t focus your attention when training. The best way to build your skill as a musician is to divide and conquer. Focus on the specific area of yours that needs improvement.  Some people need to work on their scales, others need to practice reading music, some need to improve their sense of rhythm. These are all a part of playing music well, and improving one skill will compliment the other.

The Stoics think of the cultivation of our character the same way. It is a craft to develop a great soul. As Seneca said:

“Nature does not bestow virtue; it is an art to become good... Even in the best of men, before you refine them by instruction, there is but the stuff of virtue, not virtue itself.”  
Id: 1439  
Source: Letters  
Location: 90.45

Some people might have a well developed sense of justice, and want to stand up to bullies, but are also too cowardly to follow through. 

Some people may have self-control but lack the wisdom to recognize that now would be a good time to use the discipline we have and hold back. 

To truly be happy, you need to bring everything together. The virtues complement each other. Refined by instruction they become excellence manifested in action, rather than the inert potential for excellence. 

As a group they should be used as ways of thinking about how you can be happy and flourish. 

For the Stoics, there was no happiness without virtue. No one lives a good life if they have money, fame, an external success, but lack the distinct aspects of human excellence \- a good character.

Just like a musician should be judged on their music, not the records they sell, the Stoics thought we should judge people on how they act, not external things. 

This series will provide an overview of each of the four Stoic virtues, so that by the end you will understand what the Stoics meant by each, what they look like in practice, and some strategies for cultivating them. 

Of all the things we can gain in life, virtue is the most important. It is the only thing we can take with us through any difficulty. As the Stoic teacher Musonius Rufus said:

“The person in exile is not prevented from having courage, justice, self-control, wisdom, or any other virtue, just because he is in exile.”

### **CT: Courage**

CD: Know what is worth fearing

Imagine you are at work. Your boss is insulting a coworker behind their back. It has crossed over into bullying. Your coworkers know they can gain some favor by joining in. It will make them look good, maybe increase their chances of doing well in the next promotion round, or at least avoid being the target of future bullying.

You want to say something. The situation seems unjust and cruel. But you know that if you do, you will likely be the target of their ridicule. Or, as embarrassing as it is, you are worried you may not get the raise you want. 

You act anyway. You speak up. 

This is an example of Stoic courage. But it is a type of courage that we sometimes downplay today.

When thinking of Courage, it is easy to think of our responses to simple basic stimuli. We think of how we would respond to the monster in the horror movie, the rollercoaster, bungee Jumping.

But the Stoics think there is just as much, if not more courage in how we handle ourselves in the day to day.

This is because the Stoics define courage as an application of knowledge. Specifically the knowledge of what is and is not worth fearing.

So if we return to our example, you had two choices. First, you could team up with the bully, do something cruel but secure your reputation, or criticize the bullies, do something kind but damage your reputation.

Courage is not just about jumping out of a plane or running into a burning building. Nor is it about being numb to fear. It is understanding that being cruel is the scarier outcome than being unpopular.

The courageous person knows that it is not worth fearing being disliked by cruel people you don’t respect – and more importantly they see that what they should really be afraid of is acting poorly. Courage is knowledge of what to fear, put into practice.

Consider a more dramatic ancient example told by Epictetus.

When Helvidius Priscus, a roman senator and Stoic, was commanded by Emperor Vespasian to not go to the senate, he replied:

“'It is in your power not to allow me to be a member of the senate, but so long as I am, I must go.' 'Well, go in, but say nothing,' the Emperor responded.

Helvidius shot back: 'Do not ask my opinion and I will be silent.'

'I must ask your opinion.'

'And I must say what I think is right.'

'But if you do, I shall put you to death.'

Helvidius concluded: 'When did I tell you that I am immortal? You will do your part, and I will do mine: It is your part to kill; it is mine to die, but not in fear.'”

Helvidius’ courage may seem dramatic but it is grounded in a simple insight. It was simply the secure knowledge that it is scarier to betray your values than to die an honest senator who fulfilled their role to the people.  
This is what understated, Stoic courage looks like in practice.

So how do we build Courage?

The simplest way to improve our understanding of what is worth fearing is by visualization of bad things, also called premeditatio malorum.

As Seneca says:

“Everyone approaches courageously a danger which he has prepared himself to meet long before, and withstands even hardships if he has previously practised how to meet them. But, contrariwise, the unprepared are panic-stricken even at the most trifling things. We must see to it that nothing shall come upon us unforeseen.”  
Id: 1492  
Source: Letters   
Location: 107.4

In this exercise we imagine something we would prefer to avoid. Maybe this is you failing a test, flubbing an important presentation or facing a bully.

At it’s most extreme, Epictetus encourages students to remind themselves, when they tuck their children into bed at night, that these children are mortal just like them, and they will die one day. 

Often people treat this exercise like it is about becoming cynical, or numbing ourselves to the bad. They think Stoics want us to fully detach from caring about the outside world.

But this exercise is really about clarifying values. We develop courage by solidifying our knowledge. 

So we imagine what would become of us if these bad things happened. What would happen if we were faced with a bully and stood up to them.

Well, likely we’d face some sort of short term punishments. Either there would be physical consequences or social ones. Likely this would be a very bad couple days. But we’d also keep our character, maybe make some new friends who were inspired by our bravery, or, if it is in our control, leave a social or work environment that is not a good for us.

This situation would be difficult but not insurmountable. Humans have done things like this before. 

Through this visualization we should realize that in most cases we are ok. We still possess the tools for a good life. We come to understand that while failing a class or facing a bully seems worthy of fear, it is not such a bad thing at all and that should it come, we are prepared to face it. 

That is courage.

### **CT: Discipline**

CD: Have self-control

The famous Roman writer Publius Syrus once said:

“Would you have a great empire? Rule over yourself.”   
Id: 1664  
Source: Publius Syrus  
Location: 

Imagine you set a goal for yourself, one that requires sacrifice. You want to become a great writer, and so you set aside time each day to write. But when you look at the blank page, you feel the urge to do anything else, to go outside, to see friends, to watch tv. An onslaught of options present themselves that in the moment you’d rather do. But you know that over the long term you want to be a writer, achieve your goal, and so you sit down and write. 

This is the virtue of discipline, mastery of your own impulses and desires. It is the ability to engage in a healthy way with pleasure, pain, and the many opportunities that arise as you try to achieve your goals. 

If your boss is bullying another employee, discipline is the virtue that stops you from over-reacting. Courage lets you intervene, but discipline means you don’t exert yourself too strongly, making the situation worse for everyone.

Discipline is naturally a middle ground between pleasure and pain. Consider two example of people who display it in different ways:

First, there is the athlete, who must subject themselves to pain on a daily basis. They endure brutal exercises, abstain from food, decline invitations for parties. The athlete masters self-control in relation to pain. But they also don’t celebrate pain on its own. The athlete needs to know when to rest or when to let off some steam with friends.

Discipline in the role of an athlete is a balance between ascetic suffering and undisciplined carelessness.

Next imagine a bartender at a popular club. Their life is surrounded by pleasure, people partying and drinking. The bartender has to resist this pleasure, they can’t take every offer for a free drink, nor can they leave their role to go dance or talk with friends.

But at the same time, the bartender can’t be a buzzkill. They can’t judge those drinking, or decline every offer for a drink. They can’t dislike the people at work, or find themselves unable to empathize with the people in the bar around them.

Discipline in the role of the bartender is a balance between hedonistic partying and being a buzzkill.

These examples show that Discipline is about hitting the right balance in how we engage with pleasure and pain to fulfill our larger role. It is about being consistent, instead of being pulled off course by strong experiences. It could also be called moderation, or self-control.

If this sounds like a boring virtue, consider what Seneca has to say on this:

“What is a happy life? Peacefulness and constant tranquility. Loftiness of mind will bestow this, and consistency which holds fast to good judgment. How are these things reached? If all of truth has been seen, if orderliness, moderation, and seemliness are preserved in actions, and a will which is guiltless and kindly, focused upon reason and never departing from it, as lovable as it is admirable. To put it in a nutshell for you, the wise man’s mind should be such as befits god.”  
Id: ???  
Source: Letters  
Location: 92.3

According to Seneca’ the wise man’s self-control should befit a god.

A mind befitting a god is not one where you are pulled around moment by moment to each new impression you receive. It is a life of consistency, dedication to ordered goals and focused priorities. 

One way to cultivate self-control is through exposure to pleasure and pain.

The Stoic’s advocate doing this by practicing voluntary discomfort.

Seneca recommends sleeping on the floor intermittently.

Epictetus describes taking a sip of water after a hard workout, and spitting it out. Or, in relation to pleasure, going to fancy parties but not eating more than you should. 

But voluntary discomfort is not just about self-denial. It has a few steps to make it useful.

First, make sure it is related to what you actually need to practice. Cold showers won’t necessarily help you find the discipline to get to work on time or finally commit to learning an instrument. 

Second, make sure it is leveled correctly. Too easy and you are not practicing. Too difficult and you are just hurting yourself for no benefit. It needs to be at a level that feels uncomfortable but attainable, like someone running just a bit faster.

Third, it needs to be repetitive. A single night on the floor may not change you. But a year leaving your bed immediately when your alarm goes off will.

Finally, it needs to be connected to what you value. You need to practice being more like the person you want to be. Seneca didn’t want to be anxious about losing money, so he practiced living without a fancy bed. 

What do you value now, but haven’t yet cultivated the discipline to achieve? Use your answer to this question to choose how to practice voluntary discomfort.

If you value friendship, but find yourself too shy to meet new people, practice making small-talk, of reaching out to someone new.

If you value people that are physically strong and able to endure pain, practice physical hardship.

If you value consistency, practice waking up at the same time everday.

You should direct the exercise of voluntary discomfort to what matters to you.

But don’t push voluntary discomfort too far. Stoic discipline is not about self-denial, but moderation between extremes. It is about being the kind of person that can follow through on the long term plans you set for yourself, even if things become difficult or other options become tempting. 

And the ability to do that, the Stoics thought, is an ability that befits a divine mind. 

### **CT: Justice**

CD: Treat others as they deserve

There is this trope in philosophy, of finding the sage alone on a mountain. Often the student has to seek them out, maybe make a pilgrimage. 

And the implication to this trope is that virtue is either more easily attained, or better suited, for a life of isolation. Enlightenment belongs on the mountain, not in the city. 

But the Stoics didn’t advocate for us to be hermits. They thought virtue was something we showed in our community, in how we act with others.

That’s because of the third part of virtue, Justice.

Justice is not like courage and discipline. Courage and discipline are focused inward. We can think of courage as proper action in relation to fear, and discipline as proper action in relation to desire. The courageous person only fears what deserves fear. The disciplined person only desires what deserves desire. 

But Justice is the virtue that extends our attention outside of ourselves. There is no justice without other people and how we treat them. In other words, the hermit on the mountain can show courage and discipline. They can face their fear of death and minimize their desires.  
But they can’t show justice without being around other people. It is a virtue that describes the way we treat others. 

If you ever find yourself in a situation where your boss is bullying another employee, justice is the virtue that tells you to intervene. That you owe it to the other person to do what you can to improve the situation.

Seneca defines Justice as being about paying the proper debts, knowing what is owed to each person. He goes on to add that Justice is about recognizing our shared connection with other people, grounded in a shared human nature. To quote Seneca:

“Nature produced us related to one another, since she created us from the same source and to the same end. She engendered in us mutual affection, and made us prone to friendships. She established fairness and justice; according to her ruling, it is more wretched to commit than to suffer injury. Through her orders, let our hands be ready for all that needs to be helped.”  
Id: ???  
Source: Letters  
Location: 95

Justice was broken down further into sub-virtues, such as fairness, friendliness and kindness, but they are all connected to that idea presented by Seneca, a recognition that we, as humans, come from the same source and aim at the same goal. 

There is a common criticism against Stoicism that it promotes passivity and inaction. The Stoic emphasis on Justice is the virtue that shows this criticism is false.

Stoicism is about improving ourselves to help others, because we share a common nature with, and responsibilities to, other people. Consider this famous Marcus Aurelius quote from the perspective of justice:

“When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: the people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous and surly. They are like this because they can't tell good from evil. But I have seen the beauty of good, and the ugliness of evil, and have recognized that the wrongdoer has a nature related to my own \- not of the same blood and birth, but the same mind, and possessing a share of the divine.”  
Id: 26  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 2.1

Marcus Aurelius and the rest of the Stoics believed  that all humans are connected by a shared nature that desires what is good and rational. In this way, we are all members of the same group, the human race, or citizens of the cosmopolis as they would say.

And as people on the same team, we have responsibilities to help those who have become confused about the difference between good and evil. We have responsibilities to all humans, not because they share the same blood or birth, but the same mind. This is what grounds stoic justice.

In terms of how to become more just, like anything else it comes with focused practice.   
Consider the sub-components of virtue:

We can practice kindness by giving our time and energy freely, and not be stingy with sharing our possessions.

We can practice friendliness by associating well with others, treating them with respect, and listening to what they have to say.

We can practice fairness by upholding our end of the bargain in any deal or agreement we make. This means both doing our part, and standing up for when others take advantage of us.

Justice calls us to expand your attention beyond yourself. Because of this it is often considered a more advanced virtue. Epictetus doesn’t recommend beginners worry about their duties to other people at all when starting Stoicism, because it can be easy to get wrong and fall into focusing on things outside our control.

But as we advance as Stoics, it is crucial we develop our sense of justice. We show our virtue most clearly in how we act with others.

### **CT: Wisdom**

CD: Know what is good

Imagine, as a teenager, there is a situation that is very difficult for you. Maybe it is your first break-up. Maybe it is deciding between going to college, working, or taking a year off to figure things out.  Maybe it is the dread of not knowing what comes next, after the structure of high school.

Now, think about who in your family you would go to talk to about this. Is it a grandparent? An aunt or uncle? One of your parents? A good friend?

Who would you rely on, not to make you feel better, or console you, but to help you navigate this complexity; to help you get perspective on what seems murky?

This is probably the person in your life who best embodies the virtue of wisdom. They contextualize things for you, change your frame of reference. Point out what is important and what won’t matter to you in 5 weeks, let alone 5 years.

Wisdom is about perspective and prudence. It focuses on what we give attention to in the moment, what we aim at as being most important in the long term, and how we go about achieving our goals.

It is arguably the most important virtue because it contextualizes all the others. Seneca compares wisdom to the ability for a painter, with all his brushes and colors, to imagine the final painting he is aiming towards. As Seneca said:

“No man can set in order the details \[of his life\] unless he has already set before himself the chief purpose of his life. The artist may have his colours all prepared, but he cannot produce a likeness unless he has already made up his mind what he wishes to paint. The reason we make mistakes is because we all consider the parts of life, but never life as a whole. The archer must know what he is seeking to hit; then he must aim and control the weapon by his skill. Our plans miscarry because they have no aim. When a man does not know what harbour he is making for, no wind is the right wind.   
Id: ???  
Source: Letters  
Location: 71.1-3

Like the other Stoic virtues, wisdom was broken down into its sub-components. These include good judgment, shrewdness, aim, and resourcefulness.

Good judgment is knowing how to do things in a practical and convenient way. Shrewdness is the ability to find the appropriate action quickly in the moment. Aim is about having the right target in life. And resourcefulness is knowing how to get out of a tough situation undisturbed. 

All of these are connected by a broader perspective and attention. Do you know what’s happening, what the important things to care about are, and how to aim at them? That’s wisdom.

In our workplace example where your vicious boss is bullying an employee, if justice tells you why to intervene, courage gives you the motivation, and discipline stops you from overreaching, then wisdom tells you how. You don’t want to stand up for someone in a way that gets you both fired, or does more harm than good. You want to be actually helpful, not be someone who reacts with no perspective for the practical consequences.

To understand this better, think of the wisdom of a server in a restaurant, who has to balance so many different factors at the same time, when the food will be ready in the kitchen, the needs of different customers, the staff member who called in sick.

Or think of a teacher, who balances the needs of different students, with what is good for the class as a whole, and makes strategic decisions about when to teach difficult content and when to give the kids an easy day.

We all show wisdom in some areas of our lives. The Stoic goal is to take that skill into the rest of our life.

If you understand cars, you know what maintenance is crucial, and where you can take short-cuts. If you are a skilled communicator, you know how to set a room at ease in a way other people don’t, and pick up social faux-pas others miss. If you have an eye for fashion, you will know what combinations of clothes work for what contexts.

Wisdom in any area is sensitivity to what matters most and a practical understanding of how to achieve it.

So wisdom in life is extending that same skill to the project of living well. Instead of being a prudent server, become a prudent human. Someone adept at paying attention to what matters most for flourishing and being happy

To become more wise, start with an aim in mind. Maybe that is to be Stoic, maybe that is to get a little better everyday.

As Seneca said: 

“When a man does not know what harbour he is making for, no wind is the right wind.”  
Id: ???  
Source: Letters  
Location: 71.1-4

So, if you want to develop wisdom, pick the port you aim at. 

The server might aim at providing a great experience for customers, while having fun. The teacher aims at preparing their students for success. The mechanic aims at mastering their craft until every engine issue can be solved. The Stoic would aim at virtue, showing courage, discipline, justice, and wisdom in their every action.

Then you practice reaching that port with intention, presence of mind, and a growth mindset that incorporates lessons from mistakes. 

By paying attention to what works, you can develop good judgment around how to do things in a practical and convenient way. By facing enough situations aimed at the same target, you will develop a shrewd ability to find the appropriate action quickly. Aim is about having the right target in life. And if resourcefulness is knowing how to get out of a tough situation undisturbed, then this is gained by being in tough situations, moments where it feels like you might fail to reach the port you aim for.

If there is a single key idea, it is this: developing wisdom requires a target. When you set an explicit goal for yourself, and earnestly try to achieve it, you learn wisdom. 

Just make sure that goal is worth achieving.  And if you don’t have one yet, you don’t know your passion or calling, consider applying yourself to the goal the Stoics say is most important for every person: Becoming virtuous, being happy, and showing courage, discipline, justice and wisdom in your everyday life.
