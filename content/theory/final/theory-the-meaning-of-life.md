### **CT: What is Stoic Happiness?**

CD: Living well as a craft.

Every craft has a target.

The runner aims to run well. The painter aims to paint well. The writer aims to write well. Ancient philosophers saw philosophy as a craft, no different than these others except it had a different aim.

The philosopher’s craft is living. The philosopher aims to live well.

The ancient philosopher has an easy answer to the question of the meaning of life. What are we here for? We are here to live well.

Often, we translate this idea of ‘living well’ into the English word ‘happiness’. The Greek word is eudaimonia. Literally this meant having a blessed spirit watching over you, ‘eu’ meaning good, and Daimon meaning spirit. Daimon is the same root word we get the modern word demon from. To have a eu-daimon was to be protected by a good spirit, as opposed to a bad or a cursed one.

Practically, this meant having a kind of blessed life. One that people could point to, and say: that is a good life, something I aspire to and admire. Some people, like Aristotle, argued that it was something you couldn’t even properly evaluate until the end of someone’s life.

So, in this course we will refer to Eudaimonia as happiness, but it does not mean fleeting pleasure, or a state of elation. It is not a great night out with friends, or the feeling of a big accomplishment, or even gentle contentment \- as nice as those things are.

Happiness for the ancients, and the Stoics, means a good life, a prosperous life, the kind of life other people want. It is not something gained or lost on a daily basis. Think of this in terms of a deathbed reflection. A happy life was one that you could reflect back on with pride and contentment. 

Many great people, who lived great lives, faced challenges and setbacks. A happy life, for the ancient Greeks, is not necessarily a life that is easy, or free of pain, grief or challenge.

So almost every school of ancient hellenistic philosophy aimed at Eudaimonia, or happiness, as the end of life.

What sets the Stoic idea of happiness apart, is that it is achievable for anyone, because it depends on things that are up to us. 

In this course, we will cover the Stoic idea of happiness. What are its features, how is it achieved, and what kind of life does it promise? In any craft there are tradeoffs. All painters aim at painting well, but they must decide if they want to be modernist or impressionist. All writers aim at a good book, but there is a large difference between James Joyce and Tolstoy. Athletes must specialize too. A marathon runner cannot lift as much as a wrestler, but they can run farther. 

To be a stoic is to aim at living well, but it's to pursue a certain understanding of what that means, at the expense of other kinds of lives. By the end of this course, you will understand Stoicism’s answer to the craft of living. You will understand the aim of Stoicism, the kind of life it promises, and how to get there.

### **CT: Living in Accordance with Nature**

CD: The often misunderstood goal of Stoicism.

There is a Stoic story, of a man tied to a cart. The cart continues to go down the road, regardless of if the man resists or tries to pull back against it. It is then the man’s choice if he wants to walk alongside the cart, or be dragged by it. The destination is the same, the experience is very different. 

The Stoics, beginning with the founder of the school Zeno, and refined by the second leader Cleanthes, defined happiness as living in accordance with nature. In other words, happiness is a kind of smooth flow of life.

Living in accordance with nature means acting how you *should* act, given the state of the world. Subjectively, it is characterized by a lack of friction between yourself and the world, caused by eliminating false judgements about things outside of your control: for example, how things should be different, or how something is owed to you, or something wasn’t fair.   
When we have false judgements about the world, ones that put us opposed to how things are, we are dragged by the cart. We resist and fight against what is inevitable and not up to us. People’s opinions change, they get sick and pass away, we are sometimes successful and sometimes failures in our pursuits, sometimes lucky and sometimes unlucky. These things are not up to us, they will happen to emperors or slaves. They are the cart, fate, the inevitable. We can walk alongside it, or, if we resist it, we can be dragged and experience it as a very harsh thing.

When we learn to walk with the cart, to accept things as they are, our life is smooth. 

The Stoics saw nature, or the universe, as an intelligent god. All things within the universe are a part of god, and so imbued with intelligence and order. Humans have a special ability to be self-reflective and we should use that ability to intentionally live in accordance with nature.

The universe is like play. We don’t get to talk with the director, but the play has a story it is trying to tell and we each have a part to perform. As Epictetus puts it: 

“Remember that you are an actor in a drama, of such a kind as the author pleases to make it. If short, of a short one; if long, of a long one. If it is his pleasure you should act a poor man, a cripple, a governor, or a private person, see that you act it naturally. For this is your business, to act well the character assigned you; to choose it is another's”   
Sentence Ids: 98-101  
Source: Handbook  
Location:

To live in accordance with nature is to understand our part. We need to understand our particular talents, abilities and potential. This decreases our suffering: we aren’t lamenting that we weren’t born in a different body, or a different time, or into a wealthier family. But it also increases our joy: we seize the opportunities life presents to us particularly. We maximize our own talents, and recognize the beauty that is present. 

To understand your role, to learn the part and to play it well. This is a smooth flow of life. It is walking along the cart, so that you have the time to admire the view and enjoy your journey, instead of being dragged along as you try to pull against it. For the stoics, it is happiness, the goal of life. 

### **CT: Virtue, Knowledge and Happiness**

CD: Why virtue and knowledge are necessary to be happy.

So the Stoic pursues a smooth flow of life, one in accordance with nature, where there is little to no friction between the world as it is, and the world as we understand it, or want it to be. 

This is the unique definition of happiness that the Stoics adopt, that sets them apart from their contemporaries.

The Epicureans, a contemporary rival, defined happiness as a life of ataraxia, the pleasurable state of being free from physical or emotional distress. For the Epicureans this was achieved by having minimalistic desires that could be easily satisfied. The ancient sceptics also pursued ataraxia, seeing happiness as a state of emotional satisfaction achieved when you don’t overestimate your ability to know anything with certainty.  

Notice the subjective focus from the Epicureans and the Sceptics. Happiness is, for them, something you feel. A state of freedom from pain or distress.

But for the Stoics, happiness is something we do. It is a way of acting in the world. 

A calm, positive feeling is a side effect of Stoic happiness, but it is not happiness. Not all calm people are living in accordance with nature. Someone may be delusional, for example, telling themselves lies they want to hear. 

The reason for this focus on action, goes back to the idea of Eudaimonia, or a flourishing excellent life. For the Stoics, an excellent life is determined by how we act and what we do, not by what we feel. 

Stoicism does promise, if put into practice, a life free from extreme negative emotions. It also promises a kind of deep, healthy joy that comes with living in accordance with nature.

But these are by-products of happiness. They are welcome additions that follow happiness. They are not the target, or what Stoics judge happiness by. Stoics judge happiness by how we act.

The Stoics also argue that there is only one thing necessary and sufficient for acting well and achieving happiness: virtue, or excellent character. As Seneca says:  
   
“A good character is the only guarantee of everlasting, carefree happiness.”  
Id: 522  
Source: Letters  
Location:

Already we see, if virtue promises happiness, Stoic happiness can’t be about just feeling good. The virtuous person obviously still gets sick, may starve, may be enslaved and put on the torture rack. Good character does not promise freedom from suffering, only freedom from unnecessary suffering, from friction. The sage is still walking along the side of the cart, and sometimes the path is painful. 

To be happy for the Stoics is not to be free from pain, but to be a good person. To do the right kinds of things, and think the right kinds of thoughts. Because we are rational creatures who act on our beliefs, to be a good person is to perfect our rationality. 

The stoics defined virtue as knowledge. In other words, to be a good person is to understand the way the world is, and our place within it. 

Even their sub-virtues: Courage, justice, temperance and wisdom, they defined as types of knowledge. Courage is the knowledge of what is and is not worth avoiding, for example. 

The idea that virtue is knowledge can seem counterintuitive at first, but the Stoic argument is simple. We do what we think is right in each situation. Psychologically, we have to. All people follow their understanding of what is best. 

So to do the right thing, to be courageous, or just, or temperate, is just to know the right course of action.

Someone who courageously stands up to the bully, is the person who knows bullying is wrong, and that it is better to risk getting punched than risk letting someone else get bullied. 

To be happy is to live in accordance with nature. This looks like virtuous action and an excellent character, and an excellent character is one that possesses knowledge.

This is why Musonius Rufus, Epictetus’ teacher tells us:

“In our control is the most beautiful and important thing, the thing because of which even the god himself is happy — namely, the proper use of our impressions.”  
Id: 1132  
Source:  
Location: 

Living in accordance with nature, and so also happiness, depends on our opinions and beliefs, how we make use of impressions. It depends on how we think.

This is why so much of Stoicism takes the form of exercises that empower or improve our thinking. Whether that is mindfulness, active journaling, or tools like the Dichotomy of Control or View from above. The Stoic program of therapy, self-improvement and happiness is about transforming our minds to be in accordance with nature and to see things how they really are. It is about learning to make proper use of impressions.

### **CT: Pleasure, Success and Happiness**

CD: Why external success doesn’t matter.

When you talk to a non-Stoic about happiness, they likely won’t think of virtue and they almost certainly won’t list knowledge as being the crucial piece of the puzzle. 

Most people aspire, when they think of a good life, to some degree of material success. Wealth, a great career, or pleasure. Pleasure sounds crude, but it doesn’t have to be. Pleasure can just be the warm feeling you get sitting on the couch, watching a good movie with your family, comfortable in your own home, eating your favorite snacks.

That doesn’t sound like a bad thing to me, nor does healthy retirement savings and a good job. These things are not bad on the Stoic view. They just aren’t the path to happiness. A life can be unhappy with them, or happy without them.

Viktor Frankl was not a Stoic, but he came to a number of Stoic realizations. Frankl was a psychologist and survivor of the holocaust, and wrote about his experiences in the masterpiece ‘Man’s Search for Meaning’.

In that book, he echoes the Stoic view of pleasure, success, and happiness when he writes:

“Don't aim at success—the more you aim at it and make it a target, the more you are going to miss it. For success, like happiness, cannot be pursued; it must ensue…as the unintended side-effect of one's personal dedication to a cause greater than oneself.”  
Id: 1733  
Source: Viktor Frankl  
Location:

Frankl was not a Stoic, but he came separately to this Stoic realization.

The Stoic’s don’t ask us to abstain from pleasure, wealth, or career success. They even encourage these things, calling them preferred indifferents, the class of things we should pursue if they are available at no risk to our happiness.

But Stoics warn us about making them the target of our life, the main object of pursuit.

Success, wealth, and pleasure are what the Stoics call externals. They sit outside of our minds, and they are not up to us. What this means is that we don’t determine if we achieve them or not. We can influence things, we can increase our odds, but circumstance and chance always has the ability to remove or deprive us of them.

Frankl studied medicine at the university of Vienna. He worked hard and played his part to become a successful psychologist and doctor. But the rise of Nazism disrupted that career, and could have just as easily ended his life. Frankl influenced his success, but did not control it.

Zeno, the founder of Stoicism, lost his entire fortune in a shipwreck before he would go on to found Stoicism. He experienced how external success depends upon fortune, not character.

When you desire external success as the goal of life, you commit two harms. First, you compromise your subjective well-being because you will inevitably feel anxious and worried if you think your happiness depends on something someone else can take away from you. 

Second, you direct energy away from the real target, which is living in accordance with nature. And sometimes, nature, fate, luck, or chance, will put you in circumstances that are not very pleasurable, or feel like failure.

As Epictetus said:

“There is only one road to happiness – let this rule be at hand morning, noon, and night: stay detached from things that are not up to you.”   
Id: 1655  
Source: Discourses  
Location:

The Stoic accepts difficult moments for what they are and refocuses their attention on their character and how they respond to these moments. Not for the chance at future success, but because responding well is the goal. 

### **CT: The Stoic Sage**

CD: What is the happy Stoic actually like?

Many philosophies and religions have a special, almost transcendent state achieved when the individual reaches their goal. Buddhism has enlightenment, and the buddha. Christianity has sainthood.

But what does the final stage of Stoicism look like? What does stoicism promise those who practice it?

For the Stoics this final stage is the sage. The person that has achieved virtue, a smooth flow of life, and happiness.

The sage was incredibly rare. It is almost an aspirational target, rather than something we ourselves can hope to achieve. Seneca says they come around once every 500 years, like a mythical phoenix. 

Still, while we might not become sages, it serves as both a useful target and a helpful demonstration of what Stoicism promises. So what is the sage like?

Intellectually the sage is at the top of their game. 

The sage has achieved knowledge, which means they perfectly understand themselves, their own nature, and their position within the broader universe. They have no false beliefs, only true ones.

They also have a disposition that does not allow for the introduction of new false beliefs. A sage wouldn’t be secure if you could just insult them and disrupt their equanimity. They have mastered the Stoic discipline of assent, and so are careful not to accept any new beliefs unless they have been tested as true. 

Some stoics even argue that knowledge, and so happiness, would crystalize when sagehood was achieved into a kind of permanent thing that could not be lost.

Because the sage only has true beliefs, they neither generates false ideas, nor accepts false ideas, and so their happiness is impervious to faults. It is unbreakable. 

In terms of needs, the sage is also entirely self-sufficient. Their happiness depends on their knowledge, which is an internal quality. As Seneca says:

External circumstances have very little importance either for good or for evil: the wise man is neither elated by prosperity nor depressed by adversity; for he has always endeavoured to depend chiefly upon himself and to derive all his joys from himself.  
Id: 1653  
Source: Letters  
Location:

Emotionally, the sage experiences equanimity, a freedom from disruptive harmful passions, as well as gentle positive emotions like joy, derived from an appreciation of the beauty and goodness of the world. 

Because the sage is self-sufficient, they are immune from passions like fear or greed or jealousy, which derive from feeling either insecure, harmed, or desiring something you don’t have.

As Seneca points out, the self-sufficient person can’t get angry:

The wise man is without anger, which is caused by the appearance of injury, and he could not be free from anger unless he were also free from injury, which he knows cannot be done to him.   
Id: 1653  
Source: Letters  
Location:

However, just because the sage is self-sufficient does not mean that they are lonely or isolated. As Seneca tells us, the sage still desires friends and community, because of human’s pro-social nature. But how is the sage self-sufficient if they desire friends? Seneca explains:

In this sense the wise man is self-sufficient, that he can do without friends, not that he desires to do without them. When I say “can,” I mean this: he endures the loss of a friend with equanimity.  
Id: 1219  
Source: Letters  
Location:

The sage has perfected the ability to navigate preferred indifferents with an easy come, easy go attitude. They seek out good things not up them, like friendship. And they take them when they come. But they also acknowledge that sometimes we will lose friendship. Sometimes because of death, and other times because of conflict or changes in circumstances.

The Sage is able to both seek friendship, and other preferred indifferents like success and health, while accepting their loss with equanimity. 

Epictetus tells us that to be Stoic, we should act like a good dinner guest. When we are offered a drink, we should accept it. When there is food given to us, we should eat it. But we don’t complain when the wine runs out, or grab food off someone else's plate if it looks appetizing. 

In summary, to live smoothly is just like dining well. It is to be happy with little, but gladly take what presents itself to us. 

The Stoic sage is self-sufficient, but not alone. They have equanimity through hardship, but joy in success.They are kind, just, courageous and wise, not for their instrumental value, but only because they see these things as their own reward. They are consistent, but not inflexible.

They rely on others, without being dependent on them. They are skeptical, but pragmatic. They understand that all friction or suffering is alleviated by better understanding either ourselves, the world we live in, or how these two fit together. They always understand both themselves, and their place in the greater whole. 

This is the life that Stoicism promises, and that our practice works towards.
