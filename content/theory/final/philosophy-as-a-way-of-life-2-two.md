# CT: Confucianism

CD: Moving beyond individualism and freedom

Confucianism goes against two of the central ideas of Western philosophy: individualism and freedom.

Many in the West believe that the best relationships are chosen. We are born free and must determine who to be, who to love, what to do with our lives.

But as Confucian scholar Bryan Norden writes:

“We are not born free. We are born with obligations to the parents and other relatives who will raise us to adulthood, to teachers who shape us far beyond what is required to just earn a paycheck, and to the preexisting civilization that makes all our individual contributions possible.”  
id: ?

The Confucian today finds meaning in their life by rejecting the idea that we are absolutely free to determine who and what we are.

Confucianism is a deep philosophy with a rich history, centuries of development and plenty of controversy – we can only skim the surface in this brief lesson. But we can explain why it’s a powerful philosophy, give pointers for further study, and note where it connects to Stoicism.

It emerges with the figure of Confucius who was born in 551 BC. For comparison with the Stoics, Socrates was born at 469 BC\! We have a number of Confucius’s teachings written down in the form of The Analects, a collection compiled by his disciples. Later philosophers such as Mencius, who lived two centuries after Confucius, further developed Confucian teachings.

The first thing one notices in Confucianism is its focus on relationships. For example, the Confucian philosopher Mencius says:

“The Way lies in what is near, but people seek it in what is distant; one’s task lies in what is easy, but people seek it in what is difficult. If everyone would treat their kin as kin, and their elders as elders, the world would be at peace.”  
id: ?

Fundamentally, we are social beings. We are defined not by our personal attributes, but by our relationships with other people. A key difference between Confucianism and Buddhism, another Eastern philosophy, is that the Confucians don’t erase the individual – after all, there needs to be individuals that stand in relationships. While  Buddhists teach that there is no self, Confucians teach that we exist and are fundamentally dependent on other people.

You can see this in the Confucian emphasis on filial virtue – what we might call today family values. The central way in which you express virtue is by becoming an excellent family member. As an example of this, Confucius prescribed a 3 year period of mourning. In ancient China, when your parents passed away you would need to return to their village and perform rites of mourning. A death in the family could completely derail political careers. This shows just how essential respect for the family is for Confucians. It is why Mencius also said:

“Among babes in arms there is none that does not know to love its parents. When they grow older, there is none that does not know to respect its elder brother. Treating one’s parents as parents is benevolence. Respecting one’s elders is righteousness. There is nothing else to do but extend these to the world.”  
id: ?

The Confucian doesn’t care about becoming their authentic self or becoming a sovereign individual. Self-help isn’t about helping the self, it’s about being the best child, parent, employee, or official we can be. Who we are is determined by the relationships we have. Some of these are hierarchical, such as the division between boss and employee, parent and child, student and teacher. Many of these roles we don’t choose.

One of the central similarities with Stoicism and Confucianism is this idea of role ethics. Epictetus writes:

“it is necessary that I not be unfeeling as a statue, but that I preserve my relationships, the natural and acquired ones, as a pious man, son, brother, father, and citizen.”

These are roles that a Confucian would respect. Like the Stoics, they would urge us to excel in them.

What does this look like in action? Instead of asking questions like “what do I want to study?”, or “What career do I want to take?” and searching for answers in yourself, find answers in your relationships. Follow in the family business, ask what your community needs, and be a model citizen.

It’s fair to say that Confucians take the natural relationships of the family and community more seriously than the Stoics. The Stoic Musonius Rufus taught that we should not obey parents when they are in the wrong. Confucians may agree with this in principle, but would emphasize skillful disagreement with authorities. 

If you do disagree with a superior, you must do so with tact and skill. Many in the West see the lone men and women who stand against misguided authorities as a hero – the figure who stands up during a meeting, boldly asserts their position, and would rather walk out than be apart of a sinking ship. Confucians would see this approach as self-centered. Instead they would appreciate someone tactfully and skillfully expressing disagreement while respecting hierarchies and allowing everyone to keep face.

So even though Confucians do not blindly follow authority or the status quo, I see Confucian ethics as giving more power – and meaning – to following established social roles than Stoicism. Nonetheless, the focus on achieving happiness by playing your social roles well – both chosen and unchosen – is a key similarity between the philosophies. 

Mindfulness and presence is another core similarity between the two philosophies. Confucianism has been described as a philosophy that promotes always being on. The idea is to be in the zone for everything in life – even the little things.

A part of his students' lives was preceding over and witnessing religious sacrifices. As such, Confucius once said:

“If I am not fully present at the sacrifice, it is as if I did not sacrifice at all.”  
id: ?

Rituals were not something that Confucius thought you could phone in. Why do anything without complete effort? If you have decided to do something, devote yourself to it and be aware when it happens. This sense of presence is an essential part of living a good life in both Stoicism and Confucianism. Be present. Do your best, even in the little things. That’s where happiness is found.

That’s a realistic and inspiring message. 

# CT: Pragmatism

CD: Focusing on what works

In a classical myth, it was prophesied that whoever cut the Gordian knot would rule all of Asia. Wannabe kings and conquerors came to it and tried to untie it in hundreds of ways. They all failed. When Alexander the Great was faced with the challenge, he simply took his sword and cut through it. And so the knot was cut and he came to rule.

For Pragmatists, so much of philosophy is about useless theorizing. Like conquerors struggling to untie an impossible knot, we try to describe reality and then devise solutions. The pragmatist asks: why not focus on what works above all else? 

Cut through the noise. Remove all useless theorizing. And simply focus on what works.

Pioneered by thinkers like William James and Charles Pierce, pragmatism developed as a fundamentally practical philosophy. It arose in the late 20th century in America: a philosophy for a rising power that just survived a civil war.

Its focus on action has much in common with the philosophy of Stoicism. However, like any two philosophical traditions, there are many differences.

A central difference between pragmatism of all varieties and Stoicism is that the Stoics sought to understand objective reality. Something is true only if it accurately describes nature.

For the pragmatists, what’s true is what works. Indeed, one could argue that pragmatism has roots in the ancient Greek school of Sophism – enemies of the Socratic schools.

Like Sophists , pragmatists were skeptical of the notions of objective truth, arguing that it is either incoherent or inaccessible. 

William James gestures at this when he says:

“We carve out groups of stars in the heavens, and call them constellations… we carve out everything, just as we carve out constellations, to suit our human purposes.”

The very idea of a thing is relative. We determine what things are by their use – not their internal nature. Think of a hammer. Hammers are defined by their function. Humans use them to knock in nails. They are artifacts invented by and defined by humans. Everything is like that for the pragmatist. Even the cardinal virtues of justice, moderation, courage, and wisdom. Virtues are not external standards that we try to meet, but concepts developed by humans, for humans.

Fundamentally, this reorients our perspective.

Instead of stepping back and asking, “what’s true?” The pragmatist asks, “what purposes does this serve? Does my philosophy work?”

These questions force one to reckon with whether the way you live is effective. The focus is on whether your thinking serves your purposes, not whether it matches up to some external reality.

So the pragmatist defines the truth as what works, but what does that mean? Something works if it fulfills its purpose. But where does this purpose come from? For Stoics, it is grounded in reality. Pragmatists, however, cannot ground things in this way. They are closer to the Stoic rivals, the Epicureans, who defined the good life as the life of pleasure. For the Epicureans, you’re living well if your character is shaped to reduce pain and promote the feeling of tranquility. Pragmatists agree with this, but often define the good in a broader and more relative way: something works if it fulfills your individual purposes. 

Indeed they go one step further and define truth itself as relative to a belief’s practical effects. It's the practical consequences of beliefs and the usefulness of ideas in solving problems that matter.

For example: some people look at religion and reject it because they believe it is not true. The pragmatist believes this is a mistake. What matters is whether religion works. What difference do religious beliefs make? Does it promote happiness and beauty or misery and division? These are the questions that matter.

It’s important to clarify that pragmatism isn’t an excuse to believe whatever you want. It’s a challenge to believe what works.

Many preachers of religious or political beliefs insist that theirs is the true path – but if you look at the preachers themselves, they do not seem to be happy. The pragmatist would seize on this and use it to question whether those beliefs are really true at all. What’s the point of using a hammer that doesn’t work? Pick up a better hammer.

Likewise they recognize that we can’t just believe anything – there are constraints. Some belief systems are better at achieving our purposes than others. And likewise they realize that some belief systems do not work. If you need to get somewhere quickly but are lost, believing you are not lost will not get you where you need to be.

To some extent, Stoics can find a lot of value in pragmatism. It forces us to think about our life in concrete terms. Does our thinking add to or detract from our health, relationships, thinking, or self development? Pragmatism reminds us to get out of our head and look at our actions. In other words, it deemphasizes Stoic logic – working to get at the truth – in favor of checking in on Stoic ethics. It cries out: stop trying to untie the cord with thought – simply cut through the mess of life and achieve your purposes.

However, the crucial difference between pragmatism and Stoicism remains: the Stoics believed in aligning themselves with the way things really are. They believed that the universe had a nature and that humans themselves possess a nature and that these things determine how one should live. By practicing Stoic logic, Socratic questioning, and, ultimately, philosophy, Stoics aim to live well by obtaining knowledge.

This is especially true when it comes to the very question of purpose. Ancient philosophers like the Stoics distinguished between things like hammers and human beings. Hammers are defined relative to humans. They have a derivative function. But human beings themselves? Stoics saw humans as fundamentally social and rational creatures – these facts ground our purpose. Indeed, the key way we exercise our reason is by determining what is true. That means understanding reality as it really is.

The pragmatist defines the truth as what works, but is that good enough? 

For the Stoics, and other philosophers we’ve covered in this course, it is not. Our purpose must be grounded in something objective and bigger than ourselves. It’s the very nature of reality that determines how we should live.

The pragmatist believes the idea of nature having an objective reality is incoherent or unknowable. They wouldn’t waste their time on abstract theorizing or discussion. There is wisdom in this approach. Some Stoics may spend too much time trying to understand the theory of Stoicism and not enough applying it.

The pragmatist focus on action is a good one. As Epictetus said:

“Don't explain your philosophy. Embody it.”

However, there is the risk that action in itself is not valuable. You need a philosophy to ground it. Action without understanding is often misguided. Action without purpose is meaningless. By declaring that truth is simply what works – pragmatism is a philosophy without foundation.

So, Stoics can learn from the pragmatic emphasis on action. But they must reject their account of truth. The way things are must be respected. As Seneca said:

“The willing, fate guides. The unwilling, it drags.”
