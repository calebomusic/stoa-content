Goodhart’s Law  
Theory

Goodhart’s Law says that when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

Think about this in terms of school. We want to make children smart so we measure their test results. But then students make good grades their target. They optimize for test results and become good at taking tests, but not good at learning.

Or, society admires health, so we measure and judge people’s appearances. But then people become so focused on developing their bodies, they undereat or take drugs. They achieve the body they are told they should have at the expense of their health.

When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

Goodhart’s Law also applies to the practice of Stoicism.

Epictetus would notice a tendency in his students, when he asked them what they had learnt at his school, to recite lines of text, or brag about their interpretations of difficult philosophy.

Epictetus would laugh at this:

It is as if I were talking to an athlete and said, ‘Show me your shoulders’, and he then replied, ‘Look at my weights.’ That is quite enough of you and your weights: what I want to see is what the weights serve to achieve  
*Discourses* 1.4 Robin Hard Translation 

Philosophy as a way of life is an action. Virtue is something you do. Sometimes, we measure our progress by other things: How much we’ve studied, how well we can explain a concept, how many books we have read, or the number of obscure words we understand.  
These can be measures of progress, but they can’t become targets.

If we become proud of how much we’ve studied, we will fall victim to Goodhart’s law. We will start optimizing for the things that are meant to enable progress, rather than excellent character itself. We become like the athletes who show off our weights, instead of our bodies.  
Or, as Epictetus puts it:  
If what I admire is the mere act of interpretation, what else have I ended up as but a grammarian instead of a philosopher? Except, indeed, that instead of interpreting Homer I interpret Chrysippus.
