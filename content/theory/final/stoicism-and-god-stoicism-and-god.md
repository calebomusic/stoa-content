### **Stoicism and God \- Theory**

### **Introduction:**

In Marcus Aurelius’ meditations, his personal journal, he wrote a reminder to himself about how to behave:

“Just that you do the right thing. The rest doesn't matter….to move from one unselfish act to another with God in mind…” 

id: 30

To just do the right thing is sage advice. It is easier said than done and requires practice, but it is the Stoic goal. To focus each moment on how we act, what is up to us, and cut out the noise. 

However, the final part of the quote deserves attention too. Marcus says to do the right thing “With God In Mind.” 

What does that mean for Marcus? And why is it an important part of a good life?

In this series, we will talk about the Stoic God, and how this connects with the Stoic’s idea of living well. What do the Stoics think God is? Does God care about our lives? How are we connected to God, and does God connect us to others?

Learning about the Stoic God is an opportunity to spend time reflecting on this broader, cosmic perspective. The Stoics knew that contemplation of the whole universe helps us improve ourselves. It’s why they taught exercises like the view from above.

But Marcus also thought that this perspective was necessary for getting the day-to-day, mundane things right as well. To return to Marcus’ quote, you can’t do the right thing, without also having God in mind.

This series will cover what the Stoics thought of God and why the Stoics thought humans were divine or connected with God. This will lead to a discussion of the Stoic idea of living in accordance with nature. This idea underpins all of Stoic ethics and guides how the Stoics live, but requires an understanding of the Stoic God to fully unpack.

If you don’t believe in God, then you may think this series is not for you. However, it’s important to understand that the Stoic image of God is distinct from the religious pictures of God you may be familiar with. As we’ll learn, for the Stoics, God is, in some sense, nature. 

Many Modern practitioners relate to this idea of the Stoic god differently. Some try to capture the same religious attitude held by the ancients and put their teachings into their day-to-day practice. 

Others see the ancient Stoics' references to God as metaphorical and use this metaphor as a mental framework for improving their life, even if it is not entirely what they literally agree with. 

Regardless, learning about the Stoic God will help you understand Stoicism at a deep level, practice living in accordance with nature, and employ the broader perspective that allows us to flourish in the day-to-day.

### **The Stoic God:**

So what do the Stoics think God is?

Before answering this question, it is crucial to understand that the Stoics were materialists. Everything that exists is material. They didn’t believe in immaterial things like ghosts or souls. They saw everything as physical and concrete, including you and me. Like everything else material, we take up space and can interact with other material things. Crucially, Stoics don’t believe in some supernatural world outside of the material one. 

The fact that the Stoics were materialists doesn’t mean they don’t believe in God, only that God has to be a material thing. Something that sits inside the material world, not outside of it. And something that can be acted upon, and can act upon others.

So what did these materialist Stoics think God was? This quote by Aetius, a 1st-century scholar, best sums up the Stoic position:

“The Stoics made God out to be intelligent, a designing fire which methodically proceeds towards the creation of the world, and encompasses all the seminal principles according to which everything comes about according to fate, and a breath pervading the whole world, which takes on different names owing to the alterations of matter through which it passes.” (Aetius LS 46A)

Let’s break this down piece by piece. First, notice that the Stoics called God a ‘breath pervading the whole world.” This is the essential metaphor for understanding the Stoic God.

Imagine a person, whose ‘breath’, or heat or energy has left their body. They are a lump of flesh in the Stoic view, decaying. But when they have the ‘breath’ of life, they are unified, inspirited, acting as a whole towards a greater end.

With breath, we are people. Without it, we are just a cold body. As Epictetus said: 

“You are a little soul carrying around a corpse.” 

id: 271

The Stoics teach that God is this breath that turns a corpse into a living thing but for the whole universe. It imbues all things with life, it is inside all matter, and it gives all matter movement and shape.

So everything, ourselves included, is part of God. God is in each part of the universe. God is nature.

The Stoics also thought that God, as the breath of nature, was responsible for the order of the universe. To quote Aetius again, the god is the ‘seminal principle according to which everything comes about according to fate.’

In other words, the Stoics thought that the material world follows natural laws. The Stoics didn’t have the formulations of modern science in mind, but we can think of these as today’s laws of physics that explain why matter acts the way it does.

Gravity, thermodynamics, and causation: these are foundational principles about the universe that determine and predict how matter interacts with the world. God, as the thing that imbues the universe with movement and form, is then responsible for these laws.

So the world unfolds the way it does, both because God has set the world up to unfold a certain way, but also because God’s presence in the world maintains these laws. 

God is not someone who sets up dominos and watches them fall. God is embedded in the entire chain of dominos and maintains the physical laws that cause them to fall in the same direction when struck, instead of doing nothing at all. 

This is worth calling out because to understand the Stoic idea of God you need a sense of how ‘material’ and ‘practical’ this conception is. God is not abstract or removed from the universe, it is a direct part of it, keeping its form and shape.

Sometimes people reference God to explain why there is something instead of nothing. They posit God as a divine watchmaker, someone who has created and designed the world.

For the Stoics, God did not create the watch and leave it ticking, God is inside the watch holding it together and making it tick the whole time. 

The idea of the Stoic God is difficult. If you take something away about the Stoic picture of god, it should be this: God is the breath that animates the physical world. As this breath, God is both a part of the material of life, planets, and the stars and an animating force that gives them movement and shape.

So when the Stoics talk about expanding your perspective to keep God in mind, they are not asking you to consider some abstract thing, but the world around you. A world that you yourself are a part of.

When you practice the view from above or consider what God has planned for you, you are not considering the perspective of a detached observer, but the perspective of the entire universe. 

### **God as Reason and Nature**

One of the reasons the Stoic idea of God can be confusing is because the ancient Stoics called God so many different things, particularly one of three: Zeus, nature, or Reason. 

And it’s not like these are similar to each other. Replace nature with reason in a normal sentence, and you will get a lot of confused faces. Replace it with Zeus and it will be even worse.

This lesson will explain why the Stoics called God, or Zeus, both nature and reason.

Consider this quote by Marcus Aurelius from his meditations:

“So other people hurt me? That’s their problem. Their character and actions are not mine. What is done to me is ordained by nature, what I do by my own.” 

id: 210

In this quote, God is nature or the universe. It ‘ordains’, or confers holy orders unto us to experience certain things. 

The Stoics describe God interchangeably with nature, for reasons we also discussed in the last lesson. By nature here the Stoics don’t mean just plants and animals, but the greek word *physis*, which we know use as the english word physics. This means, more literally, the things that exist, the whole universe.

We also know that God is in everything that exists, providing its order. God permeates all things in the universe, so God is nature.

But God is also called reason. As Seneca says:

“Reason shapes matters and directs it wherever it wants, and from matter produces its manifold creations.” 

Here Seneca is talking about the power of God to shape nature, but calls God Reason. Why would the Stoics refer to God as reason?

This is because God was thought to be intelligent. God is material, but this does not mean that God is unintelligent or inert. Our human minds are material but we are intelligent, we have plans, we can understand what is better or worse, and we act.

In fact, the human mind has a lot in common with God. Our minds are called by the Stoics the *hegemonikon* or commanding-faculty and according to Cicero, Chrysippus described God as “The world’s own commanding-faculty, since he is located in intellect and reason.” 

God is the commanding faculty, or mind, of the universe. The part that decides how the universe will unfold.

For the Stoics, the universe is not random. It is working towards a bigger plan. The plan decided by God, or reason. But reason is also nature, it doesn’t stand outside of the universe. So it thinks, and acts upon itself to create a fated universe, determined by physical laws.

This is why Cicero says that Stoic fate is “not the ‘fate’ of superstition, but that of physics, an everlasting cause of things \- why past things happened, why present things are happening, and why future things will be.”

God decides how the world will unfold, but then God does the unfolding. 

This is a complicated idea, but in its simplest form, God is called nature and reason because God is both those things. The name used depends on what you are talking about, just like I am both Michael and a Canadian, but the name you use will change depending on what part of me you are referring to.

God is intelligent and has a plan for the world, so it is called reason. But that intelligence permeates the whole of the universe, it does not stand outside of it. So God is also called nature. The two are the same thing; Different sides of the same coin.

This distinction is crucial to understand, because seeing the connection between god, nature, and reason, can help us live well.

This is why Marcus Aurelius points out that:

“To the rational animal, the same act is at once according to nature and according to reason.” 

id: 966

One part of living well is to understand how things are in reality. That’s the nature part. But living well also requires understanding what things are striving towards or should be. God’s plan towards a certain end. That is the reason part. 

When we live, we don’t want to fight against nature or reason. We fight against nature when we want to avoid or change things that are a necessary part of life. People have to get sick and pass away, some people will be awful and mean, and not all of our plans work out. This is nature in action. 

We fight against reason when we lose perspective of the small but important part we play in something greater than ourselves. This might happen because we view ourselves as being too important, the main character in the world. Or it might happen when we view ourselves as too insignificant, as if our life doesn’t matter or doesn’t have meaning. 

What links these two together, is that we are fighting against nature or reason when we don’t accept or try to change the things that are not up to us to control.

We align ourselves with both sides of God’s identity, both nature and reason, When we do the right thing, we understand what is ours to control or influence, and what is not up to us. When we accept the things that must be accepted and if possible, even love them. When we act in accordance with the reality of things, by understanding where we have the power to make a difference, and accepting where we can’t.

### **Why humans are divine.**

The Stoic philosophers constantly refer to our divinity. For example, you may have read Marcus Aurelius talking about God or the Divine in us. Consider this famous quote from Marcus’ Meditations, and pay attention to the line at the end. 

“When you wake up In the morning, tell yourself: The people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous, and surely. They are like this because they can’t tell good from evil. But I have seen the beauty of good, and the ugliness of evil, and have recognized that the wrongdoer has a nature related to my own- not of the same blood or birth, but the same mind, and possessing a share of the divine.” 

According to Marcus, we “Possess a share of the divine”. But what does this mean? And why is this important for how we live?

Marcus says this because God is literally in us. 

In the lesson where we discussed the Stoic idea of God. We defined that idea as the "breath" that animates and orders all things. This force is within us. It is the part of ourselves that animates us and makes us distinctly human – namely our reason.

However, the Stoics thought of God as in all things not just humans. It is the breath of the universe. This breath is made of fire and air, but it changes form depending on the ratio of fire and air. The more fire, the more divine the breath is. 

This creates a hierarchy of things.

At the bottom of the hierarchy, the divine breath is its most basic and air-like. In inanimate objects, like rocks, it gives structure. God literally stops the rock from falling apart.

Then in living but unintelligent objects, like plants, this divine breath is hotter, it has a bit more fire. In this form It provides the powers of movement and growth.

In intelligent creatures who are not self-aware, like animals and young children, this divine breath takes the form of a soul and allows for basic reasoning and reactions to the world around them.

But in adult humans, it reaches its highest and most pure form. It has the most fire of any divine breath in the universe. This provides the ability to reflect and respond to the world around us. 

This is why Epictetus says when a student asks if animals are also works of God (2.8.10-12):

“Indeed they are, but they’re not of primary value, nor are they portions of the divine. But you for your part are of primary value; you’re a fragment of God.”

Epictetus literally means that we as humans, have inside us the portion of God, the divine breath, in its highest form in the known universe. This is why Marcus says we ‘share a portion of the divine’ with all other humans.

We have a special status and a special responsibility. This divine breath gives us an ability that separates us from the rest of the universe. That is the ability to reflect and to make decisions based on that reflection. 

Humans alone can separate our first impressions of the world from our final decisions. We can reflect on what we feel initially, and decide if we think it is true or not. We can separate how things seem to us initially, from what we take to be the truth. 

This is self-awareness. The highest form of reason in the universe according to the Stoics.

God has given us alone the ability to reflect on our beliefs and impressions and actually participate in shaping our character and future.

Our ethical job is to live up to that divine potential. To use our special ability to make the right choices, and act as well as we can act.

### **Living in accordance with nature.**

The Stoics place the highest ethical goal as living in accordance with nature.

We can’t live in accordance with nature, without understanding nature.

As Epictetus said:

“Education has no goal more important than bringing our preconception of what is reasonable and unreasonable in alignment with nature.” 

id: 601

Here is another quote from Epictetus on this topic. When asked what we should do in life, he says: 

“Make the best use of what is in our power, and treat the rest in accordance with its nature.” 

id: 596 

But what is this nature we are supposed to be in alignment with, and how do we live in accordance with it?

In one sense, Epictetus is talking about human nature. We know our essential nature as humans and we should not try to be different than we are. We shouldn’t neglect our rational abilities, nor act in an anti-social way. This is to live in alignment with our human nature. But the Stoics mean nature in a broader sense too.

The Stoics also want to live in accordance with the nature of the universe more broadly and by association, with the nature of God. 

If God is responsible for fate, then we live out of accordance with nature when we want the world to be a different kind of thing than it is. When we lament the things outside of our control or the way the world has to be, we pull ourselves out of alignment with nature.

When we imagine ourselves to be the center of the world, and more important than we really are, we are not thinking in accordance with nature. And for the Stoics when we forget that everything is connected and that we are a part of that connection, we are living out of accordance with nature.

Happiness then comes from living in harmony with our individual nature, and the nature of the broader universe, by achieving knowledge or seeing the truth of the way things really are.

This is why in Stoicism virtue (also translated as human excellence) is knowledge.

One part of knowledge is knowing how to live well on a moment-to-moment, action-to-action basis. How to be courageous kind, and just. This is our individual nature as humans in a community of humans.

But the other part of knowledge is knowing how to play our role and place within a larger universe. Coming to appreciate that role, and to not be surprised or unexpected about the things that come with naturally being human.

The Stoics saw themselves as part of a greater process, created by God. Sometimes this process involves hardships and tragedies. The Stoics think we should align ourselves with this as well, and see the part we have to play in something greater decided by God. 

This is why Epictetus says: “If I in fact knew that illness had been decreed for me at this moment by destiny, I would welcome even that; for the foot, too, if it had understanding, would be eager to get spattered with mud.” 

ID: 714

For Epictetus, the foot doesn’t mind getting muddy if it knows it is contributing to something bigger. We too can find purpose and meaning when our lives get messy expanding our perspective.

Whether or not you believe in a Stoic God, this idea that our lives go better when we live in harmony with the way things are underpins all of Stoic ethics.

To live in accordance with nature, we have to both act according to our nature as humans and according to our place in a world larger than us. 

And this requires keeping both perspectives in mind. The Stoic gives their attention to both the personal and the cosmic.

They care about how they are to their friends and family. They are kind and courageous in their day-to-day life.

But they also recognize their contribution to something greater than them. 

As Epictetus says, we are all actors in a play. The Stoic understands that their role may be a small one, but that does not mean it isn’t crucial.

Regardless of whether you believe in the Stoic god, or just take it as a metaphor, this lesson is an important one. We can’t perform our part well in a play without understanding what the story is about.

By meditating on the Stoic god, the ancient Stoics practiced seeing reality as it is, reminding themselves of what was up to them, and living their life to the fullest.
