### **Introduction to Stoicism \- Theory**

### **Introduction**

Welcome to Stoa. My name is Michael Tremblay. I have a PhD in Stoic philosophy and practice living my life as a Stoic. I am also the co-founder of Stoa. Our goal is to help you live a more Stoic life, by putting Stoicism into practice.

Stoicism is a philosophy aimed at living the best life possible. It teaches us how to focus on what we can control and how to accept what we cannot. 

We can’t control what domhappens to us, but we can control how we respond to it. 

Are you resilient or fragile? Courageous or cowardly? Kind or cruel? These qualities are all up to you. They are all the result of how you respond to situations. 

Stoicism calls our attention to this fact, and then gives you a set of tools to have better responses. 

When combined with practice, this is a roadmap to becoming the kind of person we want to be. Someone that is Courageous, Disciplined, Just, and Wise.

But being Stoic is a lot of work. It requires genuine self-reflection on your own flaws, mindful attention to our behavior, and continued study. 

The Stoic Epictetus compared studying Stoicism to a visiting hospital: when we leave we will be better for it but in pain. 

What Epictetus is saying is that becoming healthy and happy can ironically feel unpleasant. Self-improvement takes work. It involves honestly looking at parts of ourselves we might be embarrassed by, or not like. It requires consistent practice, not momentary inspiration.

Stoa is designed to help you through this journey. 

This introductory series will show you what a Stoic life is like and provide you with some beginning steps to achieve it. 

It will cover happiness, character, and judgment. In Stoicism these ideas are tightly related. Happiness requires good character. Good character is achieved by good thinking. And good thinking is a result of disciplined mindful judgment, combined with a Stoic set of principles to call on. 

Then, with this introductory series complete, it will be your task to use Stoa to apply these lessons in your own life. Stoicism only because valuable when it leaves the world of theory, and affects how we live.

As Marcus Aurelius said “Waste no more time arguing about what a good person should be. Be one.”

### **Happiness**

Most people think their happiness depends on things outside themselves: other people, money, reputation.

These people regret the past, are anxious about the future, and can’t enjoy the present.

They are fragile. And they live like they are fragile.

Some are hypervigilant all the time, always looking for the next thing that might upset them.

Others protect themselves by detaching. They lock their feelings away so they can’t be hurt by others and numb themselves to feel safe.

Stoics are happy because of their own actions, not because of external possessions.  Stoics are self-sufficient. 

Epictetus, the famous Stoic teacher and former slave, once said:

“I must die. Must I then die lamenting? I must be put in chains. Must I then also lament? I must go into exile. Does any man then hinder me from going with smiles and cheerfulness and contentment?”

Like Epictetus heading into exile smiling, the Stoic knows that their happiness does not depend on other people, but how they respond to situations. 

The Stoics’ happiness depends on their character.

Because of this, the Stoic is calm when life changes. If you think Epictetus’ example is far fetched, he provides the story of Agrippinus:

“Once, when \[Agrippinus\] was preparing for lunch, a messenger arrived from Rome announcing that Nero had sentenced him to exile. Unflustered he replied, ‘Then why don’t we just move our lunch to Aricia.’”

That is a Stoic response to exile: a total focus on what can be done in the moment and an acceptance of what is outside of one’s control. 

Sometimes people misinterpret this to mean that Stoics are callous or self-centered. This is not the case.

As Epictetus says: 

“I should not be unfeeling like a statue; I should care for my relationships both natural and acquired – as a child, a sibling, a parent, a citizen.”

The Stoics loves other people and cares deeply about their relationships. They are grateful for what they have. But they also acknowledge it is temporary.

The Stoic knows it is not in their control to keep anything the same, and so they are prepared when things change.

Zeno, the founder of Stoicism, defined Stoic happiness as a “Smooth flow of life achieved by living in accordance with the nature of things.” 

You can think of Stoic happiness like someone surfing the waves of an ocean. They don’t try to fight the current, or go against the water. But when they see an opportunity to ride a wave, they seize it. 

Because of this a Stoic’s happiness is asymmetrical. They have access to the joys of life, but limited exposure to the downsides of jealousy, anger, or grief. These come when we try to fight the way things are, when we go against the current.

Try today, to accept something for what it is. Maybe that is a difficult personality in your life, or something you regret in the past. Try to pick something in your life that is outside of your control, and ride with it, instead of against it. This does not mean accept or glorify it, but try to find the opportunity in it, like a surfer on a wave. 

A surfer on a wave is an example of a smooth flowing life. And life surfing, this smooth flow is not easy. The Stoics argue that it comes from perfecting our character with effort and time. Epictetus once asked his student:

“What is the goal of virtue, after all, except a life that flows smoothly?”

This is what Stoicism offers us. A smooth life achieved by good character.

### **Character**

It is not very common to talk today about things like “Character” or “virtue”. 

Your character is who you fundamentally are. It is how you act in a situation. It is how you choose when faced with a dilemma or trade-off.

This is one of those things that is up to us. As the Roman Stoic Seneca said:

Fortune has no jurisdiction over character.

If character is the choices we make, Virtue is the quality of those choices. Was this choice the right thing to do? Did it take into account other people? Was the intensity of your response proportional to the severity of the situation?

Stoicism teaches that a consistent, virtuous character leads to happiness, a smoothly flowing life. 

But, to the surprise of no one, we all have some work to do to improve our character.

Some people hold their values too lightly, and change who they are to get what they want out of the situation.Such a person lies when they want something, but complains about how unfair it is when they are lied to.

Some people mean well, but are too easily influenced by their surroundings. Like the impressionable friend who succumbs to peer pressure only to regret it after.

Others have a character that is consistent, but bad. There is no moral relativism in Stoicism. Stoics have no issue saying some characters are worse than others.

An example of a poor character would be one that neglects either others or themselves. For example, people who are unnecessarily cruel to others or those who allow themselves to be the punching bag.

The Stoic aims for a consistent, good character.

They cannot be bullied into acting differently. They understand their roles and obligations to other people, in their family and community. And they are kind without being a pushover. A Stoic is always firm but never cruel.

A character like this is also in reach of everyone, the Stoics argued.

Part of what makes Stoicism so special is that it views character as a craft, or a skill.

Just like we become better painters or athletes through practice, we transform ourselves into better Stoics through effort and training.

The Stoic looks at building themselves like a craftsman.

This is why Seneca thinks we should all have a model we strive for. He says:

“Choose therefore a Cato; or, if Cato seems too severe a model, choose some Laelius, a gentler spirit…For we must indeed have someone according to whom we may regulate our characters; you can never straighten that which is crooked unless you use a ruler.”

There are better and worse ways to be, and so we should pick a role-model, or an example to strive towards, just like an athlete admires the legends of their sport. 

Except for a Stoic, these are character models. 

Something you can do today in five minutes to improve your character is to pick one character model. Someone you admire and want to be like. This will give you a target to aim for in everything you do. 

This is called the ‘contemplation of the sage’ exercise. And is one of the exercises we practice throughout Stoa’s meditation section.

The Stoic puts the responsibility for happiness on themselves. A smooth life is achieved by a good character, one that is just, courageous, wise and self-controlled.

And a good character is something built overtime, like any other skill.

This is the promise of Stoicism. An improved life in exchange for effort.

### **Judgment**

We all know the phrase “you are what you eat”. This is an argument about input and output. If we put garbage in and eat poorly, then your physical health will suffer.

The Stoic phrase might be “you are what you think”. As Roman Emperor and Marcus Aurelius said:

The happiness of your life depends upon the quality of your thoughts; therefore guard accordingly.

The Stoics thought that a consistent character is about how we think. How we act depends on how we judge a situation.

Just like there are better or worse ways to act, there are better and worse ways to think.

Many people are poor at thinking. We can see this in how people respond to their first impressions of situations.

Bad thinking is undisciplined and reactive thinking. It is when we judge quickly and rashly, without careful consideration. 

It is also stubborn. Poor thinking does not want to change, even when new evidence is introduced.

Finally, bad thinking is non-reflective., and does not have a critical standard to evaluate things itself.

But it is not a surprise we sometimes think poorly. All these aspects of bad thinking share something in common: they are easy. They do not require effort or intention. They are almost a human default.

The Stoic combats this with Stoic mindfulness. 

A Stoic judges slowly, carefully, and mindfully. They check that their first impression is true before making decisions.

A Stoic judges in good faith. This means that they will change their mind when presented with contrary evidence. Thinking is a tool to understand the world as it really is, not a weapon to argue for what you already believe.

This is why Marcus Aurelius, the most powerful man on earth at the time, said:

“If anyone can refute me—show me I’m making a mistake or looking at things from the wrong perspective—I’ll gladly change. It’s the truth I’m after, and the truth never harmed anyone.”

Imagine that humility as Emperor. That is Stoic thinking. 

a Stoic thinks according to the principles of Stoicism. That’s what makes them a Stoic, not just a disciplined thinker. 

This means that they use Stoic arguments to help make decisions. For example, when judging if something is worth an extreme reaction, they ask if it is up to them or not.

Something you can try today, to improve your judgment, is to practice pausing between your first impression of a situation and your action. Take a moment to see what impression your mind has produced, and recognize that you do not have to go along with it. You can choose to agree or disagree with your first impressions, but only if you see them for what they are: they are *first* impressions, not your last judgment.

This ability to pause and reflect, instead of reacting, is one of the core skills built with a mindfulness practice. Stoa’s meditation exercises are designed to help you build this skill, not just to have a good meditation session, but to apply it out in the chaos of your life.

At this point, it should be clear how happiness, character, and judgment are related in Stoicism.

Happiness requires good character. Good character is achieved by good thinking. And good thinking is a result of disciplined mindful judgment, combined with a Stoic set of principles to call on. 

This is how Stoicism builds on itself to produce a good life. 

Through a combination of mindfulness practice and Stoic theory, the Stoa App is designed to be your guide in building those skills. 

The final piece is to commit yourself to action. As Marcus Aurelius said:

“You have to assemble your life yourself, action by action.”

Time to start building yourself into the person you want to be.
