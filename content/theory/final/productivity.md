## Choosing the destination

When you imagine a productive person, who do you see?

You may picture someone who is always on. An ambitious professional or athlete. Someone who works harder and smarter than the rest – and whose work pays off in outsized rewards. A pantheon of entrepreneurs, athletes, statesmen, artists, builders, and warriors may flash across your mind.

Stoicism can take you to heroic levels of productivity.

Epictetus encourages his students to take their life seriously, like an Olympic athlete:

"When faced with anything painful or pleasurable, anything bringing glory or disrepute, realize that the crisis is now, that the Olympics have started, and waiting is no longer an option; that the chance for progress, to keep or lose, turns on the events of a single day."  
id: 845

At its simplest productivity is doing more with less. Productive people go further faster. The more productive one is in a field, the better chance you have of making it to Olympic levels of performance in record time.

The psychological tools and teachings of the ancient Stoics can help you achieve that.

But before doing that – Stoicism throws a life changing question at us: are you on the right path?

Productivity can take you further faster – but are you headed towards a meaningful destination?

The essential question that any project of self improvement must ask is where are you going? As Seneca says:

"If a man knows not which port he sails, no wind is favorable."  
id: 1779

Before seeking efficiency, it’s essential to ask what all this efficiency is for.

For Stoics, productivity is an essential skill. They praise the traits of relentlessness, resourcefulness, good planning, and disciplined action. Each of these are an aspect of a life well lived. 

What’s essential is that each of these skills is pointed towards meaningful ends.

Excellent work for the sake of happiness – that’s what matters.

Productivity is not a goal in and of itself. It must serve a greater purpose. Stoic productivity is doing the right thing with less.

Consider the shallow man who finds success but whose riches and fame are soon forgotte. Contrast him with the disciplined artist whose beautiful work survives long after they are gone. Or think about the woman who thrives in a cutthroat business but ruins both her friendships and her competitors. Compare her to the happy small business owner who earns less but enjoys a stellar reputation in her own community.

When you picture a productive Stoic, imagine people who are excellent and happy. Their productivity flows from their character. It’s a part of who they are – not inauthentic striving. They work well with others in the best sense: contributing to their communities and cities. If they have the fortune to be able to reflect on their life towards its end, they do so with the satisfaction that they did their best. Without regret or remorse.

So, who would you rather be? Most people want to succeed – but not at the cost of sacrificing their character. To do that, you must ensure you’re headed in the right direction.

There’s no question that Stoics have been productive throughout history. But they’re most productive when they’ve found their craft and intentionally set their purpose. When you seek to get somewhere faster, smarter, and better, ensure you’re on the right path.

## Emulate successful people 

If Stoic productivity is about doing the right things with less – how do you determine what’s right. In other words, how do you determine what your purpose is?

Other people provide us with answers for the questions philosophy brings.

The Stoics taught that we all possess an innate sense of what is good and what is bad. We use this when we decide who is admirable and worthy of emulation.

This is why the ancient traditions encourage us to find heroes and become like them. This is an excellent approach, one whose effectiveness cannot be underrated.

We see peoples’ philosophy in their actions. Ultimately, this is what matters. 

Seneca advised his readers to use role models as guides:

“Choose therefore a Cato; or, if Cato seems too severe a model, choose some Laelius, a gentler spirit. Choose a master whose life, conversation, and soul-expressing face have satisfied you; picture him always to yourself as your protector or your pattern. For we must indeed have someone according to whom we may regulate our characters; you can never straighten that which is crooked unless you use a ruler.”  
Seneca *Moral Letters* 11

To get the most out of this practice, it’s important to understand its practical, motivational, and ethical power.

The practical reason to find role models is that they help us achieve our goals.

If you want to excel at a craft, find the people who already have. Don’t necessarily go to the busy big name person. Find someone who you can talk to and understand. If you’re a beginner, choose the person who has been at your craft for three years, not thirty. Then learn from their example.

But you must choose who you admire carefully. Some gurus who appear skilled are not. Other teachers may be excellent – but if they’re not teaching what you need to know they will not be the best for you.

Find people you can learn from. People who are on the same path as you, but slightly further ahead. And then learn from their excellent decisions and mistakes.

The motivational reason to emulate successful people is obvious. Because humans are social creatures, the example of another life fires us up.

Whenever you doubt achievement is possible – bring to mind the examples of so many people like you who did great and wonderful things. Whenever you encounter obstacles and setbacks summon memories of others – humans just like you – who experienced the same things and persevered. 

The ethical reason is perhaps the most important. As Seneca says:

“You can never straighten that which is crooked unless you use a ruler.”

The best role models guide us through life. They don’t need to be perfect, but they show us what’s possible and how we can fully develop ourselves. 

So you must choose what you admire carefully. When you get close to a hero you may learn that you don’t really want to become like them. They are not happy. You may leave admiring their skill, but not their craft.

The first skill of Stoic productivity is choosing what to work on. Select a craft worthy of your time. 

Living philosophically puts a question mark next to many of your big decisions. What should you do with your life? Who should you be?

You can make progress by looking at examples of the people – past and present – who have lived well. Let them provide something for you to measure up to.

## Praemeditatio Malorum

A Stoic doesn’t view the world as they want it to be, but as it truly is. And the truth is, things often don’t go the way we want them to.

Whatever plans, goals, or timelines you have – the larger world doesn’t care. It will dash them against the rocks.

Stoics take pride in not shrinking away from this reality. Indeed, they actively practice negative visualization.

They called this practice praemeditatio malorum – or the premeditation of evils.

Let’s discuss why you’d do this practice and then how to get the most out of it. 

The Stoic visualizes ways things can go wrong for three key reasons. 

First, to improve their plans. If you bring to mind ways things may go wrong, you adjust your plans so that you are more likely to succeed. 

The best way to do this is to imagine the world where you fail. Then determine the most likely reason things didn’t go the way you want. Ask, “If I fail, what is most likely to have stopped me?"

This exercise will bring to mind the most realistic and important obstacles. It helps you make contact with reality. 

Too often we overcommit. Some plans will go awry simply because they are unrealistic. By confronting this fact we can improve our thinking. Like an athlete visualizing different moves of their opponent, we can form a game plan and prepare, to the extent that we can, for the curveballs life will throw at us.

As Seneca counsels:

"In the meantime, cling tooth and nail to the following rule: not to give in to adversity, not to trust prosperity, and always take full note of fortune’s habit of behaving just as she pleases."  
id: 496

Part of this process involves coming to terms with the fact that we cannot know every possible obstacle. Yet there’s something crucial that we can capture just by thinking about ways things can go awry. A quip goes “plans are useless, but planning is indispensable.”

Second, by bringing to mind ways things go wrong we can handle it when they do. Often anticipation is worse than the event itself. To combat this, bring to mind whatever you fear. Imagine that it comes to pass. And then visualize yourself handling it with skill.

One of the most powerful Stoic quotes is Seneca reminding us:

"We suffer more often in imagination than in reality."  
id: 366

Third, and this is the most important point, one where Stoicism can take you further than many other schools of thought. By bringing to mind evils you can become invincible to them. 

Through this practice you can internalize the idea that things in themselves cannot hurt you. Only your opinion of them can. 

In other words, by practicing praemeditatio malorum well you will internalize Stoic value theory. This provides a new meaning to your productive endeavors. Setbacks and obstacles are not evil. What matters is whether you perform your task well. To see this in action, consider three business owners. 

The first is unrealistically optimistic. They never consider the fact that there could be a significant market downturn. As such, when the economy tightens up, they are not prepared psychologically or strategically for that reality.

The second one is too pessimistic. They constantly worry that a downturn will crush their business. When the downturn happens, they immediately move into crisis management. They react to the situation. 

The last one is always focused on pushing their business forward. In prosperity or poverty. When the market takes a dip down, they are not surprised – that’s something that markets do. Instead, they immediately realize what they must do. They take advantage of the moment.

By seeing things like the last entrepreneur, you can bring the focus back to what is up to you. Acting with excellence and good character. Making good decisions. That’s all.

Fortune will come and go. Setbacks will happen. They always do. But they will not be final. Wherever you are, you can bring to mind your task and do your best to see it through. That’s all you're called to do.

So, when you visualize bad things happening, remember these three questions. Remember these three questions:

* How can I make it less likely that this occurs?  
* How can I act skillfully if it does occur?  
* Whatever happens, what is my purpose?

The answer to this last question unlocks the rest. 

From time to time, practice praemeditatio malorum. Start with realistic, but smaller challenges. Then build to greater ones. Be creative, but realistic. And keep your focus on what matters.

## Critical Assent

In one of the final lessons from our live courses, a student shared that this simple Stoic technique made him more productive and happier at work than anything else he tried.

It’s called critical assent. 

Fundamentally it’s simple: pay attention and don’t agree to anything that is false.

To understand it more deeply, we need to know the Stoic theory of the mind.

The Stoics believed that we all receive impressions from the world. 

When our manager at work says something – we immediately take in the content of their words, the tone, and more. 

Then we reflect on what was said. At this stage, we bring to mind how we can interpret the manager's statement. How does it align with our past experience?

Finally, we decide whether we assent to the impression. Is it true that the manager means what I think they mean?

After making our decision, we act.

This process – of impression, reflection, assent, and action – is instantaneous and ongoing. 

After interpreting the meaning of our manager's statement we may immediately move to other topics, like “My manager is speaking out of line” or “My manager is confused” or “I am doing a bad job” – we must then decide what to do with these impressions.

Unfortunately, many people simply react to their impressions. They don’t spend much time reflecting. They let their decisions be governed by their feelings or other people. Even more of us go on autopilot. We receive impressions – but barely notice them. We sleepwalk through life.

These ways of life are simply not productive. 

If we’re not paying attention, we won’t learn as much. If we’re always reacting, we’ll make worse decisions.

Critical assent involves paying attention to our impressions at every moment. And then critically deciding whether we should assent to them.

This opens up a space to ignore distractions, think through what to do strategically, and then commit to getting it done well.

With critical assent, our student said that he was able to find the zone more easily. Interactions with others went more smoothly. He was able to focus for longer periods of time. Simply by reminding himself, again and again to notice and then critically assent to every impression.

He was following in the footsteps of the Roman Epictetus’s students. Epictetus told his pupils to:

"Make a practice at once of saying to every strong impression: ‘An impression is all you are, not the source of the impression.’ Then test and assess it with your criteria, but one primarily: ask, ‘Is this something that is, or is not, in my control?’"  
id: 808

The first step to this practice is to simply notice what is happening in your mind. At any moment, cultivate the awareness to know what is happening. 

Then move to reflection and assent. Do not go along with whatever feels better or give into peer pressure. Carefully and critically decide to act on what is true.

If your manager is acting out of line, you may be tempted to make excuses for their behavior if that’s what everyone else is doing. Peer pressure may distort the way you see the world and, if you are not careful, cause you to see things in an incorrect way.

Take another example. You may say you’re not good at noticing things around you because you’re not a “details person.” But is that story really true? This is where critical assent comes in. Look at reality. Don’t blindly accept narratives from others or even yourself.

Surprisingly, when done right, critical assent can be calming. If we carefully notice and pay attention to whatever is arising we avoid moving our attention from one thing to the next. That process of constant external stimulation is fatiguing. Critical assent brings us to the present and cultivates our focus. Tranquility can be found in this practice.

As Epictetus says, the best way to get going with this practice is to apply a principle to your impressions. Namely, if you’re judging something as good or bad, ask, is this up to me? What is up to me here?

At work, you cannot manage the behavior of others, customers, clients, whatever it is. You can control your own behavior. You can show your skill and expertise – and how quickly you learn.

## Productivity is indifferent

For the Stoics productivity is indifferent. 

Is it always better to do more with less? It depends on what you’re doing.

As Seneca said:

"If a man knows not which port he sails, no wind is favorable."  
id: 1779

Let’s understand the idea of indifferents more deeply.

To understand it, it’s essential to understand Stoic views on the good.

The Stoics saw the good as the guiding north star to our lives. The thing that always provides direction. Something that, if we see it, we’re motivated to follow it.

The ancients were not satisfied with something that sometimes worked and other times didn’t  – they sought the unconditionally good. 

For this reason, they settled on virtue. No matter the situation, acting with virtue is good. 

You cannot say the same about other things we spend so much time pursuing: fame, reputation, relationships, pleasure. These things are not unconditionally good. For the Stoics only virtue is. An excellent character is worth having, no matter the circumstances.

They called everything else indifferents. Some of these indifferents are preferred. That means they are naturally worth pursuing, but not all the time. Think of health. In general, it’s good to be healthy. But sometimes we need to put ourselves in danger and take risks. Health isn’t worth maximizing at the expense of our relationships, duties, or personal excellence. A long life isn’t necessarily a good one.

The Stoics were fond of saying:

“It is not things in themselves that harm us, but our opinion of them.”

We could also say:

“It is not things in themselves that benefit us, but how we use them.”

What’s ultimately bad is vice and what’s ultimately good is virtue.

Think about a game or competition. What’s good is being an excellent player and doing your best to win. That’s the ultimate goal. Every game will have techniques that are typically good things to do, but most games, especially complicated and sophisticated ones, do not have strategies that work 100% of the time. Some strategies are preferred – they are good rules of thumb – but in sophisticated games no strategy will work 100% of the time.

The technique and strategy you choose depend on the circumstances, your opponent, and who you are. How you deploy your chosen techniques and stratagems will determine how good of a player you are. 

In life, virtue provides that ultimate goal. How you use indifferents is what makes you virtuous. 

Whenever you're playing a game, what matters is winning. And in complicated games that are like life, there’s no single strategy that works in every situation. You need to know the best practices and strategies – and when to break them. The same principle applies to indifferents like reputation, health, beauty, pleasure, and wealth – each of these things are worth pursuing in some situations, and some ways, but not others. 

Stoics build the skill to choose indifferents well. They know when to pursue a good reputation – and when to sacrifice their fame for something more important. 

So, let’s circle back to productivity. 

For the Stoics, efficiency in itself doesn’t matter. What matters is the end you are productive *for*. A productive person who lacks discipline, wisdom, and courage does not live a good life. 

Someone who does the right things with less – who exhibits self control, intelligence, and bravery in their everyday – that person is admirable.

So, as you become more and more productive the question is – what will you use efficiency for?

## Summoning Motivation

A common problem people have with productivity is that they lack motivation. 

You want to write, go to your desk. But there’s no inspiration. You leave.

You want to work out, but the very thought fills you with fatigue. There’s no energy there. So, you skip it. Just this once.

We all know the feeling.

It’s unavoidable.

Even the Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius had to remind himself to get up in the morning, writing that his nature wasn’t to sleep like an animal, but to live like a human. 

The Stoics recognized this and offered strategies to turn to in these moments. You can summon motivation.

The key is to reframe the issue.

For the Stoics, what motivates action is our values and who we are.

We can use rewards and change the environment. These are important techniques that many productivity experts talk about. But we can go deeper with philosophy.

If you’re not motivated to do something rather simple – the Stoic may ask does it matter at all? 

The point of the question is to get at your values. If you want to write, why do you desire to write at all? Is it important? Have you fully internalized its importance? Do you want to create great books – or be seen as writer?

Sometimes the lack of motivation is a sign that whatever you want to do, simply doesn’t matter. It’s an expectation put on yourself for no reason.

This can be illuminating – but it doesn’t solve the issue when we aren’t motivated to do something that is meaningful.

This is what Marcus Aurelius is facing when he wants to stay in bed – instead of to do the most important job in the world at that time. 

At this crucial point, he reminds himself of his values. Who does he want to be?

He writes:

In the morning when you rise unwillingly, let this thought be present- I am rising to the work of a human being. Why then am I dissatisfied if I am going to do the things for which I exist and for which I was brought into the world?  
id: ?

Sometimes, something should be done, no matter how you feel.

When you’re in those moments, bring to mind who you want to be.

Contrast the world where you follow through with the one where you fail. At first, both are possible. Make the world where you fail an impossibility. Use the contrast between success and laziness as fuel. The essential point is to keep in mind your values and who you want to be. This is what we see Marcus Aurelius do over and over again in his Meditations. By historical accounts of his reign as emperor, it worked. 

Remember that the point is to build habits. The ancient philosophers didn’t think about ethics in terms of single decisions. Instead, they saw the project of living as a long term one where you build character. 

As Epictetus says:

"If you wish to be a writer, write."  
id: 290

Bring to mind urgency. You do not live forever. You will only have this chance once.

Start small. Get out of the covers, wake up, and live.

## Build habits

One of the largest shifts the Stoics force us to make is to think not in terms of actions, but in terms of character.

If you’re facing a decision you can evaluate it by asking the question:

“Are the outcomes of this decision good or bad?”

Or by asking:

“Do I prefer to be the person who chooses this path or avoids it?”

For example, you might consider a choice between making more money doing something you despise or doing something you love for less..

There are different ways to approach this decision.

On one hand, you can bring to mind the pros and cons of each option. Or you can simply ask: who do I want to be? The person who does what they hate for money? Or the person who sticks to what they love?

This framing calls us to focus on who we are becoming in every moment. Who we are is shown over time – in other words by our character. And character is nothing more than habit. 

In one sense, this means we shouldn’t focus too much on today. Don’t worry that you didn’t get everything done. What matters is creating a life where you are a productive person. That requires time, learning, and practice.

On the other hand, today becomes even more important. What we decide to do today will build who we become tomorrow. Create excellent habits now.

And don’t break them. When you do you’re not just wasting a day – you’re interrupting a lifetime of work.

In other words, productivity isn’t just about one off efficiency for a day. It’s about becoming someone. Someone who will do their best to get the right things done, no matter the situation. Why? Because it is a part of their character. Who they are.

Day by day, measure your progress. Track your ability to follow through on things. Take note of your capacity to get more done with less. Daily decisions add up to a character you’ll take with you for years. Make today count and tomorrow will be even better.

Don’t waste the day – otherwise you risk postponing tomorrow too.

As Epictetus says:

"If you like doing something, do it regularly; if you don’t like doing something, make a habit of doing something different. The same goes for moral inclinations. When you get angry, you should know that you aren’t guilty of an isolated lapse, you’ve encouraged a trend and thrown fuel on the fire."

id: 683

## Success Spirals

If you succeed at one task, you'll increase the expectation that you'll succeed at the next one. 

Take advantage of success spirals – build on one success after another.

Winning gives us energy. It makes it easier to make further progress.

You can see this in athletic matches. Victors become even more energized as the match goes their way, the losers become more and more defeated as their loss becomes a reality.

To take advantage of success spirals, you need to ensure that your goals are specific and realistic. 

Choose something that you know you’ll do. Then do it. Build more difficult tasks on top of it. 

Remember, you don’t necessarily need to complete a single task in one go. What matters is that you set a specific and achievable aim. It can be as simple as spending 5 focused minutes on something you’ve been putting off. Once you’ve done that move to another task. Come back to that thing you’re putting off and finish it later – action by action.

We can link this strategy to Stoic theory of mind. Remember what drives action for the Stoics are our beliefs – how we see and respond to the world. By getting things done you are giving yourself evidence that you are productive. You’re building confidence – the expectation of future success. That’s why this strategy works.

The ancient Stoics talked about this in terms of emotional change. Here’s Epictetus:

“If you would not be of an angry temper, then, do not feed the habit. Give it nothing to help its increase. Be quiet at first and reckon the days in which you have not been angry. I used to be angry every day; now every other day; then every third and fourth day; and if you miss it so long as thirty days, offer a sacrifice of thanksgiving to God.”

Epictetus *Discourses* 2.18

Want to break a habit? Start small – a single hour, day, whatever it is. Then build on that success.

When you do this, ensure you’re going directly towards your goal. 

The point isn’t to define fake tasks you know you can check off. Some people waste time answering emails, researching what they should be doing, even reading about productivity – doing everything they can to avoid taking on that difficult task that must be done.

The point is to get everything done that needs to be done, and make concrete progress doing so. That last point is essential. You can break down tasks and make them easier – as long as you are making concrete progress.

There’s an opposite practical strategy that’s worth trying here too – doing the most difficult thing first. This can work for some people. It’s worth trying. Many struggle with it, because taking on the most difficult task at the beginning of the day can be intimidating. Finishing that main thing can be freeing. Further it also provides you with an immense amount of evidence: when you’re successful you get the difficult things done first.

Try both of these strategies. When you break down tasks, you can weave them together.

Let’s end with some motivation from Epictetus:

"If you come up against anything that requires an effort, or is pleasant, or is glorious or inglorious, remember that this is the time of the contest, that the Olympic Games have now arrived, and there is no possibility of further delay, and that it depends on a single day and single action whether progress is to be lost or secured."  
id: 1867

## Cultivate self-respect

Trusting and respecting yourself is a superpower.

To cultivate this respect, the Stoic philosopher, Epictetus, sometimes shamed his students.

This may be surprising because shame is a negative passion. 

But Epictetus argues that the human ability to feel shame was unique – and that there was a special ability here:

“And what are we by nature? We are free, honorable, and self-respecting. After all, does any other animal blush or feel shame?”

Discourses, 3.7.27

Essentially, when he shamed his students, he was reminding them that they could be better.

You can see this in action when Epictetus accosts his students for showing off their book learning, without embodying Stoicism in their actions:

“But if what I prize is just the interpreting itself, haven’t I ended up as a literary critic rather than a philosopher, with the only difference being that I explicate Chrysippus rather than Homer? So when someone asks me to expound some passage of Chrysippus, I blush at my inability to show him that my actions reflect and are consistent with the words I’m reading.”

Handbook, 49

Epictetus shows his students that their self-image does not line up with reality. This should motivate them to be better. forming and reinforcing our self-image is a part of the project of shaping our character. 

The Stoics see shame as the fear of disgrace. When we’re ashamed we want to hide what we've done from ourselves and others. The fear of public humiliation keeps many from acting virtuously. It has prevented many from living the life they want to lead.

And yet the judgment of others is not up to us. It’s not what ultimately matters.

So, there’s an unhealthy aspect of shame – one that keeps people from living freely and honestly. Life isn't about chasing applause or running from embarrassment. Whether or not we are praised or humiliated by others is external. So, we cannot let it determine how we live.

But shame can also be healthy, when it helps you cultivate self-respect. I won't betray my friend if I know I am the kind of person who is above betrayal. I won't miss a deadline if I see myself as someone who turns in work on time. 

Many are dependent on others' approval. But self-respecting people are free. They set the standards for their own behavior and depend only on their self-image.

When you fail to live up to your standards, some amount of embarrassment is appropriate. Use that as fuel to act better next time. 

There’s a story about the philosopher Diogenes – Diogenes wandered through Athens with a lamp. When asked why he went about with a lamp in broad daylight, Diogenes said, "I am looking for an honest man." Seeing a young man blush, he remarked that it was what virtue looked like.

The young man’s embarrassment is the beginning of self-transformation and respect.

Let the discomfort of not meeting your standards guide you, not guilt you, towards embodying a better self.

## Be direct

One of the key problems of productivity literature is that it is a distraction.

We should distinguish between training and performance. Performance is getting things done – in your personal life, work, or projects. Training involves getting better at getting things done. Reading or listening to productivity can be a part of good training. But at some point, you need to perform.

Don’t ever let training be an obstacle to performance. At some point we need to put away our books, podcasts, courses – and get what needs to be done, done.

Further, training itself must be directly tied to your craft. So many different kinds of training and practice are nothing like the real thing. The best kinds of training demand specific practice, active involvement, and repetition. They aren’t always fun.

Living philosophically doesn’t mean reciting famous philosophers from memory or showing off your bookshelf. 

Epictetus captures this perfectly in his dialogue with a student who has mastered a fancy philosophical treatise called “The Treatise on the Active Powers”:

“If I were talking to an athlete, I should say, ‘Show me your shoulders’; and then he might say, ‘Here are my dumbbells.’

I would reply, ‘I wish to see the effect of your dumbbells.’

So, when you say: ‘Take the treatise on the active powers, and see how I have studied it.	‘

I reply, ‘Slave, I am not inquiring about this, but how you exercise pursuit and avoidance, desire and aversion, how you design and purpose and prepare yourself, whether conformably to nature or not.’”  
id: ?

What matters is an athlete's strength, not his equipment. What matters for a philosopher is his thoughts and decisions, not books or academic learning.

The good news is, discovering what to do is often simple. When a student asked about becoming an Olympic wrestler, Epictetus answered:

"So you wish to conquer in the Olympic Games, my friend? And I, too... But first mark the conditions and the consequences. You will have to put yourself under discipline; to eat by rule, to avoid cakes and sweetmeats; to take exercise at the appointed hour whether you like it or not, in cold and heat; to abstain from cold drinks and wine at your will. Then, in the conflict itself you are likely enough to dislocate your wrist or twist your ankle, to swallow a great deal of dust, to be severely thrashed, and after all of these things, to be defeated."  
id: 309

The point? No nonsense. Go directly to the goal. To be an Olympic champion, do what a champion does.

He also says:

"If you wish to be a writer, write."  
id: 290

Ask who you would be and then determine how to be that person. 

If you need more information, seek it. When you have enough information, act. Go directly to the goal.

There’s nothing more to it.

## Use urgency

Marcus Aurelius once said:

"Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good."  
id: 915

The essence of productivity is doing more with less and ensuring that our work is meaningful.

Because our lives are finite, it’s an essential art. As each moment passes, we have less and less time.

As the Roman poet Horace said:

"Life ebbs as I speak – so seize each day, and grant the next no credit."  
id: 1877

Remember this when you’re stuck. If you think you’re wasting time bring to mind the same words the Roman emperor did:

"In everything that you do, pause and ask yourself if death is a dreadful thing because it deprives you of this."  
id: 230

You can’t realistically live each day as if it were your last. But you must face the reality that time is finite. Synthesize the perspective of today with the reality of an uncertain future.

## Invert Your attitudes

The most productive people have an inversion of attitudes: what others see as unpleasant they see as fun. Successful athletes like getting up at 4am. In so many endeavors people don’t like to do the basics or the fundamentals. They want to do the flashy things, find the shortcuts. However, if you find what makes people uncomfortable, you may do well to learn to love it.

Remember the Stoic maxim:

“Men are disturbed not by things, but by the views which they take of things.”   
Epictetus, Handbook 2

If you want to become great – at work, your craft, relationships – do the things required. Follow Epictetus’s words:

"When trouble comes, think of yourself as a wrestler whom God, like a trainer, has paired with a tough young fighter. For what purpose? To turn you into Olympic-class material. But this is going to take some sweat to accomplish."  
id: 1816

For the things you must do, go above and beyond. Do what would make others uncomfortable. Whether that means starting up earlier, mastering boring details, listening deeply to everyone you meet – whatever it is.

And learn to love that part of life. If it’s truly what you’re called to do and is meaningful work, it is worth embracing. 

There’s a wonder and joy to be found by pushing the limits of your own capacities. Develop your talents as much as they can be. You may surprise yourself. 

Embrace the difficulty. Learn to love it. And live well.

Let’s end with a powerful line from the philosopher Nietzsche:

"My formula for what is great in mankind is amor fati: not to wish for anything other than that which is; whether behind, ahead, or for all eternity. Not just to put up with the inevitable--much less to hide it from oneself, for all idealism is lying to oneself in the face of the necessary-but to love it."
