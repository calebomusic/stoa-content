# Stoic Fundamentals: 3 Disciplines

## Discipline of Judgement

Stoic philosophy centers around 3 disciplines: the discipline of judgement, desire, and action. 

Think of the three disciplines as teaching us how to think, want, and act.

The Stoic discipline of judgment is about how to think.

The philosopher Marcus Aurelius once wrote the following reminder to himself:

If you are distressed by anything external, the pain is not due to the thing itself, but to your estimate of it; and this you have the power to revoke at any moment.

This gets at a key idea of the discipline: focusing on seeing the world as it is, objectively.

Stepping back from the stories and cognitive distortions that fill our minds, and simply focusing on what is true.

The Stoic teacher Epictetus told the following to his students:

“Your ship is lost. What has happened? Your ship is lost. He was sent to prison. What has happened? He has been sent to prison. But the observation 'he has suffered evil,' is an addition coming from you.”

As we go throughout life, we’re presented with sensations and thoughts. Our habit is to agree with each thought that arises. Indeed, we add our own narratives or the stories of our society out of habit. Epictetus’s students said that going to prison was an evil because that’s what everyone else says and thinks \- but is it?

The discipline of judgment is about pausing before assenting to thought. What we think isn’t necessarily so. Our sensations and experiences are rich sources of information about the world. But they can mislead us.

Why does this matter?

Human minds commonly confuse our beliefs about the world with reality itself. When we become angry our fury colors everything else we perceive. Passionate emotions become like glasses that distort our vision. Other negative thought patterns and distortions can have a similar effect.

Experiencing thoughts like this is normal, it's how we react to them that matters.

The discipline of judgement is about vigilantly checking each thought and only agreeing with the ones that are true. Many of them are not. Indeed, judgements like such and such are awful, bad, or evil cause us suffering \- but these ideas are additions from us.

It's this part of Stoicism that inspired contemporary forms of psychotherapy, like cognitive behavioral therapy or CBT. CBT is one of the most evidence-based therapies \- and it's grounded, in part, on ancient Stoic ideas.

Both approaches share the cognitive model of emotion. This model holds that beliefs determine our emotions. Behind anger, anxiety, joy, and excitement are judgements. When we're angry, we believe others have treated us unfairly. When we experience joy we believe something wonderful has happened. Whatever it is, how we think determines how we feel. And what we think is often shaped by cognitive distortions. By thinking well and catching these distortions, we can see the world as it is and be more resilient.

In Stoa, we practice the discipline of judgement through mindfulness meditation and reflection. Mindfulness meditation gives us the ability to pause before making judgments. As Viktor Frankl said: "Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom." Meditation helps us see and take advantage of that pause. With reflection, we can question our assumptions and judgements. We can identify cognitive distortions and get closer to seeing the world as it is. Tools like Socratic Questioning are a part of this effort.

Let's end with some motivation from Epictetus:

“We ought to exercise ourselves daily against appearances; for these appearances also propose questions to us”

## Discipline of Desire

The French Philosopher Pierre Hadot said that:

“The discipline of desire is about relegating your desire to only what is in your control. Accept what is out of your control and change what you can.”

This discipline of desire is about how to want. 

Our expectations, desires, and dreams shape how we experience life. They determine what causes us satisfaction and what brings misery.

To live well then demands being reflective and intentional about what we want.

The discipline of desire starts with the dichotomy of control: some things are up to us, others are not. The Stoics believed that character is the only thing truly up to us \- nothing else. Everything else is external and not under our direct control.

Yes, we can make some outcomes more likely than others. Preparing for an interview will make it more likely that you’ll get the job. But perhaps the interviewer won’t like you. Perhaps the quality of their day biases their judgement. Eating well and exercising make it more likely that you’ll be physically healthy and live longer. But you ultimately can’t control your genes or your environment. Perhaps the interventions won’t work. Or some other illness will inflict you. Ultimately, these matters are out of our control.

This has radical implications for our lives.

We want things outside of ourselves: good reputation, career success, wealth, and the ability to make a difference.

But these desires are the product of fantasy.

In the end, our reputation isn't up to us. We can't control what others think about us. Nor can we ultimately control career success, our wealth, or the impact we make.

Think of life like a game of poker. So much is out of our hands: the cards we're dealt, who we play with, and whether we win or lose.

What can we control? Our thought and decisions. We can choose to play the cards we’re dealt well. Just as we can be an excellent poker player, we can be an excellent person. Just as excellent poker players lose – so, in some senses, will excellent people. But if you’re able to keep in mind the discipline of desire you can find freedom even in defeat. And, more importantly, you can win in what matters.

The serenity prayer captures this attitude: "“God, grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, Courage to change the things I can, And wisdom to know the difference."

The discipline of desire is about willingly accepting what is out of our control and changing what is, our character. Decisions, values, and judgements determine who we are.

So, what Stoics want, what we focus on, is constructing a virtuous character. Showing courage, self-discipline, justice, and wisdom in our actions. Our role is to make good decisions, let the cards fall where they may.

We can decide to be courageous, just, wise, and kind. And through each decision we define who we are.

There are a number of ways to practice the discipline of desire and make this happen.

Meditate on willingly accepting what is out of your control. 

Contemplate what is truly up to you. 

Reflect on how to live virtuously.

When you catch yourself valuing externals, return to what is under your control.

When you face frustrating decisions, remember the excellent poker player. It’s your job to do and be your best. Once you’ve acted well – let the cards fall where they may.

Create a daily practice of reminding yourself of this. Contemplate it often. And overtime, build the resilience and character you want.

Let's end with a quote from Epictetus: "The more we value things outside our control, the less control we have."

## Discipline of Action

The three disciplines: judgement, desire, and action cover how to think, want, and act. 

The Discipline of action is all about acting virtuously – acting with excellence.

The two previous disciplines are useless if they aren't combined with action.

What does this mean? Acting with excellence is a matter of acting in line with the four Stoic virtues: courage, self-control, justice, and wisdom.

Let’s go through each of these.

Courage falls between the mean of recklessness and cowardice. Courageous acts are prudent. But they require facing our fears. This is no easy task. Yet courage brings deep satisfaction to ourselves and others. 

Self control is all about cultivating strong habits. It's about avoiding overindulging in external goods or internal emotions. Both have serious risks. Overindulging in food or substances carries health risks, overindulging in anger or grief makes it more likely we'll make terrible decisions with long lasting consequences. With self-control we can focus on the other virtues and avoid falling into vice.

Justice is a matter of treating people well. We each play important roles in our communities as friends, coworkers, parents, sons and daughters, citizens \- each of these roles carries with itself proper roles of conduct. A Stoic takes note of these roles and fulfills them to the best of their ability.

Wisdom is a matter of recognizing what is good and acting accordingly. We hone wisdom through experience and reflection.

Each of these four virtues work together. They are all a part of the same art.

At the end of their life, when looking back from their deathbed, many wish that they had acted in line with their values. That they stuck up for what is right and treasured those around them. This is what the discipline of action is all about.

With Stoa, you will reflect on what acting with virtue means for you. And you will devise concrete strategies for how to become more virtuous. The contemplation of the sage exercise is perfect for this. In this exercise, consider how role models would act in your place, what advice they’d give you, or how they would advise you. By doing this often you will clarify your values and be inspired to pursue them.

You can prepare for acting well even when things go wrong, with praemeditatio malorum exercises or negative visualization. Use these exercises to plan for the randomness of life.

Finally, hold yourself to account by reviewing your life with evening reflections. Consider when you acted with virtue and vice. Make a plan for improving. By doing this regularly you will make progress.

By setting up a habit to contemplate and meditate daily you’ll build self-trust. Nassim Taleb said that a modern Stoic transforms desire into undertaking – that’s what the discipline of action is all about. Each action increases your confidence to shape yourself into who you want to be. And with each increase in self-confidence, you can improve even further. Every success compounds itself.

So start small. Surround yourself with books, apps, and people that embody your values.

Follow through with your commitments to read and meditate.

And move the focus of your life to living virtuously. Know yourself and the world. Make excellent decisions. And live well.

Let's end with a line from the Stoic philosopher, Seneca:

“Virtue alone affords everlasting and peace-giving joy; even if some obstacle arises, it is but an intervening cloud, which floats beneath the sun but never prevails against it.”
