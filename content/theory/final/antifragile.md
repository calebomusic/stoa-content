### **Become Antifragile \- Theory**

### **What is it to be Antifragile?:**

When was the last time you felt fragile? Like the stress of life was too much for you, and you were suffering as a result? A time when you became irritable, made mistakes, felt your mood swing. Or maybe you even acted in a way you regret, Lashing out at others.

As Stoics, we don’t want to be fragile people. We want to be calm in the storm, someone other people can turn to in these chaotic moments for assurance, stability, and help.  
The best way to be this kind of person is to be Antifragile.

Antifragility is an idea from Nassim Taleb, author, philosopher, and financial trader. Nassim credits the Roman Stoics as, if not originators of the idea, then those who best-embodied antifragility.

The idea is simple:

Imagine a fragile vase. If you put it in a box and shake it, as if it was being delivered by a careless Amazon driver, it comes out of that box in pieces. In this metaphor, the shaking represents the chaos of life. An unexpected family emergency, a crisis at work, a stressful period of little sleep. As humans, we don’t want to be fragile. If you shake us with these things, we don’t want to crack and fall apart.

So what is the opposite of fragility? Many think it is to be resilient or robust. If you put the vase in a box and shake it, it comes out looking the same. This is the person who is unphased by what is thrown at them. Such a person is certainly not fragile, but they are not the opposite of fragile either.

The opposite of being fragile, Nassim argues, would be something called “antifragility”. This is when you put the vase in a box, you shake it, and it actually comes out stronger, in better condition, or worth more. 

Antifragility already exists in nature. Our bodies are antifragile, up to a limit. As long as we don’t injure ourselves, we train our muscles by lifting weights and putting our bodies under stress. Our body then acclimatizes by increasing the size and strength of our muscles. 

In martial arts, you kick pads or a heavy bag with your shins to cause little micro-breaks in the bone. These heal, the bone becomes stronger, and this is how you can kick with confidence in a real match. 

Antifragility already exists in nature. We want to harness this for our character.

Imagine an antifragile person. They experience an unexpected family emergency, a crisis at work, a stressful period of little sleep, and they come out better. They learn something useful to apply next time this happens, or they develop a perspective that changes how they approach these situations. Because of the chaos of life, they have developed into an even better person.  
Such a person benefits from change, chaos, and the unknown. They don’t want to hide from chaos, nor are they numb to it. They turn it to their advantage and seek it out when helpful.  
We all want to be antifragile, not just robust. 

Nassim Taleb understood that the Stoics embodied the idea of antifragility long before he wrote about it. According to him:  

“A Stoic is someone who transforms fear into prudence, pain into transformation, mistakes into initiation, and desire into undertaking.”   
id: ?

This course will identify, unpack, and explain these Stoic lessons on how to become antifragile, so you can begin your own transformation.

### **Fuel for the Fire:**

The last lesson introduced antifragility as the opposite of being fragile. The fragile person is harmed by chaos, change, and a volatile environment, but the antifragile person is improved by these things. 

If you are interested in Stoicism, then antifragility likely sounded to you like an amazing quality, something worth being. 

But how do we become antifragile? The first strategy is to train ourselves, in each difficult situation, we find ourselves in, to focus on what is up to us. 

The Stoics taught that no matter who you are, your perspective is always up to you. This is the message behind this famous quote by Epictetus:

“I must die. Must I then die lamenting? I must be put in chains. Must I then also lament? I must go into exile. Does any man then hinder me from going with smiles and cheerfulness and contentment?”   
id: 296

In life, there are many things outside of our control and just a few things that are. We don’t determine the situations we face, but we do determine how we respond to them.

Fortunately, antifragility is one of those few things that is up to us. Being antifragile is just a quality of your character. It is a result of how you respond to situations.

It is up to us whether we see hardship as something unfair, or as an opportunity to improve. It is up to us whether we see a challenge as another thing to suffer through, or as a moment to practice being the kind of person we want to be. 

The fragile person depends on things going a certain way to be happy. How are they treated by others? How successful are they? Does their life turn out as they planned? Do people act how they want them to act?

The antifragile person does not have to worry about the answer to these questions. They just have to be antifragile. If they do that, if they do this one job, they will be fine. Not just fine, but improved from the experiences. 

Whether you approach life as someone fragile or antifragile also influences how you control your emotions.

The non-Stoic approach to protecting their emotions is to try to control everything external to themselves. Who they will meet, what will happen to them, how will they be treated. The non-Stoic tries to make themselves resilient and robust by focusing on things outside of themselves.

The risk to this is that we can’t effectively control the things outside ourselves. We will never control what people say or do. And even if we are successful for a time, the risk of things going poorly will make us anxious. A knowledge of our fragility makes us nervously focus on the things outside us, waiting for something to go wrong. 

But the Stoic approach is to focus on themselves. They won’t worry about what will happen to them, only that they are prepared to approach what comes with an antifragile mindset.   
One focuses on controlling the externals, and the other focuses on getting the internal things right.

We grow up learning to focus on things, not up to us. We are trained to care about what people think, our possessions, and our reputation. We are taught that the quality of our lives depends on controlling the things outside of ourselves.

It is normal to feel this way since this message is everywhere. But it is a false message. And you will never become antifragile if you keep this focus. You can’t become antifragile by controlling your environment. This is only a way to hide your fragility.

After you have turned your attention inward, the next strategy for becoming antifragile, and not just robust, is to focus on improving your responses, not your outcomes. If we focus on outcomes, did we win the game, did we get the job, does that person like us, then we will always be fragile because our happiness will be vulnerable to chance and the decisions of others. But if we focus on our internal processes, did we try our best, did we learn from our mistakes, then there is always something to learn from every situation.  
In both examples, we move past robustness to real antifragility by turning your attention inward. By caring about our responses to situations, and by accepting that these responses, one by one, compose the quality of your life and are the most important thing to living well. 

As Marcus Aurelius said:   
“The happiness of your life depends upon the quality of your thoughts; therefore guard accordingly.” id: 119

Marcus is someone who has turned his attention inward. He is more focused on guarding against a poor reaction to an insult than controlling the behavior of the insulter. 

The first step to becoming antifragile is to focus on what is up to you. Recognize that your ability to become better doesn’t depend on the people or things around you, only on decisions and judgment.

Consider this quote by Marcus Aurelius, which captures the idea of antifragility:

“Our inward power, when it obeys nature, reacts to events by accommodating itself to what it faces…As a fire overwhelms what would have quenched a lamp. What's thrown on top of the conflagration is absorbed, consumed by it \- and makes it burn still higher.”   
id: 168

We become antifragile by focusing on what is up to us: namely, that our fire is ready to consume whatever is thrown into it.

### **Obstacle is the way:**

In the last lesson, I said that if you are antifragile you don’t have to worry about your surroundings, just focus on yourself. But this is not exactly true. When you are antifragile, you care about your environment, because you recognize it can improve your character.

The antifragile person benefits more from a challenging life than a smooth one.

The antifragile person wants a healthy amount of challenge and struggle, so they can learn from them, and experience the benefits of being antifragile. This is the paradigm shift from going from fragile or robust to antifragile. The antifragile actively seeks out adversity.

This is the key idea behind the Stoic concept of the obstacle is the way. Roman Emperor Marcus Aurelius described it like this:

“Our actions may be impeded ... but there can be no impeding our intentions or our dispositions. Because we can accommodate and adapt. The mind adapts and converts to its own purposes the obstacle to our acting. The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way.”   
id: 941

The meaning behind Marcus’ quote is this: If you are concerned with controlling the external world, then things outside of your control will be genuine obstacles to your goals. If you are a basketball player who only wants to win the championship, then the amazing skill of the other team is an obstacle to that goal. 

An obstacle must be defeated or your goal is blocked. But some obstacles can’t be defeated, and so our goals depend on luck and chance. None of us are winning the tournament if a prime Michael Jordan shows up on the other team.

This is how most people experience the world. They set an external goal and then overcome or lose to the obstacles in their path.

But if you set an internal goal for the tournament, for example, to become a better basketball player, then the situation is entirely different. The skill of the other team becomes something to use or incorporate into your improvement. Maybe learn where your skills are weakest. Maybe you learn something about not underestimating yourself.

A skilled opponent is not an obstacle to learning, it is only an obstacle to something else external, namely winning a tournament.

So the internal goal, learning, can incorporate the skill of the opponent into its objective. You can use the skill of another person to learn. This is what Marcus means when he says “The obstacle becomes the way”. In this case, the skill of the opponent, previously an obstacle, contributes to your ultimate goal of improving how good you are at basketball. 

Or consider a more weighty example. In a famous passage, Epictetus talks with a father who ran away from home because his daughter is sick and he can’t emotionally handle the pain. The father has been, in the Stoic sense, a coward, unable to face the pain of the situation even though his daughter needs him.

The father has an external goal, to have a healthy daughter, that he can’t achieve and so he is fragile, breaking apart.

Epictetus instead reminds him to set an internal goal instead: To be a good father to his daughter, To be a good head of the household. Not only does this give him back control and an achievable goal in a terrible situation, it gives him an opportunity to learn.  
If the father can navigate this tragic situation well, or even learn from his failings, then he can be a better father to his living children.

In this way, what was previously an obstacle becomes a path to improvement.

This is not a philosophy of compromise or finding the silver linings. When you compromise, you make the best of a bad situation. 

Antifragility is different. It is finding a way to turn a bad situation to your advantage, to make fuel of what could have put out the fire.

This is how the Stoic sees the world. Remember that Antifragility is an internal goal relating to your character. It is how you respond to things. Do you get stronger, more courageous, more resilient, and more kind, when bad things come your way? 

So if your goal is anti-fragility, you need to realize that the obstacle becomes the way. Some of the best people I know have had awful things happen to them. But they have channeled a lesson from that awful experience. They have practiced this Stoic principle without realizing it.  
But if you make it intentional, if you keep Marcus’ idea at the front of your mind, it becomes easier to find and incorporate the lesson from each bump and bruise.

Because there is always a lesson or an opportunity. And it is the Stoics' goal to find it and use it.

### **The Olympics are now.**

One of the most fearsome Greek monsters of myth was the hydra, a giant snake-like water monster with nine heads. The hydra would rise from the swamps of Lerna and terrorize the nearby farmers. It was so dangerous, that Hercules was asked to defeat it as one of his trials.  
But what made the hydra so dangerous? Well, it had an antifragile ability. Every time a head gets cut off, the hydra grows back two. So the Hydra becomes stronger the more it fights and experiences.

Compare this with the phoenix which represents resilience. If the phoenix dies, it is reborn the same, unaffected by death, as if nothing had ever happened.

The resilience of the phoenix is a passive attribute. The antifragility of the hydra is an active one. The resilient person endures, but the antifragile person grows. Resilience is unaffected by the outside world, the antifragile person uses the bumps and bruises from the outside world to its advantage. Antifragility requires putting yourself at risk, so you can learn. 

Because of this, to be antifragile you must develop an excitement for action. This is the next Stoic strategy for becoming antifragile. Become excited for difficult moments. If the obstacle is the way, then learn to love obstacles.

This is what Epictetus means when he says:

“When faced with anything painful or pleasurable, anything bringing glory or disrepute, realize that the crisis is now, that the Olympics have started, and waiting is no longer an option; that the chance for progress, to keep or lose, turns on the events of a single day.”   
id: 841

Epictetus, the former Roman slave who ran a school of Stoic philosophy, is trying to shock his students out of complacency. 

If you are an athlete, you train for the Olympics, and once every few years there is an opportunity for glory. To put what you have trained for to the test. But if your goal is to be a Stoic, a good person, or antifragile, then this difficult situation you are facing right now is your Olympics.

This moment right here is your moment to focus, and give it your all. This cruel boss, bully, health scare, stressful work week, or even this tragedy is your moment to put into practice what you have been training for.

In other words, when you encounter an obstacle, not only can you learn something from it. But you should be, in a certain sense, excited and grateful for it. Life has given you a chance to learn, to practice, and to get better.

Epictetus channels this same idea when he says:

“When trouble comes, think of yourself as a wrestler whom God, like a trainer, has paired with a tough young fighter. For what purpose? To turn you into Olympic-class material. But this is going to take some sweat to accomplish.” id: 1816

When you feel the universe is against you, it is tempting to feel down about it. Instead, the Stoic looks for the benefit in these moments. They see these struggles as training partners, as opponents to practice against, to wrestle with, and to build themselves as a person.

Every antifragile person cultivates this perspective to some degree. 

Antifragile people do not want their life to go worse. But each of them finds some solace in the opportunity to improve and test themselves, even when circumstances are terrible. Just like the Hydra benefits from having their head cut off.

They know that these are the moments that matter most; their olympics. This is the time to put into practice what they have trained for and become the kind of people they want to be. 

### **CT: Via Negativa**

CD: Improve with elimination

### 

We’ve talked about becoming antifragile so far using imagery of action and growth. I even used the metaphor of the hydra, who grows two heads where one was cut off. For the hydra, challenge and difficulty add something to it that it didn’t have before. It makes the hydra more complex and effective.

But we can improve through elimination as well.

Instead of only thinking of your antifragility like a hydra, try thinking about yourself like a block of marble. Every hit or scratch reveals something amazing. Every blow unveils more detail to a beautiful sculpture, hidden beneath what was once a plain, inert piece of stone.

Difficulty, when you respond in the right way, chisels away. It makes you better through removal, not addition.

This is improvement via negativa, the process of improving through simplifying your life and removing the bad, instead of adding something good. When, through hardship, you learn what to remove from your behavior or character, then this is antifragility via negativa.

A good example might be your first bad relationship and break-up. The fragile person is broken, the resilient person keeps chugging along, and the antifragile person has learned something about what they don’t want in a partner, and what to avoid in the future. Improvement via negativa.

Nassim Taleb defines Via negativa as follows. It is:

“The principle that we know what is wrong with more clarity than what is right, and that knowledge grows by subtraction. Also, it is easier to know that something is wrong than to find the fix. Actions that remove are more robust than those that add because addition may have unseen, complicated feedback loops.”

Another example of this is with diet and exercise. Anyone researching how to get healthier knows there is a lot of complicated debate about what the best way to build muscle is, or which foods have the greatest cardiovascular benefit. Without a PhD it can feel really difficult to know which path is best.

However, there is a general consensus about what is bad for you. Most of us know what junk food is. We know we shouldn’t sit on the couch for too many days in a row, or drink and smoke too much.

Instead of optimizing for the best benefit, via negativa is about cutting out what is undeniably bad first. Stop eating junk food. Stop staying up late. Avoid multiple days of no exercise.  
This is less complicated to action, and easier to maintain than optimization. We can also be more confident that it will have a benefit than any other intervention. 

The Stoics were aware of via negativa.

Epictetus applies it to the topic of anger. He says to his students:

“If then you wish not to be of an angry temper, do not feed the habit: throw nothing on it which will increase it: at first keep quiet, and count the days on which you have not been angry. I used to be in passion every day; now every second day; then every third, then every fourth. But if you have intermitted thirty days, make a sacrifice to God. For the habit at first begins to be weakened, and then is completely destroyed.”  
Id: ???  
Source: Discourses  
Location: 2.18

He doesn’t tell his students to practice smiling or take up exercise, or read a certain book. He doesn’t recommend a complex addition to their life at all. Instead, he tells his students to build a negative habit, a habit of not doing something. 

Becoming calm does not have to be any more complicated than not being angry. 

What makes this approach antifragile is that we can learn what to remove from mistakes, errors, and challenges.

When you become angry, note what went wrong and avoid that moving forward. Is there a certain topic that sets you off? Do you get angry easily when you are tired, stressed, or overwhelmed? Are there certain people that push your buttons?

Via negativa is about removing what doesn’t work for you. It is improvement through subtracting the bad. And as Nassim Taleb says: “it is easier to know that something is wrong than to find the fix.”

To be truly antifragile, we want to both add and subtract.

If you don’t practice via negativa, you are missing half of your potential.

### **CT: Black Swan Events**

CD: Expected the unexpected

Marcus begins book two of his meditations with a warning to himself:

“When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: The people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous, and surly. They are like this because they can’t tell good from evil.”  
Id: ???  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 2.1

Marcus knows people have bad qualities, and so he is preparing himself to be antifragile to them, or at least resilient.

It is easy to prepare ourselves for what we know is coming.

Our mindset is itself ultimately fragile, and at risk, if it can be destroyed by uncommon, unpredictable, and unforeseen disruptive events.

Take the mauy thai fighter, who strengthens their shin bones by practicing kicks, but has their legs shattered in a car crash. Or consider the financial trader who manipulates to their benefit predictable dips in the market, but has their portfolio entirely wiped out in a market crash.

These are people who are antifragile against some challenges, but risk having all their progress destroyed by something they did not predict. They are antifragile in one sense, but fragile in another.

Earlier, I mentioned that antifragility, although pioneered by the Stoics, has been popularized by Nassim Taleb. Another book by Taleb is called ‘Black Swan’. A black swan is an unpredictable, rare event with high impact. Two examples of black swan events Taleb describes are the September 11th Terrorist attacks and the invention of the internet. These are extreme situations which altered the rules of normal life and that would be difficult to predict with confidence. 

It is easy to be well prepared for what is certain. As Marcus says, when you wake up in the morning you know you will meet people that are ungrateful and rude. To make yourself truly antifragile, you need to be prepared for the black swan events as well. 

The Stoic strategy for dealing with this is premeditatio malorum, the consideration of bad things before they happen. Seneca explains this exercise as follows:

“If an evil has been pondered beforehand, the blow is gentle when it comes. To the fool, however, and to him who trusts in fortune, each event as it arrives, ‘comes in a new and sudden form,’ and a large part of evil, to the inexperienced, consists in its novelty.”  
Id: ???  
Source: Letters  
Location: 76.34

Seneca’s point is that often it is not events themselves that overpower us, but their novelty. So we need to consider these things beforehand. The issue with black swans though, is that they can’t be imagined in specifics, that is what makes them black swans. They are unpredictable.

What we do know for certain, is that unpredictable situations will arise. We can accept, anticipate, and imagine handling well unpredictable events when they happen. We can’t imagine the specific events, but we can realize that we will be surprised. If you accept the unpredictability of the world, you will not be caught off guard by it.

That might sound unhelpful because it is vague. How can we imagine a black swan, something we can’t say anything about? But consider Marcus’ advice here:

“Never let the future disturb you. You will meet it, if you have to, with the same weapons of reason which today arm you against the present.”    
Id: ???  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 7.8

Black swans sound intimidating, and they are. But if we accept them as an inevitable part of our lives, if we diminish their novelty when they first occur, then we can view them as challenges to be overcome with the weapons of reason.

In other words, we use our wits and character to navigate our own life. And we can use that to navigate a black swan event, whatever it may be. 

It is the unexpected nature of the black swan that renders it most dangerous, not the new reality itself. 

People always say to expect the unexpected. 

This might sound trite, but I want you to consider it again with a new focus. This might just be the most important step to true antifragility.

Expect the unexpected.
