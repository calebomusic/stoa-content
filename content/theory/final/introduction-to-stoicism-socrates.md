# **Socrates**

You’ve probably heard of Socrates. Maybe you’ve heard of Socratic questioning. Maybe you know that he was a direct influence on Plato and arguably the start of Western philosophy as we know it. Or maybe you know of his reputation as a philosophical gadfly, someone who was always willing to pursue the truth, even if it meant at the expense of people liking him, or even his own life.

Socrates is, in many ways, the inspiration of Stoic philosophy. To better understand Stoicism, you need to go back to the source.

In this short lesson, we will cover the life of Socrates, and three of his most important ideas. At the end of it, you will have a better grasp of how Socrates contributed to shaping Stoicism into the philosophy that it is.

**Who was Socrates?**

Born around 470 BC, Socrates is one of the most famous philosophers of all time. Although well known today, like Epictetus he left none of his own writing. Instead, we know about the philosophy of Socrates because of the writings of the students he inspired. His two most famous students were Xenophon and Plato, the first was an Athenian military leader, and the later was possibly the most important philosopher of all time.

The Stoics were heavily inspired by Socrates. As Anthony Long says, Socrates is the primary Stoic role model, the source of inspiration, and the original Sage. For example, Socrates is the most mentioned person in Epictetus' discourses. If we in the 21st century look up to Epictetus and Marcus, Epictetus looked up to Socrates in the same way. As Stoics, we should be interested in Socrates' key insights for self-improvement. 

Despite being known as a philosopher, Socrates lived a long and varied life. Socrates was an Athenian citizen, and as an Athenian citizen, he fought in the Peloponnesian War, a 25-year war between Sparta and Athens for control of the Ancient Greek city-states. He was known for his bravery in the war, and his experience as a soldier likely grounds the practical nature of his philosophy. When Socrates talks about the importance of character above all else, it can be useful to view this through the lens of a soldier rather than an academic. This is someone who has both seen the damage of violence and war, and the danger of poor character, whether that is tyrannical anger or paralyzing cowardice.

After the war, Socrates remained in Athens and took up the social role of resident philosophy. He referred to himself as a gadfly, someone who would annoy and frustrate people with his questioning, asking them to clarify their ideas about “justice” or “Good”. This method of questioning called the Socratic Method, is a central Socratic technique we will discuss further.

The motivation for pursuing this Socratic method, and his gadfly behavior, came from a comment from the Oracle of Delphi, a famous ancient Greek oracle. The Oracle of Delphi claimed Socrates to be the wisest of all Athenians. this confused him because he didn't think himself particularly wise. So he went about questioning others, to try to find out what they knew. But when Socrates questioned people, he realized that they didn’t know the things they claimed to know. They seemed to act in the name of justice, but could not define it in conversation. They would act in the name of piety to the gods, but couldn’t tell him what that meant. 

It was from this experience that Socrates became obsessed with the idea of self-knowledge and deception. If everyone was ignorant, then the Oracle of Delphi must think he is the wisest because he knows at least one thing: that he knows nothing.

In 399 BC Socrates was accused of impiety and corrupting the youth. He would have been 71\. In a trial lasting a day, Socrates was found guilty. In ancient Athens, you had to propose your own punishment, and Socrates, according to Plato’s account, shockingly proposed that his punishment be to be honoured by the city, like an Olympic Athlete because he deserved praise for teaching philosophy. The judges sided with the punishment proposed by the prosecution, death. According to Plato, Socrates' supporters concocted a plan to see him escape his punishment. Socrates refused, accepting the judgement of the trial. He died of hemlock poisoning in his cell.

Most of what we know of Socrates’ thought comes from Plato. Plato wrote his philosophy in dialogues, and with a few limited exceptions, in all of them Socrates features as the main character. Plato's writing is divided into 3 periods, early, middle, and late, and it is generally agreed that early Plato is a fairly accurate representation of Socrates' thinking, while Socrates character becomes more a mouthpiece for Plato over time.

In these early Socratic dialogues, we see Socrates' true self. Socrates is especially important for us, because most Hellenistic schools of philosophy, the Sceptics, Cynics, and the Stoics, saw themselves as direct offshoots of Socrates. He was, in some ways, their holy figure. Their sage. And each group attempted to deconstruct his main teaching. For the Sceptics, it was his belief that he knew nothing. For the Cynics, it was his rejection of social norms in the pursuit of truth. And for the Stoics, it was the belief that virtue is knowledge because no one does wrong willingly, and that we should never compromise our virtue because of external circumstances.

Now that we have explored the life of Socrates, we will examine three Socratic ideas, already touched on above, that inspired Stoicism.

**Key Ideas:**

Three key ideas from Socrates are:

1. The Socratic Method  
2. The importance of being humble about what you know  
3. Our character is more important than anything else

**The Socratic Method and the Stoics**

The Stoics believed that what we think determines who we are. Our behaviours and emotions come from our beliefs, so it is crucial we think carefully and only believe what is true. They also identified that we can change how we think by identifying contradictions between two positions. So we can change who we are by improving our thinking and removing our contradictions. This is what the Socratic method is meant to do. As Epictetus says:

“That is why Socrates, placing full confidence in this capacity \[to reason\], used to say, 'I'm not in the habit of calling another witness to speak in support of what I'm saying, but I always remain satisfied with the person who is engaging in discussion with me, and call on his vote and summon him as a witness, so that he alone suffices for me in place of all the others. (Disc. 2.26.6)

So we don't convince people through peer pressure, or showing that many people believe what they don't. You convince them genuinely. So that they are persuaded, not just defeated in debate. This means that their behaviour will genuinely change in earnest, which is what the Stoics are after.

The Socratic method is a conversational technique meant to evoke a contradiction in beliefs and force the listener to refine their position, moving towards the truth. It follows the following steps.

* Step 1: Interlocutor takes an unreflective, often intuitive belief  
* Step 2: Socrates develops the full implications of that belief, including related belief the interlocutor holds that they might not realize contradict the original position.  
* Step 3: Socrates identifies a contradiction between our intuitions and the implications of our belief, and demands one of these be dropped or updated  
* Step 4: Interlocutor refines or updates their original position  
* Step 5: Repeat until a proper definition is identified, or all participants admit ignorance.

To take an example from Plato’s Republic:

Socrates' companion says that justice is to give a person what they are owed. This is an intuitive, unreflective position.

Socrates then points out a contradiction. It does not seem just to return an axe you borrowed to someone planning to use it for murder. 

The companion acknowledges the contradiction. That wouldn’t be just. And so they go about trying to find a new definition of justice. 

This is the Socratic method in action. Stoics looked at this as an educational technique. 

But you need to be a gadfly to others to use the Socratic method. I often do the Socratic method BY MYSELF as a mindful self-reflective exercise. When I feel conflicted about something, I list my beliefs and try to identify where the contradiction is between them. And if some weak belief contradicts with someone I know is certainly true, I use that criteria to admit that it must be wrong. 

For example, when I was a competitive athlete, I sometimes  would be particularly hard on myself after losing a tough match. I would talk down about myself, or question my skill. Maybe I am bad at this sport? Then I’ll do some Socratic reasoning about what it means to be a bad athlete. Are those who lose bad athletes? Well, no, because then even an Olympic Silver medalist would be a bad athlete and I know plenty of people that lose that I think are great athletes. So the fact that I lost alone is not enough to judge myself when I would never apply that label to others. This is the Socratic method in action to get out of a spiral of self-judgment. 

At its core, the Socratic method is a tool for disciplined thinking. This can be caused by a teacher, but we can be our own internal Socrates.

**Socratic humility:**

Another key Socratic idea we find in the Stoics is Socratic humility. Socrates was perhaps most famous for saying: “I know that I know nothing.” What Socrates meant by this is complicated, but there is one clear take-away: The person who acknowledges their own ignorance, knows more than the person who does not realize they are ignorant.

Put differently, self-deceit is a particularly dangerous type of ignorance.

In modern times, we often view ignorance as a vice. We are embarrassed to admit to others that we don’t know something. Likewise, we don’t often reflect on the limits of our own knowledge. We focus instead on what we do know, or at least, what we think we know.

Socrates instead encourages us to think carefully about what we don’t know, and to understand the limits of our own knowledge. This is what I am calling Socratic humility. If the Socratic method is about improving our thinking in particular situations, then Socratic humility is about adopting the kind of disciplined thinking that makes the Socratic method unnecessary. 

We use the Socratic method to question what we think we know. We adopt Socratic humility to avoid self-ignorance in the first place. Both are about thinking better to live better. 

If we lack humility we will not strive to improve. In Socrates time’ everyone thought they knew what justice or good and bad meant. So why study philosophy? By recognizing your ignorance you are spurred to self-improvement.

Not only does recognizing ignorance encourage self-improvement, but Socrates was also aware that overconfidence is dangerous. We can think of simple examples, it is better to know you don’t know how to scuba dive than to think you do. But the same thing goes for choices of character and virtue, right and wrong. In one famous Platonic dialogue, Euthyphro, Socrates questions a man named Euthyphro about bringing a legal charge against his own father. Socrates is shocked because he thinks Euthyphro must really understand the nature of justice and piety to be making such an impactful choice. But over the course of the argument, Socrates reveals Euthyphro’s ignorance.

In summary, Socratic humility encourages us to hold ourselves to a high standard. The bar for claiming knowledge is raised higher than for most people. And when we raise that bar, we are a lot less likely to endorse things casually or find ourselves with false beliefs.

**Virtue as the highest good:**

The last idea of Socrates that we find in Stoicism is the idea that our character is the highest, and maybe only good. The heart of this idea can be summarized in the Socratic phrase that is it is “Better to suffer injustice than to do injustice.”

For a normal Athenian, this idea would seem preposterous. A non-Socratic would say: Better there is no injustice at all, but if there is injustice in the world, better them than me\!

Socrates explains the radical position in more detail in Plato’s Apology, the dialogue covers Socrates’ trial, the one that condemned him to death. When speaking about those who accuse him, Socrates says:

“I do not think it is permitted that a better man be harmed by a worse; certainly he might kill me, or perhaps banish or disfranchise me, which he and maybe others think to be great harm, but I do not think so. I think he is doing himself much greater harm doing what he is doing now, attempting to have a man executed unjustly.”

Imagine this scene in greater detail. Socrates is at risk of being executed, exiled, or spending his life in prison, for a crime he does not believe he has committed. Not only does he believe himself to be innocent, but he thinks he has been framed. The accusers are not mistaken but good-intentioned, rather, they are his enemies.  And instead of lamenting his bad fortune, or pleading for the mercy of the audience, Socrates is unfazed. More than unfazed, he actually thinks his accusers are in a worse situation than him\!

Socrates’ point here is essentially a Stoic one. Our character is the most important thing. Our character is damaged when we commit injustice, but it is not damaged when we suffer injustice. Nor is it damaged when we are exiled or killed. Therefore it is better to suffer injustice than to do it. His accusers have harmed themselves, but they cannot harm Socrates.

From a scene like this, it is not a surprise why Epictetus and the other Stoics thought of Socrates as a sage. He faces his fate with courage, not because he is ignorant of the danger he is in, but because he knows what is worth being afraid of and what cannot actually harm him.

In terms of our own practice, the phrase “Better to suffer injustice than to do injustice” is an excellent tool to refocus perspective, especially in situations that may turn you to anger, bitterness, or a desire for revenge. Remember that if Character is the most important thing, in a sense the real victim is the perpetrator of injustice. They have to be the thief, the bully, or the liar. The Stoics believe that in any situation where you have been wronged by someone’s poor character, you have always come out of that exchange better off than the person who did the wrong. 

This might take a lot of work to put into practice. We can’t all be Socrates, who pities his accusers at his own trial. But we can start smart, and build from there. 

**Conclusion**

In Summary, three key ideas from Socrates we find in Stoicism are:

1. The Socratic Method  
2. The importance of being humble about what you know  
3. The idea that our character is the most important thing

We find in Socrates the seed of what would grow into Stoicism. He may not be a Stoic, predating Stoicism but a hundred years, but the Stoics took up his arguments, and pointed to him as a sage. So if you admire the Stoics, and want to know who they admired, look to Socrates.
