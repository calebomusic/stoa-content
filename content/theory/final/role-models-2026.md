## **Introduction**

The purpose of this course is to introduce role models you can use to make your life better.

For the Stoics, it was crucial to keep role models in mind. Such figures motivate us and guide our actions. As the Stoic philosopher Seneca wrote:

"The soul should have someone whom it can respect, by whose authority it may make even its inner shrine more hallowed. Choose therefore a Cato; or, if Cato seems too severe a model, choose some Laelius, a gentler spirit. For we must have someone according to whom we may regulate our characters; you can never straighten that which is crooked unless you use a ruler." Seneca, Moral Letters 11

id: ?

A role model provides a standard and guide for who we can be.

To use them well, we need to understand their life in some detail. By doing so, we can simulate how they would reason about a situation, what advice they would give us, or how they would guide our behavior. That is what this course will help you do. We'll cover Stoic figures and offer concrete stories where they displayed virtue. Use these as ingredients for your own contemplation.

When you meditate on the lives of ancient heroes, think of yourself like an athlete studying the legends of the past. A basketball player might simulate the moves of their idol, running them through their mind again and again. By doing so, they deepen their knowledge of the game.

This only works if the basketball player understands what their role model is doing in sufficient detail. To do that, they must watch footage, read analysis, and study their idol. Otherwise, they might imitate what they think their model is doing, not what they are actually doing. You can't imitate someone's style of play unless you comprehend it.

No figure here is perfect. Not all of them will appeal to you. That's okay. All you need to do is determine what admirable traits you can turn to for guidance.

Like the athlete, you can use your hero to motivate you when things get tough.

Role models are inspirational, but their lives contain tactical lessons too. The human experience has changed over the centuries, yet the fundamentals remain the same.

Take note of the figures here. Meditate on their virtue in order to live a more resilient and virtuous life.

## **Cato the Courageous**

Cato the Younger is one of the most principled men to ever live.

He did whatever he believed was right, even when it brought social scorn or risked his life. In other words, he was courageous. He acted with integrity whatever the situation.

Cato lived during the Late Republic, from 95 BC to 46 BC. He was a statesman, Stoic, father, and friend. He lived during a time of political turmoil and civil war. Cato squared off against men like Julius Caesar, who aimed to destroy the Republic and usher in dictatorship. Whether he made the right call is a difficult question to answer, even in hindsight. Nonetheless, even Cato's enemies admired his resolve.

Cato fearlessly negotiated with armed mobs, refused generals, and stuck to his principles without deliberation. His unwavering commitment to his ethics made him a legendary figure in history, and someone every Stoic can take as a role model.

When you think of Cato, think of someone who is honest despite threats. As quaestor, supervisor of the Roman treasury, Cato cleaned the books and purged corrupt bureaucrats. He did not play the usual game of paying off people for political favors. Instead, he identified people who did that and fired them. Did this make Cato friends? With the Roman people, yes. With many of his peers, no.

In *Rome's Last Citizen*, Jimmy Soni and Rob Goodman write:

"He is in politics yet somehow not of politics. He is a man of action and a spellbinding orator, but he is also deeper; in quieter moments, he gives the impression of barely restraining the urge to retire and become a philosopher. He is the man brought into being by the crisis, and he is its solution, the mirror image of the greed and self-seeking that surround him."

As senator, Cato stared down armed mobs, prestigious politicians, and murderous dictators. Physical violence and cruel gossip did not sway his resolve. He stood in contrast to statesmen who shrank from public life out of concern for their safety, or others who moved to appease anyone for their own self-interest.

Once, Julius Caesar ordered his guards to remove Cato from the senate because Cato fiercely opposed Caesar's legislation. As armed men dragged him off to prison, Cato continued to protest against Caesar's law.

Deeply moved by Cato's example, one senator remarked to Caesar, "I prefer to be with Cato in prison rather than here with you\!" He left the senate to follow Cato. One by one, the senate chambers emptied. Caesar was left alone. Words can be more powerful than the sword.

Another incident highlights Cato's courage and social mindedness. At the end of his war with Caesar, Cato sought to save the lives of civilians from rampaging soldiers. The Roman historian Plutarch records that, as soon as Cato heard that soldiers were plundering the people of Utica, he ran straight towards them as fast as he could. He convinced many to return what they had stolen. He either paid the soldiers off with his own funds or simply marched into their camp and persuaded them to be peaceful. Either way, Cato safeguarded civilian lives and livelihoods.

These actions provide motivation and fuel for acting courageously ourselves. Cato did the right thing without hesitation. He understood that some situations require risking our reputation and safety.

He took Marcus Aurelius's line to heart:

"Just that you do the right thing, the rest doesn't matter."

## **Marcus Aurelius the Moderate**

Marcus Aurelius was one of Rome's greatest emperors and one of history's most influential Stoic philosophers.

Living from 121 to 180 AD, he was Roman emperor from 161 to 180 AD. He grew up in the Roman court and had the reputation for honesty, sincerity, and dedication from an early age. The historian Edward Gibbon labeled him the last of the five good emperors.

The historian Herodian said the following about him:

"Alone of the emperors, he gave proof of his learning not by mere words or knowledge of philosophical doctrines but by his blameless character and temperate way of life."

Marcus Aurelius was a practicing Stoic. From Stoic philosophy he learned that nothing is good unless it leads to justice, discipline, courage, and wisdom. Similarly, nothing is truly bad unless it erodes our virtue.

You can see him walk the Stoic path in his *Meditations*. This work was never meant for public consumption. In it, we see Marcus Aurelius reminding himself of central Stoic doctrines, recalling maxims to use throughout the day, and motivating himself to act better. We find beautiful summaries of Stoicism in passages like this one:

"Objective judgment, now, at this very moment. Unselfish action, now, at this very moment. Willing acceptance, now, at this very moment, of all external events. That's all you need." id: 193

There are many virtues one can use Marcus Aurelius as a model for. In this lesson, we'll focus on moderation. Moderation, also called discipline or temperance, is one of the four virtues. It is captured by the maxim "nothing in excess." We practice it when we know the right amount of things and act accordingly.

As emperor of Rome, Marcus Aurelius was in a place where immoderation would be easy.

Nonetheless, he never let power get to his head. Not all Roman emperors accomplished this. Think of emperors like Caligula or Nero. Caligula acted with capricious cruelty. Instead of serving as emperor, Nero used the office to satisfy his artistic ambitions. He performed on stage, competed in chariot races, and reportedly sang while Rome burned.

Marcus Aurelius didn't do any of this. He may have preferred to be a philosopher. Nonetheless, ruler was the role that Nature gave him, so he performed in it the best he could.

Marcus Aurelius was fully dedicated to his role. He exemplified moderation by devoting himself to his office with the sincerity it called for. As Roman emperor, he heard hundreds of cases. He waged war. He fought off a plague. He sold off items from the royal treasury in order to fund the Empire. He managed a coup attempt. In all this, he was known for dedicated, focused, and high standards of work.

None of us faces the same kind of responsibility or power Marcus Aurelius had. Nonetheless, we're each called to act with discipline and not succumb to distractions.

The Romans knew that with great power comes risk and delusion. This is why, when celebrating a triumph, a slave whispered into the commander's ear: *memento mori*. Remember you are mortal. You will die.

By doing so, Roman generals were reminded that they are not gods. They should not think too highly of themselves. Mortality demands moderation.

In an age when Roman emperors were deified, Marcus Aurelius reminded himself of this constantly.

"Alexander and Pompeius and Gaius Caesar, after so often completely destroying whole cities themselves, too, at last departed from life."

In addition to reminding himself of human mortality, Marcus Aurelius reminded himself to see things as they are. Fine meals, wine, and royal clothing signaled power. The kind of power that got to some Roman emperors' heads. Marcus Aurelius kept his cool by reminding himself:

"When you have savouries and fine dishes set before you, you will gain an idea of their nature if you tell yourself that this is the corpse of a fish, and that the corpse of a bird or a pig; or again, that fine Falernian wine is merely grape-juice, and this purple robe some sheep's wool dipped in the blood of a shellfish." id: 1726

So much of what is high status loses its value when you describe it as it is, objectively.

Use Marcus Aurelius as a role model for moderation. Nature calls us to be devoted to some things and people and restrained from others. Despite his position, Marcus kept his focus on acting well. History has rewarded him with the good reputation he didn't seek.

## **Epictetus the Earnest**

Epictetus resolved to live with excellence no matter what happened. For that reason, he is a fine model for all Stoics.

He was born a slave in present-day Turkey around 50 AD.

He came into the world shortly before the Stoic philosopher Seneca died. Epictetus passed away himself shortly after the Stoic emperor Marcus Aurelius was born.

The name "Epictetus" translates to "acquired." He was reportedly lame due to an injury he may have sustained while enslaved. Eventually, he studied with the Stoic philosopher Musonius Rufus. Whether he received this training because his owner demanded he be a better slave, or as a freeman, we do not know. Regardless, he was eventually granted freedom. With his education, he became a Stoic teacher himself.

As it does, Fortune threw another obstacle onto his path, and he was banished from Rome. So Epictetus left for Nicopolis in Greece and founded a school of philosophy there. There he trained young men in Stoic philosophy.

Epictetus modeled excellence by refusing to let circumstance budge his principles. He lived out the idea that physical injury harms only the body, not the mind.

As he said:

"Lameness is an impediment to the leg, but not to your purpose. Say this to yourself at each thing that happens and you will find the thing to be an impediment to something else, not to yourself." Epictetus, Handbook 9

He put this into practice. He did not complain about his injury or wish things had turned out otherwise. Nor is there any indication that he held useless grudges against his former masters. Instead, he focused on what was up to him. Acting well requires facing obstacles the best we can. Epictetus kept his attention on excellence, not adversity.

By all accounts, Epictetus was a great teacher. He took the art of teaching seriously and perfected his craft. He was a sought after Stoic for a reason. As a model, Epictetus fit his message to his audience without sacrificing its integrity.

In *The Discourses*, we see Epictetus persuading a friend not to kill himself. We see him convincing a father to return and care for his daughter. And we see him accosting and motivating his students.

He is someone who applied the principle "have the fight." This means he didn't shrink from saying what was true just because it was uncomfortable. He did not mince his words. He was always direct.

He is a model of someone who never beat around the bush or hid in vague language. He held himself and those around him to a high standard.

Keep this in mind when reflecting on Epictetus's example: his resilience, focus on excellence, and sincerity of spirit.

Let's end with some motivation from him:

"When trouble comes, think of yourself as a wrestler whom God, like a trainer, has paired with a tough young fighter. For what purpose? To turn you into Olympic-class material. But this is going to take some sweat to accomplish."

## **Seneca the Wordsmith**

Seneca saw emperors come and go. At one point, he was rumored to be the richest man in all of Rome. Despite a life of political intrigue, risk, and bustle, he wrote plays and philosophical works fated to outlive him.

It's not much of an exaggeration to say he was a combination of Shakespeare, Socrates, and Thomas Jefferson. This is a combination so rare that, for most of history, scholars believed Seneca the playwright and Seneca the philosopher were two different people.

His writings and life contain deep lessons about how to live well. Seneca is worth emulating, and yet he also made decisions that are lessons in how to avoid ruin.

Born around 4 BC in modern-day Spain, he moved to Rome, where he eventually served as a senator. He was renowned for his rhetorical skill. Indeed, he may have been too good. He was exiled from Rome on grounds of being involved in an affair with an emperor's sister. This charge was likely fabricated by his political enemies.

Almost 10 years later, Seneca was called out of exile by the ambitious and strategic Agrippina, the mother of the Roman emperor Nero, to serve as Nero's tutor.

This role placed him in the powerful and precarious position of advising Rome's most powerful man. Unfortunately, despite Seneca's lessons, Nero was neither the most sane nor the most virtuous emperor.

Nero's early years were prosperous. It's surmised that, at this point, Seneca, Agrippina, and another one of Nero's tutors, Burrus, essentially ran the Roman Empire.

Yet Nero's later reign was disastrous. Though some historians have argued that he's painted in too negative a light, Nero was greedy, impulsive, and unstable. Senators were pressured to play the sycophant to his childish behavior. Not all did. Those who didn't faced exile or death.

In time, Nero's rage turned to Seneca. The emperor ordered Seneca to commit suicide in 65 AD.

And so ended the life of a Stoic philosopher, advisor, and playwright. Seneca lived a complex life.

One lesson that shines through is his dedication to language.

When we remember Seneca, we should remember someone who saw speech as a craft. Today, many of us treat our conversations with ourselves and others in an informal and trivial manner. However, language is a weighty thing.

What do you say to comfort someone grieving? This is a situation Seneca was placed in again and again. One of his most famous letters, the *Consolation to Marcia*, was written to a mother who had lost her son three years earlier and was still in deep mourning. Someone who treats language seriously is prepared to know what to say and think in life's high-stakes situations. Anyone who treats words in a flippant way is not.

A focus on language may seem odd, but it is through words that we communicate with others and think. By devoting himself to the word, Seneca's ideas outlived him hundredfold.

In a life of politics and action, Seneca practiced his craft in philosophy, speeches, and plays. Many politicians write or ghostwrite books today. Yet how many of them are truly great works?

Seneca's work is sublime. His plays heavily influenced Renaissance playwrights. His philosophy has stood the test of time. This is only so because Seneca took his ideas, and the vehicle by which he expressed them, seriously.

We should picture Seneca perfecting his thought through language in private, not merely in public. He reflected often. In *On Anger*, he writes:

"When the light has been taken away and my wife has fallen silent, aware as she is of my habit, I examine my entire day, going through what I have done and said. I conceal nothing from myself, I pass nothing by. I have nothing to fear from my errors when I can say: 'See that you do not do this anymore. For the moment, I excuse you.'"

Each day, he asks what he did well and where he could improve. His *Moral Letters to Lucilius* show the fruits of this practice. In those letters, he passes on his hard-won wisdom on life to his peer and friend, Lucilius.

He was able to do this because of how devoted he was to his craft and to philosophical reflection.

Seneca's life was far from perfect. He is the first to admit it. Nonetheless, behind all of his practical advice and wisdom is something we must not miss: the love of language.

## **Socrates the Wise**

Socrates is the ultimate Stoic role model.

His ideas that the good life is the virtuous one, and that virtue is knowledge, are fundamentally Stoic.

His unending search for the truth made him a model in action as well as theory.

For this reason, he was idolized by every other Stoic model: Seneca, Cato the Younger, Epictetus, and Marcus Aurelius.

Born in 470 BC in Athens, Socrates served the city by fighting in the Athenian military and philosophizing with others.

The oracle of Delphi famously announced that he was the wisest of all men. Confused why this would be so, Socrates went looking for wisdom in others. He didn't find it. He realized that neither he nor anyone else knew much at all. What made him different was that he didn't pretend to know. The search for wisdom begins with admitting ignorance.

Eventually, he was accused of corrupting the youth and disrespecting the city gods. His case is recounted by his two biographers, Xenophon and Plato, in *The Apology*.

In it, we see someone who is principled, fearless, and thoughtful. This is most evident in his unyielding search for truth.

Socrates sought knowledge wherever he was. Whether he was at a friend's party or waiting for a court case, he was always testing his own and others' knowledge. He asked people why they believed what they did, disregarding social niceties.

He always asked questions, listened carefully, and followed up with more questions. Often it is clear that he had thought through the issue more than his interlocutor, but Socrates was not usually trying to win an argument or find a convert. He was trying to get to the heart of the matter.

At his best, he didn't engage in discourse to show he was smart or to stump opponents, but to get at what others truly knew. He described himself as a midwife of ideas, helping others give birth to their own knowledge.

In all this, Socrates cultivated deep friendships. In the dialogues of Plato and Xenophon, both accounts of Socrates's life always show him in discussion with others. The pursuit of truth is a communal, not individual, exercise.

When Socrates was put to death, his friends cared for him so much that they were willing to risk their lives to save him. His friend Crito even arranged a plan for him to escape from prison. Socrates refused, because fleeing would betray the laws of the city he had lived under his whole life.

Men came to Socrates for profound and practical matters. In Xenophon's *Memorabilia*, the book that inspired the founder of Stoicism, Zeno, to turn to philosophy, we see Socrates giving sound advice to those honestly seeking it, on everything from education to real estate.

Through careful questioning, listening, and dialogue with others, Socrates exemplified the quest for knowledge.

So take these lessons from his life: pursue truth, build deep relationships, and play your roles well.

## **Porcia the Principled**

Porcia Cato was the daughter of Cato the Younger. She rivaled her father's integrity and daring.

In 49 BC, Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon and marched his soldiers on Rome. Years later, he declared himself "dictator for life."

This pitted him against Porcia's husband Brutus, who preferred the freedom of the Roman Republic.

One day, Porcia got the sense that her husband was planning something secret.

It wasn't rare to be subject to torture in those days. If you were suspected of harboring essential secrets, you might be pressured to give them up. Knowing this, Porcia planned to show that she was capable of withstanding pain.

So, she took a knife to her thigh. Porcia's pre-emptive strike proved that Brutus did not have to fear she would reveal his plans. This drastic measure was made more necessary by the social climate, one where women were not often trusted with the affairs of men.

This act convinced Brutus to share his plans with his wife. He planned to assassinate Caesar. He saw Caesar as nothing more than a petty tyrant, the destroyer of Rome.

His secret was safe with her.

Brutus and his co-conspirators killed Julius Caesar. Romans then, and historians now, debate whether Brutus should be seen as a noble tyrant-killer, a tragic character, or a political opportunist. The Stoic emperor Marcus Aurelius says the following about him:

"\[He\] grasped the idea of a state based on equality before the law, which is administered according to the principles of equality and freedom of speech, and of a monarchy which values above all the liberty of its subjects." Meditations 1.14

Stoics have tended to see Brutus and other aristocrats who sought to restore the Republic as figures who fought for justice, even if imperfectly. This was a fight Porcia deserved to be a part of. So, she took part. She kept his secret and offered unwavering support.

After Julius Caesar's death, Rome was plunged into civil war. Brutus faced off against Mark Antony and Octavian, soon to be Augustus, the emperor of Rome. Brutus lost.

Like her father Cato the Younger, Porcia was a principled player on the losing side.

Plutarch records that Porcia ended her days like her father, in noble suicide.

Porcia offers proof that Stoicism can be held by anyone, in any circumstance.

Like her father, Porcia showed her principles through action. She didn't communicate through cheap talk. When she said something, we must imagine her following through with her deeds. She stabbed herself to prove her worth. By all accounts, this was not surprising behavior. Porcia applied the maxim: who you are is what you do.

Her family ties granted her an aristocratic Roman life, but they were also dangerous. Her father ended his life fighting Julius Caesar. Her brother and husband would end their lives fighting Caesarians too. Her life was at risk from the beginning.

Nonetheless, ancient historians portray her as steadfast and determined. She was not someone who complained.

Through her trials and persistence, she showed more bravery than many of her fellow Romans thought possible. By doing so, she challenged them to be better. She reminds us that Stoicism is available to all.

The Roman Stoics were renowned for arguing that philosophy was for slaves and women, not just the property of aristocratic men. After all, no matter your sex or ethnicity, every human is capable of reason. This insight is commonplace today, but it has needed to be defended throughout the ages. Porcia Cato provides fuel for that defense.

More than that, she offers a model of constancy and principle for all.

## **Diogenes the Free**

Epictetus, the gruff slave turned Stoic teacher, was not known for giving respect lightly.

His role models were few and far between.

But one of Epictetus's real idols was Diogenes the Cynic.

Once, Diogenes was walking through his city in broad daylight with his lamp lit. When people asked him what he was doing, he said he was searching for an honest man. When one young man blushed, Diogenes responded that this was the complexion of virtue, because at least this man recognized his dishonesty and felt some embarrassment.

Diogenes is famous for stories like these. He was a performance artist, shocking those around him out of philosophical complacency. The Stoic Epictetus modeled his high standards on Diogenes.

Diogenes was born between 412 and 404 BC in Sinope, a Greek colony on the northern coast of modern-day Turkey.

At a young age, he was captured by pirates and sold into slavery.

At some point, he gained his freedom, reached Athens, and studied philosophy under Antisthenes, a student of Socrates. He is reported to have said that he earned his real freedom from Antisthenes in the form of an education, and that after learning philosophy he could never be enslaved again.

As a philosopher, Diogenes dedicated his life to pulling people out of complacency and getting them to question their assumptions. He did shocking things in public. He would sleep in the streets of Athens in a barrel, point at things with his middle finger, and accost Athenians.

Now all of this might sound shocking, and not particularly Stoic.

But Stoicism is a direct intellectual descendant of Cynicism.

Diogenes's main pupil was Crates of Thebes. And Crates of Thebes was the philosophical teacher of Zeno of Citium when Zeno first arrived in Athens.

Stoicism as a tradition is just two steps removed from Diogenes.

Diogenes's life shows us that freedom comes from philosophy.

We are rational, social animals on this planet for a short period of time. We need, and should desire, very little to make good use of that time. When we lose track of that, and when we lose sight of what we really are, we feel anxious and desire things outside our control.

Diogenes was himself a former slave, so he knew a thing or two about freedom. When being sold at a slave market, he is reported to have pointed at a man and said, "Sell me to this man, he looks like he needs a master."

Diogenes's point is that real freedom is about how you internally relate to others, not who owns whom according to the law.

Other anecdotes from the life of Diogenes show this radical freedom in action.

When Alexander the Great, the most powerful man in the world at the time, traveled to meet the famous Diogenes, he came upon Diogenes on a sunny day lying in the grass. As Alexander stood over him, he asked Diogenes if there was anything he could do for him. Diogenes responded that Alexander could "get out of his sun."

After this encounter, Alexander is reported to have said, "Had I not been Alexander, I should have liked to be Diogenes."

Diogenes could be who he was, himself, in front of Alexander the Great.

This is because he knew who he was and didn't need a lot to be happy. Not much more than a dog: a place to sleep, something to eat, and a community to exist in.

Some might find living like Diogenes humiliating, unnecessarily painful, or even wrong. I know I am not selling my bed for a barrel any time soon. But Diogenes really knew himself. And with that self-knowledge, and limited desires, came a degree of freedom we should all aspire to.

Looking back at the life of Diogenes, a word of caution is in order.

As Epictetus says, the life of a Cynic is not for everyone.

Stoicism is a more social, gentler form of Cynicism. It keeps some of the best parts of Diogenes's lessons but doesn't ask us to live in a barrel.

Both Stoicism and Cynicism agree that we should strive to understand who we really are, and enjoy the freedom that comes from needing little. As the ancient saying goes:

"It is not the man who has little, but he who desires more, that is poor."

We don't need to live like Diogenes the Cynic to benefit from the lessons of his life.
