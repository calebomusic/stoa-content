### **CT: The Key Distinction**

CD: Understand Epictetus’ key lesson

The dichotomy of control, the Stoic distinction between the things that are up to you, and the things that aren’t, is famous for a reason. In one pithy idea the Stoics articulated something that is true, intuitive, and helpful, but often forgotten or not put into practice.

One of the best parts of studying philosophy is when a philosopher puts into words something that seems deeply right, but you had never been able to put into words before. The dichotomy of control was like that for me, and one of the first things I learned from Stoicism. It sparked my interest in learning more, and showed me immediately that Stoicism was a practical philosophy that could teach me about how to live well. 

But what is the dichotomy of control? Fundamentally, it has two parts: a descriptive and prescriptive claim.

The descriptive claim is that all things fall into one of two categories: they are either in your control or not.

The prescriptive claim, or practical advice, is that we should not treat these things the same way. We should focus on, care about, and value the things that are up to us, while remaining detached and accepting of the things not up to us. 

Here is what Epictetus actually says, this is from the very first chapter of the handbook:

“Some things are in our control and others not. Things in our control are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever are our own actions. Things not in our control are body, property, reputation, command, and, in one word, whatever are not our own actions.The things in our control are by nature free, unrestrained, unhindered; but those not in our control are weak, slavish, restrained, belonging to others. Remember, then, that if you suppose that things which are slavish by nature are also free, and that what belongs to others is your own, then you will be hindered. You will lament, you will be disturbed, and you will find fault both with gods and men. But if you suppose that only to be your own which is your own, and what belongs to others such as it really is, then no one will ever compel you or restrain you. Further, you will find fault with no one or accuse no one. You will do nothing against your will. No one will hurt you, you will have no enemies, and you will not be harmed.”  
Sentence\_id: 1-9  
Location: Handbook 1

In other words, we suffer because we make a categorization mistake. The things up to us are different from those that aren’t, and should be treated differently. We should commit to and care about the things up to us, and be more flexible in our expectations of the things not up to us. But we forget this distinction and categorize things incorrectly.

We make two kinds of categorization mistakes according to Epictetus.

First, we treat things not in our control as they are up to us. We care too much about the things not up to us: If you think you can control someone, you will be disappointed when they don’t do what we want.

If you think that promotion at work is a full meritocracy, you will be angry when it goes to someone worse than you. If you think you can control your reputation, you will be in for a rude surprise when something you said or did takes on a life of its own.

Second, we can also treat the things up to us improperly. We can fail to recognize the degree of control we have over these things.

This is the mistake of the apathetic teenager who doesn’t try at school because ‘it doesn’t matter what they do’. Or this is the self-destructive person who is always blaming others, but not taking the steps up to them to improve the situation. This is the hopeful person, who prays for a good result but doesn’t take the steps in their power to get there.

The Dichotomy of Control is famous because of the depth and accessibility of its insight. Anyone, from those who don’t care about philosophy to those who actively think Stoicism is wrong, can see the truth and immediate practical value in Epictetus’ distinction. 

One way to approach philosophy is as an eclectic. You read broadly to collect tools for living well. Maybe you fall in this category. These people aren’t looking for a cohesive system for how to live from any single person or school, but they do think the great philosophers have something to teach. So they read broadly and collect insights, perspectives and bits of wisdom. They are looking for ‘diamonds in the dirt’, as my old philosophy professor put it.

The dichotomy of control is probably Stoicism’s most popular diamond. It can be applied to your career, relationships, or normal life stress without requiring any further commitments to Stoicism. A diamond of insight you can remove from the dirt, but its value remains intact.

I am making this course, because I think the dichotomy of control is not a diamond that can be plucked from the rest of Stoicism without any consequences. In fact, trying to do so leads to many misunderstandings, and misapplications of the idea. In other words, when people try to apply Epictetus’ ideas in isolation, they misunderstand them. 

To apply the dichotomy of control requires a deeper understanding of both Stoicism and Epictetus’ actual argument. The value of the dichotomy of control runs as deep as its appeal is wide, but most people only ever engage with it on a shallow, surface level.

I’ve even seen serious Stoics treat the dichotomy of control this way. Something interesting at first, but to be moved on from when diving deeper into the Stoic philosophy. But everyone is missing out on what Epictetus is really saying here. And understanding this distinction deeply is the key to understanding his whole way of thinking. 

It is at risk of being one of the most misunderstood parts of Stoicism. Its strength, its intuitive appeal, becomes its weakness. Because it is more intuitive than Stoic theories like oikeosis, kataleptic impressions, or eternal recurrence, it graphs immediately onto people’s existing understanding of the world. 

It is less like a diamond, and more like a tool people pick up right away and start using, but not everyone understands all the features. 

So in this course, I will be your guide through all the ins and outs of the dichotomy of control. I’ll cover: What Epictetus actually thinks is up to us. Why control might be a bad translation, or at least not mean what you think it means. Why the dichotomy of control is also about our identity, and the implications the dichotomy has on what we should value as good, bad, or not of value to us.

This is not just a theoretical distinction or supplement. The dichotomy of control is a great tool for living well. And its value only increases when it is properly understood. 

### **CT: Why it’s not really about control**

CD: Know what the dichotomy of control means

Since the dichotomy of control is a dichotomy, then it is like two circles that don’t intersect at all. In one circle are the things up to us, in the other circle are the things not up to us. The first thing to clarify when understanding the dichotomy of control is what is the actual quality the things in each circle share. What does it mean when Epictetus says something is in our control or up to us?

 This is one of the biggest misunderstandings of the dichotomy of control. The dichotomy of control isn’t really about control at all, at least not in terms of how we typically think about it colloquially in modern english.

But this focus on control leads to people either rejecting Stoicism as incoherent, or hating themselves for failing to live up to the simple promise that we should be able to ‘control’ our opinions, reactions, and emotions. 

To understand what Epictetus really means, we will have to quickly go over some Greek. 

Remember the first line of the handbook is: 

“Some things are in our control and others not.”  
Sentence\_id: 1  
Location: Handbook 1

Often it is also translated by other authors as ‘Some things are up to us and others are not’.

The phrase being translated as ‘in our control’ is eph’ hēmin. These are two words. Hēmin is just a pronoun. It means “us”. The other word is epi, a preposition. It appears as eph’ because Greek words ending in vowels are often elided, which means their spelling changes before a word beginning with a vowel or a rough breathing. The meaning of the preposition epi changes based on the grammatical case of the word it proceeds. In this circumstance, hēmin is in the dative case.

What does this tell us about what this phrase means? Well, epi with a dative can mean a number of things, but most relevantly here, it means ‘to depend upon’ or ‘to be in the power of’. What Epictetus is literally saying is that some things ‘depend upon’ us, or are caused by us, and some things do not. This is represented in the other common way to translate the dichotomy of control I mentioned earlier, which is that some things are ‘up to us’.

There is no mention here of ‘control’ in the Greek, and this was on purpose. The Stoics were much more concerned with causes, than with the concept of ‘controlling’ other things. Imagine someone insults me and I get incredibly angry. The relevant question for the Stoics is what ‘caused’ the anger? What does the anger depend on? They would say that the anger depends upon me. My beliefs and interpretations of the situation caused it. This is shown by the fact that someone else can get called the exact same nasty name and not get angry at all. So since I am the cause of my anger, it is my job to fix or resolve the anger. Does the other person’s mean comment depend upon me? Did I cause it? No, it is caused by that person’s character, and thus they are the person responsible for changing it. 

What we are left with is not a Dichotomy of Control, if we want to speak precisely, but a dichotomy of cause or dependence. I am responsible for, and should focus upon, the things that depend upon me, namely, my beliefs, my decisions, and my character. These are the things that matter, which determine if I am a good or bad person, and If I live a happy or unhappy life. I do not mistakenly think my happiness or value is determined by the things not ‘up to’ me. As you can see, control has nothing to do with this idea of dependency.

But what is wrong about the word ‘control’? At first, there is a large appeal to it. People want to have ‘control’ over their lives. They want to be able to determine the kind of person they are. And any philosophy that offers absolute unalienable control will strongly appeal to this desire, even if it is just control over something as small as our reactions and judgements. 

You will also notice that I’ve been using the word control throughout this series. I’ve chosen to do this because most people are familiar with the dichotomy of control by that name. Also, because, unlike some, I don’t think it gets things so wrong we can’t describe things this way.

We just need to be clear on what we mean by control, and avoid the common misunderstandings that come up when we encounter this phrase. 

Talking about control seems to lead to one of two major misunderstandings about Stoic philosophy, depending on how control is understood. According to the Cambridge Dictionary, control is the ability “to decide or strongly influence the particular way in which something will happen.” However, both of these ways of interpreting ‘control’ lead to misunderstandings of Stoic philosophy.

First, there are those who take ‘control’ to mean something very strong. They think the Dichotomy of Control means that the Stoics believe we have the ability to decide, in any given moment, our reactions, our behaviours, and our choices. 

But the Stoics do not believe that. If I have an addiction, or am ignorant about something, or have a tendency to get angry at the slightest provocation, the Stoics do not think I can just ‘decide’ not to be like that. They do not think I have control over these ingrained aspects of my character, if we take ‘controlling’ my actions and behaviors to mean I can just choose to be immediately different. Likewise, I cannot immediately change my habits, my subconscious ways of thinking, or my dispositions to act in certain ways in response to trauma or stress. No Stoic argues that I can ‘control’ or ‘will’ myself into being a perfect person without years of strenuous work, and that we lack this ability is apparent to anyone who has tried and struggled with self-improvement. 

What the Stoics do believe is that I am the cause of these poor or undesirable parts of my character. These parts of my character depend upon me. And so they are my responsibility to change. This is a subtle point. I am responsible for my character. I cannot put the responsibility for who I am onto others, or past circumstances. But I also do not have total control over my behaviour. Changing takes work, time, and practice. But the more nuanced Stoic position is only apparent when we drop our focus on ‘controlling’ ourselves.

Whatever the Stoics think we have this kind of absolute ‘control’ over, it is certainly less than the number of things that are ‘dependent on us’. If we think of the Stoic dichotomy of control as a division between things we control in this strong sense \- that it can change immediately – it might seem incoherent. The idea that we can totally ‘control’ our responses or behaviors makes Stoicism seem like an idealistic philosophy oblivious to complicated problems associated with addictions, habits, and emotional turmoil. And this would be a problem for Stoicism, if it was what Stoics were committed to. Fortunately it is not.

Another issue with understanding the dichotomy of control in the absolute sense of control, is that it can make people feel like failures. It is easy to fall into thinking of this sort: “Stoicism teaches that we should be able ‘control’ our behaviors and responses. I cannot seem to do that. Therefore I am failing as a Stoic, or I am doing something wrong.” But, once again, this kind of reasoning arises from a misunderstanding of the dichotomy of control. You are not failing if you are struggling to put Stoicism into practice. That is part of the journey.

The second way of understanding the word ‘control’ is to take it to mean ‘to strongly influence’. I see this use all the time in athletic contexts. A coach, channeling his inner Stoic, might say something like: “You don’t control the opponents/referees/your teammates, you only control how well you play”. Or when someone thinks: “I can only control how I live, and the things that I do with my life.” This seems to be the idea that we control our ‘actions’, or what we do out in the world.  

This picture of ‘control’ is much too loose and includes far too many things that the Stoics would say are ‘not up to us’.  We certainly strongly influence what we do out in the world, but it is not up to us. For example, how well I play a soccer match is not ‘up to me’, because it is dependent on not getting the flu, or spraining my ankle, or being hit by a car on the way to the game. These are external things that can stop me from playing well. 

The problem with this view of the dichotomy of control, is that it lacks some of the most important lessons Epictetus is trying to teach us. Yes, it is probably prudent for all of us to focus on developing things we strongly influence, like our hobbies or our personal relationships, instead of worrying about things we don’t influence. But it is not Stoic to think that our happiness depends on them. You will inevitably find yourself disappointed and anxious if you place too much importance on these external pursuits which are not fully ‘up to you’. 

If we can’t simply use the English word ‘control’, then what is a snappy way to think of this division between what is up to us and what is not? 

My favorite way to think about what is up to you, is in terms of ‘what something external to you cannot stop’. By external, I mean something that isn’t your character, choices, or beliefs. You might not have control over your temper yet, but that anger is still up to you, because what is stopping you from being calm is your own character, something internal. Travelling to a foreign country, or playing well in a soccer game, or being forgiven, are things not up to you, because these can all be prevented by circumstances external to you.

This kind of division might be less immediately appealing than thinking of things we ‘control’ vs. things we do not, but ultimately it is more faithful to Stoic philosophy. It is a key to understanding Stoicism, and unlocking its benefits. You can’t put this tool to use, much less understand its role in the larger system, without understanding what it really means.

For this course, I will continue to alternate between control and ‘what is up to you’. I think both are fine if you keep in mind what Epictetus intended. But what he intended was a division between the things that depend on us, that only we can determine, and the things external to us, that depend on things we cannot determine.  

### **CT: What actually is up to you**

CD: Understand the key distinction 

In the last lesson, we dug into what it means for something to be up to us. For something to be up to us, means that it depends on us, or that we are the cause of it. If something depends on us, then nothing external to us can stop it. It doesn’t mean we can immediately determine it. Nor is it so weak to only mean that we have influence over it. 

Since the dichotomy of control is an actual dichotomy, a separation between two things with no intersection, it’s like a ven diagram where the circles don’t touch. Last lesson was about the qualities of each circle. This lesson is about what objects sit in each circle. What are the actual things that are ‘up to us’ and ‘not up to us’ according to Epictetus.

Well, let’s go back to Epictetus’ list:

“Some things are in our control and others not. Things in our control are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever are our own actions. Things not in our control are body, property, reputation, command, and, in one word, whatever are not our own actions.”  
Sentence\_id: 1-3  
Location: Handbook 1

Epictetus lays out really clearly, some examples of what is up to us: opinion, pursuit, desire & aversion.

Often those progressing in Stoicism take these as examples of psychological states. They think Epictetus is saying that we control what we think and feel. And this is true in a sense.

But what is often missed is that these are actually translated Greek technical terms. Epictetus is referring to specific psychological functions that would be well understood to all ancient Stoics, and that are fundamental in how the Stoics understand human thought. 

Opinion, or Doxa, are the beliefs we form that are not quite knowledge. Orthodox, for example, means to hold the correct or normal opinions. In Stoicism, we form opinions when we assent to an impression. 

Pursuit, or Horme, is the psychological pull we feel towards things we judge are beneficial to us but not necessary for happiness. Like what we feel when you’d prefer to watch a movie or go bowling, but won’t let a change of plans ruin your night. We feel horme when we assent to an impression, form an opinion, concerning what is preferable. Its opposite is aphorme, or an impulse not to do something.

Desire, or Orexis, and Aversion, Ekklisis in Greek, are like horme but much stronger. They are the psychological impulses you experience when you form an opinion about something you judge to be good or bad.

And for the Stoics, including Epictetus, ‘good’ and ‘bad’ have their own specific meaning too\! Good and bad are not indications of subtle preference. You wouldn’t say, speaking technically, that this was a ‘good’ movie. Good and bad, for the Stoics, are things happiness depends upon. 

This is in contrast with preferable things, which we want but our happiness doesn’t depend upon. So desire and aversion are technical terms. They refer to specific responses we feel when we form opinions about what is good or bad, with the strong, technical stoic definitions. Remember, good things should always be pursued and may even be necessary for happiness. So desire is what you feel when you think you need to date that one person to be happy. Aversion is what you feel when you are being bullied and don’t want see or interact with the people making you uncomfortable. They are strong sensations.

Desire and aversion also lead to our strongest passions. We feel the most intense emotions, grief, anger, jealousy, when we think something good or bad has happened, something that our happiness depends upon.

So pursuit is the gentle feeling we have when we judge something to be preferable, or not preferred, and desire and aversion are really strong feelings we experience when we form opinions about good and bad things. 

So with that context in place, what is up to us?

Actually very little. Just the opinions we form, and then the psychological impulses, either pursuit, desire, or aversion, that follow from those opinions. More simply, what is up to us is the decisions we make and the impulses and emotions that follow from them.

Remember that for the Stoics emotions or passions were seen as the direct results of our judgements. So anger is the desire to get revenge on someone that has harmed you. Fear is the aversion to avoid something you think will harm your ability to be happy.

When Epictetus is talking about pursuit, desire and aversion he is including our emotions in this list.

It’s also important to understand the link between opinion and assent. The Stoics talk a lot about assenting carefully to your impressions of situations. If someone insults you, be careful before you assent to the impression that you have somehow been harmed. 

The reason for this is that assent leads to opinion. Assent is just the act of agreeing with an impression so that it becomes a belief instead of, well, a first impression. When it’s a belief, it becomes a part of you instead of something happening to you.

So while Epictetus doesn’t mention assent, if we have an opinion that means we’ve assented to something. Our opinions come from our assents. We decide what we think.

There are two important implications of this precise understanding of what is up to us:

First, all of these things that are up to us are internal. They are all psychological processes. Our opinions are up to us, and how we respond to them, but there is nothing external to us that is up to us.

Second, the quality of being up to us is binary: Things are either up to us or they are not, and anything external to our psychology doesn’t make the list. 

There are obviously things external to you that you influence but recognize are not up to you: how well your business does is something you influence, even though it’s not up to you. That’s because a business has a lot of moving parts besides you. There are the behaviours of your coworkers and customers. You can exert influence on them, more than you can change the global economy, but still only influence. Most beginning Stoics would understand this as an example of something that is not up to us. 

But even how we impact the world is not up to us. 

You might think, if you are a football player, that how well you play is up to you but whether you win is not. But how well you play depends on things outside your opinions and the impulses to act that they generate.

For example, how well you play depends on if you got a virus the night before, or if you sprained your ankle walking to the game. This is because there is a gap between the mind and the world. What we decide to do, play well, is not necessarily what happens, no matter our conviction or drive. There is a strong connection between how we decide to act, and what we actually do in the world, but it’s not a direct link.

The excellent person might decide to help a child in a flood, but they can be held back by the waves. What is up to us is internal to our minds. What we actually do out in the world, is not. 

So there are two key points from this lesson to carry forward:

First, the things up to us are very few, and specific. It is the opinions we form when we confront impressions of the world, or, in other words, the results of how we assent to our impressions, and then the impulses or psychological compulsion we feel to act depending on those opinions. 

Second, the things up to us are all internal to our psychology. They exist within our minds. There is nothing external that is up to us. My sphere of control ends at my decision to help the child or score the goal. Whether that occurs is no longer up to me.

### **CT: The dichotomy of identity**

CD: Apply this to yourself

So far, we’ve discussed how Epictetus thinks what is up to us are our internal reasoning faculties: the decisions we make and the impulses and emotions that follow from these.

But why are these the things that are up to us? Well, I argued because they are the things that nobody outside of us can stop.

But what is implicit here, is an argument around identity. The dichotomy of control can also be thought about also as a dichotomy of identity. It can go beyond improving your focus, to changing your picture of who and what you believe you are. 

As I mentioned earlier, the things up to us are a part of who we are. The things not up to us, are like that because they are external to us. They are not a part of who we are. This subtle shift will help refocus your application of the dichotomy of control

Let’s turn to one example of the dichotomy of control in practice in the discourses. Epictetus, in his usual style, isn’t talking about navigating traffic or gossip, but about how a Stoic could respond to the threat of imprisonment by a tyrant. 

The hypothetical tyrant is threatening Epictetus, and as a teaching technique, Epictetus plays out a conversation between a skilled Stoic and the despot: 

“I will put you in chains. Man, what are you talking about? Me in chains? You may fetter my leg, but my will not even Zeus himself can overpower. I will throw you into prison. My poor body, you mean.”  
Sentence\_id: 10721-10726  
Location: Discourses 1.1

On one hand, we can read this as a traditional example of the dichotomy of control. Epictetus is focusing on the things up to him, his choices, and not on the things not up to him, the choice of the tyrant, imprisonment, threat of physical pain. 

It’s not up to him if the tyrant chains him up, but how he acts is up to him. This is why the Stoic is always free, even when threatened. Epictetus goes on to note that he must be exiled or imprisoned, but he doesn’t have to lament or cry about it. That freedom, the freedom to choose how we react, always exists for the Stoic.

This is a good lesson, but I want to shift our focus to the identity argument implicit here. The tyrant threatens to chain him up, and Epictetus clarifies that you can only chain his leg. The tyrant threatens to throw him in prison, and Epictetus responds that you can only throw his body in prison. 

Throughout the discourses, Epictetus clarifies that he is not his body, but the choices he makes. He has a body, but that is not who he is. In Book 3, he says to a student.

“You are not flesh and hair, but you are will; and if your will is beautiful, then you will be beautiful.”  
Sentence\_id: 13858  
Location: Discourses 3.1.39

Εpictetus says we are not even our flesh, let alone our reputation or our careers. We are our will. If we want to be beautiful, we must make our will beautiful, because they are the same thing.

The word translated as ‘will’ here and in the example of the tyrant earlier is another technical Greek term. It is the prohairesis. 

A full discussion of the prohairesis could take an entirely new course, but at a high-level it means something like the faculty of choice.

Hairesis is selection or choice. To select or pick something out of a set of options. ‘Pro’ is a prefix, it means ‘before’ or ‘pre’. So the pro-hairesis is the thing that pre-selects. It is the thing that works pre-selection to decide what you will choose. 

This is why it is translated as will. My preferred English translation is the faculty of choice. That makes clear that we are talking about a psychological faculty, but it has a distinct enough name it seems like a Stoic technical term, unlike the more generic will. But Prohairesis is fine as well in the original Greek.

According to Epictetus the prohairesis’ main faculties are assent and impulse. It is the part of our psychology that assents to impressions, and then experiences the impulse, or psychological response to that impression. 

This should sound familiar. As a reminder, in the handbook Epictetus says: 

“Some things are in our control and others not. Things in our control are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever are our own actions.”   
Sentence\_id: 1  
Location: Handbook 1

We discussed earlier that opinion is what you form with assents, and pursuit, desire, and aversion are forms of impulses. In other words, if our prohairesis is that part of our minds that makes assents and feels impulses, Epictetus is saying what is up to us is our prohairesis. 

We are the prohairesis, and it is what is up to us. It is up to us, because we are *it*. Someone else can chain our leg, or throw our body in jail, but they can’t harm the things up to us or control the things up to us, our choice and the consequences of those choices. And if that is our identity, if we are a choice making thing, then this means they can’t harm or control us.

Let’s use a concrete example. People have different conceptions of themselves. Some people identify with their work or their art, the things they produce out in the world. Some people identify with their body, they want to be beautiful or strong. Some people identify with their reputation, they think they are what people think of them.

For any of these people, because they identify themselves with something external to their minds, they can be harmed by others. Someone can tarnish your work, damage your body, or sully your reputation. 

For the Stoic, we are our prohairesis, the faculty of choice, the part of us that makes decisions. If that is beautiful, then we are beautiful. It doesn’t matter what happens to our body or reputation. That is why the Stoic can laugh at the tyrant’s threat, because the tyrant can only damage the body, but that doesn’t scare the Stoic. 

With that context in mind, let’s consider the ethical and practical implications of this distinction. 

There are two aspects of the dichotomy of control: Focus on the things up to you, and detach from the things not up to you.

When we focus on detachment, often the dichotomy of control is taught like a pragmatic argument towards a goal. It is pointless to be upset about the weather because we can’t control the weather, so focus on what you can control and bring an umbrella.

But that’s only part of it.

The dichotomy of control is also teaching us to not see ourselves, or attach our identity, to the things outside ourselves.

Epictetus says in chapter 1 of the handbook:   
“Be able to say to every harsh appearance, "You are but an appearance, and not absolutely the thing you appear to be." And then examine it by those rules which you have, and first, and chiefly, by this: whether it concerns the things which are in our own control, or those which are not; and, if it concerns anything not in our control, be prepared to say that it is nothing to you.”  
Sentence\_id: 10-11  
Location: Handbook 1

This is often taken as a lesson about detachment to externals. But it’s also about identity. Say to the things not in your control that they are nothing to you, because they are not you.

It’s not just that we get upset at weather or traffic. It’s that we attach our identity to what other people think of us. We see our value in our career, our hairline, our bodies. And when we attach ourselves to something external to us, we feel as if we are harmed when the external thing is damaged.

If our body is damaged, or ages, we think we have become worse. If someone insults us, we think they have damaged us. So we grieve over our lost beauty. Or we strike out in anger at the person who has insulted us. We feel these extreme emotions, because we see them as the correct response to a world where our happiness depends on the actions of others. A world where we are vulnerable to others.

But, the Stoic says, these are not who we are. We are not our body, or our reputation. We are the choices we make. So we should practice detachment from these external things, because they are not what matters or what makes us happy. 

Second, when we focus on the things up to us, Epictetus is not just telling us to care about the decisions we make for the pragmatic benefits. The advice is not that it is raining so we should bring an umbrella if we don’t want to get wet.

It’s more than that, it’s that we should care about developing ourselves as the ultimate goal of the project of life. We should be attached to being the best people we can be. 

When you view the dichotomy of control through the dichotomy of identity, it takes on a whole different flavor. We should care about who we are. Because that is up to us and making ourselves better people will bring us happiness. We should not be attached to things external to us. Our wealth, our appearances, our reputation. Because these are not us. We often think they are, but they aren’t. 

In Plato’s republic, Socrates argues that it is better to be a good person that is hated, than a bad person who is adored and rich. 

To believe this is to really have internalized the dichotomy of identity. Better to have perfected yourself, than be a bad person and fortunate with the things not up to you. Because, at the end of the day, these things are nothing to you.

### **CT: The dichotomy of Value**

CD: Live like a Stoic

In the previous lesson, I argued that you can also think of the dichotomy of control as a kind of dichotomy of identity. We are our prohairesis, the part of us that makes choices, and it is the one thing up to us. 

This leads into a final nuance for the dichotomy of control, what I’ll call the dichotomy of value. The dichotomy of control does not just divide what is up to us and who we are. It also divides things that have value, from things that don’t have value.

According to Stoicism, the only things that can be good or bad, are the things up to us. Anything not up to us are indifferent. 

As Epictetus says:

“The being (nature) of the Good is a certain Will; the being of the Bad is a certain kind of Will. What then are externals? Materials for the Will.”  
Sentence\_id: 11967-11971  
Location: Discourses 29.1

So, the only good and bad things are certain dispositions of our will. Again, ‘will’ here is a translation of the prohairesis, our ability to make choices about which impressions we assent to, and the psychological impulses that follow from those choices.

But now we have the necessary background to understand why. Good and bad, in Stoicism, are terms meant to describe things that can make us happy or ruin our lives. Someone is happy by achieving good things, and unhappy by facing bad things. 

As shown in the last lesson, we are our prohairesis. And so, Epictetus is saying the only thing good for us is to be good, to make good choices. The only thing bad for us is to be bad, make bad choices.

The quality of our lives depends on the quality of our choices, the classic Stoic idea.  
Our choices matter, they can be good or bad. Things external to us are indifferent. They are materials to be chosen from and navigated. 

But this division, between what has value and is indifferent, also falls along the dichotomy of control. Good and bad is something we can only say about things up to us.

One of the biggest misunderstandings of the dichotomy of control I see is that people think we should practice detachment from externals for pragmatic reasons. There is no point crying about spilt milk *because* you cannot fix the milk. 

But really, Stoicism is saying we should not care about the spilt milk because it does not matter in the same way our choices do. We are not rendered unhappy by spilt milk, as we are by anger or jealousy or envy. Spilt milk is not directly related to us. Those passionate responses, and the assents that led to them, are directly related to us. My day is ruined by my anger, not by a bit less milk.

The dichotomy of identity, that I am only my choices, leads to the dichotomy of value, that my choices are the only thing that is good or bad. My choices are the only things that are good or bad, because I am a choosing thing. Their quality is what makes me excellent or terrible, happy or unhappy, nothing else.

Epictetus says:

“I have learned to see that every thing which happens, if it be independent of my will, is nothing to me.”  
Sentence\_id: 12033  
Location: Discourses 29.24

This is not some cavalier quote. We can take him literally here. If it is not a part of my choice, my prohairesis, then it literally does not concern me. It is nothing to me *because* it is not a part of me. 

This is a radical Stoic claim, that good and bad, happiness and unhappiness, greatness and ruin, are determined only by your character. That these are things that play out inside of ourselves, and are not dependent on luck, fortune, or good standing. 

Many of the most common misunderstandings, or bastardizations of Stoicism, come from failing to make this distinction \- this dichotomy of value. Focusing on what is up to you is not about mental resilience on your way to career success, or money. It’s not pragmatic advice for achieving an external goal.

It is the key ethical claim of Stoicism reduced to a single call to action. Focus on what is up to you, namely your choices and your character, because that is where good and bad is. In other words, this is the domain where we become excellent. The material through which we achieve happiness.

The Stoic does not have extreme emotions because they understand this. They understand that they are empowered. That the quality of their life depends on them. 

As Epictetus said:

“Whom then do I fear? the master of what? The master of things which are in my own power? There is no such master. Do I fear the master of things which are not in my power? And what are these things to me?”  
Sentence\_id: 11984-11989  
Location: Discourses 29.4

The Stoic is brave and serene because they have no one to fear but themselves. There is no master that can control them, or tyrant that can imprison them. 

There are only masters of the things not in our power, the external things. And what are these to us, Epictetus asks rhetorically \- because he understands that his students will know the answer \- They are nothing to us. 

With this in mind, let’s return to Epictetus’ original quote. 

“Some things are in our control and others not. Things in our control are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever are our own actions. Things not in our control are body, property, reputation, command, and, in one word, whatever are not our own actions.The things in our control are by nature free, unrestrained, unhindered; but those not in our control are weak, slavish, restrained, belonging to others.” 

Sentence\_id: 1-9  
Location: Handbook 1

Now we should be able to understand Epictetus’ meaning: 

What is up to us are the psychological components of our prohairesis. It’s up to us, meaning it depends on us, but not that we can control or determine it immediately.

Our prohairesis is also our identity, what we are and should rightly identify with and be judged by. Because the part of us that makes choices is our identity, it is also where good and bad exists. Happiness or unhappiness depend upon only the quality of these choices.

So when you feel compelled, or harmed, or have enemies, it’s because you have forgotten this distinction.

You have made one of several errors.

You have tried to control something that is not up to you. You have identified with, or put your self-conception, into something that isn’t you, like your body or your career. Or you have ascribed value to something that isn’t your faculty of choice. You have judged something that is nothing to you, as being good or bad.

The dichotomy of control is so much more than a tool for anyone, Stoic or non-Stoic, to use to reduce or manage stress. It goes much deeper than that. It is a reminder of the radical Stoic claim that our happiness depends only on our character, a call to focus on Stoic self-improvement and a reminder to avoid these common mistakes along the way.
