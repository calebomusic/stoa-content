### **Stoic Psychology \- Theory**

### **Introduction:**

Imagine you are at work in your office. It is a normal day, and then, suddenly, there is smoke. Someone screams “Fire\!”, and everyone starts to panic. Some of your coworkers scream, and others rush for the exits. What do you do? Do you leave? Do you look for others? Do you wait to learn more? If you do leave, do you take the door on the right or the left? How do you know it is the right one?

Consider another example. You are happy, you like your job. But you receive an offer to travel and work in another country. The pay is better and this is a professional opportunity, but you won’t know anyone there, you will be leaving your family and friends behind. You have a few weeks to either accept or decline. If you decline, you likely won’t get this chance again. What do you do?

Both of these examples are life-changing decisions. 

Sometimes we make decisions very quickly because the situation demands it. Other times we have time to think about it. Either way, For Stoics, a good life is just a series of good choices; moments when, faced with multiple options, you make the right choice and do the right thing with conviction.

But Stoicism is a practical philosophy. It doesn’t just tell us to choose well, it trains us to make good choices.

Sometimes, Stoic philosophy is like a fire drill. It is something we practice beforehand so we are ready to make quick decisions in the future.

Other times, Stoicism is like a friend or mentor. Something we can turn to in moments of deliberation when we have time to decide carefully between multiple options. 

In either case, the Stoics know we act well when we think well. And we think well by understanding and training our minds.

In this course, you will learn about what the ancient Stoics think goes behind a good action. Namely, how do the Stoics think our minds work, and how does an excellent mind, one that leads us to good decisions, look different than a normal mind? You can then use their framework, just as they did, to make better decisions and manage your emotions.

The Stoics see every choice as being made up of 4 critical stages: Impression, Reflection, Assent, and Impulse.

First, there is the impression, the initial stimulus. That’s the smell of smoke or the job offer phone call.

Then there is a period of reflection, even if it is short. This is where you weigh up the options available to you.

Then there is what the stoics call ‘assent’ the moment of choice when you commit to an option.

Finally, there is ‘impulse’ The psychological pull towards the action you have chosen with your assent. The motivation you feel to act out your plan.

This course will cover this critical four-part psychological process of impression, reflection, assent, and impulse.

We will also explore Stoic lessons for how you can perform well in each of these stages. 

At the end of this course, not only will you have a better understanding of how the Stoics thought about human psychology, but you will be prepared to put Stoic advice, training methods, and tools for thinking well into practice.

Stoic psychology provides a model for how to make better decisions, and manage your emotions, and a method for transforming yourself into the kind of person who can make confident choices \- just like Marcus Aurelius or Epictetus.

### **Impression:**

In the past lesson, we introduced the idea that according to the Stoics, every decision has four stages: Impression, Reflection, Assent, and Impulse.

Impressions are where this process all begins. 

In Stoic psychology, An impression is just that, an impression upon the mind that something is the case. The Greek word for this is phantasia. This is where we get the English words fantasy and fantastic. 

It comes from the verb Phantazo, Which means “to become visible or appear”

Essentially, an impression is a mental picture of how things are. It is an initial appearance. 

When a car honks its horn, I receive an impression of a loud sound. When I see the sun, I receive the impression that it is day. 

But why this focus on impressions? 

The key idea behind impressions that the Stoics want to make clear is that the mind does not have direct access to reality, only impressions. Impressions are produced, or caused by reality in some way, but they do not necessarily accurately represent reality. 

As the popular slogan goes “Don’t Believe Everything you think”. 

We can prove this Stoic idea rather easily by showing that the same event can cause two different impressions.

If someone screams “Fire\!” then one person might receive the impression that there is a fire. 

The other person, who knows the alarmist to have a history of inappropriate jokes, might receive the impression that Steve has made another joke in bad taste.

The same external reality, with two different impressions. Now either person could be wrong. Steve might be lying or he might be trying to warn others of danger. Both are possible. The key takeaway though is that the same external stimulus can cause different irreconcilable impressions.

So just because you experience an impression does not give you any reason to believe that this impression is a true or accurate representation of reality. 

Impressions are produced by something but are not identical to that thing. They might be accurate, but this always requires further investigation.

Modern psychology might not use the language of impressions, but the Stoics are pointing to a psychological fact that our initial perception is never to be accepted as automatically true. We don’t perceive reality as unbiased. We receive an impression of reality filtered through our past experiences, knowledge, and even our moods or emotions.

Just like two people can have different impressions of the same event, you can have different impressions depending on what you know or feel that day. 

For a parent, the same behavior from your child can seem either cute or unbelievably frustrating, depending on your mood or energy level. Again, the same reality but, a different impression.

Similarly, the Stoics argued that skilled experts have different impressions than beginners. A car mechanic literally sees something different than me when he looks at an engine. He receives an impression of the year, the model, and some ideas about quality relative to other cars. I only receive the impression of a hunk of metal that is an engine. 

But we can become experts and change over time, so we can receive different impressions. If you read a book now that you enjoyed as a child, it will be the same words but a different experience. 

Once we understand the idea of impressions, what do we do about it? How should it change how we live? If this idea of impression demands a behavior from us, it is to *actively recognize impressions*.

As the Stoic Epictetus said: 

“Make a practice at once of saying to every strong impression: ‘An impression is all you are.’” 

id: 1670

The Stoic does not live an unexamined life. They don’t take in impressions and pump out behavior. How they act is in their control because they are aware of and respond to their impression. 

The first step to taking control of what is up to you is to recognize that you receive impressions. Some of these accurately represent reality and some don’t. We call these impressions true or false depending on how accurate they are. But even true impressions are not reality. Like a picture or a TV documentary, they might show you an accurate picture of something, but they are not the thing itself. 

This sounds simple, but it is one of the hardest things you can do. It requires an intense mindfulness, of the kind we practice and develop through Stoa meditations. 

One practical way to start this is to focus on big moments that create strong emotions, not simple practical things.

Technically, your impression of a person walking down the street is just an impression and not reality, but this is not really where you should be focusing your attention.

Instead, practice reminding yourself of impressions when something happens that makes you feel something: Nervous, happy, angry, sad.

For now, just practice reminding yourself that what is producing your emotion is an impression. It might be true, but it might not.

Doing that is the first step to controlling the parts of your life that are up to you: your choices and responses to situations. 

### **Reflection:**

In the last lesson, we discussed the idea of impressions or the Stoic idea that our minds engage with representations of the world, but not the world itself. If there is a healthy behavior associated with impressions it is *actively recognizing impressions as impressions*, and not forgetting they exist.

As Epictetus says:

“Make a practice at once of saying to every strong impression: ‘An impression is all you are.’” 

id: 1670

The Stoics think the next step in the human psychology of action is reflection. Reflection is the gap between impression and belief. It is the space between what we initially perceive, and what we take to be true. 

The full Epictetus quote actually goes like this:

“Make a practice at once of saying to every strong impression: ‘An impression is all you are.’ Then test and assess it with your principles. Ask, ‘Is this something that is, or is not, in my control?’” id: 1670

That second half is reflection. To test and assess the impression against your principles or current beliefs, before you take it to be true.

There is a famous modern quote that reads:

“Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom.” id: 1862

The space between stimulus and response is reflection. It is the opportunity to act differently and to change how we think; what grants us freedom to transform ourselves into who we want to be, instead of just responding to our surroundings. 

The Stoics use several metaphors to talk about reflection.

Epictetus compares it to a set of scales. On one end of the scales are your principles. Then you weigh the impression against them. You measure the impression to test its truth.

Another metaphor is that your mind is an inner citadel or city. Reflection is the border guards. Some idea, in the form of an impression, tries to enter, but it has to pass interrogation first. A weak city will either let anyone in or no one in at all. 

A strong city has guards that are tough but fair. They examine the documents and let those impressions that deserve to enter, and refuse all others.

This is what Epictetus means when he talks about making good use of your impressions. It’s putting them to the test through reflection. Epictetus says:

“What quality belongs to you? The intelligent use of impressions. If you use impressions as nature prescribes, go ahead and indulge your pride, because then you will be celebrating a quality distinctly your own.” id: 814

There are two ways to practice reflection. Let’s take a simple example. 

Imagine someone has insulted you, and pointed out an insecurity. Whether that is physical or social, they try to hurt you by bringing up something you don’t like about yourself.

Those words are just sound waves until they reach your ears. They enter your mind as an impression, filtered through your own beliefs about yourself, the other person, and what you value.

At first, that impression is going to manifest like something bad has happened. You have a good reason to be offended. So how do you practice Stoic reflection?

First, you need to pause. You can’t reflect well if you are running on auto-pilot; there is no good reflection if you are not reflecting at all. Make sure there is a space between stimulus and response. Maybe this means you have to walk away from a situation or take time to consider it. 

If pausing is hard for you, consider a mindfulness practice to build this skill. This could be meditation, or it could be journaling. It could even be going for walks to think through a problem.

Reflective people think about the decisions they make and how they respond to the impressions in their lives. They take this process seriously and don’t skip it.

If the mind is a city, then you want your border guards to stop and question anything that tries to enter. It may be less work for everyone involved if the guards just relax and let everyone through \- but the best cities have diligent guards.  

The second way to practice reflection is to improve the quality of reflection.

If the border guards are interrogating the impressions we want them to get it right. Let in those ideas that are true, and refuse the ones that are false.

To improve the quality of your reflection, you need a standard to judge impressions against. For example, Epictetus asks us to examine if the subject of our impression is inside our control or not. If It isn’t, then you shouldn’t judge it to be good or evil, because the only things good and evil are up to us. 

Another testing principle would be to apply the view from above. If I zoom out and look at myself from the sky, does this impression still matter to me? Or if I expand my time horizon, does it still matter? Will it still matter in 5 days or 5 years?

These principles give us a standard of judgment to apply against whatever impressions come our way. When an impression is about the nature of good and evil, we should use Stoic theory, but the Stoics think a diligent person applies equally rigorous standards to their other roles in life.

A good parent makes sure they have a fair standard of judgment to punish their kids, and not just respond to whatever impression comes their way. A good doctor has a consistent standard of judgment for when to diagnose a patient and when not to. They don’t want their biases to get in the way of sound judgment.

Reflecting on our impressions helps us act excellently in all kinds of situations. 

The idea is to have a good sense of what you believe and stand by it. 

In our example, if you are not an appearance-driven person, who doesn’t judge other people by how they look, then why should you be insulted if someone else insults your appearance? If you don’t care how others look, you shouldn’t care for yourself.

That kind of reasoning is applying your principles to impressions. That would be an example of good Stoic reflecting.

So again, let’s consider that quote from Epictetus in full:

Make a practice at once of saying to every strong impression: ‘An impression is all you are.’ Then test and assess it with your principles. Ask, ‘Is this something that is, or is not, in my control?’ id: 1670

There we see three steps. Identify, pause, and reflect by comparing the impression against our principles and values. In Epictetus' case, he is encouraging students to consider if the impression passes the test of being something up to them. Which, in the case of an insult, it is not.

Reflection is the second step of Stoic psychology and a necessary part of living well. Be sure, no matter what, that you don’t lose the freedom between impression and response.

### **Assent:**

Welcome back to this course on Stoic psychology. In the last two lessons, we covered the ideas of impressions and reflection. 

This lesson is about the third step in the psychology of human behavior: Assent. 

Assent is the Stoic term for when we turn an impression into a belief. 

According to the Stoics, most impressions can’t cause us to believe or do anything. 

We participate in what we believe, by adding our assent to impressions.

For this reason, we are morally responsible for our beliefs and actions. Impressions don’t make us do anything. We are responsible for what we do because we always participate with our assent.

There are some exceptions, some extreme impressions that the Stoics thought could cause an instinctive reaction from us.

A bang of thunder will make even the sage flinch, and anyone who throws their hands in my face will make me blink. These reactions are natural and inherent to humans.

But a good life does not depend on these exceptions. Good character is not built on changing basic human reflexes. It depends on whether you are angry at the people in your life, whether you can motivate yourself to complete the plans you commit to, or whether you are overcome with frustration and sadness, or focus and purpose.

These more complex feelings and personality traits are not caused by impressions. They are caused by our beliefs and character.

And our beliefs and character are caused by our assent. 

Let’s return to the metaphor of our minds like a city. The impression is someone looking to enter, and reflection is the boarding crossing process. We want our reflection to be like guards, who evaluate those that enter. Are they who they say they are? Or maybe they are lying, and trying to smuggle in something bad into the city?

Assent then is the passport stamp the guards give the visitor. It either welcomes them into the city, demands that they leave, or makes them wait for further processing.

The Stoics thought we could do three things when we assent. Either accept the impression as true, reject it as false, or admit that we do not know enough to assent further.

The key to understanding the Stoic idea of character development was that they identified that no one can control our assent. These are decisions we make, and not something someone else can force us to do. 

Someone can control our body against our will, but they can’t make us accept anything into our minds without our participation.

Epictetus once argued with one of his students about this. You can see many modern students of Stoicism saying the same thing. The student argued that other people can control their behavior, for example by controlling their bodies.

The student said:

*“But suppose I choose to walk, and someone obstructs me?’* 

*(Epictetus responds) What part of you will they obstruct? Certainly not your power of assent?*

 *‘No, my body.’ (The student answers)*

*Your body, yes – as they might obstruct a rock.*

*(The student continues) ‘Perhaps…but now I’m not allowed to walk.’* 

*(Epictetus concludes) Whoever told you, ‘Walking is your irrevocable privilege’? I said only that the will to walk could not be obstructed.”* id: *780*

The lesson here is that assent is the one action that can’t be controlled by others. It is the most important thing for a Stoic to get right, and the thing, above all else, that is up to us.

People often talk about what is in our control or outside of our control, like an internal versus external categorization.

They think that Stoicism argues we control the things inside of us, but not the external world.

This isn’t quite right.

Epictetus points out that we don’t control our impressions, only our assent when he says:

“Impressions, striking a person’s mind as soon as he perceives something within range of his senses, are not voluntary or subject to his will, they impose themselves on people’s attention almost with a will of their own. But the act of assent which endorses these impressions is voluntary and a function of the human will.” id: 848

So assent is the psychological process where we accept an impression as true, reject it as false, or admit we need more evidence.

But what does this look like in practice? How can we start practicing assent well?

Well, by now it should be clear that good Stoicism is cumulative, it builds upon itself.

The first step to getting assent right is to just keep practicing what we discussed in the previous lessons. Identify impressions as being just impressions, not necessarily reality. Give yourself time for reflection, a space between impression and assent. And then in that reflection, consider what you believe, and test your impression against that.

If you do that well, the border guards in your mind should begin to have a lot of success stamping the right passports and rejecting the wrong ones.

But I want to add another Stoic strategy that sometimes gets missed. This is the fact that you don’t have to give assent to an impression, either accepting it or rejecting it. It is ok to admit that you don’t know. 

As Marcus Aurelius says: “It is in our power to have no opinion about a thing and not to be disturbed in our soul; for things themselves have no natural power to form our judgments.” id: 962

You don’t need to let that belief into the city of your mind, but you don’t need to reject it as untrue either. You can let it sit for now, and get back to it later.

So practice assenting well, because the quality of your life depends on it. But don’t expect, or think you need to, get this right immediately.

### **Impulse:**

The picture of Stoicism so far might sound a bit sterile. It is all about reasoning, reflecting, and judging. But what about feeling? What about desire and disgust? Elation and frustration? What about that pull inside ourselves, that makes us strive towards a goal until we are exhausted?

These sensations, the Stoics call impulses. They are the final category in the Stoic psychology of human behavior. 

An impulse is what happens when we form a belief that something is worth pursuing or worth avoiding. It’s the basic psychological pull towards what we believe is good and pull away from what we decide is bad.

To stick to our city metaphor, all kinds of people want access to the city of our minds. Some carry simple information or facts like “The United States was founded in 1840” or “The capital of Canada is Winnipeg”. We might call these factual impressions. 

We want to make sure we judge factual impressions correctly. They can lead to poor decisions down the road if we get them wrong, from failing a history test or accidentally booking a flight to Winnipeg, thinking you are visiting Canada’s capital (it is actually Ottawa).

But factual impressions don’t elicit an immediate reaction from our minds. I can learn about the Capital of Canada without feeling anything at all.

But some people who want access to the city of our minds are more energetic. They carry with them ideas about value, like “This food is disgusting” OR “It was wrong what that person did to you, and you should get them back”, OR “It would be a good thing to vacation to Winnipeg”. 

These are what the Stoics called *hormetic* impressions. Horme is just Greek for impulse, so they are called Impulsive impressions.

Impulsive impressions, when you let them into the city of our mind, if you stamp them at the border with an assent, produce a reaction. These reactions are called impulses. Impulses are psychological reactions towards or away from something.

If I think my food is disgusting, I will push away my plate. That is an impulse. Or rather, more technically, my impulse is my attempt to push away my plate, my will to push the disgusting thing away from me.

If I think someone has harmed me, then I will seek out revenge. I will experience an impulse toward revenge.

And if I think it is a good thing to visit Winnipeg, I will take a plan of action to do so, whether that is booking a ticket, or taking an unplanned exit on the highway.

Impulses are the psychological pull towards certain plans of action. They are caused by assenting to impulsive impressions, those about value.

Impulses matter because they determine what you do. We decide what we value, and what actions seem best to us, and then impulses take us the rest of the way.

Good reflections lead to good assents, which means good impulses. And good impulses are a good life of healthy actions.

Impulses are very closely related to what the Stoics call passions as well. There is some debate, both in ancient Stoicism and contemporary academia, about how to classify these, but I think the simplest way is to think of it like this: An impulse is when you feel something because you believe something. A passion is when you feel something extreme and harmful because you believe something false. 

That’s all. When I want to do well on a test I study hard because I think being a good student matters to me: That is an impulse to study. When I break down in tears when I receive a bad grade, because I think my life is ruined, that is a passionate response to a bad grade. My life is not ruined, the situation does not deserve that response.

The easiest way to summarize the Stoics on emotions is that they try to eliminate passions. They don’t want extreme reactions that come from letting in false and harmful impressions.

The reason they want to avoid passions is because, not only do they feel bad, but they also mess up our behavior moving forward. 

The Stoics talked about passions with physical imagery. Getting passionate is like running down a hill or stepping off a cliff. It carries with it a certain momentum that takes us with it, even if we want to slow down or change course.

So how do we practice having the right impulses? The funny thing about impulses is that by the time you have one you don’t want, it is already too late. You can’t stop an impulse. Like someone running downhill, you will have to fall to the bottom.

But you don’t have to keep an impulse going. Once you reach the bottom of the hill, you don’t have to keep running.

So to practice impulses, practice a strategy called “Take 5”. When you are feeling a strong emotion, recognize that this impedes your ability to think well. The time to fight this feeling has already passed (it was assent), now we just ride it out.

Ride out the feeling by ‘taking 5’. Pause for 5 seconds. Practice your breathing. Focus your attention. If you need to step away from a conversation, then do that.

After 5 seconds (or minutes, or days) have passed, return to the idea with a fresh mind that has lost the momentum of the impulse. 

The point here is that you don’t practice having good ‘impulses’. Impulses are something that happens to you after you form a belief about the world. They are a consequence of caring about things and wanting things.

Good impulses are like the jewel on the crown. They are what everyone notices. They are what differentiate the feelings of a happy person from an unhappy one, and the actions of a kind, brave, and generous person from a mean, cowardly, and selfish one.

But like the jewel, they are held up by a foundation. We can’t attack impulses directly. Good impulses are the conclusion of a good chain of reasoning, starting at impression, and going through reflection and assent. 

### **What is up to us:**

Great work making it to the end of this theory course about Stoic psychology on Stoa. I want to bring these ideas back to a core idea in Stoicism: the dichotomy of control. The idea is that we need to focus on what is us because this is what matters and what leads to a good life.

You might not have noticed it, but by completing this theory course you will have mastered the key ideas behind the dichotomy of control. In chapter one of his handbook, Epictetus says: 

“​​Of things some are in our power, and others are not. In our power are what we take up as true, impulse, desire, aversion; and in a word, whatever are our acts. Not in our power are the body, property, reputation, offices, and in a word, whatever are not our own acts.” (Michael Translation)

Epictetus provides a very short list of what is up to us, but it should all be clear by now. What is up to us first, is what we decide to take up as true. What we assent to or form beliefs about.

Our impressions are not up to us, those are produced by the outside world, but how we reflect on them and assent to them is up to us, Epictetus is saying. What follows them are types of impulses: Impulse more generally, and then the strong versions of desire and aversion.

Because what we assent to is up to us, then what we feel is up to us. What we value and desire, and what we hate and want to avoid. These things are up to us because, as we talked about, they follow from assent. They follow from what we take up as true.

What follows then is a list of things not up to us. These are things outside of our psychology. Our body, property, and reputation. These are not up to us, because they are outside of the small psychological process where our true freedom lies: the way we process impressions and how we feel about it after words.

By understanding Stoic psychology, you will have unlocked what Epictetus really means when he is talking about the dichotomy of control.

Let’s end this course with a final reflection from Epictetus on why understanding Stoic psychology is so important to living well. In book 3, chapter 22 of the Discourses, Epictetus says:

“My mind represents for me my medium – like wood to a carpenter, or leather to a shoemaker. The goal in my case is the correct use of impressions.”  id: 751

The Stoic thinks of themselves as a skilled artist. Except, instead of working with wood or leather, they work with their mind. Instead of producing a large house or a beautiful shoe, the Stoic builds an excellent mind and character.

The carpenter understands everything about wood: different types, their relative strengths, their cost, and ways to cut them. Likewise, the Stoics should know everything about their minds: How we form beliefs, why we feel strong emotions, what parts of changing our behavior are up to us, and which aren’t.

In Stoicism, mastering ‘what is up to us’  is making good use of our impressions. Making good use of impressions is the process of skilled Reflection, Assent, and Impulse. 

This is all there is to being a Stoic and living a great life. Everything else is advice and practice for how to do this well. 

It is simple, but it is not easy.

But now, with this understanding of Stoic psychology, you are ready to become like the carpenter of the mind and build yourself into the kind of person you want to be.
