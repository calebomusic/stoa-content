# Productivity

Let’s prepare for a productive and disciplined day.

A day where you finish what needs to get done and do it well.

Because that is who you are. 

Not productive for the sake of busyness – but for the sake of meaningful action that matters. The work itself may not be glamorous, but it is done with purpose.

Let’s start.

Take a few deep breaths.

20

If you want, in order to concentrate, close your eyes.

4

And focus on the breath. At this very moment.

20

Concentrate precisely on the breath. With focus, but without strain.

30

Envision who you will be and the decisions you'll make as you move through your day. Picture yourself acting with self-control and discipline.

30

Continue visualizing yourself going throughout your day. Picture yourself making excellent, and productive decisions.

30

Finally, consider what you’ll do if you lose the rhythm. If you become distracted or make a mistake – return your attention to what matters. Returning to being productive is like regaining our focus during meditation.

30

And bring to mind the most important tasks of the day. Visualize yourself doing them. In this short meditation, focus on the most critical aspects of these tasks.

30

Summarize what you’ll take with you from this meditation.

30

And take a few deep breaths, returning your concentration to the breath.

25

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

4

Take this sense of purpose and concentration with you throughout your day.

Let’s end with a line from Epictetus:

"First say to yourself what you would be; and then do what you have to do."

# Calm Day

Let’s prepare for a calm day.

You cannot control what happens to you. But you can control how you respond. 

This meditation aims to ground us in this Stoic wisdom, preparing us to meet any situation with calm and confidence.

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes.

4

Start by noticing the sensations arising in your body. See them without judgment.

20

And move your attention to the parts of your body involved in breathing. Follow the breath as it moves through your body – and then follow its path as it departs.

20

As you track the movements of the breath, relax. Observe with concentration and ease.

30

Now bring to mind events that may arise today. Consider them like you just considered the breath: see them as they are without unnecessary value judgments.

30

Now visualize yourself facing adversity with calm, whatever it is. Rehearse your moves like a musician mentally practicing a performance. Do it in detail.

30

As you visualize yourself facing adversity well, focus on what is up to you. Your decisions and judgments.

30

Losing your focus and calm is natural. If you make a mistake you can always return to what matters. With this in mind, picture yourself losing your composure. What will you do to return to what matters? 

30

As soon as you lose your composure – that event is in the past. In the present moment, you are faced with the decision to regain it. As Marcus Aurelius says:

"Today I escaped from anxiety. Or no, I discarded it, because it was within me, in my own perceptions—not outside.”

For the next few moments, finish rehearsing your responses with this in mind.

20

And return to the breath. Watch it as it moves through your body.

30

Summarize what you’ll take with you from this meditation.

20

And take a few more deep breaths.

25

Well done.

Bring this sense of calm and confidence with you throughout your day.

Let’s end with a line from Marcus Aurelius:

"Objective judgment, now, at this very moment. Unselfish action, now, at this very moment. Willing acceptance—now, at this very moment—of all external events. That’s all you need."

# A more Stoic day

You can be excellent. Imagine going throughout your life making brilliant decisions, keeping your cool, and acting with virtue.

You can do it. There’s no magic or inborn talent you need – happiness is available to everyone.

But it requires training.

Happiness isn’t achieved in a moment. It is the result of improving day by day. 

Let’s meditate on how you’ll become more Stoic today.

Take a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes.

4

Center yourself by concentrating on the breath.

20

And bring to mind what you’ve done well recently. How have you acted with virtue? It doesn’t matter how small or big these acts are – what matters is that you focus on the good parts of your character.

30

And visualize yourself exhibiting these traits of yours today. How will you maintain the good character you’ve built so far?

30

And now consider times when you’ve missed the mark recently. Where can you improve?

30

Focus on a single trait that you’ll improve today. As Seneca reminded us: we must remove our vices while we can.

30

Set a realistic yet ambitious goal for today: Identify one actionable step, big or small.

30

And summarize what you’ll take with you from this meditation.

30

Take a few deep breaths.

20

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

Well done.

Take steps on the Stoic path today. A better life is possible. Seize it.

Let’s end with motivation from Epictetus:

“If you come up against anything that requires an effort, or is pleasant, or is glorious or inglorious, remember that this is the time of the contest, that the Olympic Games have now arrived, and there is no possibility of further delay, and that it depends on a single day and single action whether progress is to be lost or secured.”

# Presence

The Stoic philosopher Seneca counsels us:

"Life is very short and anxious for those who forget the past, neglect the present, and fear the future."  
id: 1784

Stoics live in the present. They are not held back by the past or disturbed by the future.

This way of being is a skill. Let’s practice it today with meditation.

Start by taking an alert, yet comfortable posture.

4

And take a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes.

4

Pay attention to your environment and the sounds around you, noting them as they arise without judgment.

30

Be present now. Notice the sounds that are occurring in your environment at each moment.

30

Shift your focus to any sensations in your body, observing them come and go without attachment.

30

As you do this, keep your awareness open. Do not hold onto sensations too tightly. Watch them come and go.

20

And move your attention to the breath. Watch it move through your body.

30

And narrow your concentration to the part where the sensation of the breath is the strongest. For some, that is in the stomach, for others is in the nostril.

30

In each moment, be aware of the breath. See it in detail.

30

If you become distracted – by thoughts about the past or future – by sounds – simply note that you became distracted. And return to the breath.

40

Whenever you lose your focus, simply return to the breath. Practicing this movement is an essential part of remaining in the present.

30

Let’s end by focusing precisely on the breath as it is now for the next few moments. Notice it in as much detail as you can.

20

And take a few deep, natural breaths.

20

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

Take this concentration, this way of being, with you into your day.

Be aware of what’s happening. Pay attention to what matters. And live well.

As Seneca encourages us:

"The whole future lies in uncertainty: live immediately."

# Good day

Most people say that they have a good day only if good things happen to them. Whether or not they have a good day is not up to them – but to other people.

For a Stoic, having a good day depends on their decisions and judgments alone. They can experience joy in any circumstance.

With this in mind, let’s meditate on having a good day today.

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

20

Close your eyes. Breathe naturally.

20

And consider events that may happen today. Just run through possibilities, like an athlete imagining what may happen before a match.

30

Now visualize yourself acting well – in whatever circumstance occurs. Focus on what is up to you.

35

As you visualize this, embody emotions of joy, energy, and vitality. 

30

Contrast this possibility with the world where you put your happiness in the hands of external things. Imagine putting your hopes in factors out of your control – and forfeiting your control over your happiness.

35

Use this contrast to motivate you to seek happiness in what is up to you. Get energy from your vision of success and your vision of failure – and confidence in the thought that success is up to you.

20

Summarize what you’ll take with you from this meditation.

20

And take a few deep breaths.

20

Good job.

Having a truly good day isn’t easy. But it’s possible.

Let’s end with the Serenity prayer:

"God grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and wisdom to know the difference."  
id: 1689

Have an excellent day.
