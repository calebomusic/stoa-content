# Intro

Stoicism provides tools to deal with the fact that grief and loss are unavoidable.

Many assume that Stoicism is about refusing to grieve and suppressing emotion. This is not correct, but it is useful to understand where that misconception is coming from.

Even in ancient times, the ancient Stoics were sometimes accused of being too logical and harsh. 

Some of their role models, for example, were unfazed by the deaths of their loved ones. Seneca talks about a Stoic man, Stilpo, facing down a warlord with rebellious resolve after the warlord has murdered his family and sacked his city. The warlord asks why he is not weeping like the others and the Stilpo responds with "I have all my goods with me\!" Stilpo has internalized the Stoic lesson that only what is up to us is what fundamentally matters. Because Stilpo still has command of his thoughts and decisions, the warlord has taken nothing from him. 

Stoicism can offer a kind of invincibility. The Stoic sages like Stilpo could handle any loss and never lose their composure. They meet the definition of a sage given by the Persian poet Rumi:

“The feeling of joy when sudden disappointment comes.”

Yet, we are not sages. It is our role to become better one step at a time. 

To that end, the Stoic Seneca once said:

“While it is a foolish weakness to give way to endless grief when you lose one of those dearest to you, yet it shows an unnatural hardness of heart to express no grief at all: the best middle course between affection and hard common sense is both to feel regret and to restrain it.”

This is the attainable Stoic attitude for people like us.

It is natural to feel sadness. We are bound to others and the world, when we experience loss, we will grieve. The Stoic would never advise suppressing emotion or not caring about loved ones.

What is problematic about grief isn’t sadness itself, but when it obstructs the project of living well.

In Seneca’s words:

“We may weep, but we must not wail.”

How can we form such resolve?

This series will focus on preparing for loss. Grief cannot be escaped. But, we must prepare for it and reflect on how we want to respond. That much is up to us. If we do that, when we experience grief we can ensure we grieve well, without letting loss get in the way of living well.

To that end, this course is all about the Stoic view of loss and how to prepare for it.

First, what is the Stoic view of loss?

The Stoics distinguished between feelings and emotion. Feelings are natural reactions and initial impressions. We do not have direct control over our feelings. In contrast, emotions, of all kinds, are judgments. They are more intense and, to some degree, under our control. The emotion of rage cements our initial feelings and reactions. It includes judgements like the idea that we are certainly correct, others are obviously mistaken, and they deserve to be punished. To understand the difference, consider times when you have quickly experienced anger only to let it disappear after you discovered you had no reason to be angry. If someone cuts you off you may initially feel anger, but that would disappear if you quickly saw that they were experiencing a medical emergency. Contrast this with times when the anger does not disappear, perhaps a time when someone cut you off and you let that experience shape the rest of your day.

The Stoics are concerned with emotions, not feelings. They do not deny natural feelings but compel us to turn our attention to cultivating virtuous emotions based on true judgements, instead of mistaken ones.

Why? Because many emotions, like anxiety, grief, and anger result in suffering. Yet we can change our emotions because we can change our thoughts and judgements.

This is why the Stoics say it’s not things in themselves that cause us suffering, but our interpretation of them. 

The Stoic concern with negative emotions is that they’re built on false judgments and impair our ability to act virtuously. The key problem is not that negative emotions feel bad, but that they can lead to a worse life. This is a subtle, but important difference.

Stoic techniques help us address feelings before they transform into full-blown passions.

Let’s cover three techniques now.

First, there is the strategy of mental preparation. Too often we assume that our life will continue as it is now, without taking into account the possibility of change. Then when our life does significantly change we feel like the world is turned upside down. In such a world, we may be unprepared and make foolish decisions. To avoid this, the Stoics encouraged mentally preparing for all outcomes. 

Second, there is the idea of returning to what is under our control. According to the Stoics, our attention should be focused on what is up to us: our thoughts and decisions. That is it. Everything else can be accepted. With this attitude, we can learn to accept grief and loss.

Finally, we can cultivate positive emotions. Instead of experiencing negative emotions, we can experience tranquility and joy. Of course, not all emotions that feel good lead to virtue. For example, giddiness can lead to overconfidence, which in turn leads to poor decisions.  But good thinking and positive emotions often come together. Emotions built on true judgements help us make better decisions and make us more resilient. 

The exercises in this series will work on each of these strategies.

With such techniques, we can be prepared for tragedy, manage what is up to us, and build a happy life. This is not an easy task, but it is one that all of us must embark on.

# Impermanence

## Intro

Everything is impermanent, nothing lasts. 

As Marcus Aurelius once reminded himself: 

“In a little while you will have forgotten everything; in a little while everything will have forgotten you.”

id: 969

This is one of the key lessons of Stoicism. If we can internalize it, we will gain wisdom and resilience.

The Stoic teacher Epictetus advised his students to constantly inquire into the nature of things. If you care about anything impermanent, whether it is a jug, necklace, or car, remind yourself that you are fond of an impermanent thing. 

The point of this exercise is to highlight the mistaken judgement behind much of our suffering: that whatever is good must last forever. This is not something we go around saying, but our thought patterns and behaviors often reveal that we haven’t fully internalized the temporary nature of things.

It’s the frustration of expectations that causes so much suffering. Our expectation that everything and everyone good will last is mistaken and causes suffering.

So how can we remove this distortion? One way we can do so is by contemplating the vast and temporary nature of things.

That is what we will do today.

## Outro

Our lives are the smallest slice of history. What we feel is important and urgent will fall away. Pondering the scope of time through Stoic exercises like the view from above helps us internalize it.

From this larger perspective, we can take joy in the meaningful relationships and benefits we experience today. We can also avoid gripping too tightly onto what is impermanent.

We ought to live like travelers. Every destination should be savored and fully experienced. But we must not assume that we will stay nor even that we will see as much of the world as we like.

Remember this perspective as you go throughout your day.

Let’s end with the reminder to see things as they are from Epictetus:

“When anything is an object of affection, remember always to say to yourself, 'What is its nature?' If you are fond of a jug, say you are fond of a jug; then you will not be disturbed if it be broken.”  
id: 1738

# Gratitude

## Introduction

The purpose of this series is to build resilience and character to withstand grief. To that end we’re mentally preparing ourselves for loss, internalizing Stoic philosophy, and cultivating positive emotions.

Today we will focus on the last of those.

The Stoic philosopher Seneca wrote several letters of consolation, letters that were aimed to serve as advice and comfort for people who had lost loved ones. 

A key theme of his letters is the focus on living with excellence now. We must be grateful for our friends and treat them well when we can. In his letter “On grief for lost friends” Seneca writes:

“Let us greedily enjoy our friends, because we do not know how long this privilege will be ours. Let us think how often we shall leave them when we go upon distant journeys, and how often we shall fail to see them when we tarry together in the same place; we shall thus understand that we have lost too much of their time while they were alive.”

Let us contemplate this thought today.

## Outro

Excellent work.

Preparing ourselves for grief is a matter of taking the correct stance towards things now. 

Our lives are on loan from fate. Reality can change from moment to moment in ways that are completely out of our control. We cannot say how long our current situation will last. 

For this reason, we should not grip onto things too lightly. At the same time, we should not take anything for granted and ensure we experience it fully while we can.

In one of Seneca’s letters of consolation, On The Death of Metronax,  he reminds us that things can matter no matter the length. He writes:

“There are books that contain very few lines, admirable and useful in spite of their size.”  
id: 1739

Our role is to ensure that the narrative of our life and others is as meaningful as it can be, no matter how long we have.

Keep this in mind today.

# Purpose of Grief

## Intro

What is the purpose of grief? Why do we experience sadness at all?

In *Good Reasons, for Bad Feelings* the psychiatrist Randolph Nesse argues that bad feelings can be useful. They have a purpose.

People who do not experience *any* anxiety act rashly. Those who do not experience any sadness or frustration fail to learn from tragedy. 

Feelings of anxiety make dangers more salient. If the sensation is reasonable, we can avoid making stupid decisions.

Nesse suggests that sadness “was shaped to cope with the situation of loss.” When we feel sad, we are preoccupied with whatever or whoever we have lost. In this state, we are more sensitive to reminders, small things can remind us of lost opportunities or loved ones. Nesse suggests that this preoccupation can be adaptive – it focuses our attention on preventing loss in the future. The idea is that someone who experiences grief after losing a family heirloom at the beach through no fault of their own will be much more attentive to tracking their belongings.

Feelings of sadness can then help us learn from loss.

It is useful to keep this in mind when we suffer. Pain is not always pointless. 

We can distinguish between negative feelings and negative emotions. Feelings are sensations and reactions. Emotion is the judgment that follows such feelings. We can experience the feeling of sadness, without letting it transform into the full-blown emotion of sadness. For example, the person who lost the family heirloom may feel sadness – perhaps it will be present as a less energetic mood and pain in the chest – but they do not need to need to let that feeling transform into judgments like the thought that their day is ruined, that they are worthless, or that they lost what is most important to them. Instead they can treat the feelings as information and commit to doing whatever they can to reduce the likelihood of such an event happening again.

To appreciate feelings as useful, it helps to remember that feelings can serve a useful purpose.

With this in mind, let us reflect.

## Outro

Excellent work.

Thinking through the usefulness of negative feelings is something that many people fail to do.

If you can do this clearly, you can appreciate the information and purpose behind negative feelings, without letting them transform into negative emotions and false judgements.

Remember Seneca’s dictum: “You may weep, but you must not wail.”

What this means is that sadness is natural. Letting it warp our lives is not.

Go through your life remembering the purpose of grief and sadness, without letting it destroy what really matters: your character and relationships.

# Tragedy Planning

## Intro

In this course, we’ve been preparing for hardship.

We often hold contradictory attitudes toward tragedy. We expect it to happen, but do not expect it to happen to us. So, when it does occur, we are surprised.

Marcus Aurelius cautioned himself against this attitude in his *Meditations*:

“Remember: you shouldn’t be surprised that a fig tree produces figs, nor the world what it produces. A good doctor isn’t surprised when his patients have fever, or a helmsman when the wind blows against him.”

Today, we will combat surprise by planning for tragedy.

We do not recommend doing this exercise if you are in the grips of grief. Return to it when you are in a different state of mind.

If you’re ready, let’s start.

## Meditation

Ensure that you’re somewhere you can sit for some time without being disturbed.

Take a few deep breaths.

16

Let’s start by focusing the mind. Set your attention on the breath for a few moments.

30

Relax your attention and let the mind wander.

16

Now bring to mind the span of your life so far. What has happened? Do this in an objective way.

45

And bring to mind tragedies, hardships, or trials that you have faced. Remember that you endured.

30

When you’re ready, consider tragedies that could occur. Whether it’s the loss of employment, health, or loved ones. Bring each to mind.

40

Let us plan for just one of these possibilities. Choose one and then imagine that it comes to pass. What would happen next? Do this in a concrete and visual way, focusing on your decisions and thoughts.

30

Clarify what you would do the day after. Focus on simple rules or actions you can follow. Leave room for grief. 

30

And where would you be in a month? Again, focus on actions you can take.

30

And where would you be in a year? Remember \- you will endure.

30

And where would you be in 10 years? Contemplate this briefly now.

30

With these thoughts in mind, consider what is most important to you, were this eventuality to occur. What would you need to do in such a situation? What virtuous actions would be required?

45

Spend a few more moments mentally rehearsing how you can endure tragedy.

30

Is there anything you can do now, either to reduce the likelihood of tragedy or take advantage of the benefits of the present?

30

With that in mind, summarize what you will take from the exercise.

30

Take a few deep breaths.

16

And open your eyes.

4

## Outro

This is not an easy exercise, good work.

When we’re surprised and overrun with emotion, it helps to be able to return to simple rules. Such rules can capture our values. 

For example, if we’re suddenly fired, it’s better to follow the playbook for getting a new job, than wallow in shock.

If a loved one passes, it is important to return to your values, grieve naturally, and help those around you who are in grief. Simple plans can help make that happen.

Return to this exercise from time to time to prepare for tragedy.

Let’s end with another quote from Marcus Aurelius, one which contains a lesson he had to remind himself of multiple times:

“Is anyone surprised at being cold in winter? at being sick at sea? or at being jostled in the street? The mind is strong enough to bear those evils for which it is prepared.”  
1625

# Facing Loss

## Intro

In his final moments, we’re told that Marcus Aurelius was as serene as the situation allowed. Because he had prepared.

Infected and in pain from the plague, Marcus said farewell to his son and friends. He is said to have asked, “Why do you weep for me, instead of thinking about the pestilence and about death which is the common lot of us all?” 

He said goodbye with “If you now grant me leave to go, I bid you farewell and pass on before.” 

He perished shortly after.

Honored in life and death, Marcus Aurelius made an honest attempt to live well despite his circumstances – falling prey to neither the temptation nor adversity of reigning the most powerful empire in a plague and war-ridden time. He was able to do so because he made a regular practice of reminding himself of the prospect of his and other’s death.

The Stoics took to heart the idea that preparing for death was the true task of the Sage.

Once one has come to terms with one’s mortality, one can find it easier to come to terms with the mortality of others.

Let’s reflect on our mortality today.

## Outro

Today you faced the prospect of your death.

You pictured an imaginary funeral where admirable people praised your decisions and deeds. Let this serve as an ideal for your actions today.

You further contemplated how you want others to deal with the prospect of your loss – with grace and resilience. Let this serve as a model for how you approach loss.

Let’s end with a line from Seneca:

“Most men ebb and flow in wretchedness between the fear of death and the hardships of life; they are unwilling to live, and yet they do not know how to die.”

id: 1207

# View from above

## Intro

In one of his letters of consolation, Seneca writes:

“Why need we weep over parts of our life? the whole of it calls for tears.”

From our narrow perspective on the world, it is difficult to see that this is so. Too often, instead of seeing the whole, we magnify shards of our life.

What Seneca is pointing out is that we lose our composure over single events, whether it is being fired or an unwelcome diagnosis. Yet we all know that we live in a world where these events happen many times every day. Eventually, it will be our turn. Knowing that tragedy and fortune occur to other people, all over the world, stops us from hyper-focusing on our particular shard of time like it is special.

We can get better at seeing the world from a larger perspective through practice. With training, we will become more resilient.

Let's do that today.

## Outro

The View from Above calls for wisdom. We must see the big picture, instead of losing ourselves in trivial and daily concerns. 

This does not mean that we should disassociate – the present right in front of us now does matter. Instead, we should take joy in what we have and make excellent decisions. Our role is to be virtuous and good, every day. The French philosopher and modern Stoic, Pierre Hadot, was fond of saying that the sage, the ideal Stoic, managed to see the world from their local vantage point while keeping the whole in mind. 

To that end, let’s end with a quote from Marcus Aurelius:

“Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good.”  
id: 24
