## Desire

### Amor Fati

Stoics train themselves to love fate.

They don’t just tolerate reality, but welcome it with open arms. When you do this, you may find that reality responds in kind.

Let’s meditate on this theme today.

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes.

20

And pay attention to the sounds occurring in your environment.

20

Whatever sensations are arising, notice them. Then let them go. Stay alert in the present.

30

Move your attention to the sensations occurring in your body. See them as they are without judgment.

25

Remain curious and attentive. What is happening here?

25

As sensations arise, experience them in as much detail as possible. 

25

If you notice a sensation like pain or pleasure, note the difference between the raw sensation and the value judgment associated with that sensation.

30

If you become distracted, return your attention to your body. What sensations are occurring here?

30

45

Take this attitude with you throughout your day.

### Agreement

An important advanced technique in Stoicism is the thought that with every act, you are agreeing to reality as it is.

It’s easier to withstand something if you’ve chosen it. The pain and discomfort of exercise when you’re at the gym is normal. If you felt the same feelings before going to bed, you would rightly be alarmed. 

Stoicism teaches that we have the ability to freely choose. Our decisions and judgments are up to us. Everything else is not. Once you make a choice, welcome the results. They will come whether or not you welcome them. But your ability to respond to them well will be enhanced if you see them as natural outcomes of your decisions. 

Let’s apply that idea in today’s meditation.

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes.

20

Rest your attention on the sounds occurring in your environment. See what you can pick up, even if it is quiet.

20

Whatever sounds arise, notice them. If you hear atmospheric sounds, a car honking, birds singing – whatever it is that is what you hear. Do not add additional stories to your experience.

25

And move your attention to the sensations occurring in your body. See them as they are without judgment.

30

Keep your attention open and aware. Experience the sensations in your body and the sounds in your environment.

30

As sensations arise, embrace them. Imagine yourself willing them into existence.

45

And, if you become distracted, see that distraction like you see your sensations. Embrace it and then return your attention to meditation.

45

Whatever you experience, whether pain or pleasure, see it as something you’ve agreed to. Something to embrace. A natural result of your decision to meditate today.

45

And relax your attention further. Let it wander naturally.

20

Summarize what you’ll take with you from this meditation into your life today.

20

Take a few deep breaths.

20

And open your eyes.

4

As you go throughout your day greet, success and triumph, obstacles and defeat. Remind yourself of what is up to you. And love what is not.

Let’s end with a quote from the German philosopher, Friedrich Nietzsche:

“Everything that is necessary, when seen from above and from the perspective of the vast economy of the whole, is in itself equally useful. We must not only put up with it, but love it… Amore fati: that is my innermost nature.”

### Direct And Harmony

Direct reality when you can; Harmonize with it when you can’t.

Stoicism is making excellent decisions. One fact that always matters for our decision making is how much influence we have. Some things in the external world we can change, other’s we cannot.

The Stoics remind us to change what we can and accept what we must.

Let’s contemplate this today.

Begin by taking a few deep breaths.

20

We’ll start by focusing on the breath. In an instant, bring your concentration to your breath. See it in as much detail as you can.

30

Build your concentration by trying to find something new in the sensation of the breath. It doesn’t matter if you can’t, look for details you haven’t picked up before to hone your attention.

30

Continue meditating. See if you can notice the exact instant an exhale finishes and the precise moment an inhale begins.

45

If you become distracted, acknowledge that and then return your focus back to the breath.

45

Let your attention wander naturally.

20

Meditate on these words for a moment:

“Direct reality when you can; Harmonize with it when you can’t.”

30

Visualize yourself applying this idea as you move throughout the day.

45

Let’s get specific. How will you direct reality today? Start with what is up to you: your decisions and judgments.

40

And turn to times where you may need to align yourself with and accept reality, instead of trying to bend it.

40

With these thoughts in mind, summarize what you’ll take with you from this meditation, into your day.

30

Take a few deep breaths.

20

And open your eyes.

As the saying goes, reality can be bent, but not broken. That is, we all have the power to influence reality. But at the end of the day, it’s not something that is directly up to us. At the end of the day, we’re responsible for doing our best. 

Let’s end with the serenity prayer:

"God grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and wisdom to know the difference."

## Judgment

### Opinion

You are not harmed by things themselves, but by your interpretation of them.

That is one of the central ideas of Stoicism. It’s proved by the fact that two people can experience the exact same thing – and for one, it may be a victory, for the other defeat. 

Whether or not you are harmed is up to you. This doesn’t mean you can immediately withstand any adversity. It takes training. But it does mean you have the power to shape your character into someone who focuses on what really matters and isn’t harmed by the obstacles of life.

With this in mind, take a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes.

4

Listen to any sounds that arise. Pay attention, in the present.

20

Notice how you responds to the sounds – or lack of them – that arise in the moment. Watch the movements of your mind.

30

Now observe any sensations occurring in your body. Acknowledge and release them. Stay present and aware.

25

Keep your focus on any bodily sensations you are experiencing. Observe them impartially, like a curious scientist.

25

If you become distracted, note that distraction itself is just a set of thoughts and sensations and include that in your meditation.

30

As sensations come and go, explore them in detail. Take in as much as you can.

40

When sensing discomfort or enjoyment, distinguish between the actual sensation and any judgments you attach to it. Practice the Stoic discipline of judgment.

40

If you become distracted – note what happened and any judgments associated with that distraction and then gently bring your focus back to the physical sensations present in your body.

40

Narrow your attention to the breath. Don’t let everything else fall away – keep an open and aware mind ~~keep and open and are min~~d – but while you do this, narrow your attention to the breath. Watch it move in and out.

30

As you do this, notice any judgments that arise.

45

And let your mind wander naturally. Release your focus.

20

Reflect on what insights from this meditation you will carry into your daily life.

20

Take several deep breaths.

20

And open your eyes.

### Space

There is a space between stimulus and response. In that space, we find our freedom. With meditation you can train yourself to see the stimulus – and then take advantage of the space. No longer act like a robot who is programmed to act in accord with the triggers they receive –pause and act freely.

Let’s train the ability to pause today.

Take a moment to remind yourself of the purpose behind meditating today.

10

And commit to following through.

10

If you like, close your eyes. And take a few short breaths.

20

Follow the breath as you inhale and exhale – move your attention with it as it moves through your body.

20

And zoom into the point where the sensation of the breath feels the strongest – for some people it's the nose, for others, the stomach. Wherever it is, rest your attention there. 

30

And if you get distracted, notice that you became distracted, pause, then return your attention to the breath.

35

Simply focus on the breath as closely as you can.

35

Now, see if you can notice the pause between each inhale and exhale. Pay attention to the sliver of silence after one ends and the other begins.

45

Hone your attention by noticing the pause between each exhale and inhale. Notice precisely where one begins and the other ends.

45

Again, if you become distracted, pause, and then return your attention to the breath.

30

Meditate on this question, as you go throughout your day, how can you bring this concentration into each moment and notice the space between stimulus and response?

35

And crystallize what you’ll take with you from this meditation.

30

Take a few deep breaths.

20

If your eyes are closed, open them.

Well done.

Today, take advantage of the space between stimulus and response. Do not lose sight of the events happening in the external and internal world – and respond to it freely.

### Facts

Do not tell yourself anything more than what the facts tell you.

A key part of the Stoic discipline of judgment is applying Stoic training to see the world as it is, not as we want it to be.

Let’s meditate on this idea today.

Breath deeply and close your eyes.

20

Begin by focusing on your breathing. Notice each inhale and exhale as they come.

30

Maintain your focus on the breath, resisting distractions. If you do get distracted, gently refocus on the breath.

30

Let’s build concentration through counting. After an inhale and exhale, count one. After the next inhale and exhale, count two. See if you can count to 10 without losing focus. If you do, simply restart the count.

45

Continue this breath-focused attention for a while longer. If you become distracted – even for a moment – restart the count.

60

Continue counting. Again, if you become distracted – even for a moment – restart the count.

90

Good work, now, let your attention relax and wander where it wishes.

20

Now, contemplate this question. Where, in your life, are you telling yourself a story that goes beyond reality?

30

How can you keep your attention on the facts, without going beyond them?

30

Perhaps you already practice the mental movement you need – accurately catching where you became distracted and then returning your attention to the breath. Can you take this skill with you into your day?

20

Let’s finish by summarizing what you’ll take with you from this meditation.   
20

And breathe deeply.

20

If your eyes are closed, gently open them.

Well done.

If you catch yourself going beyond the facts, notice what happened and return your attention to reality. This is a mental movement you’ve trained in meditation. With practice, you’ll become better at this skill.

Don’t believe everything that you think.

## Action

### Right Thing

Do nothing but the right thing.

Marcus Aurelius once wrote: 

"Just that you do the right thing. The rest doesn't matter."  
id: 47

For the Stoics happiness was the life of virtue. Doing the right thing. Again and again. 

The Stoic practitioner energetically and joyfully does their best. Whatever situation they are in. Whoever they are. 

Let’s meditate on this today.

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

20

Begin by calming your mind. Rest your attention on the breath.

30

Start by bringing to mind the situations you may face today. Turn them over in your mind, in a careful, precise, and relaxed way.

30

Now bring to mind what doing the right thing looks like. Visualize making excellent decisions in each situation.

45  
As you visualize each situation – remember to focus on what is up to you, your thoughts and decisions. Nothing else.

45

Take solace in the fact that, according to the Stoics, nothing else is needed for a good life, except acting well, with virtue.

20

To finish, let’s perform a short breathing exercise. Breathe in motivation to do the right thing. With each in-breath summon the feeling of acting with excellence. With each out-breath, relax.

As you inhale – remember the feeling you have when you’ve acted simply and done the right thing in the past.

6

As you exhale, relax.

6

As you breathe in, breathe in the feeling of acting well.

6

As you breathe out, relax.

6

Continue this practice for the next few moments.

40

Let your attention flow naturally.

45

Let’s end by summarizing what you will take from this exercise. What will you apply to your life?

30

When you’re ready, take a few deep breaths.

20

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

4

Good work.

### Social Animal

Stoics advised living in accordance with nature. One aspect of this message is living in accord with our human nature. When we don’t do that, our lives become fundamentally misaligned – and so our lives become disordered, painful, and unhappy.

But a life that recognizes our human nature and fulfills it is a pleasurable and happy one. 

For the Stoics, humans are fundamentally social creatures. We can cooperate with others and live well with them. In addition to our ability to reason, that is the defining human ability.

Let’s meditate on how you can use this idea throughout your days.

Begin by taking a few deep breaths.

20

Breathe naturally. As you breathe, rest your attention on your breath.

30

Follow each inhale and exhale. To aid concentration, think in for each in-breath, out for each out-breath.

40

With this focused state, reflect on the situations you may face today. What events could happen?

30

Pay special attention to situations that may arise involving other people. Be objective and impartial. Visualize these circumstances now.

30

And bring to mind the Stoic idea that you are a social animal. How can you live well with others? 

35

The ancient counseled us to act with moderation, justice, and courage. How can you show these virtues in your dealings with others?

35

Focus on a specific virtue – one way in which you’d like to improve yourself and live according to nature. Rehearse acting virtuously again and again. Like a musician mentally practicing their movements.

45

And bring your attention back to the breath. Cultivate focus.

30

Relax your attention. Let your mind wander naturally.

20

Summarize what you’ll take with you from this meditation into your day.

30

Take a few deep breaths.

20

And open your eyes

### Roles

Pay attention to your roles and be excellent in them.

Stoicism calls us to recognize the parts we play in life and excel in each and everyone.

It simplifies the demand to live a meaningful life. A life lived according to Nature \- one where we play roles well is a happy one. 

Let’s meditate on this idea today.

Begin by taking a few deep breaths.

20

Let yourself breathe naturally. Concentrate on the breath.

30

Rest your attention on breathing. Follow each inhale and exhale.

35

Let’s reflect on your roles today. Begin by contemplating this question: what does meditation train you for?

30

And turn your attention to the general role we all share as humans: being excellent rational and social animals. How can you pursue knowledge and live well with others?

30

Make this general question more concrete – what relationships do you occupy that allow you to be rational and prosocial? Visualize yourself acting well in each of these. Whatever it is: student, child, spouse, parent, or friend.

45

Continue visualizing acting well in your relationships. These are the roles you occupy. Some chosen, others not. You play your part in the greater whole of Nature by being virtuous in each of them.

45

Picture yourself going throughout your day with the knowledge of what role you are in and how to fulfill it well – whatever happens. Like an athlete focusing on playing their position to the best of their ability, no matter what happens.

40

Focus on a specific situation – the most important one for you to get right.

30

And bring your attention back to the breath.

20

With these meditations in mind, crystallize what you’ll take with you into your day.

20

Take a few more deep breaths.

20

And open your eyes.

Well done.

Let’s end with a quote from Epictetus:  
"I should not be unfeeling like a statue; I should care for my relationships both natural and acquired – as a pious person, a child, a sibling, a parent, a citizen."
