# Anxiety Breath

This meditation is meant to quickly address anxiety by bringing you to the present. We’ll do this by focusing on your breath.

Let’s start by reading Seneca’s line to his friend Lucilius:

“True happiness is to enjoy the present, without anxious dependence upon the future, not to amuse ourselves with either hopes or fears but to rest satisfied with what we have, which is sufficient, for he that is so wants nothing.”  
id: 537

Just start by taking a few deep breaths.

16

If you like, close your eyes.

4

Watch the breath move in through your body. See how it feels entering your nose or mouth. Experience it filling your belly. Notice the variety of sensations always coming and going, temperature, weight, friction, sound, and energy. 

20

Keep your attention on the sensations surrounding the breath now. Just pay attention to them. That’s your only task now.

20

If other thoughts are rushing through, just let them come, let them be, and let them go. Your role is to focus on the breath now.

30

And if you get distracted return to the breath. Remember that it is always here – serving as an anchor to the present.

30

Just take a few more deep breaths now.

25

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

Well done. Let’s finish with a reminder Marcus Aurelius left for himself:

"Do not let the future disturb you, for you will arrive there, if you arrive, with the same reason you now apply to the present."  
id: 180

# Anxiety Model

This meditation addresses anxiety by reminding you that you’ve faced it before and can do so again.

 We’ll start with a few deep breaths. Breathe in through your nose.

20

If you like, close your eyes. And take a few more deep breaths.

20

Bring to mind the simple fact that you’ve experienced stress and anxiety before. You can think of a concrete time you’ve done this or simply remember it as a fact – you’ve met adversity before and can do it again.

20

And remember, that billions of people experience obstacles in their own lives, the people who surround you today and our ancestors who were here before. They found the strength within themselves to live on. 

20

You've faced setbacks before and have always persisted. Today is no different.

20

Visualize yourself facing whatever you are facing with calm composure. Picture it in detail. Like an athlete mentally rehearsing their moves.

30

Continue rehearsing facing obstacles with excellence.

30

And relax your attention. Take a few deep breaths.

16

Well done. 

Let’s end with a line from Marcus Aurelius

“The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way.”  
id: 56

# Anxiety VFA

This meditation is meant to quickly address anxiety by reminding you to inhabit the bigger picture.

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

16

If you like, close your eyes. And bring your attention to the breath. Watch it move in through your nostrils and expand your stomach. 

20

And visualize yourself meditating here. Picture yourself looking down from a point in the ceiling or sky.

20

And now imagine the city that you are in. See the thousands of people going throughout their day – each with their own obstacles and advantages. 

20

And zoom out even further – imagine looking down on the earth – a rock floating through space. Try – to the extent that you can – remembering that you are one person among several billion, one animal among trillions.

30

From this perspective, ask yourself, what is truly important? What is your role in this larger picture?

20

Let the trivial fall away while the important remains.

20

Now return to looking down on yourself meditating. View yourself from above.

20

And ground yourself in the breath. Watch it move in and out.

20

And take a few deep breaths.

20

And open your eyes.

Let’s end with a line from Marcus Aurelius:

"Dwell on the beauty of life. Watch the stars, and see yourself running with them."  
id: 2

# Anger VFA

This meditation alleviates anger by focusing on the brief nature of life.

Seneca once wrote:

"Why do we concern ourselves with conflict and plotting? That man you are angry with – can you wish for him anything worse than death? He is going to die without your doing a thing."  
id: 1727

Let’s meditate on the shortness of our lives.

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

16

If you like, close your eyes. And bring your attention to the breath. Watch it move in through your nostrils and expand your stomach. 

20

And bring to mind the span of your life. Picture it as a film reel or long tapestry. Imagine the events of your life represented by the image.

20

And now expand your image – remember that you are just one member of a long lineage. Bring to mind the fact that you are one person in a line of millions. In the scheme of things, your life is transitory and brief. Visualize this by picturing the span of the lives that came before you. 

20

And zoom out even further – remember that the human story includes billions of lives. Most of them no longer exist. We come and go, each experiencing joy, tragedy, virtue, and vice. Visualize the scale of time to the extent that you can. You are one human life among the billions of others today. One among the hundreds of billions that have come before.

30

From this perspective, ask yourself, what is truly important? What is your role in this larger picture?

20

From this perspective, let your anger fall away. It is small. It will pass. Whatever it is, it has been faced by the rest of humanity too. 

30

And return to visualizing the shortness of your life. Remember that you are just one story among billions. Each one must come to an end.

20

And ground yourself in the breath. Watch it move in and out, with an ephemeral nature of its own.

20

And take a few deep breaths.

20

If your eyes are closed, open them

Let’s end with another line from the Stoic philosopher and Roman emperor, Marcus Aurelius:

"In a little while you will have forgotten everything; in a little while everything will have forgotten you."  
id: 969

# Anger Breath

This meditation is meant to alleviate anger by taking a few moments to focus on the breath. Remember – it is our judgment of what occurs that harms us.

Let’s start with a reminder from Marcus Aurelius:

"It is our own opinions that disturb us. Take away these opinions then, and resolve to dismiss your judgment about an act as if it were something grievous, and your anger is gone."  
id: 1046

By focusing on the breath, we can ground ourselves in the present, clear our mind, and see that it is our judgment about things that harm us, not the things themselves.

Just start by taking a few deep breaths.

16

If you like, close your eyes.

4

Bring your attention to the sensations surrounding the breath now. Just pay attention to it at each moment. That’s your only task now.

20

And as other thoughts are rushing through, just let them come, let them be, and let them go. Your role is to focus on the breath now.

20

Watch the breath move in through your body. See how it feels entering your nose or mouth. Experience it filling your belly. Notice the variety of sensations always coming and going. View the breath as it is. 

30

And if you get distracted return to the breath. See it as it is. Do not add unnecessary value judgments.

30  
Let your attention wander to wherever feels natural.

30

Just take a few more deep breaths now.

25

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

Well done.

Let’s end with the reminder the Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius wrote to himself:

"It is our own opinions that disturb us. Take away these opinions then, and resolve to dismiss your judgment about an act as if it were something grievous, and your anger is gone."  
id: 1046

# Anger Preparation

This meditation prepares you for encountering rude and infuriating people. We’ll do this by meditating on the lines that Marcus Aurelius wrote to himself hundreds of years ago.

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes. 4

Let’s meditate on Marcus Aurelius’s lines from Meditations 3:

"When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: the people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous and surly. They are like this because they can't tell good from evil. But I have seen the beauty of good, and the ugliness of evil, and have recognized that the wrongdoer has a nature related to my own \- not of the same blood and birth, but the same mind, and possessing a share of the divine. And so none of them can hurt me."  
id: 26

Like the Roman emperor, imagine encountering rude, obnoxious, and challenging people today.

30

As you do this, be like the athlete mentally rehearsing what could happen – the athlete is distracted by value judgments about what should or shouldn’t happen. Instead, they’re focused on what can arise so they can perform as well as possible. 

30

And remind yourself that people typically do wrong out of ignorance, not ill intent. 

20

And, like Marcus Aurelius, remember the Stoic teaching that no one can harm you. It is your character that ultimately matters. Your character is determined by your judgments and decisions – and these are up to you, no one else. 

20

Finally, remember that other people, no matter how they behave, are human just like us. It is our job to treat them well and joyfully put up with them.

20

Take the next few moments to summarize the takeaways from this meditation. What will you take with you into your day?

20

And take a few deep breaths.

16

If your eyes are closed, open them.

4

Let’s finish by listening to the next line from Marcus Aurelius’s meditations:

“To work against one another therefore is to oppose Nature, and to be angry with another or to turn away from him is to tend to discord.”

# Motivation

This meditation is meant to give you motivation. After performing it, return to the task at hand. 

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

16

If you like, close your eyes.

4

And remember Marcus’s admonishment to himself:

"At dawn, when you have trouble getting out of bed, tell yourself: I have to go to work—as a human being. What do I have to complain of, if I’m going to do what I was born for—the things I was brought into the world to do? Or is this what I was created for? To huddle under the blankets and stay warm?"  
id: 1734

It is not our job to seek pleasure and comfort. We each have more important roles to play.

16

Now bring to mind the task at hand. Whatever it is – the task you are looking for motivation to complete.

10

And now visualize yourself performing it. Do it in detail. Picture it, like a musician mentally rehearsing a performance in detail.

30

Continue imagining yourself completing the task at hand. Be realistic and detailed.

30

And now imagine a different world – the one where you stay under the covers. Picture the world where you do not do what you need to do. What does that world look like?

30

Use the contrast between this world and the one where you complete what needs to get done as motivation.

20

Take a moment to summarize any takeaways from this meditation.

20

Take a few deep breaths.

16

And open your eyes.

4

Well done.

Let’s end with motivation from the Stoic philosopher Epictetus:

"Whenever you encounter anything that is difficult or pleasurable, or highly or lowly regarded, remember that the contest is now: you are at the Olympic Games, you cannot wait any longer, and that your progress is wrecked or preserved by a single day and a single event."  
id: 1722

# Important event

This meditation is meant to prepare you for an important event – whatever it is.

The Stoics advised mentally rehearsing adversity in order to prepare for when it arises. As Seneca wrote:

"Everyone approaches courageously a danger which he has prepared himself to meet long before, and withstands even hardships if he has previously practised how to meet them. But, contrariwise, the unprepared are panic-stricken even at the most trifling things. We must see to it that nothing shall come upon us unforeseen."  
id: 1492

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

16

And close your eyes.

4

Bring to mind what you’d like to prepare for.

10

Now picture yourself in the situation – imagine yourself acting with excellence. Focus, not on outcomes, but on your decisions and judgments, what is up to you.

30

Continue picturing acting with excellence. You’ve done it before, you can do it again.

30

Now bring to mind ways things could go wrong. What is the most likely way that something goes wrong with this event?

20

Remember the Stoic teaching that what ultimately matters is up to you. If things go wrong for reasons outside of your control, that is a new obstacle to handle. However, it does not fundamentally harm you. With this in mind, picture yourself handling adversity with excellence:

30

And consider whether there is something you can do, anything, in order to remove the likelihood that something will go wrong. If there isn't, let it be. If there is, makea note to handle it.

20

Finish by remembering that you’ve acted with excellence before and will do so again.

10

And take a few deep breaths.

16

If your eyes are closed, open them.

Well done. 

Let’s end with the motivation to pursue excellence from Epictetus:

“When trouble comes, think of yourself as a wrestler whom God, like a trainer, has paired with a tough young fighter. For what purpose? To turn you into Olympic-class material. But this is going to take some sweat to accomplish.”

# Focus Present

This meditation will bring your attention to the present and away from distractions. We will do this by focusing on the breath.

Remember Marcus Aurelius’s line to himself:  
"Wipe out the imagination. Stop pulling the strings. Confine thyself to the present."  
id: 587  
And take a few deep breaths.  
16  
If you like, close your eyes.  
4  
Watch the breath move in through your body. Bring your full attention to bear now. Notice the variety of sensations always coming and going, temperature, weight, friction, sound, and energy.  
20  
Keep your attention on the sensations surrounding the breath now. That’s your only task now. Build focus.  
20  
Watch the breath move in through your body. Register as many details as you can. The breath’s temperature, weight, friction, sound, and energy.   
30  
And if you get distracted, simply return to the breath. Managing distraction well is a core skill of focus. Distractions occur when they do, return your attention to the task at hand.  
30  
And a few more deep breaths now.  
16  
If your eyes are closed, open them.  
Today, focus on the task just as you focus on the breath. Whenever you miss the mark, return to the breath and return to what matters.  
Let’s end with a quote from Pierre Hadot’s book, *The Inner Citadel*:  
"Attention is the fundamental Stoic spiritual attitude... Thanks to his spiritual vigilance, the Stoic always has “at hand” the fundamental rule of life: that is, the distinction between what depends on us and what does not."  
id: 1720

# Focus Model

Use this meditation to bring your attention to the present by focusing on a role model.

Remember the line from Seneca:

"For we must indeed have someone according to whom we may regulate our characters; you can never straighten that which is crooked unless you use a ruler."

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes.

4

Bring to mind a role model. Someone with discipline and focus. Someone who acts with excellence. They don't have to be perfect. They could be the ideal version of yourself. 

20

Now picture this person going throughout your day with vigilant focus. They manage distractions well and keep their mind on the task at hand.

What would that look like?

40

And consider how your model acts differently than you do. Would they change their environment? How would they remove distraction? How would they ensure focus?

30

With these thoughts in mind, imagine yourself going throughout your day with the same focus. Mentally rehearse acting well. 

30

Continue rehearsing acting with focus today.

30

And relax your attention. Take a few deep breaths.

16

Well done. 

If you get distracted today, bring to mind your model. Use them as motivation and guidance for returning to the present.

Let’s end with a line from Marcus Aurelius:

"Objective judgment, now, at this very moment. Unselfish action, now, at this very moment. Willing acceptance—now, at this very moment—of all external events. That’s all you need."  
id: 193
