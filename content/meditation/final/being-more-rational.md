2. #### Slow Down Judgment

**Intro**

The first step to thinking rationally, is to avoid forming opinions carelessly. It is crucial to be intentional about what we believe, and why we believe it. 

The Stoics called our first sense of what is going on in a situation an “impression”. When we form beliefs carelessly, then we just believe whatever impression we have at that moment. But we know from experience that our impressions are often false.

Because our impressions can fool us, every Stoic needs to learn to slow down their judgment, and reflect carefully. On this matter, Epictetus said: “Don’t let the force of the impression when first it hits you knock you off your feet; just say to it, ‘Hold on a moment; let me see who you are and what you represent. Let me put you to the test.’” (ID: 686\)

In this meditation, you will follow Epictetus’ advice, and practice putting our impressions to the test.

**Meditation:**

Today, we will practice slowing down judgment. Let’s start by thinking of a type of situation that evokes a reaction in you. This could be the behavior of another person, a frustrating situation at work, or something that makes you anxious. 30

Now that you’ve picked a situation \- imagine that situation without judgment. Imagine it as if you were describing the facts of the matter without personal opinion. 30  
   
If it is someone insulting you, think, this is a person saying words. If it is someone arguing with you, think, this is a person with a different opinion. 60

Now think of other situations you will encounter in your day. Try describing them in a way free of judgment. Focus on specific situations that might occur. 60

Now imagine yourself encountering those situations with equanimity. Imagine yourself not jumping to judgment, but describing the situation as is. Be ambitious, but realistic. 60

Whenever we encounter any situation, we want to slow down our judgment and first observe the situation as it is. Only once we have done this should we form an opinion. It is ok to have an perspective, but this should be something intentional and thoughtful, not reactive. 60

When you’re ready, let’s end by summarizing your thoughts. Bring to mind what you’d like to take with you today. Perhaps a reminder to slow down your judgment, and avoid reacting to situations immediately. 30

And commit to making progress today. 20

And take a few deep breaths. 20

And, if your eyes are closed, open them. 

**Outro:**

Good work reflecting on your impressions and practicing, in the words of Epictetus, putting them to the test. As you go about your day, try to create space between impression and judgment. It is in that space that we are free to build ourselves into the person we want to be.

3. #### Remember you don’t have to have an opinion

**Intro**

Forming judgements is not always a good thing. Humans, unlike animals, have the incredible ability to reflect upon the evidence in front of us, and choose if we want to believe something is true or false. 

But we often forget that there is a third option. We can suspend judgment, and admit that we do not know enough to have an opinion on that matter. 

As Marcus Aurelius said: It is in our power to have no opinion about a thing and not to be disturbed in our soul; for things themselves have no natural power to form our judgments. (ID: 962\)

When we get upset about things, or feel strongly, about something we know very little about, we are to blame. This situation is not making us have an opinion, we are choosing to judge that situation, and often we do so with poor evidence.

In this meditation, we will practice suspending our judgements, and avoiding having an opinion.

**Meditation:**

Let’s start by bringing to mind what you will do over the next week. What do you expect to happen? 30

What situations will you encounter? What frustrations might occur? 30

Take the time to imagine in detail the kinds of situations you might encounter next week that will evoke a strong response. The ones that might make you anxious, angry, or frustrated. 45

Now that you’ve done this, imagine experiencing these events without judgment. Imagine encountering them, but not reacting to them, just observing them. 45

Our strong responses come when we make value judgments about situations. Consider just experiencing them, as things occuring in the world, not as things that are necessarily good or bad, true or false. 45

It is not unusual to think that we must have an opinion about everything. But without judgment these events can just exist as part of the world. Consider if there are recent events you should suspend your opinion on. 30

With this in mind, visualize yourself navigating your week with equanimity. Only having an opinion when you have enough information to make a judgment confidently. 30

Finally, summarize your thoughts from this exercise. What will you take with you through your day? How can you practice avoiding unnecessary opinions? 30

And commit to following through. 20

Take a few deep breaths. 16

And open your eyes. 4

**Outro:**

Good work practicing the suspension of judgment. This is a key Stoic skill, a fundamental way to avoid false beliefs and emotional turmoil. Moving forward with your day, try to apply this skill actively. 

4. #### Use a consistent standard of judgment

**Intro**

Often when judging a situation, we do not use what the Stoics would call a ‘consistent standard of judgment’. We judge some situations by one criteria, and other situations by another. Sometimes we require strong evidence to believe something, and other things we believe carelessly.

A standard of judgment is the key to sound judgment.  Regardless of the situation, you should require the same standard of evidence to believe something as true or false. You should also have the same criteria in each situation for judging a thing to be good or bad, worthwhile or worthless.

For the Stoics, the standard of judgment for whether a situation was truly good or bad, and truly worthy of our happiness or concern, was if it is something up to us. So in all situations, we should apply that test.

As Epictetus said: “Make a practice at once of saying to every strong impression: ‘An impression is all you are, not the source of the impression.’ Then test and assess it with your criteria, but one primarily: ask, ‘Is this something that is, or is not, in my control?’ (ID: 808\)

In this meditation, we will focus on applying a consistent standard of judgment to all impressions.

**Meditation:**

Let’s start by thinking of a claim someone could say to you, or has said, that you do not think is true, and you would prefer to be false. Maybe this person is saying something bad about someone you care about, or they are saying something that reflects poorly on you. 30

Now, think about what evidence you would require to admit this claim was true. Even though you do not want it to be true, what proof would you require to change your mind? Would you have to see evidence with your own eyes? Would someone you trust have to confirm it? 30  
   
This evidence that would change your mind is one of your standards of judgment. It is a criteria that, if met, results in you accepting something as true. Think of other standards of judgment you might apply in this situation, or ones like it. 60

Now think of a situation where someone has told you something you want to be true. Maybe what they are saying confirms a suspicion you had. Maybe it compliments you. 20

What is your standard of judgment in these kinds of situations? How easy or hard is it for you to believe these kinds of claims? 30

Was your standard of judgment different from before? If so, why? 30

Many people employ a different standard of judgment when it benefits them. They quickly accept the things that confirm their opinions, and strongly resist the things that disagree. A Stoic works to see the truth of the matter, which means always being consistent when evaluating truth. 30

Take a moment to think of some standards of judgment that you want to apply in all kinds of situations, regardless of what is being claimed. 60

And commit to applying these consistently, and without bias. 20

And take a few deep breaths. 20

And, if your eyes are closed, open them. 

**Outro:**

Good work learning to apply a consistent standard of judgment. This skill takes time to develop, but like all things, it comes with practice. Moving forward, focus on being consistent in the judgements you make, even if the situations seem very different. 

To conclude, let’s return to the words of Epictetus: “Make a practice at once of saying to every strong impression: ‘An impression is all you are, not the source of the impression.’ Then test and assess it with your criteria, but one primarily: ask, ‘Is this something that is, or is not, in my control?’ (ID: 808\)

5. #### Always reconstruct your opponent’s position charitably

**Intro**

Rationality is not just about how you think and argue, it is also about how you respond to the arguments of others. We often want to disprove counter-arguments that challenge us, or that disagree with our own beliefs. But rational thinking involves confronting arguments charitably, and admitting that you could be wrong, or have something to learn from the other side.

As Marcus Aurelius said:  
“Consider that you also do many things wrong, and that you are a man like others; and even if you do abstain from certain faults, still you have the disposition to commit them” (ID 1044\)

As flawed humans we also commit many mistakes in our reasoning, just like those we argue against. We also believe things that are false, have contradictory beliefs, disregard compelling counter-evidence, and fall victim to confirmation bias.

The best way to avoid these mistakes is to be open to the possibility that you might be wrong, and your opponent might be right. Or at the very least, listen to others and the reasons for their beliefs.

In this meditation, we will focus on being charitable to the arguments of others.

**Meditation:**

Let’s start by thinking of a moral, social, or political argument you disagree with, but you know other people believe is true. 30

Now, think about the premises to their argument as accurately as possible. What do these people you disagree with believe? Work on stating this in plain, non judgemental language. Do not use language that assumes them to be wrong from the start. 45

Next, try to find as many common points between what you both believe. Maybe you both believe you should help other people, but you disagree about how to do it. Maybe you both think people should be respected, but you disagree on what respect looks like. Find these similarities even if it feels like there are none. 45

Now consider the points you still disagree on. There may be fewer of these than you originally assumed. Again identify these in non-judgmental language.45

What are the best arguments in defense of these points you disagree with? Is there anything you may not have considered? 60  
   
For the points you disagree with, next consider the circumstances that led these people to believe what they do. What environments, circumstances, or situations have they experienced that might lead them to know or understand things that you don’t?  60

Even though youdisagree with them, consider one thing of value that you could learn from their position. 30

Consider your own environment, circumstances and experiences. How might these be biasing you, or give you insight into this topic, just as the experiences of others bias and inform them? 45

Take a moment to recognize that you may be wrong, and you may have something to learn from others. Maybe not in the example you thought of, but certainly in other examples. 30

And commit to viewing the arguments of others charitably. 20

And take a few deep breaths. 20

And, if your eyes are closed, open them. 

**Outro:**

Good work practicing being charitable to the arguments of others. Try to practice this skill as you navigate life. If you become good at understanding the positions of others, then one of two things is likely to happen. First, you may learn something from them. Second, even if you still disagree with them, you will be less angry about why they hold that position, and more understanding of what got them there, and where their ignorance arises from.

Stoics aim to accurately understand the world, and that includes the opinions and beliefs of others. 

6. #### Make decisions based on what is true, not what feels good.

**Intro**

As we strive to become rational, it is important that we reflect upon why we care about rationality. The Stoics value rationality, because they care about the truth of the matter. As humans, we can use our intelligence for goals other than discovering truth. We can come up with a complex plan to rob a bank, or persuade others to act how we want them to act. We can use our intelligence to construct fake stories that protect our ego, and place blame on others.

But none of these projects serve the goal of finding the truth of the matter. The Stoics wanted to live in accordance with nature, which meant accepting the world as it really is, not as we want it to be. Stoics make decisions based on what is true, not what feels good in the moment. 

As Marcus Aurelius said: “If someone can prove me wrong and show me my mistake in any thought or action, I shall gladly change. I seek the truth, which never harmed anyone: the harm is to persist in one's own self-deception and ignorance.” (ID: 62\)  
   
In this meditation we will practice making decisions based on what is true, not what feels good or convenient.

**Meditation**:

Let’s start by thinking of something emotionally resonant in your life that you are afraid might be true. For example, maybe a romantic interest does not reciprocate your feelings. Maybe you will fail at a professional ambition. Be specific. 45

Why is the example you chose important to you? Why are you afraid of it? 30

Now consider Marcus’ point about self-deception. Are you ignoring or explaining away evidence that this fear might be true, because it is painful to consider? 30\.

 Next, align yourself with the truth, not what feels good. Remember that if your example is true, you should desire to know it is true, even if that is painful. Consider this for a moment. 30

Consider what would happen if you knew your example was true. How would your life be different? 45  
   
Likewise, think of how you might respond to the situation. If it was true, how would you act? How would you change your behavior, or make different choices? 45

Consider what opportunities you could find in this situation. 30

With every situation there is potential opportunity. But if we ignore painful truths, we do not protect ourselves from the harm, we only limit our ability to respond to the situation properly. Reflect on this and consider if it applies to your example. 60

Stoicism tells us to desire the truth, even the hard ones. 30

Commit to getting better at this each day. 20

And take a few deep breaths. 20

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.   
   
**Outro**:  
Good work meditating on valuing the truth over what feels good in the moment. Remember that the Stoic values accepting the world as it is, not as we want it to be. A key component to rationality is valuing the truth for its own sake. Without valuing truth for its own sake, our reasoning can be used to deceive ourselves in an attempt to protect ourselves from short-term disappointment and negative emotion.

To conclude, let’s consider again our quote from Marcus Aurelius: “If someone can prove me wrong and show me my mistake in any thought or action, I shall gladly change. I seek the truth, which never harmed anyone: the harm is to persist in one's own self-deception and ignorance.”

### **CT: Limit Passion**

CD: Stop emotions from disrupting reason

**Intro:**

Applying reason well may be up to us, but our ability to reflect does not exist in a vacuum.  
Emotions, and especially extreme passions, disrupt our capacity to reflect well.

The Stoics described the passionate mind as an object in motion. 

Reason is like the steering system for a car. When you turn the wheel, it shifts the car’s direction sharply. The car responds to your input. 

Passionate emotions add momentum to the car. 

When you are angry, it is like you are trying to change the direction of a car already moving, or sliding downhill.

All of a sudden your steering wheel isn't responsive. When you turn the wheel to the left, your car doesn’t move, or maybe it over-corrects too far. 

For the Stoics, emotions stand in defiance to reason, not because there is some irrational part of the self, but because they add momentum to the mind.

Momentum stops our ability to control what we give attention to.  And if you’re going in the wrong direction, that’s not a good thing

To get back that control, we have to limit extreme, harmful emotions.

As Seneca says: 

“If reason prevails, the emotions will not even get started; while if they begin in defiance of reason, they will continue in defiance of reason.”

### **CT: Control Attention**

CD: Focus on what matters

**Intro:**

There is a popular saying that comparison is the thief of joy. If we focus on what we lack compared to others, then we won’t ever be happy. 

Focusing on what we lack is bad attention. Bad attention leads to poor judgements, because you’ve only given yourself poor material to judge.

This is why the Stoics taught that using our attention well is essential for becoming rational. Rational thinking also consists of how you direct your attention in each moment. 

Attention is what you focus on, and what you focus on is what fills your mind with impressions and ideas. 

If you have poor attention, your mind will be filled with poor quality impressions.

As Seneca says:

“No man is satisfied with his own lot if he fixes his attention on that of another.”

### **CT: Accept Others**

CD: Don’t expect rationality from everyone

**Intro:**

In his Letters on Ethics, Seneca says:

 “Would anyone think himself to be in his perfect mind if he were to return kicks to a mule or bites to a dog? ... If animals are protected from your anger by their want of reason, you ought to treat all foolish men in the like manner.”  
Id: 1619  
Source: Letters  
Location: 3.27

Not all animals are rational. Likewise, not all people live up to their rational potential.

Part of being rational, and living in accordance with nature, is accepting the world as it is.

There are many ignorant and irrational people.

As Stoics, we need to accept that.

We need not lash out at them, the same way we wouldn’t kick a mule.

Now the Stoics think all humans have the potential to be rational. Seneca is not saying that some people are worse than others. But not everyone chooses to develop their talents or achieve their potential.

As Marcus says:

“Remember: you shouldn’t be surprised that a fig tree produces figs…”  
Id: 1703  
Source: Meditations  
Location: 8.5

Just like a fig tree will produce figs, flawed people will act in flawed ways. 

Our rationality depends on understanding and accepting the limitations of others, so let’s meditate on this today.

**Meditation:**

Whether sitting or walking, maintain a relaxed and alert posture today. 

4

Start with a few deep breaths. 

30

Pay attention to the sensations or impressions occurring right now. As you do, don’t pass judgment, simply note them.

30

Now let’s practice accepting others how they are. Start by imagining something frustrating or disagreeable about someone you know. Remember to choose a trait that is unpleasant, but not debilitating. 

30

Continue imagining them acting in this way. Do so in a detailed way. Like an athlete visualizing the performance of a teammate, try to picture this behavior as clearly and accurately as clearly as possible.

45

30

**Outro:**

Good work reflecting on, and working to accept the flaws of others.

Remember that Stoics are not passive, they do what they can to prevent or limit selfish, mean, poor behavior from others.

But the Stoics are also pragmatic. They accept that imperfect people are a part of the world. 

To always expect people to be different than they are makes us frustrated, fragile, and bitter. When you accept people, including their flaws, you can be effective, resilient, and content, even in difficult situations. 

Let’s finish with Marcus’s wise words, the full quote this time:

“Remember: you shouldn’t be surprised that a fig tree produces figs, nor the world what it produces. A good doctor isn’t surprised when his patients have fever, or a helmsman when the wind blows against him.”
