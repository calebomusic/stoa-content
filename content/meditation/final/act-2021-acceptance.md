1. # Cognitive defusion I: Labeling

Welcome to another meditation program with Stoa. 

One of the core disciplines of Stoicism is the discipline of judgment or Stoic Mindfulness. This discipline is all about embracing reality and viewing the world as it is.

When we view the world, we see it through our thoughts. Sometimes we let our thoughts dominate everything else \-- so much so that we think that our thoughts are objective reality. This happens when someone becomes passionately angry. They see everything through the logic of their anger. This is called *cognitive fusion*. 2

But our thoughts are just thoughts. They are false, incorrect, or, at best, only approximately true. They do not define reality.  2

It’s important to realize this and ingrain the ability to defuse ourselves from thought.

Today, we’ll practice defusion through a labeling technique. 2

## III. Leaves

Welcome to another meditation with Stoa.

Stoicism had an important impact on the development of cognitive behavioral therapy. One of the fathers of this therapy, Albert Ellis, explicitly cites Stoic philosophers like Epictetus.

Cognitive behavioral therapy has continued to develop over the past few decades. One useful branch is called acceptance commitment therapy. An insight of this practice is that if we fight our thoughts, they only get stronger. If you actively try not to think about an elephant, you’ll likely think of an elephant. Similarly, if you wrestle with a negative thought you may find yourself ruminating and debating it for hours. Instead of reacting negatively to our thoughts we can accept them as they are.

This is Stoic acceptance. Once a negative thought or sensation has emerged, we can see it as it is. We do not need to jump into the ring and do battle with it. Instead, we can distance ourselves from the thought. Get a sense of what is under our control in the present and live in the world. 2

We’ve been practicing Stoic acceptance through mindfulness meditation over the past few sessions. Today we’ll practice with a visualization exercise from acceptance commitment therapy. 2

When you’re ready, sit comfortably but be alert. If you’re able, make sure you’re somewhere you won’t be distracted. 2

Take a few deep breaths. 16

Bring to mind any thoughts or sensations that might distract you. Whatever it is, remind yourself to return to the practice whenever you feel distracted. 15

Finally, commit to following through. You’re doing this for a reason. 10

Take a few more deep breaths. 10

Visualize yourself sitting beside a gently flowing stream. In this stream, leaves are slowly floating by along the surface of the water. 20

For the next few moments, take each thought that enters your mind and place it on a leaf. Let it float by.  Do this with each thought, whatever it is. Even the thought is pleasant or joyful, put it on a leaf and let it float by. 30

If you don’t notice any thoughts, turn your attention to the stream. Watch the stream until a new thought arises. 30

Let the stream flow at its own pace. Don’t try to speed it up. You’re not trying to rush the leaves along or get rid of your thoughts. Let everything move at its own pace. 30

If you notice judgemental thoughts like, “I’m bored” or “I’m not doing this right” place those thoughts on leaves, too. Let them float away. 30

If a thought comes up again, watch it float by another time. 30

Continue imagining the stream for a few more moments on your own. 60

And when you’re ready, open your eyes. 

I hope you found this visualization exercise valuable. It’s a useful way to practice accepting thoughts and letting them pass. 

This is an important exercise in acceptance and commitment therapy. As psychologist Steven Hayes explains:

“The “acceptance” in Acceptance and Commitment Therapy is based on the notion that, as a rule, trying to get rid of your pain only amplifies it”

Our pain is often out of our control. Everyone suffers. Instead of trying to micromanage our suffering, an impossible task, we can see it as it is without judgement. It will pass, like the leaves on a stream. This is the practice of Stoic acceptance and the discipline of desire. 2

We’ll practice this technique more tomorrow.

“Time is a violent torrent; no sooner is a thing brought to sight than it is swept by and another takes its place, and this too will be swept away.”

## VIII. Fusion II

One of the core teachings of acceptance commitment therapy is that suffering is caused through cognitive fusion.

When we view the world, we see it through our beliefs. Sometimes, these beliefs cloud our vision. We see everything through their logic. Think of someone who is deeply sad. They see the entire world colored in their sadness. Like paint dripping down a canvas, their dark mood comes to color the rest of their life. This is cognitive fusion, when a single belief determines how we experience the rest of the world.

But we don't need to let this happen. We can defuse negative thoughts and color the world the way we want.

## VIV. Meaningful Life

What's so bad about negative feelings?

The common answer is that they feel bad. They necessarily cause suffering.

But the central answer given by the Stoics and acceptance commitment therapy is that negative feelings hold us back from living a good life.

Pain and suffering are not the same.

To see the distinction, picture the person experiencing pain while exercising, but enjoying it nonetheless. Or, and perhaps you've experienced this already, picture the meditator feeling discomfort, but remaining in calm and pleasurable tranquility.

These people experience pain, but not suffering.

We don't have to let pain transform into suffering. Bad feelings aren't bad in and of themselves.
