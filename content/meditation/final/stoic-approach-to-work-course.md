# Introduction

## Quote

“What does Socrates say? ‘One person likes tending to his farm, another to his horse; I like to daily monitor my self-improvement.’”   
id: 731

## Theory

If you are like most people, you spend a third of your day, at least 8 hours, working. Your job, like it or not, is where you spend half your waking hours, five days a week. If what you do in your professional life is out of sync with what you value, then, this is not just a bad job, it is a major impediment to a happy life. 

There are many different ways we can be unhappy at work. Maybe you have a job that is ‘traditionally successful’, but you don’t find meaningful. This means you come home emotionally drained each day, without having a sense of accomplishment or progress. If you lose a sense of meaning in your work, it is all the effort without the pride of a job well done.

Or maybe your work is too easy. You feel that you are leaving your potential untapped. Your work does not utilize your talents, interests, and skills. But you don’t know how to find work that does, or you are too scared to make the change required.

Or maybe you have a job that fits your talents and aspirations, but it is at a place with awful coworkers, or an unfair boss. So those around you limit how much you enjoy your work.

As a life philosophy, Stoicism is about living well. And living well involves the work that we do.  Stoicism has a lot to teach us about how to navigate these professional problems and find meaning in our work, the courage to change jobs for something better, and the skills to navigate the crudest of coworkers and bosses.

This course will cover what the Stoics, and Stoic philosophy, can teach us about work.

Over the course of the following 6 lessons, we will cover:

- Reflecting on your interests and talents to find the kind of work that is right for you.  
- Navigating difficult coworkers and management.  
- Taking a long-term perspective to career development.  
- Embracing the challenges of work that make us better.  
- And avoiding becoming over to our work, so we stay in a career that is a poor fit.

At the completion of this course, you will be better prepared to navigate your professional life in a Stoic way. This means you will focus on what is up to you to improve your work situation. You will take action instead of complaining. You will be more emotionally calm, and forgiving of difficult co-workers, but also more willing to stand-up to those who try to bully you. Finally, you will understand what you value, and take steps to build a professional life that benefits you, matches with what you want out of your life as a whole, and fits with your nature.

To begin, we will practice reflecting on work, and its place in our lives.

# Reflect on who you are

## Quote

“How is it that when a lion attacks, the bull is aware of its own might, and hurls itself forward on behalf of the entire herd? Isn’t it clear that the possession of such power is accompanied at the same time by an awareness of that power? And in our case too, if someone possesses such power, he won’t fail to be aware of it.”   
id: 1817

## Theory

When reflecting on your own career, how do you know if it is the right role for you? There are so many options available to each of us. We have to choose between a variety of industries, roles within those industries, and companies that provide those roles. So where to start? Should you take that new job offer? Should you go back to school and train for something else entirely? Should you switch companies? Should you switch positions within the same company? These are serious questions, but daunting to answer.

If we approach these questions from a Stoic perspective, we have to approach these questions as questions about happiness. The Stoic always asks, what is the best way for me to live? 

Sometimes this means being courageous in the face of injustice, or self-controlled in the face of insults. But the same Stoic question applies when we look at our careers. 

For the Stoics, happiness, and the best way to live, comes from living in accordance with Nature. This applies to the cosmic level of nature, or the physical world. Living in accordance with cosmic nature means accepting things as they are, but also understanding that you have a small role to play in changing things for the better.

But the Stoics also argued that we should live in accordance with our personal individual nature. Not all humans are the same, and we live in accordance with our individual nature when we act in a way that honors and aligns with our personal interests, talents and values. 

So the first way a Stoic would approach the question of work, is actually by looking inward. A Stoic would ask three related questions:  
   
First, what do I like doing? This is what I have always been drawn towards.

Second, what am I good at? This is what I naturally tend to do better than other people.  

Third, what are my core values? These are the things that I find meaningful. We know something goes against our core values if we find ourselves having trouble connecting with it at a personal level, or it makes us feel uncomfortable.

A Stoic life involves self-knowledge. So to approach your career as a Stoic, you need to understand these three things: what are your interests, talents and values? And this is done, by paying attention to yourself. As Epictetus says: 

“How is it that when a lion attacks, the bull is aware of its own might, and hurls itself forward on behalf of the entire herd? Isn’t it clear that the possession of such power is accompanied at the same time by an awareness of that power? And in our case too, if someone possesses such power, he won’t fail to be aware of it.”   
id: 1817

In today’s meditation, we will focus on these topics.

# Accept or Change

## Quote 

“It is thus reasonable for one person to hold out a chamber pot for another simply in view of the fact that, if he fails to do so, he’ll get a beating and no food…whereas, for another person, it won’t just seem intolerable to hold out a pot himself, but even to allow someone else to do so for him.”   
id: 1818

## Theory

In our last lesson, we reflected on who we are and what we value, and how this relates to our work. Sometimes, our work fits our values, but sometimes there will be conflict between work and who we are.

This was also the case in ancient Rome.

In Epictetus’s Discourses, Epictetus tells the story of a slave who is faced with a job he feels is beneath him. The slave has been asked to hold a chamber-pot for his owner, a chamber-pot being where people would defecate. If the slave holds the chamberpot, his owner will be happy but he will be humiliated. If he refuses, he will be denied food and possibly beaten.

The slave considers the situation as a conflict. “Do I hold the chamber-pot and suffer humiliation, or do I keep my pride but face the consequences from my owner?” 

Epictetus’ point is simple, either hold the pot, or don’t hold the pot and continue on. But the only wrong choice is to create a conflict out of this situation by either taking the job and complaining that it is beneath you, or turning down the job and complaining about facing the consequences of being beaten and going hungry.

There are many lessons in this story. The example is extreme, but one Epictetus would be comfortable talking to as a former slave himself. One key lesson is this: Accept things or Change them. Or, in other words, either change your circumstances or accept them, but don’t do nothing and begrudge the world for how it is. When we complain, it is because we expect our circumstances to be otherwise but are not focusing our attention on changing them.

Maybe we expect all our coworkers to be nicer, or we expect the fun jobs to be better paying or the well paying jobs to be more enjoyable. 

These things are not, and will not ever be, the case. There is genuine unfairness in the world. But many situations, especially in our jobs, are not unfair, they are just trade-offs. It is not ‘unfair’ that the high-paying job has long hours. Either accept the long hours because the pay is worth it for you, or find a job that suits your needs and talents better. It is not ‘unfair’ that starting your own business has some risks, or that the job you love pays poorly. These are options in a world of options. Do it or don’t, but don’t complain. Hold the pot or don’t, but don’t hold the pot and complain. 

The Stoic point here is not to ‘suck it up’. It is to understand that complaining is what we do when we are neither grateful nor willing to change things. And a Stoic should always be committed to one of those two positions. So, again, accept things or change them.

# Difficult Coworkers

## Quote

“Begin the morning by saying to thyself, I shall meet with the busy-body, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial.”   
id: 170

## Theory

We all know what it is like to not get along with the people you work with. The problem with having difficult coworkers is that it can feel like a situation so far outside of your control. Unlike friends, you can’t always easily change people you work with without also changing jobs. So work often requires you to interact with the kind of people you would normally avoid. 

And conflict can be especially stressful, because you are forced to return to that point of conflict, every day. At its worst, you can experience bullying from coworkers, or genuinely abusive bosses. 

There is some solace in knowing that these are very human problems. Conflict with coworkers or bosses was just as much the case for Stoics living in Rome as it is today. Marcus Aurelius had one of the most difficult jobs in the world. As Roman Emperor, he possessed immense power in one sense. But in order to perform his job well, he had to be politically adept. He had to work with people who did not always share his values, and to find compromises with those he did not always respect.

In his meditations, his personal journal, he told himself to “Begin the morning by saying to yourself, I shall meet with the busy-body, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial.” Marcus’ point was not a cynical one, but one of acceptance. People are complicated and varied. To work with people means to work with some who are arrogant and unkind. We shouldn’t be surprised when this happens, but prepare ourselves for its inevitability. 

In Stoicism, we also forgive others by recognizing they are difficult because they are ignorant, and don’t know any better. In the same passage, Marcus continues: “They are like this because they can't tell good from evil. But I have seen the beauty of good, and the ugliness of evil, and have recognized that the wrongdoer has a nature related to my own \- not of the same blood and birth, but the same mind, and possessing a share of the divine. And so none of them can hurt me.”

In your own work, be like Marcus. Accept that you will work with difficult people so that it doesn’t catch you emotionally unaware or unprepared. Understand that they act this way because they are ignorant. Also understand that they are people like you, and it is your job to try your best to work well with them. 

In today’s meditation, we will focus on what is up to us in these kinds of situations so that you are not caught off-guard and are prepared to act well.

# You are not defined by your career

## Quote

Some things are within our power, while others are not. Within our power are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever is of our own doing; not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not of our own doing   
id: 806

## Theory

We tie our identity and self-worth to our work. When this happens, it is typically because we have achieved some position society or our social group considers successful. 

Maybe you are working in a high-status or powerful job. Or maybe you are working in the career your parents always wanted for you. Or maybe your career makes enough money to afford you a kind of lavish lifestyle that you are proud to show off. 

But this is a trap. The admiration of others, especially those you don’t care about, is neither within your control, nor a representation of the quality of your life or your personal merit. It is not something we can depend upon. The Stoics teach that a good life is about being a good person and having a good character. Admiration sometimes does follow from this, but Stoics don’t do anything for the sake of reputation. 

As Epictetus said in today’s quote, “not within our power are...our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not of our own doing\!”

When we identify our personal value with our work and reputation, we are placing our value in something not of our own doing, something not up to us. 

When we do this, we are likely to end up in positions that are not suitable for us. This is because we have chosen them based on what other people think. We have chosen them because *other people* find them valuable. 

Identifying with our work also makes us vulnerable to manipulation and chance. When we think the quality of our life depends on our professional reputation, we will compromise our character to defend that reputation. We will be stricken by fear at the possibility of losing our position.

If you imagine your role models, they will be the ones who did what was right in spite of the risk to their reputation or careers. Not the ones who preserved that reputation at all costs.

A career is part of most lives. But to live well, you must practice, and train yourself, to associate your personal worth with your character, and not your reputation or career position.

# No Career is perfect

## Quote

Difficulty shows what men are. Therefore when a difficulty falls upon you, remember that God, like a trainer of wrestlers, has matched you with a rough young man. Why? So that you may become an Olympic conqueror; but it is not accomplished without sweat.”   
id: 298

## Theory

Too often we expect finding the ‘right job’ to fix all our problems, professional and personal. We think ‘If only I was doing this career or that career, then I would be happy.’ But this is wishful thinking. Marcus Aurelius was emperor of Rome, arguably the most desirable job in the world at the time, with incredible wealth and power. And yet in his journal, we find the same malaise we encounter in our normal jobs. His struggle to get out of bed in the morning. 

In this course, we focused on improving our professional lives. Finding your natural talents and interests, so that you could find a job that suits you personally. Navigating difficult coworkers well. Avoid attaching our self-worth to our job. If you practice this Stoic advice, it is very likely that your relationship with work will improve. But we should not expect any job to be a total passion everyday, or for work to never feel like work.

First, a job is only one small part of who we are. It is one role that we have, along with our roles with family and friends, and our role as a human being in a larger community. Nobody is only their job, and so a happy life will never come only from doing well at your job. 

Second, a good career does not mean a career without conflict or disappointment. We must, as Stoics, accept those as a natural part of any endeavor. But too often, we set too high of a standard for ourselves and get discouraged if things do not live up to our unrealistic expectations. 

Instead, we should embrace our ability to learn from challenges. As Epictetus says: Difficulty shows what men are. Therefore when a difficulty falls upon you, remember that God, like a trainer of wrestlers, has matched you with a rough young man. Why? So that you may become an Olympic conqueror; but it is not accomplished without sweat.” (ID 298\)

If you have decided a career is worthy of your time, matches your character, and produces something of value in the world, then this is true when it’s fun or when it is difficult. Do not shy away from the challenges as they will reveal your strengths. And accept that some worthwhile things require paying a price.

This is the Stoic way to approach our career difficulties. With an understanding that no job is perfect, even one that might be right for you.

# Take a long-term perspective

## Quote

“The happiness and unhappiness of the rational, social animal depends not on what he feels but on what he does; just as his virtue and vice consist not in feeling but in doing.” Marcus Aurelius   
id: 116

## Theory

The goal of Stoicism, like other Greek philosophies, is happiness. But their idea of happiness was different from our popular conception today. For the Stoics, happiness has less to do with how we feel and more about how we act. Happiness is not a state of elation or joy, but something you say about a person who is living in accordance with their nature, and acting virtuously. In this way, happiness was conceived of as a good life not a good feeling. 

However, this is not to say the happy Stoic is emotionally miserable. The Stoics argued that the kind of people who lived well, and acted with excellent character, also tended to feel best about their lives. They were not  angry, jealous or anxious, because they did not think their happiness depended on anything outside themselves.

Because happiness depends on what we do, not what we feel, the Stoics thought that happiness was a long term project, not something that changes from day to day, or moment to moment. To paraphrase Aristotle, just as one bird does not make a flock, one happy day does not make a happy life. 

There is a lot we can learn by applying this Stoic perspective to our career. An excellent career then, from the Stoic framework, would be judged on what we do over the course of a lifetime, not how we feel day to day. Of course, what we want to do is work that has a positive impact, matches our interests and talents, we find fulfilling, and financially supports our other endeavors that we value.

But on a given day, our careers may not check each of those boxes. Maybe when we are a student, we like what we study but are paid very little. Maybe when our career begins our projects have minimal positive impact in the world, even though they match our skills and talents. Or maybe people love and praise us for  what we do, but we ourselves find it boring or unfilling. 

In all of these cases, we should adopt a long-term perspective. As long as we are doing the best we can with what is up to us, and moving towards a clear goal, one that checks the boxes for what we want for a career, then we do not need, and sometimes cannot have, the job that fulfills all of these goals immediately.

A fulfilling career, like a happy life, is a long term project. Just as one bad day does not make an unhappy life, a rough patch is not a bad career. Engage in this project over the whole of your life and adjust as you grow and change.
