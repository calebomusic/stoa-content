# Introduction

Stoics have acted with extreme courage. 

Ancient Stoics ruled empires, stood up to their masters, and revolutionized forms of art.

Modern Stoics survived prisoner of war camps and risked their reputation for the sake of doing what is right.

Every day, Stoics show their courage in moments big and small. Sometimes, someone’s courage is evident in the ordinary acts of life: how they carry themselves and treat others.

Wherever you are in your life you have the opportunity to be courageous too. To cast aside anxiety and exhibit bravery. 

The Stoics saw courage as knowing what to avoid. The main questions are – what should we steer clear of? And what should we go toward?

For the Stoics, we should aim to have an excellent character and avoid anything that damages our character. We find happiness by becoming excellent people. When we sacrifice our character, unhappiness follows.

As the famous quote goes:

“Between stimulus and response, there is a space. In that space lies our freedom and our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our happiness.”

The Stoic only fears misusing their freedom. They do not fear what is out of their control.

If we can do this too, we can skillfully and fearlessly take risks when it’s required – and, at the same time, act with caution and prudence when the situation demands it.

In other words, we will apply the words of the serenity prayer:

"God grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and wisdom to know the difference."

# Facing Fears

Courage is a skill.

We’re all dealt different hands in life. Some of us face tougher circumstances than others. Yet all of us have the power to be courageous. It doesn’t matter how smart, strong, or experienced you are. Becoming brave is within your power – because the choices you make are up to you.

Like other skills, you can improve it incrementally. It takes practice.

And the practice itself requires courage. You need to move forward on the path, even if you’re taking small steps and even when it’s not easy.

You can do that by making better decisions and judgments. 

Whenever you overcome an irrational fear, you’re taking steps toward becoming a rational and wise person. This doesn’t mean going from 0 to 100 immediately. Often it means taking that next step. Whatever size it is. That’s where your focus should be. If you’re anxious in social situations, put yourself out there in a situation that tests your boundaries. Learn from the experience. And do it again and again. Have the confidence to know that you will improve. Because improvement is up to you.

# Building confidence

This is normal. We’re surrounded by a culture that values reputation, wealth, and pleasure. Indeed, each of these things are good – but they’re not what life is all about. 

Fears over reputation and discomfort often hold us back from becoming who we ought to be. We let opportunities pass us by – because we’re afraid of what could happen – even though that is ultimately out of our control. A lifetime of doing this again and again is bound to lead to unhappiness and a lack of self-confidence.

# Strike The Balance

Courage is knowing what to avoid. For the Stoics, you should avoid anything that damages your character. You want to be an excellent person. Avoid everything that takes you off of that path. 

The brave person lives in the arena of action. The coward remains on the sideline. The reckless person joins the brave in the arena – only to exit after making a serious mistake. As Aristotle said, courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness.

Being courageous involves acting skillfully. Think about three climbers. The first, goes to ascend only to be daunted by the size of the cliff. They decide against doing it – even though they really want to. The second, immediately begins their ascent, but are overconfident. They rush into a mistake. The third, recognizes the challenge – but commits to doing it anyway. They don’t climb until they are prepared and when the time comes, they climb flawlessly.

This last climber is courageous. They strike the balance between sitting on the sidelines and rushing in. They do their best to avoid the regret of foolish mistakes and remorse for not following through.

# Having fun

Courage is about knowing what to avoid.

We act with cowardice when we shrink away from things that are not bad for us. We act rashly when we dive into those things that are good for us.

The courageous avoid the bad while pursuing the good. They act with brave and energetic prudence.

We shouldn’t think of courage as a stuffy virtue. The fact is, although it is a serious matter, many people avoid things that are awesome.  
	  
By sitting on the sidelines, they are robbing themselves of a more exciting and fun life.

There’s a thrill in pushing your limits. Whether that means skillfully taking social risks and having that conversation that makes you nervous, seizing a rare opportunity even if you’re not ready for it, or summoning the courage to see things as they are without sugar coating.

Ultimately, developing your relationships, skills, and mind requires risk – and risk can be energizing.

Changing your judgments about what ultimately matters and reinforcing those judgments with your decisions.

To become courageous you need to understand what is and isn’t worth fearing. And then act in a more and more courageous manner.

Motivate yourself with past successes – and learn to enjoy the challenge.
