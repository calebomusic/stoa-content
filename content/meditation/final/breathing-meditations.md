# Counting

One of the essential Stoic virtues is self-control.

If you’re able to keep your cool in any circumstance, you’ll live a better life. 

As a poet once urged his son:

“Keep your head about you, when all around you are losing theirs and blaming it on you.”  
2

This is the lesson that the Stoic philosopher Athenodorus gave to Caesar Augustus.

“Whenever you get angry, Caesar, do not say or do anything before repeating to yourself the twenty-four letters of the alphabet,” Augustus seized his hand and said, “I still have need of your presence here,” and detained him a whole year, saying, “No risk attends the reward that silence brings.”

Today, we’ll practice this exercise. The purpose is to keep your head and avoid making decisions out of anger, anxiety, or any other negative passion. Simply do that by counting. It doesn’t matter whether it’s the alphabet or numbers – pause by reciting a list.

“One advantage to counting, or repeating the alphabet, or any series of words, is that you’re more likely to notice when your attention inevitably wanders because you’ll probably lose your place. # Pause

Viktor Frankl’s philosophy can be summed up with the line:

“Between stimulus and response there is a space. And in that pause we find our freedom.”

If we can pause, the space opens up to us. A life of free choice, instead of immediate reactions, is waiting for us. 

The Stoic philosopher Athenodorus’s recital technique is one way to find that pause.

By reciting a list, mantra, or counting the breath – we avoid making mistakes due to passion.

When we do this, we can build our attention and awareness.

# Voluntary vs Involuntary

You have power over what is up to you.

Even in the midst of rage, you can shift your attention. 

This doesn’t mean that you can immediately turn it off. But it does mean that you can shift yourself into a different state.

This is most effective if you start early. Interrupt anger before it really gets going.

When those initial signs of anger or anxiety emerge, bring to mind a Stoic principle or technique. And let them give you the space and guidance you need to do the right thing.

Today we’ll continue practicing Athenodorus’s recital technique in order to deepen our skill into something you can use in your everyday life. By continuing your practice, you’ll increase your ability to pause and respond to the situations that come your way with wisdom.

# Focus

Life is about keeping focus. 

Not losing our minds on trivial matters. Set your sights on the goal. Don’t lose it.

Think about the Stoic archer. 

So much of what happens isn’t up to even the best archers. The decisions of the judges and the weather can make all the difference – but neither is in their power.

Yet they can choose to keep their gaze locked on their target, their body posed to hit it. 

Prior to competition, they can decide to train at their very best. Decision by decision, becoming an excellent archer.

That’s what enables them to wield their attention and awareness with skill.

Let’s work on building that focus through training today.

Focus has two aspects. How long we can keep it and then, when we’ve lost it, how quickly we can return. 

Through meditation, we can train both. It helps us keep our focus on the meditation object. With practice, you can attend to the breath for longer and longer moments of time. Equally important, we train in the ability to return to the rhythm when it is broken. If you haven’t already, you’ll find yourself noticing distractions and returning to focus faster.

These facts are not limited to meditation. Life is, in a sense, one long meditation. 

As you go throughout your day, keep your focus stable. Alert, but not tense. There’s a middle way. When you become distracted, return your attention to the matter at hand. Do this over and over again.

When life throws obstacles your way, remember the Stoic archer. They perform their best with what is up to them. Decision by decision. Action by action.

# Principles

In the previous lessons, we practiced a strategy from Athenodorus and increased our focus by attending to the breath. 

I hope you’re able to use both techniques to pause when you used to react.

That is how we become more free. The question is, how do you use your freedom? This is where you have the chance to apply your principles.

Marcus Aurelius reminds himself of a simple one:

"Objective judgment, now, at this very moment. Unselfish action, now, at this very moment. Willing acceptance—now, at this very moment—of all external events. That’s all you need."

# Awareness

Stoic tranquility involves seeing things as they are and not obstructing our vision.  
As the psychologist and author Donald Robertson writes:

“The Stoics said that all virtue entails a quality called magnanimity, literally having a great soul, or expansive mind.  One simple way of maintaining that is to remember that you’re not trying to block anything from awareness.”

There’s a lightness to this way of being. There’s an ease to this kind of awareness. The magnanimous person lets each of their thoughts and sensations come and go. They are not tense. They don’t flinch from negative feelings or thoughts. Instead, they are relaxed. They simply respond to events as they arise.

Let’s try to cultivate this way of being today.

# The Stoic Shift

So much stress and strain occurs when we try to push away negative feelings and events. But by fighting anxiety we make it worse.

The Stoic shift involves accepting negative feelings as they are – and then carefully responding with what truly matters in mind. 

The Stoic doesn’t reject pain, they accept it as it is without letting it transform into suffering. 

Let’s practice this shift today.

20

Embody the expansiveness in this moment. Don't block out any sensation or thought. Be aware of everything, without immediately reacting.

30

Now, bring your attention to the sensations in your body. As you do this, keep your sense of the outer world. Let your focus rest on your body, while remaining open to any sound in the environment.

30

Narrow your focus further. Direct your attention to the parts of your body involved in breathing. Observe your breath as it enters your nose and fills your lungs. Then follow it as it's released. While doing this, keep the rest of your bodily sensations in your broader awareness. Don’t block anything out.

30

If you notice your mind wandering, simply acknowledge it. This is natural and part of the practice. Willingly accept it as another experience. Note that you became distracted and return to what is up to you: your attention. Guide your attention back to the breath.

40

Now move your attention back to your whole body. Notice what arises here. If you can, notice the difference between a raw sensation, like pain or pleasure, and the judgment about that sensation. Pain and suffering are distinct, as are pleasure and happiness. Note this difference.

Today we practiced the ability to accept all experiences without letting them transform into suffering. By expanding our awareness and not blocking anything out, we develop the capacity to respond to life's challenges with wisdom and equanimity.

By fighting negative feelings head on, we are transforming pain into suffering. We fight pain because we believe it leads to unhappiness. But suffering never arises if we simply see the pain as it is and then return to what matters in our life: our character, relationships with others, and responses to the external world.

# Silence

If you can sit in silence, you can be at peace with yourself.

The French philosopher Blaise Pascal once wrote:

“All of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit quietly in a room alone.”
