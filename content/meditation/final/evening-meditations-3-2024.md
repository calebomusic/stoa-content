# Stress

The purpose of this meditation is to reflect at the end of a stressful and hectic day.

We will focus on two skills: Continually improving despite adversity, and accepting the uncontrollable

Feeling tense or stressed is normal; how we respond is up to us.

Let’s start.

Ensure that you’re comfortable – but remain alert. If you like, close your eyes.

4

Take a few deep breaths.

20

And bring to mind what happened today. Do this in an objective way. Imagine yourself as a scientist, calmly recording observations.

30

Continue noting what happened today, without making unnecessary value judgments.

30

And note where you did well. Where did you make excellent decisions? What steps did you take towards becoming who you were meant to be?

30

Summarize where you thought and acted well today. Focus on what was up to you.

30

And now contemplate where you will improve. Focus on what you can control and ignore external factors.

30

And continue to reflect on where you will improve.

30

Excellent work – it’s essential to focus on improvement even during difficult days. That’s when it matters most.

Let’s finish with a relaxing breathing exercise. Take a few deep breaths.

20

Now, with each inhale, accept what is not up to you. Conjure up feelings of acceptance. Let things out of your control be as they are.

5

And, with every exhale, relax. Just watch the breath as it exits your body.

5

Again, With each in-breath, accept what is not up to you. Don’t just tolerate it. Accept it.

5

And, with each out-breath, relax. 

5

Continue for the next few moments.

30

And relax your attention. Breathe naturally.

20

And open your eyes.

Excellent work.

Finish your day with confidence. Stressful days will come and go. You are the master of your fate, you are the captain of your soul.

Let’s finish with Reinhold Niebuhr’s Serenity prayer:  
"God grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and wisdom to know the difference."

# Resilience

This evening, let’s meditate on resilience.

Resilience is the ability to, in the Stoic philosopher Epictetus’s phrase, persist and resist. Sometimes “the best way out is always through.” Life tests us. We can succeed.

Take a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes.

4

Recall moments when you've exhibited resilience in your life. When were you strong? When did you survive – or even thrive – in the face of stress? Meditate on these moments – whatever they were, physical, mental, or social.

30

Deepen your reflection on those resilient moments. Pay special attention to the decisions that enabled you to push through and remain strong.

30

Now picture events or situations that may arise tomorrow. Like an athlete rehearsing for a future game – visualize upcoming challenges.

30

And picture yourself responding to each challenge well. With the same ability that has enabled you to be resilient in the past.

30

Continue visualizing yourself acting with resilience tomorrow. You have done it before. You can do it again and again.

30

If you can, rehearse enduring whatever life throws at you with Stoic joy. Sometimes, we don’t need to grin and bear it. We can learn to enjoy life’s trials.

30

And let the events of tomorrow go. Breathe naturally.

20

Take a few more deep breaths.

20

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

Sleep confidently, knowing you’ll tackle tomorrow with resilience and resolve.

Let’s end with a line from Marcus Aurelius:  
"Do not let the future disturb you, for you will arrive there, if you arrive, with the same reason you now apply to the present."

# Progress

The philosopher makes progress every day. Each day, they strive to make better decisions, acquire knowledge, and move forward on the Stoic path. 

Our progress varies: sometimes we move by leaps and bounds, and at other times it’s inch by inch. Some days, we may not advance at all. As they say, one step back, two steps forward.

Whatever the situation is, the goal is to keep moving forward. That’s what matters.

Let’s reflect today with this goal in mind.

Take a few deep breaths.

20

If you like, close your eyes.

4

And picture what happened recently. Visualize recent events like an athlete reviewing past performance. Observe, don’t judge. 

30

And continue picturing what happened recently in an objective way.

30

Now turn to moments when you made progress. What did you do well?

30

Pay special attention to where you thought well and made excellent decisions – after all, that is what is up to you.

30

Take these virtues you’ve shown recently and continue acting well.

Now turn your attention to places where you will improve. Again, focus on what is up to you. Where will you make better decisions?

30

Now, identify a specific area for improvement. Answer the following question: how will you be better? Be realistic, but ambitious.

30

Summarize what you’ll take with you from this meditation.

20

And take a few deep breaths.

20

Excellent work. If your eyes are closed, open them.

Finish the day with the confidence that you’ll progress.

You can act with virtue. You’ve done it before and will do so again. You can fix your vices. 

Always move forward.

Let’s end with motivation from Seneca:

"Test your progress, not by mere speech or writings, but by stoutness of heart and decrease of desire. Prove your words by your deeds."

# Productivity

For the Stoics, productivity is simply getting done what needs to be done. It’s about having the skill and focus to be productive for the sake of worthy ends.

Epictetus said:

"First say to yourself what you would be; and then do what you have to do."

Stoic productivity is about becoming an excellent person. It involves skillfully focusing efforts on meaningful goals and constantly improving your character. That’s what needs to be done.

With that in mind, let’s meditate on becoming more productive with Stoicism.

Start by taking a few deep breaths.

20

Bring to mind what happened recently in an objective way. See things as they are – without adding stories.

30

Focus on the decisions and judgments that you made.

30

When did you do what needed to be done? Bring those activities to mind now. Take confidence in those moments.

30

And where did you keep your focus on what matters? Not letting the distractions of the day knock you off your path?

30

And where can you be more productive? Start by bringing to mind moments where you spent time in ways you shouldn’t have. 

30

And where can you be more productive where it matters? How will you improve your skill to finish what needs to be done tomorrow?

30

With these thoughts in mind, remember that the goal isn’t to be productive for no reason. It’s to be an excellent and flourishing person. Bring to mind how your tasks fit into this story. What purposes are your actions serving?

30

With these questions and reflections in mind, summarize what you’ll take with you from this meditation.

20

And let these thoughts go. You’re resolved to act well when and where it matters. Relax and concentrate on your breathing.

30

Take a few more deep breaths.

20

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

4

Excellent work.

Go through the rest of your evening with the confidence that you’ll be productive.

Let’s end with the line from Epictetus:

"First say to yourself what you would be; and then do what you have to do."
