# Letter 1	On Time

## Quote

“What man can you show me who places any value on his time, who reckons the worth of each day, who understands that he is dying daily?”

## Theory 

Seneca wrote his *Moral Letters* later in life. The Stoic philosopher and statesman retired from politics, but, as he suspected, politics had not tired of him.

Indeed, the emperor Nero, Seneca’s former pupil, would order Seneca’s suicide shortly.

Seneca must have known that he did not have much life left. But perhaps that’s something he always knew. Perhaps that’s why he worked feverishly to improve himself, his craft, and the Roman empire throughout his life.

Indeed, he wrote that we are all dying daily. What matters is that we use what we have left well.

“While we are postponing, life speeds by”

Seneca urges us to take account of our time. Identifying how we spend our time is the first step toward balancing our time budget. Determine when you spend time well when you waste it, and the reasons behind your actions.

Then consider how you will change.

The most obvious way is to remove nonsense from our life. Do not let others waste your time. Do not lose yourself in worthless pursuits or pointless arguments. Life is too short for that.

Substitute empty time with worthwhile activities. Cultivate your craft, enrich your relationships, appreciate beauty in moments that would otherwise be squandered.

Seek to improve the quality of each moment. If you must do something, do it well. Take joy in embracing reality. Practice staying in the present. None of us know how much life ourselves or our loved ones have left. So, as the Roman Horace says:

“Life ebbs as I speak –  
so seize each day, and grant the next no credit.”

Use the breath as a reminder of the temporary nature of things. Remove nonsense from your life, substitute it with excellent activities, and experience every moment fully.

Towards the end of life, Seneca must have wondered whether he and his companions used their time well. After all, as he advises his friend Lucilius to:

“Keep what is really yours; and you cannot begin too early.”

What is the central thing that is ours? Time.

# Letter 2	Depth over Breadth

## Quote

“You do not run hither and thither and distract yourself by changing your abode; for such restlessness is the sign of a disordered spirit.”

## Theory 

This world has always contained many distractions. Romans like Seneca would have faced diversions, ranging from political intrigue, Roman games and entertainment, luxury, and the vices that appear when a culture normalizes enslavement. Not every temptation was wasteful. Seneca was also faced with the prospect of attending important lectures, reading the works of philosophical masters, or attending parties with distinguished guests.

Distraction is more available today. Most of us live in economic conditions that would render the life of even the wealthiest Roman meager. Our material comforts and pleasures are luxurious when compared with ancient standards. With the internet, any form of entertainment and gossip is merely a few taps away. It’s not just that trivial pleasures are instantly available – the great works are too. We’re blessed with the ability to read the best books ever written. We can connect with excellent people across the globe.

This provides a rich opportunity to explore what is good – a richer opportunity than what men and women of Seneca’s time possessed.

But the other side of this is that our age has a special problem with depth. Though in most ways our lives have become easier and better than in ancient Rome, the burden of distraction is greater than that of any past age. There’s always something else we could be doing, consuming, or creating.

Yes, there is a time in life to explore. Pick up many classes, books, and friends. But there’s a time to go deep. At some point, we must choose what to focus on. That’s a choice that entails sacrifice.

In his second Letter, Seneca argues for depth. He says:

“I tell you that it is the sign of an overnice appetite to toy with many dishes; for when they are manifold and varied, they cloy but do not nourish. So you should always read standard authors; and when you crave a change, fall back upon those whom you read before.”

You cannot read every good book. Dipping in and out of books risks superficiality. You cannot learn languages by picking up 10 of them in a year. Instead, you must focus on one.

This isn’t just a lesson about reading. The same focus is required in life. How do we choose? Seneca says:

“Each day acquire something that will fortify you against poverty, against death, indeed against other misfortunes as well; and after you have run over many thoughts, select one to be thoroughly digested that day.”

Reject distraction and focus on what benefits you. In the modern world, this may not be an easy choice. Like Odysseus, who ties himself to the mast to avoid the dangerous songs of the siren in the Odyssey, find creative solutions.

If you follow through, imagine how much you would change in a year. Instead of being pulled by forces external to you, you’d be grounded and focused.

Let’s end with this line from Seneca:

“Everywhere means nowhere. When a person spends all his time in foreign travel, he ends by having many acquaintances, but no friends.”

# Letter 3	On Friendship

## Quote

“Ponder for a long time whether you shall admit a given person to your friendship; but when you have decided to admit him, welcome him with all your heart and soul.”

## Theory 

So much harm occurs because we refuse to choose. Sometimes this happens because we refuse to admit that we have a choice at all. 

This principle occurs throughout life. Consider friendship.

Many of our relationships are uncertain. We don’t know how good, close, or trusting our relationships are. We cannot answer with certainty whether someone will be there when we need it. We’ve all been in relationships where both parties are so uncertain, that uncertainty translates into distrust. That distrust can, in the best case, render a relationship shallow and superficial. In the worst cases, it may result in pointless conflict.

To solve this issue, Seneca counseled that we should judge whether someone should be a friend or not. And then, if we decide that someone would make an excellent friend, we should trust him or her fully.  
   
Too many try to go halfway. They see trust as something they build slowly and only invest in a friendship if the other person puts in the exact same amount of effort. This behavior reveals distrust. If you knew that your friend was excellent, why would you insist on ensuring that every investment is repaid? What is the point of lukewarm friends?

Choose people who seem excellent and then trust them. After all – if they seem excellent they are worth your trust. And if they don’t – well, what are you doing?

As Seneca said:

“Regard him as loyal, and you will make him loyal.”

Now, does this logic mean that dishonest people will take advantage of you? Perhaps. But if you are never burned, you will not learn what good friends look like.

If someone mistreats you, you need to act appropriately. Perhaps you misjudged the situation or the people involved. 

But until then, choose whether or not someone is a friend. If you’ve made the choice, accept them fully. Be there for them. Don’t worry about a ledger. As Seneca advises:

“Ponder for a long time whether you shall admit a given person to your friendship; but when you have decided to admit him, welcome him with all your heart and soul.”

# Letter 4	Loans From Fate

## Quote

No man has ever been so far advanced by Fortune that she did not threaten him as greatly as she had previously indulged him. Do not trust her seeming calm; in a moment the sea is moved to its depths.

## Theory 

One of Seneca’s primary themes is the idea of Fortune. Fate provides us with what we need. Nature gives us the opportunity for happiness. It’s up to us to seize it.

Nature provides, but she also takes away. We each have the opportunity for an excellent life, but Nature does not guarantee any externals. Not wealth, reputation, or pleasure.

Seneca experienced the viciousness of Fortune in his own life. Early on he was a rising political star. He quickly rose through the ranks of society up to the Roman senate. And then in the span of a few years, he lost his father and son, only to be was exiled from politics. He barely escaped with his life. 

Years later, The same emperor who banished him called him back to Rome. He returned to great power. The mother of the emperor-to-be, Nero, selected him as her son’s tutor.

But this was not the gift it would seem. Nero became emperor. When he was young, Seneca and his other advisors ran the empire well. Yet as Nero aged, he became increasingly erratic and paranoid. He proved to be a poor emperor. Seneca himself could not escape from political machinations. What began as service for the Roman people began to look like a self-interested program Seneca engaged in to enrich himself. Indeed, historians debate who Seneca was today. Some see him as a figure who made the best of a tragic situation. Others see him as a cynical hypocrite.

Regardless, Seneca himself admitted he was no sage. In his *Moral Letters to Lucilius,* he writes that he sends his counsel from “a neighboring hospital bed.” We’re all afflicted with vice and imperfection. Hence, Seneca compares our situation with that of struggling patients. We’re all ill, not with physical diseases, but with imperfections of the soul. Seneca’s experience in the tumult of Roman life and politics gave him a first-row seat to see how excellent people thrived in the face of challenges and less virtuous people floundered. As such, he offers invaluable advice.

One of those challenges is the whims of Fortune. Life throws us trials that transform into benefits, and gifts that change into curses. 

Stoicism is a solution to this problem. 

Instead of depending on the gifts of chance, we can seek happiness by molding our character. That is what is up to us. Everything else is on loan from fate.

As Seneca wrote:

“The superfluous things that wear our togas threadbare, that force us to grow old in camp, that dash us upon foreign shores. That which is enough is ready to our hands. He who has made a fair compact with poverty is rich.”

Remember this today. Do not give in to adversity, do not trust prosperity, and always treat the randomness of life with the respect it deserves.

# Letter 5	On the philosopher's mean

## Quote

He is a great man who uses earthenware dishes as if they were silver; but he is equally great who uses silver as if it were earthenware. It is the sign of an unstable mind not to be able to endure riches.

## Theory 

The Stoic lives in the world, but he is not of the world.

Seneca wrote:

“Philosophy calls for plain living, but not for penance; and we may perfectly well be plain and neat at the same time. This is the mean of which I approve; our life should observe a happy medium between the ways of a sage and the ways of the world at large; all men should admire it, but they should understand it also.”

Epictetus had a similar line. He adored the philosophical cynics, these were philosophical heroes. But the cynic’s path is not for everyone.

Indeed, one of the best ways to understand Stoicism is by reference to the Cynics and Aristotelians. Aristotle claimed that happiness required virtue – but it is not sufficient. In order to be happy, someone needs friends, wealth, and power. The Cynics disagreed with this. In fact, they rejected society, lived in poverty, and scorned the powerful. They held that virtue was the only good. The Stoics sat in between these two philosophies. They agreed, with the Cynics, that virtue is sufficient for happiness. Anyone can be happy. However, they agreed with Aristotle that many things, such as pleasure, power, and wealth are preferable.

If we follow the Stoics, we should not reject the ways of the world nor should we grasp them too tightly.

We can picture Seneca as someone who takes pleasure in food, nature, friends, and accomplishment. These are each naturally enjoyable things. To reject them would be negative and nihilistic. 

This sense of moderation fits well with Stoicism. It’s this same sense of moderation, perhaps, that helped it find the middle way between Aristotelianism and Cynicism.

Living well with others requires recognizing that external things are not ultimately important. What matters is using them well.

Holding too tightly onto externals does not lead to happiness. The foodie becomes obese or a snob. The nature enthusiast isolates themselves from others. The striver ends up seeking accomplishment after accomplishment – for nothing.

Instead, we ought to hold in mind the fact that each of these things can be good. But our happiness does not depend on them. They are on loan from fate. This is why Seneca wrote:

“He is a great man who uses earthenware dishes as if they were silver; but he is equally great who uses silver as if it were earthenware. It is the sign of an unstable mind not to be able to endure riches”

# Letter 6	Progress

## Quote

What progress, you ask, have I made? I have begun to be a friend to myself.  
Hecato

## Theory 

After walking the Stoic path, look back on the progress you have made.

Whether you’ve improved yourself for months or years, there is a unique joy in reflecting on who you have transformed into.

Note how you’ve grown in courage, discipline, justice, and wisdom.

You’ve made progress and can continue to progress. We all can become better every day.

Perhaps you feel like you haven’t made as much progress as you’d like. To some extent, that is expected. We are each broken beings fixing ourselves with worn-out tools. Yet resist the temptation to downplay or forget the possibility of transformation.

Work on yourself every day. Put in the work needed for you to pause in the future and look back on the progress you’ve made with satisfaction. 

We become who we are meant to be by acquiring more knowledge. 

Seneca wrote: “No good thing is pleasant to possess, without friends to share it.”

When you find something truly good, acknowledge it. Share it with yourself and others.

He also cited the Stoic Hecato’s phrase:

“What progress, you ask, have I made? I have begun to be a friend to myself.”

It’s a lovely thought. Often we are our own worst enemies. But this is not how things must be. You can recognize that you’ve made mistakes, see clearly when you’ve made the wrong turns out of ignorance, and yet remain on the Stoic path, always moving towards a better version of yourself. Make progress every day and you will traverse great distances in a short time.

To do this, remain in the present. Look at things for the first time. Enjoy them.

And, from time to time, pause and look back on the progress you have made.

# Letter 7	On crowds

## Quote

“Many men praise you; but have you any reason for being pleased with yourself, if you are a person whom the many can understand? Your good qualities should face inwards.”

## Theory 

There’s something about crowds of people that transforms human psychology. As Friediech Nietzsche said:

“In individuals, insanity is rare; but in groups, parties, nations and epochs, it is the rule.”

The individual disappears in the sea of the mob. 

We’ve each encountered someone who has lost their minds because of crowd. Whether it was in sports, entertainment or politics. Literally. By becoming a part of the crowd, they adopted the decisions and judgments of others.

So, Seneca and the Stoics remind us to keep our heads. Only join the crowd after moments of careful reflection. Typically, the best route is to avoid masses of people entirely.

In today’s age, crowds take a physical and virtual form. It’s often the virtual crowds that have the largest grip on us. Do not let masses of people distort your thinking.

Instead, return to yourself. Form your inner citadel. Return to the sense of reason that is within you. Remind yourself of your principles. Uphold them.

Above all, avoid the temptation to appease the crowd. Remember, as Marcus Aurelius wrote:

“What then is worth being valued? To be received with clapping of hands? No. Neither must we value the clapping of tongues, for the praise which comes from the many is a clapping of tongues.”

Of course, this doesn’t mean that we do the opposite of the crowd. It may be in the right or the wrong. Its concern may be important or trivial. 

Just as we should be wary of joining any human movement that forms, we should be cautious of taking up the opposing cause. Seneca counsels exactly this:

“But both courses are to be avoided; you should not copy the bad simply because they are many, nor should you hate the many because they are unlike you. Withdraw into yourself, as far as you can.”

By withdrawing into yourself, Seneca doesn’t mean that we ought to be antisocial. He means that you ought to reach deep within yourself and think through what is true. Focus on making excellent decisions and judgments. Don’t react to others immediately. Instead, intentionally and rationally respond to them. This is not always easy. 

But it is what the philosopher is tasked with.
