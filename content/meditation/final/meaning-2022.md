# Intro

Many today feel like their lives lack meaning. 

Perhaps you worry that you are not who you should or could be. Maybe you fear that you are not spending your time on the right work or with the right friends. Or perhaps you are worried that you are on the path to a meaningless or trivial life.

The benefit of such sensations is that they force you to ask the question: am I living well?

This is the key question that ancient Greek and Roman philosophy focused on. Today, much philosophy is an academic enterprise. Only a minority of it focuses on the key practical question: how should I live?

The founder of Stoicism, Zeno of Citium, was forced to pose this question to himself by circumstance.

The sensation that one is not living well can occur at any time in one’s life. Yet it often emerges after a crisis.

The crisis for Zeno was a shipwreck. He was a wealthy merchant who lost all that he had after a storm.

While wandering around and searching for what to do next, he decided to go to the temple of Delphi and ask a delphic oracle what he should do.

The Delphic oracle told him that “to live the best life one must converse with the dead.” 

It must be said that the oracles at Delphi were not known for their clarity. 

Wrestling with this message, Zeno returned to his ordinary affairs. While visiting a bookseller he found a book on the philosopher, Socrates. He was blown away. He thought to himself: “Here is someone who lived well”. Socrates was a true philosopher.

Perhaps it was at that moment that Zeno realized what the delphic oracle’s statement meant: listen to the wise who came before you.

Zeno became a philosopher, joining an existing school and then founded the school of Stoicism.

Stoicism offers a path to a meaningful life. 

It offers an answer to the problem “how should I live?” One that has been applied by everyone from slaves to emperors. 

And how does Stoicism answer the problem?

It doesn’t – you do. 

What Stoicism does is provide the knowledge that you can live well and then offers a map for doing so.

The map starts with a description of who we are. We are fundamentally rational and social beings. So living well for us must involve living rationally with others. The Stoics expressed this with the mantra: “live according to nature”

As rational beings, we are capable of knowledge. We can understand what the world is and what is good about it. This means developing our character into one that is geared towards knowledge.

We can make this more concrete by introducing the Stoic virtues: courage, self-discipline, justice, and wisdom. 

Courage is knowing what to avoid. The brave act with prudence and avoid both recklessness and cowardice. Self-discipline is all about moderation – it’s about knowing the right amount of things and avoiding excess and deficiency. Justice is knowing how to treat others well. And wisdom is knowing what is important.

We are rational creatures, hence one of the most important things for us to get right is our thoughts.

Modern psychology shows us that how we think determines how we experience the world. Consider Cicero’s observation:

“The mere fact that men endure the same pain more easily when they voluntarily undergo it for the sake of their country than when they suffer it for some lesser cause, shows that the intensity of the pain depends on the state of mind of the sufferer, not on its own intrinsic nature.”

When we think correctly, we are more at home in the world and better able to handle what it throws our way.

However, Stoicism isn’t just about our thoughts, it’s about our decisions. Once we know what is good and how to act, we must decide to act well. It is through our decisions that we express our virtue.

By thinking and deciding well, we shape who we are. If we can shape ourselves into the best rational and social beings – someone who thinks well and treats others well, then we will have surely lived a good life.

Everything else is not as important. Because nothing else is good without qualification.

Wealth can be good. However, it can lead to ruin and vice. Pleasure feels good, but it can lead to mistreating others and yourself. Being highly regarded is often a sign of success, however, it’s not a real success when people regard you well for the wrong reasons. Further, so-called success in wealth, pleasure, or status can always get to one’s head.

The Stoics realized that it's how we use things like wealth, pleasure, and status that matters. They, of course, were not alone in doing this. This is a teaching of many other philosophies and religions.

The Stoics also gave us a set of tools and techniques to help us live better. You’ll learn about and apply these techniques throughout Stoa. These tools were a part of the inspiration behind effective modern therapies. 

Unlike modern therapies, it must be said that Stoicism is more opinionated. It offers tools to live well but also has an explicit account of what living well means: 

Living well means living virtuously. That’s it.

It is through virtuous living and good character that we can obtain joy and live meaningfully.

Zeno, the founder of Stoicism, used these insights to found a philosophical school that has influenced millions of people throughout history. In his day he taught hundreds of students and was widely recognized for his virtue and wisdom. Whatever material success he had was donated to public funds. Perhaps he would not have had so much success if he remained a rich merchant. From the little, we know about him, by all accounts he lived a meaningful life of contemplation and actions.

In this program and throughout Stoa, we’ll reflect on what living virtuously means for you, improve our thought patterns, rehearse living better and commit to following through. 

# Pessimistic Nihilism

## Intro

Some are crushed by the sense that nothing matters. Their nihilism is pessimistic. It renders their life less meaningful and happy.

This sense that everything is worthless may be best captured from the book of Ecclesiastes: 

“Vanity of vanities\! All is vanity. What does man gain by all the toil at which he toils under the sun?”

The problem with this attitude is that, in its extreme, it doesn’t make any sense.

If nothing truly matters, that’s not a reason to be pessimistic. If nothing matters, there are no reasons for anything. So whether one is experiencing joy or suffering doesn’t matter. If nothing matters, everything is permitted. 

This is not how we live. We live like there are reasons to experience joy, tranquility, pleasure, and happiness and reasons to avoid sadness, anxiety, frustration, and unhappiness.

Instead, when many of us have a crisis of meaning, we know things truly matter – but we’re worried we’re not living our best. It’s born out of the sense that there must be something better, something more.

It’s not always clear what this something better is. This is what we’ll contemplate now.

Today we’ll reflect on what living an excellent and meaningful life looks like.

# Virtues

## Intro

In the last lesson, we contemplated what a meaningful life looks like.

We’ll continue making that more concrete today.

The Stoics believed we should live according to nature.

This means seeking knowledge – the knowledge of what is good, and acting on it. To know how to act, we must know what is good. For the Stoics, what is good ultimately follows from our rational and social nature. We are capable of understanding the world and treating others well.

Coming to this knowledge requires experience and deep reflection. As Epictetus reminds us:

“This presumption that you possess knowledge of any use has to be dropped before you approach philosophy – just as if we were enrolling in a school of music or mathematics.”  
id: 682

Let’s contemplate how to live a meaningful life by focusing on the virtues of courage, discipline, wisdom, and justice.

## Outro

Today, you made the idea of living in accordance with nature more concrete. Living well requires living in accord with our rational and social parts, which in turn requires the pursuit and application of knowledge. The Stoics focused on the virtues of courage, self-control, justice, and wisdom. Each of these requires knowledge. Bravery requires knowing what to avoid, discipline requires knowing the correct amount of things, justice requires knowing how to treat others, and wisdom is a matter of knowing what is good and how to apply it.

Remember the contrast between the role models and the anti-model, the difference between those who act well and those who don’t. Let this serve as motivation for living well now.

It’s not always easy to be courageous, disciplined, just, or wise. But as they say, life is more fun if what we desire is a challenge.

Epictetus asks us to picture our lives as Olympic athletes:

“Whenever you encounter anything that is difficult or pleasurable, or highly or lowly regarded, remember that the contest is now: you are at the Olympic Games, you cannot wait any longer, and that your progress is wrecked or preserved by a single day and a single event.”

# Debugging

## Intro

Marcus Aurelius once said:

“Do not act as if you were going to live ten thousand years. Death hangs over you. While you live, while it is in your power, be good.”  
id: 24

Life is short. This is a cliche phrase, but we should take it seriously. It is short\!

Because of this we must pay attention to the present and develop virtue now. This is something that only we can do. It doesn’t matter that our vices may have been learned from others. We must throw away such excuses and improve ourselves now.

In the past few exercises, we clarified what a meaningful life looks like. Return to them again as needed and continue to reflect on what matters to you through conversation, reading, and meditation.

However, you must combine reflection with action. 

It is through action that your values, who you are, are expressed to the world. 

A more subtle point is that we learn through doing. Wisdom comes from experience.

With all this in mind, let’s consider what’s holding you back from acting virtuously now.

# Life

## Intro

Seneca once said:

“No man can set in order the details unless he has already set before himself the chief purpose of his life. ... The reason we make mistakes is because we all consider the parts of life, but never life as a whole.”  
id: 1354

At times life can feel meaningless because we see so little of it. We only see the obstacles, frustration, or trivialities in the near term. We pay attention to whatever is negative right in front of us.

The Stoics taught that negative emotions were the result of false beliefs. Often what drives a sense of meaninglessness are thoughts that neglect the deeply rewarding aspects of life. If we can address these thoughts we can live better.

When we neglect the rewarding aspects of life we don’t see how everything fits together. We fail to cultivate plans of living well over a lifetime. 

One’s life can truly be full of joy and excellence. Remember this when you’re feeling down.

To practice this now, we will reflect on the full span of our life and what it could be.

## Outro

The Stoics taught that life was made meaningful through virtue. Someone who lives well does not drift through life nor are they shaken off their path when terrible things occur.

Instead, they remain confident in who they are and their values.

The Stoics believed that anyone can retain this attitude. Slave or king. rich or poor.

It was as true then as it is today.

See the whole of your life and become who you are meant to be. Do not be pulled from your path by adversity or distraction.

# Gratitude

## Intro

The Stoic life is a joyous one. 

Our universe is a home for us. It is a place where we are capable of living well.

Marcus Aurelius once wrote:

“We should remember that even Nature’s inadvertence has its own charm, its own attractiveness. The way loaves of bread split open on top of the oven, the ridges are just by-products of baking, and yet pleasing, somehow; they rouse our appetite without knowing why. Or how ripe figs begin to burst. And olives on the point of falling: the shadow of decay gives them a peculiar beauty. Stalks of wheat bending under their own weight. The furrowed brow of a lion. Flecks of foam on the boar’s mouth. And other things. If you look at them in isolation there’s nothing beautiful about them, and yet by supplementing nature they enrich it and draw us in."  
id: 1723

In this note, to himself, Marcus reminds himself that there’s beauty in everything. 

The Stoics saw the world as a single organism. Everything plays a role in sustaining and directing that organism. Today, their view is best captured by James Lovelock’s Gaia hypothesis which proposes that the earth is a single, self-regulating system. Whether or not such views are correct, they bring us to contemplate the connection and beauty of things – things we have to be grateful for.

Let's meditate on this today.

## Outro

In one sense, the crisis of meaning has an easy answer. Of course, life is meaningful. 

We live in a beautiful world. One where our choices matter. Even in hardship, we have much to be grateful for.

Remember this as you go throughout your day.

# Urgency

## Intro

Our time is limited.

It is our role to live in accordance with our nature as rational and social beings. We should pursue knowledge and treat others well. That is what is to live well.

Nonetheless, it’s human to make mistakes and we often squander our opportunities. We get caught up in false beliefs and do not treat ourselves or others as they deserve.

Luckily, we have the power to change this. We should not let this power go to waste.

We do not know how long we will have it.

As Seneca reminds us:

“We all fall into this mistake of supposing that it is only old men, already in the decline of life, who are drawing near to death, whereas our first infancy, our youth, indeed every time of life leads thither.”

Let’s meditate on this today.
