#  Introduction

Throughout our lives, we’re all faced with challenges and adversities that threaten to disturb our peace. We seek equanimity; life has other plans.

In the uncertainty and stresses of life, Stoicism brings calm.

Indeed, meditating on a single insight helps people find the peace and confidence they need: some things are up to you and others are not.

That’s what these meditations are all about. Finding stillness by meditating on the fundamentals of Stoic philosophy.

This is a skill that anyone can learn. Understand what is up to you. Take responsibility for what is. And willingly accept what is not.

The Serenity Prayer captures this beautifully:

“God grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and wisdom to know the difference.”

id: 1689

If you’re able to take responsibility for what is up to you, you will feel the calm and confidence necessary to make good decisions. In any circumstance.

And when you recognize what is not up to you, you will have the power to accept it. Stoics experience setbacks without becoming disturbed. They experience negative thoughts and feelings – the natural pain of ordinary life – and remain calm. 

It’s not about suppressing emotions but learning to experience reality as it is.

With the Stoic techniques covered here, negative emotions like anxiety, anger, and grief lose their force over us.

The philosopher Epictetus described the good life as a smooth one. 

It’s a life of ease in the face of difficulty. The Stoic loves fate – everything that is not up to them – while trusting themself to handle whatever is.

People from all walks of life have used this philosophy to maintain their composure. When you’re just starting out, life is not going to be smooth. You’re going to be disturbed. What matters is that you return to what matters as fast as possible.

In these meditations, we’ll practice accepting what is not up to us and using what is: our ability to make excellent decisions and judgments in order to live a smoother life.

You have power over your mind \- not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength.

# The Central Insight

The Stoic philosopher Epictetus said:

"Some things are within our power, while others are not.”   
id: 806

Behind this simple division lies a deep and powerful philosophy. It’s one that ancient and modern people have used to transform themselves and come to love what’s not up to them.

Today, we will meditate on it to live a more calm and less anxious life. 

As you continue to practice Stoicism and learn more about the philosophy, you’ll find that there’s a lot of depth to this fundamental divide between what is and isn’t under your control. But right now, it’s something you can use in your life in whatever circumstance you find yourself in.

# Acceptance

Life in ancient Greece and Rome was never easy. That’s why philosophers introduced systems like Stoicism. Ways of thought to help themselves and others overcome the obstacles of life.

And they were successful. Stoicism was used by everyone – from slaves to emperors. 

But it wasn’t just a philosophy of tolerating adversity – Stoics willingly accepted everything that happened to them. They didn’t just put up with fate, they loved it. After all, what is the point of doing anything else? By definition, you cannot control what is not under your control. 

Like the Stoics, we can focus on making excellent decisions while embracing what is not up to us.

# We Suffer More In Imagination

The American writer Mark Twain captured Stoic wisdom when he said:

“I've lived through some terrible things in my life, some of which actually happened”

For the Stoics, our responses to the world are the true sources of our suffering. As the philosopher Epictetus said:

"Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by their opinions about the things."  
id: 241

Contemporary science confirms this insight. The same thing can happen to two different people. For one, it’s a triumph, for another, a disaster. What’s the difference? Their interpretation. As modern neuroscientist Lisa Feldman Barrett writes:

“You are an architect of these experiences. Your river of feelings might feel like it’s flowing over you, but actually you’re the river’s source.”  
id: 1724

We can realize this truth through experience and meditation. Let’s practice that today.

# Anxiety Is Not Bad

Negative feelings are natural and normal. 

It’s a common misconception that Stoics try to suppress them. 

That’s not so. Radically, they don’t believe negative feelings are truly bad. Even happy lives, lives well lived, are full of pain. 

Everything worthwhile hurts.

Think about anxiety. So many people are anxious about talking to that person, having that conversation, or seizing that opportunity. So they don’t. They avoid living the life they want to live because they are scared.

They suffer more in imagination than reality. 

The real harm, however, is not that they suffer from mental pain. The true loss is that they don’t become who they were meant to be. It’s that they pass up opportunities for happiness.

That’s why anxiety is bad. It’s an emotion and passion – a response to the world that leads us to fear it more than we should. Feelings of nervousness and fear are not bad by themselves. It’s who we become in response that makes all the difference.

If you talk to that person, have that conversation, or seize that opportunity, that is enough.

# Waking Up

Marcus Aurelius suffered from insomnia. Sometimes when he was finally able to pass into sleep and get rest – he had nightmares.

Perhaps it was after one of those moments when he arose and wrote to himself:

“Sober up, come back to yourself, and once you’ve aroused yourself again from sleep and understood that what was bothering you was no more than a dream, regard what you see now that you’re awake again as no different from what you were seeing in your dream.”

It’s a powerful insight. He knows that when he wakes up the power of his dreams will fully vanish. With the daylight, comes one’s senses and calm.

Why can’t we realize the exact same thing with the obstacles that face us now? They are phantoms who will lose their force with the passage of time. Just like our dreams are illusions – so are many of our negative thoughts. 

If we saw things as they truly were we’d be satisfied and tranquil – experiencing the calm of a dreamer that realizes their dream was just a dream.
