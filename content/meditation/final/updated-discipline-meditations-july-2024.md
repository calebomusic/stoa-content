## Importance of Habit

### Intro

Epictetus once said: 

“Every habit and capability is confirmed and grows in its corresponding actions, walking by walking, and running by running… therefore, if you want to do something make a habit of it, if you don’t want to do that, don’t, but make a habit of something else instead. The same principle is at work in our state of mind. When you get angry, you’ve not only experienced that evil, but you’ve also reinforced a bad habit, adding fuel to the fire.”

Whenever we fail to pursue goals we make it easier to fail next time. Humans are habit oriented and repetitive creatures. Failing to follow through, in Epictetus’s words, throws fuel on the fire.

Often we face a choice, a forking path, to act well or to act with vice. Each time we travel one path, we make it easier to travel that same path again and again next time.

This is a warning against vice – but there's also a positive side to this lesson.

Each time we act well, it will become easier to do so in the future. If we set realistic goals and achieve them each day, we’ll be like the athlete who never misses practice. What the athlete once found difficult, after years of practice, they will find trivial.

Let’s reflect on habits and discipline today.

##   Friendship

### Intro

Seneca once wrote:

"Friendship always benefits. After friendship is formed you must trust, but before that you must judge."  
id: 523

Surround yourself in an environment that molds you into the person you want to be.Our social group is one of the most, if not the most, important part of that environment. As the saying goes, you are the average of the five people you spend the most time around.

In his letter, Seneca urges us to form friendships and put our faith in our friends. Before doing this however, one must judge people and determine you that value them enough to become like them. Great friendships are chosen *and* made.

## Memorization

### Intro

Discipline demands constant vigilance. It’s easy to slip into the default of our habits.

To prevent that, we need to create environments that are conducive to acting with self-control. Self-control consists in setting yourself up so that you do not need to rely on your will. Instead one should make it easy and automatic to make the correct decision..

But one can’t always perfect one’s environment. In less than ideal situations, return to your principles to ground yourself.

The Stoics memorized maxims to do this. A simple statement serves as a reminder to live and act with purpose, instead of making decisions haphazardly. Indeed, Epictetus ends his Stoic handbook with maxims he believes one should keep close at hand.

Let’s practice memorizing a maxim today: persist and resist.

“There were two vices much blacker and more serious than the rest: lack of persistence and lack of self-control … persist and resist.”  
id: 850

## Life

### Intro

Discipline is cultivated over a lifetime. Each moment shapes you into whatever you’re becoming.

An athlete's great performance during a game is a product of a lifetime of practice. Similarly, your behavior each day is influenced by your behavior in the past. Our past history does not determine what happens each day, just as the athlete’s practice does not determine an outcome, even though it does make a large difference.

As you become more disciplined, more and more of your good behavior becomes automatic. This makes it easier to act well and also frees your attention to improve yourself further. In the words of the philosopher William James:

“For this we must make automatic and habitual, as early as possible, as many useful actions as we can, and guard against the growing into ways that are likely to be disadvantageous to us, as we should guard against the plague. The more of the details of our daily life we can hand over to the effortless custody of automatism, the more our higher powers of mind will be set free for their own proper work.”

With this in mind, let’s contemplate the span of your life. What do you want it to look like?

## Cots

### Intro 

We romanticize extraordinary ability. 

The sociologist Daniel Chambliss set out to discover what makes people excellent — to uncover their magic. He spent years with swimmers from olympic competitors to recreational swimmers in order to determine what set the gold medalists apart. In his book, *The Mundanity of Excellence* Chambliss says that:

“What these athletes did was rather interesting, but the people themselves were only fast swimmers, who did the particular things one does to swim fast. It is all very mundane.”

In other words, skill is a result of ordinary factors.

Chambliss argued that three simple things set the extraordinary performers apart: technique, discipline, and attitude. Each worked hard to perfect their technique every time they practiced. They were disciplined – always showing up and showing up on time. And finally, they take pleasure in what many think is painful. They joke about getting up at 5am to practice.

The Stoics, like other ancient philosophers, thought of virtue as *arête*, excellence. Excellence is the application of knowledge, epistêmê, and craft, technê.

Living well is a skill.

When we read about someone who was exceptionally virtuous we may be tempted to see them as driven by a kind of innate talent. Something external to us that we cannot recreate. But the truth is far more boring. Virtue is about getting the small things right. More discipline, better technique, and an attitude that values virtue. That’s all.

With this in mind let’s contemplate a role model and pay special attention to the small things. How do they apply technique and discipline in their life?

### Outro

Being virtuous is being kind, courageous, just, disciplined, and wise as much as we can. This sounds like a tall order. 

But often, it’s just a matter of listening to our friends' concerns, not texting while driving, praising others for their talents, expressing gratitude, not being conquered by nervousness, and surrounding ourselves with others who value virtue.

Pause a few times throughout your day and bring your role model to mind – what little things would they get right? What advice would they give you?

Let’s end with the following line:

“The small things are the big things”
