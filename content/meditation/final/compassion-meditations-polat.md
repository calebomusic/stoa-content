## Meditation: Introduction to Compassion in Relationships

Welcome to this series of meditations on compassion in relationships on Stoa. I’m Brittany Polat, co-founder of Stoicare and author of *Journal Like a Stoic*. Thank you for joining me. In this series, we’ll be exploring a Stoic approach to relationships of all kinds, including your relationships with colleagues, family members, and even yourself. We’ll deal with specific topics, such as managing expectations, appreciating the people around us, and staying calm when dealing with challenging people. We’ll also dive into big-picture topics like friendship, forgiveness, and love. 

A word we’ll be using frequently in this exploration is *compassion*. What does compassion mean in a Stoic sense? We’re definitely not talking about feeling someone else’s pain, because we know that doesn’t help anyone—not them, and not us. Instead, we’ll aim for rational compassion. We try to understand and care for others without feeling unhelpful negative emotions like sadness, anger, guilt, and fear. These negative emotions detract from our ability to positively interact with others, and they interfere with our reasoning skills and capacity for solving problems. We don’t want that to happen. 

Instead, we’ll work toward eliminating negative emotions of all kinds in our relationships, by focusing on our philosophical values and working toward healthy, prosocial emotions. The ancient Stoics believed humans are social animals who instinctively feel affection toward other people. Our goal is to properly develop these affections by learning to care wisely about others. Even if other people sometimes do things we don’t like, we can still maintain goodwill toward them and show friendship and love. The point is not to detach ourselves from other people but to learn to live with them in a way that promotes their wellbeing as well as our own.

Our relationships are the source of some of our deepest and richest human experiences. However, we all know it is extremely difficult to live harmoniously with everyone around us. In this series of meditations, you’ll learn how ancient Stoics like Marcus Aurelius and Epictetus approached social relationships and how you can apply their wisdom today. You’ll mentally rehearse some of their most potent practices and prepare yourself to implement Stoic ideas in daily life. Because if there’s one thing that never changes, it’s the challenge of getting along with other people.

Thank you for joining me on this exploration of Stoic relationships. I hope you enjoy this series and find it useful for your journey toward virtue and happiness.

## Meditation: Forgiving Yourself

**Intro** 

Everyone makes mistakes sometimes. Some of us have made mistakes in the past when we were younger, different people. We didn’t understand ourselves or other people very well at that time. But we can’t let that define who we are today. If we cling to feelings of guilt, shame, or remorse for our past actions, it’s difficult for us to move on and grow. We can’t allow ourselves to get stuck and wither away.

Today, we’re going to forgive ourselves for our past, learn from our mistakes, and grow into the person we’re meant to become.

**Meditation**

Begin in a comfortable seated position. Close your eyes. Take a deep breath in. And a deep breath out. 

\[16s\]

In this relaxed posture, think about a mistake you’ve made in the past. This may bring up unpleasant or painful feelings for you. Don’t run away from yourself. You can’t keep running forever. Today you’re going to hold those feelings in your mind as you continue to breathe. 

\[30s\]

Let’s think objectively about your past mistake. Let’s observe, like a scientist, what caused this behavior. There is no judgment right now, only observation. What was your motivation for your actions? Were you going through a difficult time? Did you think you were acting in the best way at the time? 

\[30s\]

The ancient Stoics believed people do not make mistakes willingly or intentionally. Everyone acts in the way they think is beneficial for themselves at the time. If we hold incorrect ideas about how to live a truly good life, we make mistakes. 

\[20s\]

Now let’s apply this ancient wisdom to your situation. At the time of your mistake, what values did you hold? Was it natural for you to act the way you did, given these beliefs and values?

\[40s\]

Now that you understand what led you to act in this way, you can forgive yourself. At the time you made this mistake, you were misguided. You held the wrong beliefs about what was good for you and others. Your mistake was understandable. 

\[20s\]

Starting now, send forgiveness and compassion to the person who made that mistake. To the person who was misguided about the world. Take a deep breath in, and as you breathe out mentally send forgiveness to your past self. Say to yourself, “I forgive you. You did what you thought was best.”

\[40s\]

Breathe in again deeply, fortifying yourself for the difficult work you are doing. Breathe out, once again sending forgiveness and compassion toward your past self. Say again, “I forgive you. You did what you thought was best.”

\[35s\]

Continue breathing in and out, with every breath feeling the weight of guilt and shame lift a little lighter off your shoulders. You are not a horrible person. You are a person who can learn from your mistakes. Everyone messes up sometimes. What defines you now, today, is where you go from here. Will you remain stuck in the past, or will you move forward? Make your choice right now.

\[20s\]

Now relax your attention. Let your mind wander for a moment.

\[20s\]

Take a few deep breaths.

\[20s\]

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

**Outro**

Remember, your beliefs about what is important in life determine your actions. Today you are following a better path. You are molding your beliefs and your actions toward something truly beneficial: wisdom, courage, justice, temperance. But no one is perfect, and it is crucial to forgive yourself when you mess up. 

Epictetus advises us to forgive other people, always being “tolerant, gentle, forbearing, and kind,” because “no mind is ever willingly deprived of the truth.” Remember to apply this also to yourself. Starting today, you will be tolerant, gentle, forbearing, and kind to others and to yourself.

## Meditation: Dealing with challenging people

**Intro**

It’s a basic fact of life that sometimes we encounter people we find annoying, unfriendly, or difficult in some way. 

But that doesn’t mean we have to get upset with them. As Epictetus says about troublesome people, “What does that mean, thieves and robbers? That they've fallen into error with regard to what is good and bad. Should we be angry with them, then, or merely feel pity for them?”

No one tries to make mistakes in their life. Rather, they think they are doing what is beneficial to themselves. They simply don’t understand that by acting against other people, they are also acting against their own best interests. 

Today we’ll meditate on this lesson and learn how showing compassion when others do wrong benefits everyone—us as well as them.

**Meditation**

Begin in a comfortable seated position. Close your eyes. Take a deep breath in. Breathe out slowly, relaxing your shoulders, your jaw, your eyes.

\[15s\]

Often, when we get angry, we build up tension in our body. Scan your body right now and see where else you may be holding in stress and anger. Relax anywhere you feel tension. Continue breathing slowly.

\[30s\]

Picture a person you are having difficulty with. It may be challenging to picture this person without feeling upset. Remember, you’re here to do difficult mental work. It’s going to be hard, but with practice you can do it.

\[20s\]

Hold this person in your mind’s eye. What do you think this person values in life? Has this person had difficulties in their life that led them to develop broken or misguided values? 

\[30s\]

Try to imagine what they are feeling right now, at this moment. Are they in inner turmoil? Are they feeling hatred toward themselves or someone else? Are they happy?

\[35s\]

Don’t you think this person needs help rather than anger? With your next out-breath, send compassion toward this person. Understanding. Forgiveness. Well-wishing. 

\[35s\]

Continue breathing deeply. With every out-breath, send this person wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance. Wish them the ability to find true happiness through a virtuous and beautiful life.

\[40s\]

With each breath, you understand this person more and more. You know they have experienced things which have somehow warped their responses to the world. You cannot fix this person, but you can show compassion. This compassion will give you strength as you deal with them. 

\[20s\]

As you continue to breathe, feel how you grow stronger, calmer, more understanding, wiser, with every breath. You have the strength and peace of mind to offer a proper response to this person the next time you deal with them. 

\[30s\]

Now relax your attention. Let the mind wander to wherever feels natural.

\[20s\]

Take a few deep breaths.

\[20s\]

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

**Outro**

The type of awareness we have practiced here provides insight into other people’s minds and motivations, allowing you to approach them with purpose, discernment, and skill. You can still handle the situation firmly, even while showing compassion for their mistaken understanding of things. As you go about your day, remember these words from Epictetus: “Whenever anyone assents to what is false, one may be sure that he does not willingly give assent to falsehood (for "every mind is deprived of the truth against its will," as Plato observes), but rather that what is false seemed to him to be true.”

Carry this knowledge and understanding with you as you return to your day. You are up to the challenge of dealing wisely with difficult people. 

## Appreciating the People Around You

**Intro**

Sometimes, when we have high standards for ourselves and for other people, we find that we are constantly disappointed. We are always thinking about how things could be better, which makes us, and the people around us, miserable. 

Today we’ll reflect on how we can start to appreciate other people more. We’re going to change from a *deficit perspective* on the world—where we have a standard in our minds that we compare people to, and always find that they fall short—to an *abundance perspective*, where we focus on the positive qualities of other people.

Marcus Aurelius tells us, “When you want to gladden your heart, think of the good qualities of those around you; the energy of one, for instance, the modesty of another, the generosity of a third, and some other quality in another.”

Now let’s apply this technique to accept and truly appreciate the people in our lives.

**Meditation**

Begin in a comfortable seated position. Close your eyes. Take a deep breath in. And a deep breath out.  
   
\[16s\]

Bring to mind someone near to you, someone you may feel a bit frustrated with, or someone you normally take for granted. 

\[20s\]

Hold that person in your mind. As you breathe in, picture them smiling at you. They are cheerful, happy to be with you right now. As you breathe out, smile at them. A big, radiant smile. You are happy to be with them right now. Feel the corners of your mouth starting to form a physical smile, and go with it. Let your face smile as you continue breathing in and out, seeing the happy person in front of you and feeling the smile you are sending to them.

\[45s\]

Continue breathing as you think about one nice quality this person has. It could be, like Marcus Aurelius said, energy, or modesty, or generosity. A beautiful spirit. A good work ethic. On your in-breath, think about this positive quality. On your out-breath, mentally say thank you to the person. Thank you for your energy. Thank you for showing me the quality of modesty. Thank you for the generosity you demonstrate. Whatever it is, continue to hold that person’s good qualities in your mind, sending them thanks with every breath out.

\[45s\]

Now, can you think of a quality of your own that you would like to share with this special person? What is within your power to share with them—love, gratitude, attention, or something else? 

\[20s\]

Visualize how you can share that quality you just generated with this person. Picture yourself acting in such a way that expresses love, gratitude, or another virtue. 

\[45s\]

On your next in-breath, hold this person in your mind, cheerful and happy, their good qualities radiating out toward you. On your out-breath, bring to mind the virtue that you will share with them. Continue breathing in and out, both enjoying and sharing these good qualities.

\[45s\]

Now relax your attention. Let thoughts come and let them be.

\[20s\]

Take a few deep breaths.

\[20s\]

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

**Outro**

As you get up and go about your day, hold those images of cheerfulness, appreciation, and sharing in your mind. Let these powerful good emotions guide your thoughts and actions for the rest of the day. Come back to this meditation as often as you wish and focus on other people you know. Because, as Marcus Aurelius reminds us, “there is nothing more heartening than the images of the virtues shining forth in the characters of those around us, and assembled together, so far as possible, in close array. So be sure to keep them ever at hand.”

## Improving Romantic Relationships

**Intro**

In our romantic partnerships, we can sometimes get caught up in the petty, everyday annoyances and forget that behind the small disagreements is the person we love. We forget the bigger picture. We allow small grievances to accumulate and blind us to our higher purpose in the relationship.

The great Stoic teacher Musonius Rufus thought that the highest goal of romantic relationships is creating a harmonious partnership of companionship and care. Maintaining a strong partnership is a worthwhile goal in itself.

Today let’s meditate on creating a loving, harmonious bond with our romantic partner.

**Meditation**

Begin in a comfortable seated position. Close your eyes. Take a deep breath in. And a deep breath out. 

\[16s\]

Choose a happy memory of you together with your partner. Hold this image in your mind as you breathe in and out. Think about the bond you share together. You love your partner. Do you also value the relationship you have created with your partner? Do you value the shared experiences, the shared commitment? 

\[16s\]

Continue breathing in and out as you reflect on the bond you have created over time with your partner.

\[45s\]

Now let’s strengthen your bond with this wonderful person. Mentally reach out and hold their hand. As you touch them, feel the bright, warm love pulsing from your hand to theirs. You smile as you send strength, care, and commitment to the person you love. They smile back as they receive your love.

\[30s\]

As you continue sending radiant love to your partner through your clasped hands, you begin to feel love radiating from them back to you. You feel a warmth climbing from your fingertips, up through your arm, into your shoulders, your chest, your heart. You are not only sending love, but receiving it too. Feel the warmth and contentment of giving and receiving love. Hold this warmth in your mind and body.

\[45s\]

Now, without breaking your bond with your partner, bring to mind something they do that drives you crazy. An annoyance or irritation you sometimes experience with your partner. Does it feel important now, bathed as you are in your bond of shared commitment and love? 

\[20s\]

Look your partner in the eye, smile, and say, “It doesn’t matter. That small thing is not as important as my love for you.” Feel that annoyance simply roll off your back and disappear into nothingness.

\[15s\]

Say it again. Look your partner in the eye, smile, and say, “It doesn’t matter. That small thing is not as important as my love for you.” Feel the warmth and strength of your bond traveling between your clasped hands. Feel your partner’s warm, happy smile as they smile back at you.

\[15s\]

Now relax your attention. Let your mind wander for a moment.

\[20s\]

Take a few deep breaths.

\[20s\]

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

**Outro**

We’ve now practiced strengthening our bond with the very special person we share our lives with. We all experience annoyances sometimes, but unless your partner is acting unethically toward you, remember that they are a good person and that your relationship itself is important. This partnership will support you through both good and bad times. And, as Musonius Rufus reminds us, a partnership in which both people provide love, care, and companionship is both beneficial and valuable: “such a partnership is beautiful.”

As you go about your day, stay focused not only on the positive qualities of your partner, but also on the positive benefits of being in a committed relationship. It’s not just any goal you’re working toward, but the long-term goal of a beautiful partnership.

## Dealing with Difficult Family Relationships

**Intro**

We usually don’t get to choose our family members. That means we sometimes need to navigate family relationships with people who have very different values than we do. 

But do you think this is a new problem? No, it’s been around for a long time. Two thousand years ago Epictetus was talking with a student who complained about his brother. “My brother shouldn’t have treated me in this way,” the student said.

Epictetus replied: “Indeed he shouldn't, but it's for him to see to that. For my part, however he treats me, I should conduct myself towards him as I ought. For that is my business, and the rest is not my concern.”

Today we’ll meditate on conducting ourselves as we ought, regardless of what our family members do.

**Meditation**

Begin in a comfortable seated position. Close your eyes. Take a deep breath in. And a deep breath out. 

\[16s\]

Bring to mind a family member you are having trouble getting along with, or perhaps a family member you don’t see eye to eye with on certain issues. Hold this person in your mind’s eye, reflecting for a moment on the family relationship that binds you together.

\[15s\]

Start by generating warm feelings of care and compassion for this person. Even if you disagree with some of the things they do, you are still connected by a family bond, and that is not to be taken lightly. As you breathe in, picture this person looking friendly and happy. As you breathe out, send well wishes to them: “May you be well. May you be happy.”

\[45s\]

Now let’s focus on your role and responsibilities in relation to this person. Just because they do not properly fulfill their role toward you doesn’t mean you shouldn’t fulfill your role toward them. We still behave properly even when others don’t. Sometimes our roles can provide guidance when we feel conflicted or don’t know what to do. 

\[30s\]

This time, as you breathe in, think about your role relative to this person. Say your role in your mind, for example “sister,” “son,” or “mother.” Breathing out, think about the best qualities of someone performing this role, for example “strength,” “wisdom,” “understanding.” Continue to breathe, and with each out-breath identify a Stoic quality you would like to embody.

\[45s\]

On your next breath in, picture your family member again. Smile at them. As you breathe out, feel yourself filling with one of those qualities you want to embody. Breathe out, picture the other person. Breathe in, fill your lungs, your body, your mind, with wisdom and strength. 

\[45s\]

Now relax your attention. Let the mind wander to wherever feels natural.

\[20s\]

Take a few deep breaths.

\[20s\]

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

**Outro**

You’ve done some difficult work here today. You might want to repeat this meditation on a daily basis, or before your next encounter with your difficult family member. Just remember that, even when other people do not act appropriately, it is still within our power to fulfill our role toward them. 

Epictetus reminds us how important these roles are by comparing our family role to a profession. “Now if you were a blacksmith and made poor use of your hammer,” he says, “you’d have forgotten what you are as a blacksmith; so if you forget what you are as a brother, and become an enemy instead…have you lost nothing at all?” 

The point is, we actually injure ourselves when we stop behaving as a family member should. In order to maintain our own integrity, our own self-respect, our own humanity, we should behave well, even when others don’t. We benefit both ourselves and everyone else when we properly fulfill our role, even toward difficult family members.

## Dealing with Difficult Colleagues at Work

**Intro**

The quality of our relationships at work can have a big impact on our lives. But most of the time we don’t get to choose who we work with. We may find ourselves forced to play nicely with people we really don’t like.

Marcus Aurelius had this same problem as Roman Emperor two thousand years ago. He had to work with backstabbers, cheaters, thieves, gossips, social climbers, and other people with very different values from himself. And for him, the stakes were really high: the entire Roman Empire depended on his performance.

Marcus had an important principle he used to help him deal with unpleasant colleagues. He said, “If you can, show them the error of their ways; but if you cannot, remember that kindness was granted to you for this.”

Today, let’s meditate on Marcus’s words and apply them to our own lives at work.

**Meditation**

Begin in a comfortable seated position. Close your eyes. Take a deep breath in. And a deep breath out. 

\[20s\]

Bring to mind a person you are having trouble getting along with at work. Let’s start by recognizing their basic humanity and some things you have in common. Like you, this person wants to be valued, respected, loved. They may go about it in the wrong way, but that’s what they desire, like everyone else. 

\[20s\]

Let’s send them some of those things today. For a moment, try to step outside your relationship as a colleague and just think of them as a person who needs help. On your next in-breath, picture this person in your mind. As you breathe out, send them a kind wish for wellbeing: “May you be well. May you be happy.” Continue breathing, sending well wishes with every breath.

\[45s\]

Now we’re going to picture you dealing calmly and kindly with this person at work. In your mind, walk over to this person with a serene smile on your face. You are not angry with them—why should you be? You have no reason to be angry just because someone is difficult to deal with. You continue smiling—you want only good things for both of you. 

\[40s\]

First, let’s practice gently showing this person the error of their ways, to use Marcus Aurelius’s expression. Picture yourself smiling at them and saying kindly but firmly, “I disagree.” Remember, you are not angry, you don’t depend on the other person for your happiness or your character. It’s a simple disagreement. On your in-breath, breathe in confidence, contentment, wisdom. On your out-breath, hold onto that serenity while you tell the other person, “I disagree.”

\[45s\]

Now let’s focus on the second part of Marcus’s instructions: remember, kindness was granted to you to deal with people like this. You can’t change this person, but you can maintain your own integrity while dealing with them. As you breathe in, continue to fortify yourself with wisdom, courage, self-control, and other helpful virtues. As you breathe out, send these virtues to your colleague. 

\[45s\]

Now relax your attention. Let thoughts come and let them be.

\[20s\]

Take a few deep breaths.

\[20s\]

And, if your eyes are closed, open them.

**Outro**

As you go about your day, remember that you can show kindness to someone even if they are morally misguided. You can even take legitimate action to rectify their mistake, while still showing compassion and kindness. 

Showing kindness doesn’t mean being a doormat or letting people walk all over you. It means taking action in a way that shows you recognize the underlying humanity of the other person. You can deal with problems effectively while maintaining your own good character.

As we close this series of meditations on compassion, let’s reflect once again on the inner peace and even joy that results from a compassionate approach to relationships. By now you have learned that, when you show compassion and understanding to others, the person who actually benefits most is you. Not only does compassion allow you to maintain your equanimity while dealing with the challenges of social relationships, but it also makes you more effective at navigating those relationships. You get to experience the positive emotions that accompany compassion, and you get to build and strengthen lifelong relationships with other people. There are no downsides to compassion.

Thank you for joining me on this exploration of Stoic compassion in relationships. I’d like to share one final quote from Marcus Aurelius, one of my favorites: “A human being finds delight in doing what is proper to a human being; and what is proper to us is to show goodwill to our own kind.”

Take this goodwill and delight with you into your life, as you share wisdom and compassion with the people around you.
